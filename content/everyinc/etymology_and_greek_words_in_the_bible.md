## Linguistic Differences

The Hebrew language is characterized as "right-brained," emphasizing intuition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>. In contrast, Greek is considered "left-brained," leaning towards analytical and logical thought <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. These two languages, combined in the Bible, can be seen as representing the two hemispheres of the brain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>.

The Book of Acts, written by Luke, who was a physician known for his precision, provides a valuable lens through which to observe these linguistic nuances in action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>. Luke's Greek writing is translated into English in various ways, revealing different interpretations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.

## Unpacking the Greek Word "Aragos"

An example of how etymology reveals deeper meaning is found in the Greek word *aragos* (ἀρχηγός) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. This word appears in several New Testament books, including Acts 3, Acts 5, Hebrews 2, and Hebrews 12 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.

When examining [[bible_translations_and_their_differences | Bible translations and their differences]] for Acts 3:15, discrepancies emerge:
*   The English Standard Version (ESV) and New International Version (NIV) translate *aragos* as "author" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>.
*   The King James Version (KJV) translates it as "Prince" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.

These two English words, "author" and "Prince," convey vastly different meanings: an author writes, while a prince leads <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>. However, the original Greek word *aragos* encompasses both concepts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>. It can mean:
*   Author <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>
*   Leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>
*   Prince <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>
*   Pioneer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>

This synthesis in Greek reveals a multifaceted meaning that English translations often split <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:56">[00:04:56]</a>.
