### The "Japanese Toilet" of AI
Rory Southerland categorizes innovation into three types:
1.  **Faster Horses:** Making existing things perform better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:01">[00:00:01]</a>.
2.  **Teleportation:** Desired innovations that don't yet have a clear path to existence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:03">[00:00:03]</a>.
3.  **Japanese Toilets:** Innovations that people didn't know they needed until they experienced them, which then become indispensable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>.

In the context of AI, the focus is on finding the "Japanese toilets of AI" – new experiences that surprise and delight users, rather than merely creating "faster horses" that offer incremental improvements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>. Many AI products currently being funded fall into the "faster horses" category, which may be arbitraged out of existence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:46">[00:14:46]</a>. The true value lies in discovering entirely new experiences that emerge from AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>. An example of such an AI "Japanese toilet" product is Granola, an intuitive AI notetaker that subtly enhances user intelligence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:31">[00:14:31]</a>. Its initial version, a tab-complete co-pilot, was too interruptive, leading to a more seamless integration that doesn't disrupt user focus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:23">[00:18:23]</a>.

## The Agentic Spectrum of AI
AI coding agents operate on a spectrum based on how long they are allowed to work before requiring feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>.
*   **Microsoft Co-pilot:** Works on a short leash, attempting to finish one line of code <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:48">[00:24:48]</a>.
*   **Devon:** Operates on a long leash, potentially running for four hours before seeking input <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:54">[00:24:54]</a>.

Finding the right "leash" setting is crucial for product innovation, as it impacts the user interface and overall flow of the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:34">[00:31:34]</a>. This involves continuous re-evaluation as new models are released <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:39">[00:31:39]</a>.

## Navigating AI Models and Trust
Users often choose between different AI models based on their needs:
*   **Claude:** Favored for tasks where language and brainstorming are important, or for auto-completing text in writing apps like Lex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:25">[00:42:25]</a>.
*   **GPT-4 / OpenAI's models (01 Pro):** Preferred for deeper reasoning, following progressions, and putting together narratives, especially when accuracy and specific answers are needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:50">[00:42:50]</a>. It's also trusted for checking complex statistical work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:40">[00:44:40]</a>.

A significant challenge with AI is managing trust in its outputs, especially when managing tasks one isn't qualified to do oneself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:02">[00:45:02]</a>. Users often ask models to explain their logic, provide confidence intervals, or offer reasons for both correct and incorrect answers to assess reliability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:08">[00:46:08]</a>.
