## Core Arguments for Open Borders

Kaplan advocates for open borders primarily based on economic benefits and the unlocking of human potential.

### Economic Benefits
A central argument from Kaplan's book "Open Borders" is the significant economic gain achievable by allowing people to move from poor to rich countries. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:50">[01:12:50]</a> He states it's an undeniable fact that a worker from a poor country, like Haiti, moved to a rich country, like the US (e.g., Miami), can see their pay multiply many times almost overnight, even before learning the local language. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:53">[01:12:53]</a> The textbook economic explanation is that productivity is much higher in rich countries, not due to inherent differences in the individuals, but due to the environment and systems in place. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:14">[01:13:14]</a> Kaplan argues that the primary issue is with the conditions in countries like Haiti, not with Haitians themselves. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:28">[01:13:28]</a>

He believes these benefits are scalable, meaning that moving millions or even billions of people could rescue them from poverty in a short amount of time, essentially fast-forwarding global development. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:51">[01:13:51]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:28">[01:14:28]</a> The missed opportunity due to immigration restrictions is valued in hundreds of trillions of dollars within a century. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:48">[01:15:48]</a> This is reminiscent of discussions around [[economic_growth_and_ai|AI and economic growth]], where unlocking potential can have far-reaching economic impacts.

### Unlocking Human Talent
Immigration restrictions trap human talent in poor countries. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:45">[01:14:45]</a> Kaplan suggests that individuals who could make significant contributions, such as finding a cure for death five or ten years earlier, might currently be in a poor village in India or China, their potential unrealized due to lack of opportunity. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:48">[01:14:48]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:10">[01:15:10]</a> Accelerating the timeline for such breakthroughs by even a few years would be immensely valuable. This aligns with [[economic_inequality_and_rent_seeking|discussions on economic inequality]], where barriers can prevent the realization of potential contributions that could benefit society as a whole. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:16">[01:15:16]</a>

## Keyhole Solutions for Immigration Concerns

Kaplan's book "Open Borders" discusses "Keyhole Solutions" as ways to address specific complaints about immigration short of stopping people from entering or deporting those already present. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>

*   **Example:** If the concern is about immigrants voting poorly, a keyhole solution could be to not extend voting rights to immigrants or to make them wait for a longer period. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a>
*   **Stability:** Kaplan believes the distinction between citizen and non-citizen is a strong "focal point" that offers stability to such solutions, at least initially. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a> However, he acknowledges that multi-generational keyhole solutions (e.g., where children and grandchildren of immigrants remain non-citizens) would likely be hard to maintain. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>

### Objections to Keyhole Solutions
The interviewer raises the "slippery slope" argument often made by immigration restrictionists: that keyhole solutions are inherently unstable and will eventually lead to immigrants gaining full rights, potentially resulting in adverse consequences that the keyhole solutions were meant to prevent. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a> Kaplan concedes this is one of the "very best objections" to keyhole solutions. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>

## Refugee Absorption and Policy: The Poland-Ukraine Example

Kaplan discusses his observations from a trip to Eastern Europe, particularly Poland, following the influx of Ukrainian refugees.

*   **Capacity for Absorption:** Poland increased its population by approximately 10% in a single month due to Ukrainian refugees, and Kaplan observed that the country "looked fine" and people were not complaining. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:03">[01:24:03]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:14">[01:24:14]</a> This would be equivalent to the U.S. taking in 33 million refugees in a month. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:30">[01:25:30]</a> Such a rapid and large-scale integration could reflect on themes discussed in [[geopolitical_implications_on_technology_and_data_centers]], where infrastructure and social systems play a crucial role.
*   **Role of Public Perception and Political Will:** Kaplan contrasts this with situations where "a thousand refugees from a country you don't like" arrive, leading to public outcry about being at a "breaking point." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:26">[01:24:26]</a> He attributes Poland's success to a "where there's a will, there's a way" attitude, stemming from sympathy for the Ukrainian refugees. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:22">[01:24:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:34">[01:24:34]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:45">[01:24:45]</a> He states that if people think refugees are bad, they will see bad things; if they are supportive and have a "can-do attitude," absorption is completely doable. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:49">[01:25:49]</a>
*   **Importance of Work Rights:** Polish policy allowed Ukrainian refugees to work from the day they arrived. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:48">[01:24:48]</a> This is contrasted with common refugee policies where individuals are prevented from working and kept in a "semi-prisoner" status on welfare for years. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:52">[01:24:52]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:14">[01:25:14]</a>
*   **Expanded View of "Doable":** The Polish experience expanded Kaplan's own view of what level of refugee intake is feasible. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:59">[01:26:59]</a> He observed refugees in Polish train stations appearing happy, with children playing, a stark contrast to the human misery one might expect. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:37">[01:27:37]</a>

## Strategy for Change: The Overton Window

The argument is raised, applying a concept from Kaplan's "Open Borders," that advocating for radical positions like open borders can shift the "Overton window," making previously unacceptable policies seem more moderate and thus more achievable. Discussions similar to those around [[the_potential_economic_and_social_impacts_of_agi]] can be observed, where advocating for advanced technological implementations can transform perspectives on acceptability. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:46">[00:17:46]</a>

## Persistence of Harms from Restriction

Kaplan argues that the harms from immigration restriction are significant and persistent. Missing out on the potential for 100 trillion dollars in global wealth, especially during a time when many are poorer than they will likely ever be again, matters a great deal. The economic effects resonate with themes in [[economic_growth_and_technological_acceleration]], illustrating the profound impact that policy can have on long-term economic trajectories. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:04">[01:12:04]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:30">[01:12:30]</a>

## Defending Real-World Immigration

Kaplan emphasizes that his arguments in "Open Borders" defend immigration "as it really is," not just under idealized or optimal conditions, in response to concerns that policies often fail due to poor real-world implementation.
