The construction of the Burj Khalifa marked a significant turning point in the history of skyscrapers, not only for its unprecedented height but also for its profound economic and cultural impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>.

## A New Era of Height
Before the Burj Khalifa, the tallest buildings typically surpassed their predecessors by a margin of 5% to 19% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:00">[00:42:00]</a>. The Burj Khalifa, completed on October 1, 2009 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>, was designed to be 829 meters tall, making it 62% taller than the second-tallest building at the time, Taipei 101, which stood at a little over 500 meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:00">[00:15:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:00">[00:29:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:00">[00:49:00]</a>. This deliberate margin was intended to discourage immediate attempts to break its record <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:00">[00:33:00]</a>. As of over 13 years later, the record remains unbroken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>.

Historically, tall structures were built for monarchs or deities, such as the Great Pyramid of Giza <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. A major turning point occurred with the construction of the world's first skyscraper, Chicago's Home Insurance building in 1884, which was designed for human habitation and office space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:00">[02:37:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:43:00">[02:43:00]</a>. Early competition for tall buildings was concentrated in New York and Chicago due to America's economic boom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:16:00">[03:16:00]</a>. The Empire State Building, for instance, held the record for 40 years from 1931 to 1971 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:00">[03:40:00]</a>. By 1998, the competition shifted to Asia, with buildings like the Petronas Towers and Taipei 101 holding the record before Burj Khalifa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:04:00">[04:04:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:10:00">[04:10:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:19:00">[04:19:00]</a>.

## Dubai's Economic Diversification and Global Recognition
The decision to build Burj Khalifa was strategic for Dubai. Prior to the 1990s, Dubai was primarily a fishing and pearl diving village that had recently discovered oil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:08:00">[05:08:00]</a>. By the late 1990s and early 2000s, Dubai's leaders sought to diversify their economy beyond oil dependence and establish the city as an international tourism hub <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:13:00">[05:13:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:19:00">[05:19:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:21:00">[05:21:00]</a>. Building an "unimaginable and record-breaking" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:00">[00:21:00]</a> structure like the Burj Khalifa was a means to capture international attention and attract foreign investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:25:00">[05:25:00]</a>.

The economic purpose of building the Burj Khalifa proved highly successful. Today, Dubai is a major international tourism and economic hub <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:13:00">[06:13:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:17:00">[06:17:00]</a>. Companies are eager to establish offices in Dubai, and people want to live and buy property there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:19:00">[06:19:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:22:00">[06:22:00]</a>. Property prices in the downtown area where Burj Khalifa is located have "skyrocketed" to the extent that the $1.5 billion construction cost was easily recovered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:57:00">[05:57:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:28:00">[06:28:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:31:00">[06:31:00]</a>.

Culturally, the Burj Khalifa also had a significant impact. It serves as a centerpiece attraction for Dubai <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>. Its exterior forms the world's largest LED screen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:40:00">[08:40:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:43:00">[08:43:00]</a>, enabling light shows, colourful designs, and even movie trailers to be displayed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:45:00">[08:45:00]</a>. This feature further enhances its iconic status and global appeal, solidifying its place as a symbol of Dubai's modern ambition.

## Challenges in Surpassing the Burj Khalifa
Despite the success of the Burj Khalifa, its record has remained unbroken for over a decade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>. While 17 of the current 20 tallest buildings were built after the Burj Khalifa, none have surpassed its height <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:37:00">[10:37:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:40:00">[10:40:00]</a>. Several planned projects, such as the Jeddah Tower in Saudi Arabia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:10:00">[11:10:00]</a> and the Dubai Creek Tower <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>, have faced construction halts primarily due to economic and political factors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:50:00">[11:50:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:05:00">[12:05:00]</a>.

### Socio-Economic and Financial Limitations
The biggest challenge to building taller structures is money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:18:00">[18:18:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:23:00">[18:23:00]</a>. There's no guarantee that every supertall building project will achieve the same economic success as the Burj Khalifa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:23:00">[18:23:00]</a>. Investors face extremely high risks when funding projects that require thousands of millions of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:31:00">[18:31:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:34:00">[18:34:00]</a>.

Furthermore, governmental support is crucial, but many governments may not support such projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:39:00">[18:39:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:42:00">[18:42:00]</a>. Funds could often be utilized more effectively for other public services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:45:00">[18:45:00]</a>. For example, in countries like India, investing all funds in the tallest possible building would be a "huge wastage" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:51:00">[18:51:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:55:00">[18:55:00]</a>. The Chinese government, recognizing the vanity and economic inefficiency, recently banned buildings taller than 500 meters, deeming them a "wastage of money" and energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:00:00">[19:00:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:05:00">[19:05:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:12:00">[19:12:00]</a>.

Only a few cities worldwide possess the unique socio-economic conditions to support the construction of the world's tallest buildings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:29:00">[19:29:00]</a>. Historically, these were New York and Chicago during their economic booms, requiring skyscrapers to accommodate growing populations and businesses in limited space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:38:00">[19:38:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:46:00">[19:46:00]</a>. In the 21st century, some Chinese and Taiwanese cities saw skyscraper growth, but not to an unprofitable extreme height <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:52:00">[19:52:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:57:00">[19:57:00]</a>. The Middle Eastern countries like UAE and Saudi Arabia, with their oil wealth and desire for economic diversification, have been unique in their ability to undertake such massive projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:06:00">[20:06:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:08:00">[20:08:00]</a>.
