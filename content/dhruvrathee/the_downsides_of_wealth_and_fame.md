## Money Cannot Buy Essential Happiness
The common belief that more money leads to more happiness is often challenged by the experiences of the ultra-rich <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a>. While money can purchase material goods like a bed or a watch, it cannot buy sleep or time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>. Similarly, it can buy food but not the desire to eat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>. The idea that if money doesn't buy happiness, one simply doesn't know how to spend it, is countered by the argument that poverty buys nothing at all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>. However, a base level of money is acknowledged as necessary for a decent standard of living and to achieve a certain level of happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:46">[00:13:46]</a>.

### The Businessman and the Fisherman
A famous story illustrates this point:
A businessman encounters a fisherman relaxing on the beach and suggests he work harder to catch more fish, earn more money, buy nets, then a boat, hire employees, and eventually, with immense wealth, relax on the beach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:00">[01:46:00]</a>. The fisherman points out that he is already doing exactly that—relaxing on the beach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:00">[03:09:00]</a>. This story questions the endless pursuit of wealth for a future state of relaxation that might already be attainable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:00">[03:09:00]</a>. It suggests that many people who don't earn a lot of money or indulge in luxuries are still happy and satisfied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:24:00">[04:24:00]</a>.

## [[psychological_effects_of_wealth | Psychological Effects of Wealth and Fame]]

#### Family Disputes Over Money
Even within families, wealth can be a source of intense conflict. ### Mental Health Struggles
Many wealthy and famous individuals experience significant mental health challenges:
*   **Elon Musk** himself has tweeted about the "great highs, terrible lows, and unrelenting stress" of his reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:00">[04:47:00]</a>. Reports indicate he takes microdoses of ketamine to combat depression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a>. When asked for advice for young people aspiring to be like him, Musk advised against it, stating, "I'm not sure I wanna be me" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:08:00">[08:08:00]</a>.
*   **Therapists to the super-rich** report that their clients, including billionaires, often struggle with shame, guilt, fear, and severe trust issues, leading to stressful and miserable lives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:23:00">[08:23:00]</a>. This echoes depictions in TV shows like *Succession* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:27:00">[08:27:00]</a>.
*   **Celebrity Suicides:** Several famous personalities, despite their wealth, have died by suicide, including actress Pratyusha Banerjee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:51:00">[08:51:00]</a>, Hollywood actor Robin Williams, whose jovial public persona hid deep sorrows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:59:00">[08:59:00]</a>, and Nirvana frontman Kurt Cobain, who took his life at the peak of his career at 27 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:09:00">[09:09:00]</a>.
*   **Public Struggles with Depression:** Other [[celebrity_influence_on_public_perception_and_behavior | celebrities]] have openly discussed their battles with depression, such as Kapil Sharma, who experienced suicidal tendencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:25:00">[09:25:00]</a>, and Deepika Padukone, who actively promotes mental health awareness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:39:00">[09:39:00]</a>.

## The Illusion of Social Media and Motivational Gurus
Social media often promotes an unrealistic image of wealth and happiness, fueling a "race for money" where people feel compelled to buy luxury items to project a certain self-image or to compete with others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:49:00">[14:49:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:00:00">[15:00:00]</a>. The self-image one projects cannot be bought with money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:52:00">[14:52:00]</a>.

Some "motivational gurus" exacerbate this problem by encouraging people to make earning money their primary goal, often through aggressive marketing tactics that instill fear of being "left behind" if they don't become millionaires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:37:00">[15:37:00]</a>. This pushes the idea that being a millionaire is the only real purpose in life, ignoring the value of other professions like scientists, teachers, or doctors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:51:00">[15:51:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:13:00">[17:13:00]</a>. Such narratives overlook that money should be a by-product of one's work and efforts, not the sole purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:25:00">[16:25:00]</a>.

## [[life_priorities_and_regrets | Life Priorities and Regrets]]
An Australian palliative care nurse, Bronnie Ware, documented the [[life_priorities_and_regrets | biggest regrets of the dying]] in her book *The Top 5 Regrets of the Dying* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:06:00">[18:06:00]</a>. Among these common regrets were:
1.  Not having the courage to live a life true to oneself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:34:00">[18:34:00]</a>.
2.  Wishing they hadn't worked so hard, regretting the neglect of family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:46:00">[18:46:00]</a>.
3.  Wishing they had expressed their feelings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:55:00">[18:55:00]</a>.
4.  Wishing they had stayed in touch with friends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:02:00">[19:02:00]</a>.
5.  Wishing they had let themselves be happier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:09:00">[19:09:00]</a>.

Significantly, not a single person on their deathbed regretted not earning more money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:17:00">[19:17:00]</a>. This emphasizes that relationships, personal expression, and happiness are ultimately valued over material wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:22:00">[19:22:00]</a>.

## Escaping the Cycle of Materialism
Philosophies like Hinduism and Jainism advocate for non-possessiveness, while Gautam Buddha's "Middle Way" encourages living in the world and enjoying it without total immersion in material things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:41:00">[19:41:00]</a>. The more one owns, the more maintenance and preoccupation it requires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:02:00">[20:02:00]</a>. This leads to a "rat race" where people become slaves to their lifestyle, constantly seeking new possessions as old ones lose their appeal, failing to find permanent happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:09:00">[20:09:00]</a>.

Real happiness is found in doing what one enjoys, having a sense of purpose, helping others, and sharing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:31:00">[20:31:00]</a>. Concepts like *Daan* (donation) in Hinduism, *Zakat* in Islam, and *Dasvandh* (donating one-tenth of income) in Sikhism all emphasize the importance of giving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:42:00">[20:42:00]</a>.
