## [[history_and_discovery_of_black_holes | Scientific and Theoretical Aspects of Black Holes]]

The [[history_and_discovery_of_black_holes | history of black holes]] is relatively short; 100 years ago, they were unknown <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:55">[00:01:55]</a>. Their eventual [[history_and_discovery_of_black_holes | discovery]] was due to [[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein's Theory of Relativity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>.

### [[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein's Theory of Relativity]]

[[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein's theory]] has two parts: the Special Theory of Relativity (1905) and the General Theory of Relativity (1915) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

*   **Special Theory of Relativity**: This theory explains how speed influences time. If a spaceship travels very fast, time slows down for those on board relative to observers on Earth, a phenomenon known as Kinematic Time Dilation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>.
*   **General Theory of Relativity**: Developed in 1915, this theory shows that gravity also causes time dilation, known as Gravitational Time Dilation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>. The stronger the gravitational force, the more time slows down <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. This was accurately depicted in *Interstellar* when Cooper and his team landed on a planet where one hour equaled seven years on Earth due to its proximity to the Gargantua black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:11">[00:03:11]</a>.

[[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein]] visualized this through a space-time fabric, a mesh that bends due to the mass of objects placed on it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. This bending not only attracts physical objects but also leads to time dilation and affects other forms of energy like sound, heat, and light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. This meant that objects with such extreme gravitational force that they could completely absorb light were theoretically possible, appearing completely black because light cannot escape them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>. These are black holes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:32">[00:04:32]</a>.

### From Theory to Reality

When [[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein]] presented his General Theory of Relativity, the concept of black holes was purely theoretical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. He found the idea of their real existence strange, despite their theoretical possibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>. By the time he passed away, the term "black hole" had not even been coined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.

A key point in [[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein's theory]] was that light's speed limits gravity's influence; gravitational effects are not felt instantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>. For example, if the Sun disappeared, its gravitational impact would be felt on Earth 8 minutes later, just as its light takes 8 minutes to reach us <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:32">[00:05:32]</a>.

After [[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein]], many scientists worked on the General Theory of Relativity, solving equations that theoretically proved the existence of black holes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>. By the 1960s, researchers agreed that black holes likely exist in space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. The term "black hole" was first used by a magazine in 1964 and popularized by physicist John Wheeler in 1967 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</a>. Despite its sensational nature, "black hole" is a misleading name, as there isn't an actual "hole" in space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>.

## Formation of Black Holes

Black holes are formed from stars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>. Stars, like our Sun, maintain equilibrium throughout their lives due to an outward force from continuous nuclear fusion reactions (producing heat and light) and an inward gravitational force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. When a star's fuel (Hydrogen or Helium) runs out, the outward force ceases, and the gravitational force causes the star to collapse on itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>.

The outcome of this collapse depends on the star's mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:46">[00:07:46]</a>:
*   **Small or Average-sized Stars**: Turn into Red Giants, then planetary nebulae, or White Dwarfs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>.
*   **Huge Stars (High Mass)**: Turn into Red Super Giants, then burst into Supernovae <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>. After this, a tiny core remains. If the core is tiny, it's a Neutron Star; anything bigger forms a black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>.

Essentially, when a star's mass collapses due to its own gravity, it becomes small and condensed, forming a black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>. For example, if our Sun were to turn into a black hole, its diameter would be merely 50 km <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>. However, our Sun will not become a black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>. This was proven by Indian-American astrophysicist Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar, who developed the Chandrasekhar Limit (1.4 times the mass of our Sun) for the maximum stable mass of a White Dwarf <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.

## [[types_of_black_holes_and_their_characteristics | Types of Black Holes]]

There are mainly three to four [[types_of_black_holes_and_their_characteristics | types of black holes]]:
*   **Stellar Black Hole**: The most common type, created from stars. Scientists estimate there are 10 million to 1 billion such black holes in our Milky Way Galaxy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
*   **Primordial Black Holes**: Theoretical and hypothetical, these are believed to be as small as an atom but with the mass of a mountain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.
*   **Supermassive Black Hole**: Enormous, with masses more than 1 million Suns combined, fitting into a sphere the size of our Solar System <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>. Scientists believe a supermassive black hole resides at the center of every major galaxy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:04">[00:10:04]</a>. Our Milky Way Galaxy's center holds Sagittarius A* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>. The black hole Gargantua in *Interstellar* was depicted as a supermassive black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.
*   **Intermediate Black Hole**: A possible fourth type, intermediate in size between Stellar and Supermassive black holes, though no proof has been discovered yet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a>.

## Anatomy of a Black Hole and Cinematic Accuracy

Contrary to the common misconception of a giant black ball sucking everything, black holes have distinct features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>.

### Accretion Disk
Around a black hole, there is an orange-colored ring called the Accretion Disk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>. Due to the black hole's immense gravitational pull, gaseous matter and debris are attracted and revolve around it at extremely high speeds, similar to planets orbiting the Sun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:14">[00:11:14]</a>. This matter heats up to over a million degrees Celsius, becoming a glowing, fluid-like fire that emits electromagnetic radiation, primarily X-rays <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>.

The accretion disk was accurately depicted in *Interstellar*, though its actual color would be closer to blue, as human eyes cannot see the X-rays it emits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:06">[00:12:06]</a>. The first photo of a black hole, taken in 2019 by the Event Horizon Telescope, also represented the accretion disk in yellowish-orange for visibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:28">[00:12:28]</a>.

### Doppler Beaming Effect
In the actual photo of a black hole, particles on one side of the accretion disk appear brighter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>. This is due to the Doppler Beaming effect: particles spinning towards us seem brighter, while those spinning away seem dimmer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:51">[00:12:51]</a>.

### Gravitational Lensing (Optical Illusion)
The accretion disk appears to cover the top and bottom of the black hole in cinematic depictions due to an optical illusion caused by gravity bending light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:18">[00:13:18]</a>. Light coming from the parts of the disk hidden behind the black hole is bent around it by gravity, making it visible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:28">[00:13:28]</a>. This illusion only occurs when viewing the black hole from the sides; from the top, it would appear as a normal, round disk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:42">[00:13:42]</a>.

### Photonsphere
Before entering the black hole itself, one would encounter the Photonsphere, a last circle of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>. In this region, gravity is so strong that photons (light particles) orbit the black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:59">[00:13:59]</a>. Theoretically, if one reached this area alive, it might be possible to see the back of one's own head due to the circular path of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:09">[00:14:09]</a>.

### Event Horizon
Beyond the Photonsphere lies the Event Horizon, the boundary of the black hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:23">[00:14:23]</a>. After crossing this point, gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:27">[00:14:27]</a>. Everything beyond this point is black <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:34">[00:14:34]</a>. If you cross the event horizon, there is theoretically no chance of escape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:37">[00:14:37]</a>.

## Inside the Black Hole: Reality vs. Film

*Interstellar*'s depiction of Cooper reaching a five-dimensional space after crossing the Event Horizon is purely imaginative and speculative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:50">[00:14:50]</a>. While the filmmakers hired a Nobel Prize-winning physicist to ensure scientific accuracy, parts unknown to science rely on imagination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:09">[00:15:09]</a>.

### [[scientific_and_theoretical_aspects_of_black_holes | Singularity]]
[[einsteins_theory_of_relativity_and_its_relation_to_black_holes | Einstein's General Theory of Relativity]] describes the center of a black hole as a [[scientific_and_theoretical_aspects_of_black_holes | Singularity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>. This is a region where the curvature of space-time is infinite, stretching infinitely due to the immense mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:36">[00:15:36]</a>. As gravity affects time, energy, and everything else, time theoretically slows down infinitely as gravitational force increases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:56">[00:15:56]</a>. What this truly means, or whether one could ever escape, remains unknown and theoretical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>. Some theories suggest that inside the Event Horizon, light might reflect at multiple points before reaching the [[scientific_and_theoretical_aspects_of_black_holes | Singularity]], making things visible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:30">[00:16:30]</a>.

The existence of black holes was practically proven on April 10, 2019, with the single photo taken by the Event Horizon Telescope, about 100 years after their theoretical proof <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:47">[00:16:47]</a>.

If one were to fall into a black hole, the chances are high that they would disintegrate into pieces due to the gravitational force, dying in milliseconds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:06">[00:17:06]</a>.

## Common Misconceptions

There is no need to be scared of black holes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:17">[00:17:17]</a>. A common misconception is that black holes suck up all matter, continuously growing and eventually ending the universe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:19">[00:17:19]</a>. This is not how they work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:27">[00:17:27]</a>. Instead, supermassive black holes at the center of each galaxy act as gravitational anchors, with other planetary bodies and stars revolving around them, similar to planets orbiting the Sun in our Solar System <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:28">[00:17:28]</a>. As long as a proper distance is maintained, objects are safe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:49">[00:17:49]</a>.
