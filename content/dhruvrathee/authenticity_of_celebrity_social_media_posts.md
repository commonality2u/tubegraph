## The Evolution of Paparazzi Culture

The term "paparazzi" refers to individuals whose job is to photograph celebrities and high-profile figures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:55">[01:43:55]</a>. The word originated from a 1960 Italian film, *La Dolce Vita*, where a character named Paparazzo was an annoying yet fearless reporter willing to go to extremes for celebrity photos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:22">[02:10:22]</a>. The director chose the name because "Paparazzo" is an Italian slang for a large, annoying mosquito, symbolizing the reporter's intrusive nature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:28:34">[02:28:34]</a>. The term gained wider popularity after a 1961 *Time* magazine article described paparazzi as "a pack of hungry wolves" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:23:36">[04:23:36]</a>.

Historically, paparazzi culture was known for secretly taking photos of celebrities without their permission, often disregarding their privacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:40:02">[04:40:02]</a>. A notable incident involved the 1997 car accident of Princess Diana, where paparazzi cars were shamelessly following her vehicle, leading to her driver speeding up and causing the fatal crash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:33">[04:50:33]</a>. This incident prompted global questioning of the [[paparazzi_culture_and_privacy_invasions | paparazzi culture and privacy invasions]]' ethical boundaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:13:58">[05:13:58]</a>.

In India, paparazzi culture began in the 1990s after economic liberalization, with the rise of tabloid magazines like *Stardust*, *Midday*, and *Bombay Times* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:20:00">[05:20:00]</a>. India's first paparazzo, Yogen Shah, would sell candid photos of celebrities to these publications, fulfilling the public's curiosity about their private lives before the advent of social media <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:34:39">[05:34:39]</a>.

However, with social media, the system has reversed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:05:07">[06:05:07]</a>. Paparazzi now often receive direct payments from celebrities to take their "candid" photos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:09:07">[06:09:07]</a>. Many paparazzi openly admit that smaller, newer stars often pay them for publicity and PR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:16:38">[06:16:38]</a>. This creates two models:
*   **Paid PR**: Celebrities seeking publicity pay paparazzi for staged "candid" photos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:39:09">[06:39:09]</a>.
*   **Genuine Candid**: Top A-list celebrities, like Shahrukh Khan, Aamir Khan, or Virat Kohli, who don't need publicity, are still secretly photographed by paparazzi due to public interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:46:11">[06:46:11]</a>.

*   **Social Media PR**: Many social media accounts that regularly post about Bollywood and celebrities feature PR and paid promotion content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:10:04">[08:10:04]</a>. An example is an Instagram post from 'Instant Bollywood' that mistakenly included direct instructions from the celebrity about what to write in the caption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:24:28">[08:24:28]</a>.
*   **Fake Twitter Trends and Reviews**: When a new movie is released, its hashtag often trends on Twitter, filled with numerous tweets praising the film <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:53:57">[08:53:57]</a>. These tweets often use identical language and phrases, indicating they are fake and originate from PR companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:14:14">[09:14:14]</a>. Such PR accounts promote various movies, companies, and even political parties from the same profile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:19:12">[10:19:12]</a>. This highlights the [[criticism_of_indian_media_and_fake_news | criticism of Indian media and fake news]] and the [[role_of_social_media_in_spreading_information | role of social media in spreading information]], often with ulterior motives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:24:40">[10:24:40]</a>.
*   **Corporate Bookings**: A new trend involves film producers engaging in "corporate bookings," where they buy most tickets for a film's initial days to create the illusion of houseful shows and motivate others to watch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:44:48">[11:44:48]</a>. A family-entertainer Bollywood film in 2023 spent ₹200 million on corporate bookings, and two other films later that year spent around ₹700 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:15:00">[12:15:00]</a>. This practice means the reported box office collections often contain lies and frauds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:14">[00:00:14]</a>.
