The [[db_cooper_hijacking_incident | DB Cooper hijacking incident]], which occurred on November 24, 1971, had a significant, albeit indirect, impact on aviation security and future hijacking methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This unsolved mystery led to changes in aircraft design and influenced subsequent hijacking attempts.

## The Hijacking Method and Initial Response
D.B. Cooper (originally identified as Dan Cooper due to a reporter's mistake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:00">[08:42:00]</a>) hijacked Northwest Orient Airlines Flight from Portland to Seattle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:03">[00:00:03]</a>. He presented himself as a businessman, dressed in a suit, white shirt, and black pants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:00">[00:26:00]</a>. Cooper claimed to have a bomb in his briefcase, showing red cylinders, wires, and a battery to air hostess Florence Schaffner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:00">[01:30:00]</a>.

His demands included $200,000 in cash and four parachutes (two front, two back) by 5 PM, along with a fuel truck ready upon landing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:00">[01:45:00]</a>. The airline's president, Donald Nairob, decided to comply fully with Cooper's demands to prevent panic and protect the company's reputation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:00">[02:37:00]</a>. The plane circled Seattle for three hours while the money and parachutes were arranged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:00">[03:36:00]</a>.

After receiving his demands, Cooper released all passengers and most crew members, keeping only pilot and air hostess Tina Mucklow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:10:00">[04:10:00]</a>. The plane was refueled, and Cooper instructed the pilots to fly towards Mexico City at a low altitude (10,000 feet) and minimum speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:34:00">[04:34:00]</a>. Crucially, he ordered Tina to go to the cockpit and lock the door <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:01:00">[05:01:00]</a>. Minutes later, a warning light indicated the rear staircase, a unique feature of Boeing 727 planes, had been deployed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:13:00">[05:13:00]</a>. Cooper then jumped out with the parachutes and the money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:34:00">[05:34:00]</a>.

## Unpreparedness and Lack of Aviation Security
At the time of the hijacking, aviation security measures were significantly less stringent than they are today. The ease with which Cooper boarded, the lack of immediate physical intervention, and the ability to deploy the aft staircase mid-flight highlighted vulnerabilities. The cockpit door, for instance, had no viewing hole, preventing the crew from seeing outside once locked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:47:00">[05:47:00]</a>. Also, smoking was allowed on airplanes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:28:00">[06:28:00]</a>.

Remarkably, no other passenger knew about the hijacking, and no one was killed or injured <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:48:00">[07:48:00]</a>. Air hostess Tina Mucklow was praised for keeping Cooper calm and ensuring his demands were met, preventing a panic situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:59:00">[07:59:00]</a>.

## The "Copycat" Phenomenon
The media coverage of Cooper's successful escape spawned a series of "copycat" hijackings, where individuals attempted to replicate his methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:00:00">[12:00:00]</a>. At least five such attempts occurred, with hijackers using parachutes to jump from planes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:09:00">[12:09:00]</a>.

Notable examples include:
*   **Martin McNally**: On June 24, 1972, he hijacked a plane using the exact same modus operandi, demanding $500,000. He jumped from the plane at a speed of 400-500 km/h, twice Cooper's estimated speed, and survived <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:18:00">[12:18:00]</a>.
*   **Richard Lapoint**: In January 1972, he performed a similar hijack, wearing similar clothes, jumping, and landing safely in the snow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:50:00">[12:50:00]</a>.
*   **Richard McCoy**: Five months after Cooper's incident, McCoy hijacked a Boeing 727, demanding four parachutes and $500,000. He deployed the rear stairs and jumped <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:01:00">[21:01:00]</a>. He was caught by the FBI two days later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:22:00">[21:22:00]</a>.

While these copycats demonstrated that jumping from a plane under similar conditions was survivable, all of them were eventually caught by the police or FBI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:01:00">[25:01:00]</a>.
