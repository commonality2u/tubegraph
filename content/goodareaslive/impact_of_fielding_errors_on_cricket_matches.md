### Contrasting Fortunes with Umpiring Decisions

An earlier incident in Pakistan's innings showcased the nuanced rules of cricket and their impact. When two tail-enders were batting, Monank Patel, the USA captain and wicket-keeper, initially prepared to throw down the stumps for a run-out chance but then decided against it as the batters returned to their crease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:40:00">[27:40:00]</a>. His changed mind resulted in a misthrow that went over the top, leading to two stolen runs by Pakistan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:01:00">[28:01:00]</a>.

However, the umpire ruled that Patel had abandoned his throw for a run-out and was merely passing the ball back to the bowler, which in cricket terms meant the ball had gone dead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:19:00">[28:19:00]</a>. Consequently, the two runs were taken off Pakistan's total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:56:00">[28:56:00]</a>. Ironically, in the super over, a similar situation involving Rizwan's throw was ruled as the game still being alive, resulting in an extra run for USA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:03:00">[29:03:00]</a>. This highlights the fine margins and interpretations of cricket's "archaic lore" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:48:00">[29:48:00]</a> regarding when a ball is dead or alive, which ultimately impacted the scoring.

Ultimately, Pakistan's "execution from the bowler and the Wicket keeper and the Fielding in general was not up to scratch" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:07:00">[24:07:00]</a> in crucial moments, leading to their defeat. This starkly contrasts with the USA's "world-class catch" of Muhammad Rizwan by Steven Taylor, which "set up the rest of that innings for the USA" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:47:00">[03:47:00]</a>.
