#### Ball Movement Under Lights
The ball tends to do more under lights due to three main factors:
1.  **Humidity**: Changes in humidity from day to night can aid swing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:44:00">[0:44:00]</a>.
2.  **Moisture**: The presence of moisture (even slight dew) after dark can help the seam of the ball move more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="1:15:01">[1:15:01]</a>.
3.  **Visibility**: Cricketers attest that the ball is never seen as well at night as it is during the day, making it harder to play even with good light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="1:15:27">[1:15:27]</a>.

Sometimes, batting under lights can become an advantage because the dew makes the ball come onto the bat better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="1:16:14">[1:16:14]</a>. For example, during Travis Head's innings in the final, the same moisture that helped Indian bowlers early on eventually allowed the ball to come onto his bat more easily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="1:16:25">[1:16:25]</a>. This change was evident when Mohammed Siraj, initially effective, resorted to bowling "massive slower balls" because pace deliveries were being smashed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="1:16:35">[1:16:35]</a>.

Overall, while the [[the_role_of_pitch_conditions_in_cricket_outcomes | role of pitch conditions in cricket outcomes]] and toss decisions are significant, they are part of the inherent variability of cricket. Teams must adapt to them, and superior performance on the day often triumphs over perceived conditional advantages.
