## Historical Context

Historically, declarations in Test cricket were often "Punchy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>, with teams regularly declaring around 280 runs ahead, confident in their ability to defend such a lead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>. However, there was also a period where the emphasis was on avoiding defeat. For instance, Brian Lara once batted strategically to ensure the opposition couldn't win, even though he was a naturally attacking cricketer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:43">[00:05:43]</a>. This mindset sometimes led to teams not pushing for a win even when close, as seen with some older Indian teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.

The runs per over rate in Test cricket stagnated around three runs per over until the year 2000, rarely exceeding it in the following years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. This indicates a more cautious approach to scoring.

## Evolution in Modern Cricket

The [[Evolution of Test Cricket strategies | evolution of Test Cricket strategies]] has seen a shift towards more aggressive, [[Impact of modern tactics in Test cricket | result-oriented cricket]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a>. This shift is highlighted by teams like England and Australia increasingly going for wins in situations where draws might have been accepted previously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:33">[00:22:33]</a>. The mindset of actively attacking to force a result, rather than settling for a draw, has become more prevalent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:38">[00:21:38]</a>.

### India's Aggressive Declarations

India's recent Test match victory over Bangladesh, despite two full days being washed out due to rain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>, exemplified this modern approach. India, under Rohit Sharma's captaincy, displayed a "Killer Instinct" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a> by declaring aggressively.

*   **Fast Scoring**: In their second innings, India scored 50 runs in three overs, marking the fastest team 50 in Test cricket history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>. They continued this [[Role of aggressive batting in modern Test Cricket | aggressive batting]], achieving the fastest team 100, 150, 200, and 250 runs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:53">[00:06:53]</a>. Their run rate reached an extraordinary eight runs an over for a significant period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>.
*   **Strategic Declaration**: India declared with a lead of only around 50 runs, aiming to have enough time to bowl Bangladesh out twice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:14">[00:08:14]</a>. This contrasts with a more traditional approach, which might have sought a larger lead (e.g., 400 runs) or an innings victory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>.
*   **Twice the New Ball Opportunity**: Declaring with "half an hour to go" in a day allows the bowling side to attack unset batters with a new ball when conditions (like light) might be challenging for batting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
