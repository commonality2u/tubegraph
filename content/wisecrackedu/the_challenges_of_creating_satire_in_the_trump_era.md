## South Park's Evolving Approach to Satire

*South Park* creators Matt Stone and Trey Parker have noted that both liberals and conservatives often praise the same episodes, each believing the show secretly aligns with their views <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>. After Season 20 was significantly impacted by the 2016 election, they vowed to avoid Trump-related narratives, stating, "We donâ€™t want to be CNN" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:40]</a>.

Despite this, their latest episode manages to critique Donald Trump in a nuanced way without resorting to partisan talking points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. The consistent message throughout the season, however, remains bleak: "we're all f***ed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>.

### "Doubling Down" and the Trump Voter Analogy

In the episode "Doubling Down," *South Park* employs a brilliant parallel narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>. The abusive relationship between Heidi and Cartman mirrors the abusive relationship between Trump supporters and Trump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.

The show uses subtly coded language to draw this connection:
*   Kyle's questioning of Heidi's loyalty to Cartman uses wording identical to critics questioning Trump voters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:03:15]</a>.
*   Kyle explicitly acts as a "liberal reporter" interviewing a "Trump supporter" in his interactions with Heidi <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>.
*   The irony is layered as Kyle uses political polling terms for Cartman, while Paul Ryan uses domestic abuse language to protect "President Garrison" (Trump's stand-in) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
*   Heidi's description of falling for Cartman sounds like reporters explaining how white working-class voters "fell for" Trump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:40">[00:04:44]</a>.
*   Cartman, like Trump, constantly plays the victim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:14]</a>.
*   Heidi represents the "regretful Trump voter" who struggles to admit being wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:15">[00:05:21]</a>.
*   The episode concludes with a montage of Heidi evaluating her relationship and "took-er-jerbs dudes" crying while watching Trump on TV, emphasizing Heidi as a stand-in for white working-class voters struggling to accept their mistake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:39]</a>.

## Critiquing the Liberal Media's Approach

*South Park* is notable for its nuanced critique of liberals, a rarity in Hollywood where most satirical shows tend to mock conservatives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:15]</a>. Shows like *SNL* have "doubled down" on Trump lampoonery, but these efforts often fail to resonate with even vaguely regretful Trump voters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:23]</a>. This climate has made Matt and Trey reluctant to take "easy shots" at Trump or his supporters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:33]</a>.

The episode features Heidi's friends bombarding her with "smug jokes" about Cartman, which only puts Heidi on the defensive and makes her feel "stupid" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:51]</a>. This sequence is a clear [[critique_of_liberal_medias_portrayal_of_trump_voters | critique of Hollywood's "Open Season" on Trump and his supporters]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:56]</a>.

Kyle, often serving as the [[meta_commentary_on_political_satire_in_south_park | meta-voice of reason]] for the show, explicitly states:
> "You can't keep saying 'we told you so' because if you make her feel dumb, she just doubles down and tries to prove to herself that it wasn't dumb." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:16]</a>

This reflects the show's urging for the liberal media to be less condescending towards Trump voters, as shaming them can drive them "right back to Cartman" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:18">[00:07:34]</a>. Just as Trump convinces his base to blame the left, Cartman convinces Heidi to make Kyle her "Jewish scapegoat" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:01">[00:08:06]</a>.

### The Darkening Tone of Anti-Semitism

While Cartman's "Jew jokes" have been a staple of *South Park*, Heidi's final speech to Kyle in "Doubling Down" felt notably different <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:37]</a>. The lack of overt punchlines and Kyle's devastated reaction make *South Park's* [[impact_of_satirical_humor_on_religious_discourse | mockery of anti-Semites]] appear darker than ever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:50]</a>. This shift may acknowledge how fundamentally Cartman's character now sounds following the rise of the alt-right, suggesting the show has consciously "graduated from goofy, childish bigotry to genuinely ugly bigotry" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:55">[00:09:01]</a>.

The episode's outlook remains bleak, with Heidi completely controlled by Cartman and the nation facing an even worse future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:15]</a>. The show poses the question of whether regretful Trump voters will be shamed back into his base, or if there is any hope for those trapped in cycles of abuse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:26]</a>.
