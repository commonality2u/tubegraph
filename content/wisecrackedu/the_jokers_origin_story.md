The [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]], widely regarded as one of [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman's]] best villains, first appeared in 1940’s *Batman #1* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>. His initial inspiration came from Victor Hugo’s hero Gwynplaine, as depicted in the 1928 film adaptation of *The Man Who Laughs* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>.

## Evolution of an Origin

The Joker's motivations have varied, ranging from sadistic to mercenary to entirely inexplicable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>. Despite numerous creative minds working on the character, his outlook on the world has remained remarkably consistent, representing an extreme reaction to the world that many have felt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a>.

### The Red Hood and the Chemical Vat

The very first account of the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] origin was revealed in *Detective Comics #168* in 1951 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. Most versions over the past six decades have followed a similar plotline: a petty villain known as the Red Hood loses a fight to [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]], falls into a vat of skin-bleaching and hair-brightening chemicals, and emerges transformed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>.

### Alan Moore's *The Killing Joke*

DC Comics later expanded on this origin story <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>. Alan Moore’s 1986 graphic novel *The Killing Joke* presented a more tragic origin for the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:33">[00:01:33]</a>. In this version, he was a reluctant villain who lost his humanity after "One Bad Day" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>. This fateful day saw him fail as a stand-up comic, learn of his pregnant wife's death, get framed by the mob, and then fall into disfiguring chemicals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

The core premise of *The Killing Joke* was the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] attempt to turn Commissioner Gordon into another version of himself by subjecting him to a similarly traumatic "bad day" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a>. His goal was to prove that anyone, even the best among us, is only "one bad day away" from becoming like him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.

While *The Killing Joke* is not technically canonical, and the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] himself indicates he isn't sure of its accuracy, subsequent comics, animated series, and games have largely adhered to Moore’s depressing version of his origins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>.

## Trauma and Existential Responses

Both [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] and the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] live their lives as responses to traumatic, world-view-shattering events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>. These events represent an existential crisis common to humanity: how one responds when life destroys their sense of meaning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>.

*   [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] reacts to his parents’ death by upholding a form of conventional morality as a vigilante <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.
*   The [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] "One Bad Day" led him to embrace meaninglessness and violence, operating under the principle that if moral nihilism is correct, then nothing is inherently right or wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>.
*   The villain Two-Face, in contrast, literally flips a coin to decide whether to uphold or rebel against conventional morality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.

According to French existentialist philosopher Jean-Paul Sartre, recognizing the meaninglessness of existence compels individuals to create their own meaning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>. This choice of how to deal with the loss of meaning animates the entire [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] universe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.

## Intertwined Destinies

In some narratives, the origins of [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] and the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] are directly linked. The 1989 film *Batman* connected the characters more explicitly by making two-bit mobster Jack Napier the killer of [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman's parents]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>. Furthermore, it was [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] who later sent Napier tumbling into the chemical vat, thus transforming him into the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>. This created a dynamic where "They gave birth to each other" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.

### The Joker in Nolan's *The Dark Knight*

In Christopher Nolan's 2008 film *The Dark Knight*, [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] and the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] are already fully formed when they meet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. Heath Ledger’s [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] was presented without a consistent origin story <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. He functioned more as an "elemental force of chaos" and was depicted as a "demon summoned by [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman's]] enemies" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. This version suggests that the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] presence in Gotham City was, in essence, [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman's]] fault <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>. By introducing oppressive justice and order, [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] unintentionally threw Gotham’s moral universe out of balance, creating an opportunity for a new kind of chaos and evil to emerge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37">[00:05:37]</a>.

This concept aligns with the philosophy of French sociologist Jean Baudrillard, who argued in *The Transparency of Evil* that the moral progress [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] seeks is impossible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. By attempting to completely cleanse Gotham of evil, [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman]] inadvertently created a "supergerm," or "autoimmunity disease," in the form of the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>. The [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]], in this context, represents a radically antithetical version of [[the_jokers_relationship_with_batman | Batman's]] justice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>.

> "Evil isn’t the opposite of good. If something unpleasant happens to you, that doesn’t mean it's evil. Evil is characterized by a radical indifference to good. This is essentially The Joker." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>

The [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] goal isn't merely to cause harm, but to "completely destroy the concept of what 'good' even means" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. He seeks to corrupt others’ understanding of goodness by seducing them into abandoning their moral foundations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>. This mischievous passion for dismantling the division between good and evil is what makes the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker]] so intriguing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:23">[00:07:23]</a>.

### The "Killing Joke" as an Obsession

The [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] objective is to expose the perceived phoniness of human morality and love, revealing people as more self-serving than they pretend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. His violence stems not from a psychiatric issue, but from an existential one; he is "pathologically sane" and has seen the fragility of conventional morality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:34">[00:08:34]</a>. He believes that anyone is "one sufficiently bad day away from total depravity" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>.

His entire career as a villain revolves around giving people that "one bad day" he believes they need to become like him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:21">[00:11:21]</a>. His signature weapon, Joker venom, kills victims by forcing them to laugh themselves to death while their faces contort into a grin similar to his own <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>. This weapon has been a part of the character since his debut in *Batman #1* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:45">[00:11:45]</a>. Proving that everyone is capable of becoming what he is—both morally and physically—is the [[character_analysis_of_the_joker | Joker's]] greatest pleasure and obsession, making him a terrifying and relevant character <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>. This is his "killing joke" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:14">[00:12:14]</a>.
