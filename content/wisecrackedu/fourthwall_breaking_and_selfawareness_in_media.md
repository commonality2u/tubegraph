The phenomenon of [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | self-awareness]] and fourth-wall breaking has gained unprecedented popularity in mainstream media, exemplified by the film *Deadpool* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:14">[00:00:14]</a>. Despite shirking traditional superhero formulas, *Deadpool* became the highest-grossing R-rated movie ever and the biggest opening for Ryan Reynolds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>. This trend, while seemingly new, is rooted in older forms of media <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>.

## Defining Features of Deadpool

Deadpool distinguishes itself within the Marvel universe through several key characteristics:
*   **Inveterate Wiseass** He is an inveterate wiseass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>.
*   **Fourth-Wall Breaking** He knows he is a superhero and constantly breaks the fourth wall, commenting on comic book tropes and culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:17">[00:01:17]</a>.
*   **Lack of Seriousness** Deadpool takes nothing seriously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:33">[00:01:33]</a>. Even the film's ad campaign was designed in a "Deadpoolian fashion," including trolling Valentine's Day audiences and creating a Tinder profile as part of a [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | self-referential]] joke <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>.

The character is noted as a Marvel ripoff of a DC competitor who constantly makes fun of the medium he inhabits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>.

## Postmodernism and its Rejection of Grand Narratives

*Deadpool* can be best described as a postmodern work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>. Postmodernism is a movement in the arts and [[philosophical_influences_in_media | philosophy]] that typically rejects "grand narratives" used to make sense of the world, such as the battle of good against evil, the promise of technological progress, or divine salvation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. Postmodern works often embrace being "[[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | meta]]" and [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | self-reference]], and are quick to mock tradition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>.

Deadpool's hyper-awareness of himself and his place in the superhero genre aligns with these postmodern principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>. The film is filled with jokes about Ryan Reynolds, including references to *The Green Lantern* and his "Sexiest Man Alive" title <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>. Instead of adhering to the grand narrative of good triumphing over evil, *Deadpool* adopts a "fuck it" attitude, focusing on revenge and love rather than a righteous battle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>.

This tension between old and postmodern narratives is evident in the film:
*   Colossus attempts to pull Deadpool into the traditional, family-friendly superhero role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:04">[00:03:04]</a>.
*   The Dopinder subplot subverts classic courtly love narratives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>.
*   Blind Al serves as a "deranged version" of a classic narrative trope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.
*   The hero's love interest is a prostitute turned stripper, defying the "fair maiden" trope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>.

## Historical Precedent: Don Quixote

*Deadpool* is considered a spiritual successor to *Don Quixote*, a 400-year-old novel that also satirized heroes, employed [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | meta-narratives]], and is considered a "proto-postmodern" work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. *Don Quixote* features a story within a story, with the fictional author discovering the fictional accounts of Don Quixote <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>. Don Quixote himself understands he is being written about, and the fictional author frequently breaks the fourth wall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>.

*Don Quixote* was written during a time of radical skepticism toward the existing order, just before the Enlightenment, which would later emphasize rationality and science <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>. It aimed to poke fun at Spanish society rather than invent a new ideology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:57">[00:04:57]</a>. Similarly, *Deadpool* signals a "disenchantment" with old, righteous narratives in modern society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.

## Modern Cynicism in Self-Aware Media

According to Joss Whedon, modern [[role_of_selfaware_humor_in_media | self-awareness]] in media has become increasingly cynical, exemplified by a scene in *Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>. In this scene, Indiana Jones delivers a "shit-eating-grin" to the audience, making an "inside joke" about the previous movie, even though it doesn't make chronological sense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:35">[00:05:35]</a>. Whedon argues this kind of [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | self-reference]] for its own sake has "engulfed" culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>.

Examples of this cynical [[role_of_selfaware_humor_in_media | self-awareness]] abound:
*   **Tarzan Trailer**: Jane overtly references her tired role as a damsel in distress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>. The film acknowledges its recycled tropes but merely apologizes for them instead of innovating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:24">[00:06:24]</a>.
*   **Star Trek Into Darkness**: The ending serves as an "excuse" to reference a great scene from cinema, but as its opposite <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:38">[00:06:38]</a>.
*   **22 Jump Street**: This film has the exact same plot as its predecessor, but it's deemed acceptable because Ice Cube calls out their "bullshit" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.

This cynical attitude has transcended [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | self-reference]] into "self-effacement" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>. Hollywood, in an effort to reinvent itself while remaining the same, "gleefully acknowledges its lazy cliches" with a wink and a nod, or by using irony to undercut dramatic moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>. This approach distances the audience from sincerity to make familiar structures feel new <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>.

## Ironic Distance and Cynicism in Deadpool

*Deadpool* embodies this cynical [[role_of_selfaware_humor_in_media | self-awareness]] and ironic distance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. The character remains unphased by torture or gruesome maiming, undercutting high-stakes situations with [[role_of_selfaware_humor_in_media | self-aware humor]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:12">[00:08:12]</a>. Deadpool is "immune to criticism" because his identity is defined by cynicism and is purely reactionary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. He deconstructs tropes that would give film meaning, and his journey is largely superficial: to become "hot again" and reclaim lost love (or lust) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:34">[00:08:34]</a>. He risks nothing and merely makes fun of everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>.

The film itself has "0 fucks to give," with its ad campaign not taking itself seriously and the movie pointing out its own flaws, such as overuse of superhero tropes or budgetary constraints <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.

## The Problem of Endless Meta and Lack of Innovation

While *Deadpool*'s [[role_of_selfaware_humor_in_media | self-reflective]] and edgy commentary can be enjoyable, there is a danger in this trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>. An obsession with being [[cultural_implications_of_selfreferential_and_meta_narratives | meta]] can devolve into an "endless spiral of meta," failing to create anything new <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:04">[00:10:04]</a>. This is likened to hipsters, who ironically embrace cliche, endlessly recycle culture, and position themselves against the mainstream <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>. Douglas Haddow suggested that this "danger of the hipster" could lead to "a culture lost in the superficiality of its past and unable to create any new meaning" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:29">[00:10:29]</a>.

*Deadpool* doesn't reinvent the superhero genre; it merely exists to make fun of it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>. While "bashing the system" may feel good and provide laughter, it is ultimately powerless and doesn't change the system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:50">[00:10:50]</a>. As Slavoj Zizek noted, "cynical distance, laughter, irony, are, so to speak, part of the game. The ruling ideology is not meant to be taken seriously or literally" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>.

## Societal Context of "Fuck It" Attitude

This "cultural impasse" reflects a society unsure of what to do with itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:28">[00:11:28]</a>. *Deadpool*'s ability to say "Fuck it" stems from his immunity to death and consequences; if life is limitless, how can anything be taken seriously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>? This mirrors the comic book's own origin, where writers adopted a "fuck it" attitude because they believed the comic would be cancelled anyway, leading them to write as if nothing mattered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:02">[00:12:02]</a>. This societal "fuck it" might stem from existential threats feeling either too distant or too close to care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:19">[00:12:19]</a>.
