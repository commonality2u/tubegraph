## Critiques of Exposition

A significant criticism leveled at *Inception* is its perceived excessive exposition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:08">[00:01:08]</a>. The film is noted for its "incessant use of not-so-subtle dialogue" to inform the audience, leading some to sarcastically dub it "Exposition" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. It takes over 30 minutes of explaining the rules of its world before the narrative truly begins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:32">[00:01:32]</a>.

Much of this information is conveyed through Ellen Page's character, Ariadne, who is named after the mythological figure who helped Theseus navigate the labyrinth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:36">[00:01:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>. While some [[Critiques of Disneys storytelling methods | critics may decry]] Ariadne as merely a "passive surrogate for the audience to learn how the logic of the world works," her role also provides interesting insights when considered through the lens of [[film_techniques_and_dreamlike_narrative_structures | film as a dream]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>.

## Narrative Style: Films as Dreams

*Inception* consistently compares the logic of cinema to the logic of dreams, arguing that both affect us on a subconscious level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. This perspective offers a new way to understand its narrative choices.

### Starting in the Middle: Narrative Ellipsis

One key similarity between dreams and films is how they often begin "in the middle of the action," without explanation of how or why characters arrived there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>.

*   **Ariadne's Cafe Scene:** During Ariadne's training, Cobb and Ariadne are seen drinking coffee at an outdoor cafe. The audience, like Ariadne, is unaware it's a dream until she realizes it, as there's no scene showing them connecting to their dream-machines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:03">[00:02:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>.
*   **Audience Acceptance:** Films frequently "jump forward in time without any explanation," and audiences readily accept this "narrative ellipsis" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:46">[00:02:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. Just as people believe their dreams are real despite starting *in media res*, they become engrossed in films even when constantly thrown into new scenes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:06">[00:03:06]</a>.
*   **Walter Murch's Theory:** Legendary film editor Walter Murch, in his book *In the Blink of an Eye*, proposes that films work because audiences are "trained by our dreams to be invested in this kind of disjointed storytelling" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:32">[00:03:32]</a>. He suggests that the "cut" in film resembles how images are juxtaposed in dreams, leading viewers to accept the film as a dream-like reality due to its visual discontinuity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:32">[00:03:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.
*   **Mombasa Scene:** The scene where Cobb meets Eames in Mombasa provides another example, cutting from one conversation to an aerial shot without indicating time elapsed or dream level changes, reinforcing that "thatâ€™s just how movies function" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>.

### Implausibility and Coincidence

Both dreams and films often feature implausible or convenient scenarios that audiences accept.

*   **Mombasa Alleyway:** Cobb is chased into an inexplicably narrow alleyway in Mombasa. This bizarre structure could be interpreted as a dream element (reflecting claustrophobia) or simply a cinematic technique to heighten tension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>.
*   **Saito's Timely Appearance:** Saito, one of the world's wealthiest men, conveniently appears in his limo at the exact moment Cobb needs him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:24">[00:05:24]</a>. While this could indicate Cobb is dreaming (wish fulfillment), such coincidences are common in movies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:24">[00:05:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:35">[00:05:35]</a>.
*   **Mal's Death Scene:** In Mal's death scene, Cobb's room is upturned, but Mal is in a neighboring building, sitting on a windowsill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:44">[00:05:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:52">[00:05:52]</a>. This spatial manipulation heightens tension and reflects the central conflict between them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. It forces the audience to question if the implausibility signifies a dream or is merely a [[film_techniques_and_dreamlike_narrative_structures | filmmaking technique]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:24">[00:06:24]</a>. Directors often manipulate spatial orientation for dramatic effect, even when it defies logic, as seen in *The Shining*'s spatially impossible Overlook Hotel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:24">[00:06:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:29">[00:06:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>.

Nolan's brilliance lies in deliberately making it impossible for the audience to discern what is "real" and what is a dream within *Inception*, precisely because the logic of films mirrors that of dreams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.

### Generic Action Settings

Another common [[criticisms_of_action_scenes_and_plot_progression | criticism of Inception]] is that for a film about exploring the subconscious, its "dreams" often resemble generic action film settings rather than the psychedelic absurdities associated with dreams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>. However, this choice reinforces the film's "clever association with cinema" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>. For example, the snow bunker chase scene is an homage to *On Her Majesty's Secret Service*, intentionally feeling like a Bond movie <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:33">[00:07:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>. Given Nolan's profound love of cinema, it makes sense that he would draw inspiration from essential film franchises to construct the aesthetic of dreams, making *Inception* his "most personal film yet" and a commentary on how films capture imagination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:48">[00:07:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>.

The film's deliberate ambiguity, even suggested by the first teaser poster featuring Cobb's back (or perhaps Nolan's), underscores that the inability to definitively distinguish between dream and reality is precisely the point of [[interpretation_of_inceptions_ending | Inception's ending]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:19">[00:08:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:25">[00:08:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>.
