## Rick and Jerry: A Dynamic of Contrasts
The dynamic between Rick and Jerry from *Rick and Morty* is often used to explore the relationship between intelligence and happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>.

Despite this immense power, he is consistently miserable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. Creator Dan Harmon notes that many people can identify with Rick's self-loathing and self-destructive behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:00">[01:36:00]</a>. Rick's despondency stems from his knowledge that "nothing matters" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:00">[00:53:00]</a>. His inner turmoil is evident through his catchphrase, "wubbalubbadubdub," which means "I am in great pain, please help me" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:00">[02:44:00]</a>, and a suicide attempt in season 2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. He is also described as "bored senseless" by mundane life, finding temporary reprieve only through his adventures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:39:00">[06:39:00]</a>.

## Is Intelligence Linked to Misery?
The idea that depression is a prerequisite for success is often sensationalized in media <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>. However, many brilliant people do not suffer from mental illness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a>.

### The Mensa Study and "Hyper Brains"
A 2017 study by Dr. Ruth Karpinski surveyed 3,700 Mensa members about their mental health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>. More than a third reported being depressed, which is five times the national average <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:00">[03:40:00]</a>. For context, out of 100 Mensa members, over 30 would likely be depressed, compared to 6-7 regular people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:45:00">[03:45:00]</a>.

Caveats for this study include:
*   **Self-reporting**: Members self-reported their well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>.
*   **Diagnosis**: Roughly 10% of those reporting depression had not been diagnosed by a doctor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>.
*   **Representativeness**: Mensa members may not be representative of all intelligent people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:11:00">[04:11:00]</a>.

Karpinski suggested that individuals with IQs of 130-plus have "hyper brains," meaning they can comprehend the world's problems, uncertainties, and possibilities more easily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>. This concept was influenced by psychiatrist Dr. Kazimierz Dabrowski, who observed that his exceptionally smart patients tended to overly contemplate everything, particularly negative aspects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:33:00">[04:33:00]</a>. They also seemed to experience deeper depression than intellectually average individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:45:00">[04:45:00]</a>.

### Dabrowski's "Overexcitability"
Dabrowski categorized these traits as "overexcitability," which he viewed as predictive of achieving one's ideal self <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:00">[04:50:00]</a>. He noted that those with multiple forms of overexcitability "sees reality in a different, stronger and more excitable manner" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>.

The five types of overexcitability include:
*   Excessive energy or nervousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:11:00">[05:11:00]</a>.
*   Deep appreciation for things like art or music <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:14:00">[05:14:00]</a>.
*   Being extremely curious or having an active mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:19:00">[05:19:00]</a>.
*   Having a vivid imagination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:22:00">[05:22:00]</a>.
*   Extreme and complex emotional experiences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:25:00">[05:25:00]</a>.

Rick is presented as a "textbook example" of all these traits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:27:00">[05:27:00]</a>.

### [[Existential depression and intelligence | Existential Depression]]
Some psychologists believe that people who exhibit these traits are more prone to [[Existential depression and intelligence | existential depression]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:00:00">[06:00:00]</a>. [[Existential depression and intelligence | Existential depression]] is characterized by feelings of hopelessness stemming from questioning whether life is ultimately meaningless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:02:00">[06:02:00]</a>.

Dr. James Webb, a psychologist studying gifted children, observes that while [[Existential depression and intelligence | existential depression]] can occur in anyone after a crisis, it is more likely to spontaneously affect individuals with above-average intelligence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:08:00">[06:08:00]</a>. Rick's statements, such as "When you know nothing matters, the universe is yours," strongly align with this concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:28:00">[06:28:00]</a>.

## Other Considerations: Wealth, Happiness, and Social Isolation
The relationship between intelligence and well-being is complex.

### [[The correlation between IQ wealth and happiness | IQ, Wealth, and Happiness]]
While some studies suggest a link between high intelligence and depression, others indicate that intelligence can lead to greater happiness by enabling access to better opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:35:00">[07:35:00]</a>. Higher intelligence is associated with greater wealth and, consequently, better health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:50:00">[07:50:00]</a>. However, criticisms of IQ tests note that children from affluent families are more likely to be tested <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:58:00">[07:58:00]</a>.

Despite this, some research directly links higher IQ with greater happiness. A UK study of over 6,800 people found a positive correlation between IQ and self-reported happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:13:00">[08:13:00]</a>.

### Social Isolation and the "Crisis of Self"
Geniuses are often associated with eccentricity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:29:00">[08:29:00]</a>. Rick's intensity can overwhelm others, a hallmark some researchers attribute to high IQ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:37:00">[08:37:00]</a>. These eccentricities can lead to social isolation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:46:00">[08:46:00]</a>. Attempting to conform to "normies" can lead to a "crisis of self," where individuals feel lost about their true identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:52:00">[08:52:00]</a>. Rick can be seen as being in a perpetual crisis of self <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:03:00">[09:03:00]</a>.

Conversely, Jerry, despite not being a genius, also struggles with social integration due to his lack of social and survival skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:29:00">[09:29:00]</a>. He often relies on pity to get by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:14:00">[10:14:00]</a>.

## Conclusion
While research suggests that the "hyper brains" of highly intelligent people might make them more prone to depression due to their intense perception of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:26:00">[10:26:00]</a>, their ability to leverage their intelligence for high-paying jobs can lead to less stress from poverty and better healthcare, potentially resulting in greater overall happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:38:00">[10:38:00]</a>.

Ultimately, whether it's "better to know the worst and be unhappy" as a Rick, or "be happy in a fool's paradise" as a Jerry, remains a subjective question without a concrete psychological answer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:49:00">[10:49:00]</a>. However, limited evidence suggests that while a "Jerry" might suffer less from [[Existential depression and intelligence | existential depression]], a "Rick" is more likely to live a better life overall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:06:00">[11:06:00]</a>.
