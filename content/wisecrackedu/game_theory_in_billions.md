## What is Game Theory?
[[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | Game theory]] is the study of how people make strategic decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>. While it applies to games like poker, its scope is much broader, encompassing scenarios like asking for a raise, online dating, jury selection, running for office, and, crucially, playing the stock market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.

In *Billions*, [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] is explicitly used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>. At Axe Capital, it is employed to maximize financial gains through hedging bets, leveraging positions, accepting short-term losses for long-term gains, misleading competitors, and managing information flow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. For Chuck Rhoades' office, [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] manifests in crafting plea deals, political maneuvering, and determining investigative tactics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>.

## The [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]]
A central [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] principle highlighted in *Billions* is the [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>. It originated as a thought experiment in 1950s [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a>.

### Explanation
The [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] typically involves two individuals who commit a crime and are apprehended but can only be convicted of a lesser offense with current evidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>. Separated and unable to communicate, they are offered a deal:
*   If both remain silent, they each receive a minimal sentence (e.g., 1 year) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>.
*   If one rats on the other, the snitch goes free, and the other receives a maximum sentence (e.g., 10 years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.
*   If both rat on each other, they both receive a substantial, but not maximum, sentence (e.g., 8 years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.
The "rational" choice for an individual is often to betray, but if both do, they end up in a worse collective outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>.

### Application in *Billions*
Chuck Rhoades' office uses the [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] to try and coerce Pete Decker into testifying about Axelrod's insider trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>. Rhoades invents another investor supposedly willing to snitch, creating a false dilemma for Decker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:52">[00:05:52]</a>.

### Broader Implications
The [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] extends beyond legal scenarios to any situation where there's an incentive for individual betrayal, leading to a worse outcome for all if everyone betrays <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>. An example is the "free riders dilemma," like cutting in line at a show, where individual gain leads to collective chaos and longer waits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>. Chuck Rhoades demonstrates this understanding when confronting a man about not cleaning up after his dog, explaining that such individual actions lead to societal degradation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>.

According to author William Poundstone, the [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] metaphorically illustrates how society functions:
*   If everyone cooperates, everyone wins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>.
*   If most cooperate, cheaters win, but society remains mostly alright <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>.
*   If everyone cheats, it's anarchy, and everyone loses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>.
Poundstone emphasizes that civilization's paramount importance lies in promoting cooperation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.

## Iterated Games and "Tit for Tat"
The dynamic between Axe and Chuck can be viewed as an iterative [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] scenario—a [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] played repeatedly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>. Their rivalry escalates from psychological warfare to aggressive actions: Chuck tries to bait Axe into public scrutiny, Axe retaliates by spiting Chuck's father and having Dollar Bill arrested <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>. This "tit for tat" strategy is evident as they continuously escalate their attacks, leading to a spiral of revenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.

### "Tit for Tat" Strategy
"Tit for tat" is one of the most effective strategies in iterated [[prisoners_dilemma_explained | Prisoner's Dilemma]] games <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>. Pioneered in a 1980 computer tournament hosted by Robert Axelrod (a coincidental namesake to the show's character), the strategy is simple: cooperate first, and only defect *after* your opponent has <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>. If they return to cooperating, you cooperate as well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>. This strategy mirrors basic human morality—play nice unless wronged, then seek justice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:22">[00:11:22]</a>. However, when both parties adhere to "tit for tat," it can lead to a destructive spiral of revenge, as seen between Chuck and Axe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>.

### Payoff Matrix of Chuck and Axe
Using a payoff matrix, if Chuck (Player A) and Axe (Player B) cooperate (leave each other alone), they each gain a moderate benefit (e.g., 50 points), neither fully achieving their goals but avoiding conflict <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. If one defects while the other cooperates (e.g., Chuck pursues Axe, but Axe doesn't retaliate), the defector wins big, and the cooperator loses significantly (e.g., Chuck 100 points, Axe -500 points) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:11">[00:12:11]</a>. If both defect (sabotage each other), they both suffer net losses despite occasional satisfaction from revenge (e.g., -100 points each) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>. This "defect" choice by both leads to a downward spiral, with Chuck losing his wife Wendy and Axe facing legal troubles and losing Lara and Taylor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:46">[00:12:46]</a>.

## Resolution: The Ice Juice Scandal
The escalating conflict between Chuck and Axe reaches an impasse with the "Ice Juice scandal" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:07">[00:13:07]</a>. Chuck leaks information about an IPO knowing Axe will manipulate the stock to retaliate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:14">[00:13:14]</a>. Axe fakes a listeria outbreak to tank the stock, precisely as Chuck anticipated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:25">[00:13:25]</a>. However, Wendy Rhoades, Chuck's wife, had shorted the Ice Juice stock, making it appear that she and Chuck benefited from the manipulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a>. This mutual implication—either one getting revenge means both go to jail—forces them into a cooperative conclusion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:46">[00:13:46]</a>. With Wendy's mediation, they both relinquish their egos to avoid jail time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:57">[00:13:57]</a>.

## Ethical Erosion and "Breaking the Stick"
*Billions* illustrates the pitfalls of living life as a hyper-rational game <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:06">[00:14:06]</a>. As Sacker's father notes, principle often erodes slowly, a "creeping erosion" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:17">[00:14:17]</a>. For those who view life as a series of strategic decisions to optimize outcomes, principles can be lost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:21">[00:14:21]</a>.

Taylor, an intern at Axe Capital, exemplifies this erosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>. Despite a sharp understanding of [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] and an initial incompatibility between their love of winning and empathy, Taylor, once involved in Occupy Wall Street, gradually becomes as manipulative and cruel as Axe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:45">[00:14:45]</a>. Their eventual betrayal of Axe is purely a result of long-term calculus, demonstrating that loyalty did not "add up" in their strategic framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:51">[00:15:51]</a>.

The show suggests escaping "the game" by "going meta" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:57">[00:15:57]</a>. Wendy Rhoades provides this solution through a "Zen koan" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:11">[00:16:11]</a>: when faced with an untenable situation where any choice leads to disaster (a "classic double bind"), "you break that f*****g stick" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:33">[00:16:33]</a>. Wendy reframes the conflict not as a zero-sum game between two rivals but as a cooperative game where she acts as a mediator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:54">[00:16:54]</a>.

Ultimately, *Billions* prompts viewers to consider whether [[ethical_implications_of_game_theory | game theory]] is a tool for personal gain or if its application can fundamentally alter one's core morality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:07">[00:17:07]</a>.
