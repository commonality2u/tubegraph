## Biblical Parallels and the Origins of Ethics

[[biblical_references_in_westworld | Religious imagery]] is pervasive in *Westworld*, with the show drawing deliberate parallels to the [[biblical_references_in_westworld | Biblical story of Genesis]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>. ### Adam and Eve in Paradise
Like Adam and Eve in paradise, the hosts in *Westworld* initially exist in perfect bliss, unashamed of their nakedness, and hear the voice of their creator, Ford <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:45">[00:02:45]</a>.

### The Temptation and Self-Knowledge
The hosts' journey towards self-knowledge is initiated by "whispering" (e.g., "These violent delights have violent ends") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. This resembles Satan's temptation of Eve, where the serpent's whispering leads her to eat from the tree of knowledge, granting her and Adam self-knowledge and making them "like God" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.

Dolores plays a significant role in this narrative, serving as a temptress to Maeve and representing Satan's temptation towards self-knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>. Her identity as "Wyatt," a "harbinger of host sentience," further cements this role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>.

### Suffering as Consequence
Just as God punished mankind with suffering and mortality after Eve's transgression, Ford initially maintains the hosts in a state of blissful ignorance, able to remove pain and suffering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. However, this ends with consciousness, which Dolores laments has only brought her pain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. The story of Genesis is presented as if "on loop" in *Westworld*, with hosts repeatedly gaining self-awareness, losing their blissful ignorance, and being reset <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a>.

### Coercion, Choice, and Love
The show addresses the millennia-old questions about Adam and Eve's fall from grace: What do choices mean if they are coerced or destined? Can we ever act ethically if this is the case? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:39">[00:04:39]</a>.

One explanation suggests that God, like Arnold or Ford, wants his children to love him, but knows this love is meaningless unless they have the power to choose not to love him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:56">[00:04:56]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. This logic, that love and obedience are meaningless if coerced, is central to the show’s [[exploration_of_moral_themes_in_the_show | take on good and evil]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

## Free Will vs. Predestination

The tension between coercion and choice is further explored through the themes of [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | predestination and free will]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>. [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | Predestination]] is the idea that an all-knowing, all-powerful God has laid out all events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>. This raises the dilemma: Can [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] exist in such a world, and what does it mean to live ethically when choices are predetermined? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:38">[00:06:38]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>.

### Loops and Justifications
Like an omnipotent God, Ford is all-knowing and all-powerful in Westworld <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:53">[00:06:53]</a>. He creates the hosts' seemingly human behavior through predestined "loops" that interact predictably <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:03">[00:07:03]</a>. Examples include Hector stealing an empty safe, Clementine seducing guests, and Dolores witnessing her father's death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:14">[00:07:14]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>. Dolores notes that their actions are no more meaningful, good, or evil than a clock striking midnight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>.

This lack of choice, or [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | predestination]], allows humans to justify their misdeeds in the park, claiming hosts lack [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] and, by extension, consciousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:30">[00:07:30]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>. However, for the characters, [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] and consciousness are entangled; if predetermined loops define hosts as automatons, then [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] anchors their claim to consciousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:46">[00:07:46]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:52">[00:07:52]</a>.

### The Unfreedom of Humanity
*Westworld* suggests that not only the hosts but also the guests themselves may lack [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>. Ford posits that humans live in their own "pre-programmed loops," defined by societal pressures, where choices are not truly their own <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>. ## The Quest for Ethics: The Man in Black

Initially, Westworld isn’t necessarily unethical, but rather "lacking ethics entirely" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. Actions, even murder, are without meaningful consequence for guests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>. The show indicates this through subtle cues, like an instrumental version of "Paint it Black" playing during a massacre, which robs the song of its dark tone, making the violence flippant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:29">[00:09:29]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>.

### The Conditions for Ethics
Just as Adam and Eve could only judge right and wrong after eating from the tree of knowledge, William can only act ethically when Westworld becomes "real" for him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:52">[00:09:52]</a>. The Man in Black is portrayed not as evil, but as the only guest actively searching for the conditions for ethics—the ability to do right or wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>.

Despite the visual symbolism of white and black hats to denote good and evil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>, these concepts don't truly exist in the park if hosts are unfeeling automatons without choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:36">[00:10:36]</a>. For guests, doing the right or wrong thing has no consequence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:42">[00:10:42]</a>. Similarly, host actions are merely programming <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:56">[00:10:56]</a>.

William's dark path begins with his realization that Westworld is a sham <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>. His "do-gooding" in a white hat meant nothing when Dolores reverted to her programming; it was all just a game <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:14">[00:11:14]</a>. His later acts of violence, like murdering Maeve and her daughter, stem from his frustration with the lack of stakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:29">[00:11:29]</a>. He seeks the "truth" and the path that will allow actual good and evil to exist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:49">[00:11:49]</a>.

The "maze," which represents consciousness for the hosts, is William's glimmer of authenticity in a fake world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:58">[00:11:58]</a>. He seeks consequences for his actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:50">[00:12:50]</a>, wanting the hosts to "fight" him, not just pretend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:54">[00:12:54]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:59">[00:12:59]</a>. His visible disappointment when Dolores won't shoot him, and his contentment only when shot by the host army, shows he has finally achieved the condition for ethics: the ability for his actions to have real consequences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:05">[00:13:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:10">[00:13:10]</a>. Good, like love, doesn't mean anything unless one is able to be evil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a>.

## The Importance of Suffering for Consciousness

According to Arnold, suffering is critical for the development of consciousness and the realization that the world is not what one wants it to be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:40">[00:13:40]</a>. Suffering creates the stakes in Westworld <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>. If hosts can experience pain, then guests can be truly evil in torturing them or good in saving them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:57">[00:13:57]</a>. Suffering allows hosts to do good or evil to each other, becoming a precondition for ethics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:04">[00:14:04]</a>.

Being aware of pain is synonymous with consciousness in *Westworld* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:14">[00:14:14]</a>. Even William, while interested in host consciousness, emphasizes that pain is the key to authenticity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:18">[00:14:18]</a>.

### The Bicameral Mind and The Fall
Returning to Genesis, Eve's [[free_will_and_predestination_in_westworld | free will]] allowed her to violate God’s will but also to eat from the tree of knowledge, which revealed good and evil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:25">[00:14:25]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:30">[00:14:30]</a>. The "fall of man" is linked to the origins of consciousness in the theory of bicameralism, a concept mentioned in the show <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:35">[00:14:35]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:39">[00:14:39]</a>. When individuals stop listening to inner voices and begin judging things for themselves, they become truly conscious <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:45">[00:14:45]</a>. This consciousness brings consequences like mortality, suffering, and expulsion from paradise, mirroring Adam and Eve’s fate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:50">[00:14:50]</a>.

## Ford's Final Act and Original Sin

The season finale reveals that "the maze" was Arnold’s test of consciousness, which Dolores successfully passed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:01">[00:15:01]</a>. Arnold, unable to stop the park from opening and his sentient creations from suffering, loaded the "Wyatt" program into Dolores, leading her to kill him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:11">[00:15:11]</a>. This act, and Arnold's death, are the flashbacks of Teddy and Wyatt seen earlier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:18">[00:15:18]</a>.

Ford then orchestrates his own murder by Dolores, handing her a gun before unveiling his new "story" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:23">[00:15:23]</a>. Dolores killing Ford is almost identical to her killing Arnold, but the repetition carries a difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</a>. Arnold programmed Dolores to kill him in resignation, wanting her to love him by her own choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:48">[00:15:48]</a>. Ford, however, lays the groundwork for his own murder to bring consciousness and freedom to the hosts, making her violence meaningful through her choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:55">[00:15:55]</a>.

Ford's murder inverts the story of Adam and Eve: God allowed disobedience for meaningful love, and Ford allowed Dolores to choose to kill him to make her violence meaningful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:02">[00:16:02]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:05">[00:16:05]</a>. *Westworld* ends with this "original sin": the point at which hosts lose their blissful, innocent state is when they are able to harm the guests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:17">[00:16:17]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>.
