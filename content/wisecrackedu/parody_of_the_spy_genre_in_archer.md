## Inverting James Bond Tropes

*Archer* explicitly borrows many thematic elements from the James Bond series <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. Bond films typically portray the titular agent as a freewheeling stud pitted against stuffy, bureaucratic rule-makers like M <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>. This dynamic was particularly significant during the "crisis of masculinity" in America in the 1950s, a period that saw the meteoric rise of the James Bond series <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>. Sociologist Thomas Andrae notes that male desires for autonomy were being displaced by pressures to take desk jobs with 401ks, making many men feel like "alienated but conforming employee[s] of a large corporate bureaucracy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>. Spy thrillers, with characters like Bond embodying independence and individualism, became a perfect form of escapism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. Bond's portrayal as a charismatic, rule-breaking hero who relied on guts was ideal for the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a>.

However, the Bond-style secret agent is pure fiction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. *Archer* takes this reality and milks the humor of unglamorous espionage bureaucracy for all it's worth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.

### The Mundanity of Espionage

Mindless paperwork, such as expense reports and checking in with desks, frequently threatens Archer’s ability to live his "best spy life" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>. The show achieves comic effect by contrasting fantastical action sequences with the mundane realities of office life, including discussions about life insurance beneficiaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>. Archer often only escapes the bureaucratic consequences of his actions due to his boss-mother, Mallory’s, forgiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:53">[00:02:53]</a>.

Later in the series, when the agency becomes a Private Investigation firm, Cyril is the only one eligible for a PI license, forcing Archer to work for "the Figgis Agency" under Cyril <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. While Cyril thrives in the world of espionage bureaucracy, Archer’s swashbuckling antics are typically counterproductive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:12">[00:03:12]</a>.

### Challenging [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | Hyper-Masculinity]]

[[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | Archer]] is the ultimate James Bond parody, exemplifying Bond's well-known qualities and taking them to their logical, unromantic conclusions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>. Like Bond, Archer is wild and untamed, using drinking, fighting, womanizing, cars, and guns to affirm his [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | manliness]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>.

*   **Cars:** Bond films famously fetishize the latest, sexiest cars, with Aston Martin sponsoring twelve of twenty-four Bond movies to offer promotional deals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:58">[00:03:58]</a>. In contrast, Archer's car is a birthday gift from his mother, presented like a "Super Sweet Sixteen" present, which embarrassingly gives him an erection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>. The show mocks how Bond's [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | hyper-masculinity]] was used to sell products like cars and beer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:28">[00:04:28]</a>. Archer’s car, a marker of his [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | manliness]], is on loan from his mother and confiscated days later, with his supposed masculine power frequently "yanked away" by his more powerful mother <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>.
*   **Sexuality:** While James Bond is a legendary seducer, Archer is also [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | hyper-sexual]], constantly hiring sex workers, sleeping with office mates, and propositioning nearly every woman <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. However, unlike Bond’s legendary prowess, Archer’s sexual experiences are often unsatisfactory, leading to STDs and requiring him to pay for abortions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>.
*   **Health Consequences:** The show takes other health effects of the Bondian lifestyle to a logical extreme <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. While Bond enjoys his liquor, Archer’s substance use is superhuman, exemplified by him chugging germicide <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>. His drinking contributes to him getting breast cancer, a stereotypically feminine disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>. Even becoming a father plays out as a twisted form of emasculation, as Lana secretly uses his frozen sperm to get pregnant without his permission or knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:12">[00:06:12]</a>.
*   **Tinnitus:** Archer also suffers from tinnitus, a hearing loss caused by too many guns firing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. The show even makes tinnitus a pet cause, with characters attending an annual fundraiser for the American Tinnitus Association <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>. This continually subverts the "bulletproof James Bondian fantasy" by confronting Archer with the bleak realities of his mortal body <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.

### Language and Character Psychology

Communications scholar Ian Dawe argues that Archer’s tinnitus is more than a gag; it’s central to the show’s preoccupation with sound and [[language_and_communication_in_archer | language]], which generate laughs and offer insights into [[archers_use_of_humor_and_character_psychology | character psychology]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:59">[00:06:59]</a>. Dawe's study found that linguistic or sound-specific references, including puns, foreign language jokes, or loud gunfire followed by Archer's tinnitus, occurred approximately every two minutes in seasons 3 and 4 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:18">[00:07:18]</a>.

Just as mundane office politics contrast with life-threatening action, the show’s [[language_and_communication_in_archer | language jokes]] are often used at inopportune moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>.

*   **"Lana?" "LAAANNNAAAAA???!!?!?!"** Archer’s frequent, loud calls to Lana, even when she is nearby, are more than an annoyance. They are reminiscent of a baby calling for its mother, signaling Archer’s inability to control his demand for attention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>. This gag spreads to other characters, emphasizing the importance of characters being heard, and the underlying themes of power, demand, control, and psychological repression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>.
*   **"Phrasing!"**: This classic joke uses what Freud called "double entendre," relying on a sexual meaning while words have an alternative non-sexual meaning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>. Freud suggested these jokes release nervous energy from repressing sexual or aggressive desires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:09">[00:09:09]</a>. When Archer directs the joke at his mother, it highlights his Oedipal complex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>. By choosing to misinterpret words and hear only the sexual innuendo, Archer willfully miscommunicates and derails conversations, ensuring the other person "won't be heard at all" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>. This tactic becomes a favorite way to hijack conversations, eventually becoming so ubiquitous it falls out of fashion, causing Archer anxiety that confirms the importance of these attention-grabbing antics to his identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>. The show portrays the "world’s image of a perfect man reduced to a man-child" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a>.
*   **Voicemail Gags:** Archer’s elaborate voicemail messages are another way he avoids hearing what characters, especially his mother, are truly saying <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>. Mallory often has entire conversations with his prerecorded voice before realizing she’s been duped <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:32">[00:10:32]</a>. This juvenile tactic allows Archer to temporarily reclaim power and undermine Mallory’s dominance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>.

Just as Archer struggles with ISIS's Human Resources department, he often struggles with the rules of [[language_and_communication_in_archer | language]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>. These restraints prevent him from living the badass spy narrative he envisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a>. When declared "king of the pirates," Archer attempts to address his subjects, but his idioms don’t translate, making him realize how much people rely on them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:12">[00:11:12]</a>. He chafes against the rules of language, much like he chafes against bureaucracy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>.

While James Bond is known for pithy one-liners, Archer is not as gifted, frequently regretting his failure to come up with the perfect witty line <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>. The show parodies Bond by turning funny off-handed innuendos into a chore where Archer still falls short <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:12">[00:12:12]</a>. Archer’s [[language_and_communication_in_archer | language]] also isolates him when he attempts to connect with others through obscure [[cultural_references_and_their_role_in_archer | cultural references]], rarely receiving affirmation and experiencing shame when they fall flat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:29">[00:12:29]</a>.

Despite fantastical fight sequences and spy missions, much of *Archer*'s humor lies in the [[language_and_communication_in_archer | linguistic communications]] between characters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:01">[00:13:01]</a>. Archer's lingering adolescent sense of being an outsider, stemming from fifteen years in boarding school, is expressed through his enthusiasm for coining group slang and his wildly obscure [[cultural_references_and_their_role_in_archer | pop culture references]] that inevitably fall flat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:16">[00:13:16]</a>. Ultimately, *Archer* embodies the perennial secret agent struggle born from the 1950s [[complexities_of_masculinity_in_archer | masculine anxiety]] about being "cogs in the corporate machine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>.
