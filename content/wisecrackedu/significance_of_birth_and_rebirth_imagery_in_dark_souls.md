## Miyazaki's Approach to Meaning

Hidetaka Miyazaki, the enigmatic maestro behind Dark Souls, creates meaning not through explicit narrative, but through [[visual_storytelling_in_dark_souls | visual design]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>. This approach is reminiscent of pioneering Italian filmmaker Michelangelo Antonioni, who often employed deliberately unresolved plots in films like "Blow Up" and "La Aventura" to focus on character studies or themes, rather than linear storytelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.

### Visual Clues and Themes

While dialogue in Dark Souls may lack clarity, the visuals provide thematic insights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>. For example, the old lady spinning yarn at the beginning of Dark Souls 2 is a visual reference to the Norns of Norse mythology, who spin the yarns of fate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>. The game's overarching theme appears to be about piecing together a broken past and understanding one's identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>. This concept is underscored at the character creation screen, which prompts the question: "Is this your TRUE self?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>. Dark Souls is presented as a nightmarish descent into the unconscious, exploring identity and deepest desires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>.

## Womb and Birth Imagery

The series is replete with imagery related to birth and the maternal womb:

*   **Dark Souls 1 Opening**: The game begins with a visual birthing scene, where the camera plunges into a crevice shaped like a vagina, through an ovary-like tunnel, and into a womb-like pit full of "things being born" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:02:56]</a>.
*   **Dark Souls 2 Opening**: A similar scene unfolds in reverse, with the player starting in a labyrinth of tunnels and caverns, working their way out through a vagina-like crevice into the light. This "tutorial" section symbolizes development within the womb before birth and the start of the quest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.
*   **Persistent Landscapes**: Throughout the games, landscapes often appear womb and ovary-like <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>.
*   **Humanity Item**: In Dark Souls 1, the essential item "Humanity" is deliberately shaped like a vagina <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a>.
*   **Mother Symbols**: Dark Souls 2, from its inception, is filled with mother symbols <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>.

### Further Birth and Egg Symbolism

Beyond the initial birthing scenes, the games continue to feature strong birth and egg imagery:

*   **Nests and Eggs**: Mysterious nests and eggs transport the player to other places <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>.
*   **Egg Monsters**: The Demon Ruins are covered with hideous egg monsters, and players can even become infected with a "horrible egg head" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:18">[00:04:18]</a>.
*   **Pregnant Monsters**: Characters encounter pregnant monsters hidden in cells, as if repressed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
*   **Bloodborne**: The spin-off title, [[bloodborne_themes | Bloodborne]], intensifies these themes with characters discussing bloody birth scenes and screaming pregnant women, and the collection of umbilical cord parts leading to a "bizarre, lovecraftian baby alien" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>. Elements like "blood dregs" resembling semen and speculation about drinking menstrual blood further contribute to this imagery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. The title "Bloodborne" itself speaks volumes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>.

Rather than having an alien *within* us, in Dark Souls, "we are the creatures moving around the alien womb-world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>. This concept of "Womb Raiding," as opposed to familiar "Tomb Raiding," is unique to Dark Souls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>. This obsession with birth is tied to the game's core theme of lost identity and the search for "who we really are" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a>.

## [[freudian_elements_in_dark_souls | Freudian Elements in Dark Souls]]: Primal Scenes and Castration Anxiety

[[dark_souls_and_psychoanalysis | Psychoanalyst]] Sigmund Freud believed that the complexity of identity leads individuals to seek understanding in their origins, including the time spent in the womb, and even further back to the "primal scene" of parental sex, which he theorized we always dream of <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>. Dark Souls 2's opening line, "perhaps you’ve seen it, maybe in a dream. A murky, forgotten land," directly echoes this Freudian concept, portraying the game as an endless search for a forgotten identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>.

The game visualizes the desire to re-enter the womb as a metaphorical, yet ultimately impossible, act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>. An opening shot in Dark Souls 2 depicts a hand desperately reaching for a mother <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:12">[00:06:12]</a>. However, social taboos and prohibitions, metaphorically represented by the "father" who says "no," prevent this access <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>.

### Phallic Obstacles and Castration

Dark Souls manifests these prohibitions through numerous metaphorical moments where players are confronted with "arched doorways" (representing maternal access) blocked by "male guards with big, erect, throbbing uh… weapons" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>. These guards are predominantly "castrating figures" wielding large, sharp, phallic weaponry such as swords, spears, and halberds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>. Freud considered swords symbols of castration, with these enemies metaphorically threatening to "cut off our d*cks" as players attempt to reach the "mother" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>. The boss "The Penetrator" in Demon's Souls explicitly embodies this aggressive phallic symbolism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.

The game's infamous difficulty, leading to frequent "You Died" screens (an average player sees it 739 times in Dark Souls 2), creates a constant feeling of failure and impotence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>. This pervasive sense of defeat, often at the hands of "giant hulking male giant[s] wielding a formidable phallus," is precisely the atmosphere of Freudian castration anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>.

### Vagina Dentata

Female enemies in Dark Souls also contribute to the theme of castration anxiety:

*   **Scorpioness Najka and Mytha the Baneful**: These characters feature attractive upper halves combined with weaponized, dangerous lower halves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:01">[00:08:01]</a>.
*   **Queen Queelag**: This major female enemy has "monstrous teeth in place of a vagina," conveying the message that attempting to re-enter the womb is the ultimate social taboo, and to do so invites being "bitten" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:19">[00:08:19]</a>. This is a classic symbol of [[freudian_elements_in_dark_souls | castration]] rooted in the concept of *Vagina Dentata* ("a vagina with teeth") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. This ancient folklore concept became significant in philosophy and [[dark_souls_and_psychoanalysis | psychoanalysis]], representing a vagina with "secretly hidden ferocious penis decapitating fangs" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>.

Thus, Dark Souls visually embodies Freud's idea of [[freudian_elements_in_dark_souls | castration anxiety]]: it presents a desire, then thwarts and symbolically castrates the player, leaving them weak and unfulfilled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:55">[00:08:55]</a>.

## The Unfulfilled Quest: Return to the Womb and Endless Want

The ending of Dark Souls 2 mirrors the beginning's birthing scene in reverse. The hero passes through a "very obviously vagina-like door" and is welcomed into the "seat at the heart of the mother’s womb" by a comforting female voice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>. The throne itself is based on a 'kiln,' historically associated with wombs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:23">[00:09:23]</a>. This "Throne of Want" represents the embodiment of ultimate desire and the fulfillment of deepest, most repressed wishes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>.

However, despite the character's apparent satisfaction upon sitting on the throne after defeating more "penis wielding giant alpha males," the player is often left unfulfilled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>. The curse isn't lifted, and the ending remains vague, leading to renewed frustration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:59">[00:09:59]</a>. This reflects the idea that the "puzzle of our desires or our identities" is never truly solved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>. The mysterious female voice states, "souls will flourish anew and all of this will play out again," implying an endless cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>.

Jacques Lacan, a follower of Freud, posited that true fulfillment is impossible because desire ceases once attained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>. Dark Souls 2's Ancient Dragon articulates this directly: "the curse of life is the curse of want" – players are destined to be eternally unfulfilled and unsatisfied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>.

## Dark Souls as a Metaphor for Life

Miyazaki has commented that the Dark Souls games explore the "strange crossover between life and death," posing questions about their very nature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:58">[00:10:58]</a>. Through this lens, Dark Souls can be seen as a metaphor for life itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:11">[00:11:11]</a>: development in the womb, birth, followed by "endless desire, frustration, and misery," leading to a confrontation with death and a return to the "peaceful oblivion of your mother’s womb" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:15">[00:11:15]</a>. The game visually portrays horrific unconscious desires, complex relationships with birth, and an endless cycle of unfulfilled endeavors with a nightmarish aesthetic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:26">[00:11:26]</a>.
