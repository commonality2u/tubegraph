Film acting has evolved significantly, moving from highly theatrical performances to a dominant emphasis on realism. This shift raises questions about what constitutes "good" acting and whether adhering strictly to realistic portrayals limits the art form's potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:09:09">[06:09:09]</a>.

### The Historical Evolution of Film Performance

The early days of narrative cinema drew heavily from popular stage entertainment, particularly melodrama and vaudeville <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:13">[03:13:13]</a>. This lineage meant that performances in silent films featured large, sweeping gestures easily understood by audiences from any vantage point in a theater <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:24:24">[03:24:24]</a>. Actors relied solely on their faces and bodies, resulting in "wide eyes, ugly crying and berserk-o gestures" that were not intended to be realistic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:38">[03:38:38]</a>. This era represents a peak in [[the_influence_of_historical_acting_styles_on_modern_cinema | the influence of historical acting styles on modern cinema]].

With the advent of "talkies" in the late 1920s, actors faced a crisis, needing to rethink their craft as synchronized sound became viable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:48:48">[03:48:48]</a>. While change was slow, films of the 1930s still often showcased a heightened reality rooted in their silent film origins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:16:16">[04:16:16]</a>.

A major turning point came with the introduction of Constantine Stanislavsky's techniques, which eventually developed into method acting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:30:30">[04:30:30]</a>. Method acting aims for actors to channel "emotional truth" from within, leading to deep immersion in characters, sometimes even living as them for the duration of production, as seen with Daniel Day-Lewis in *My Left Foot* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:42">[04:42:42]</a>. This emphasis on [[concept_of_real_vs_fake | realism]], combined with sound film, cemented its dominance in Hollywood by the 1940s and 50s, with actors like Marlon Brando becoming famous for disappearing into their roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:13:13">[05:13:13]</a>.

Today, there's an overwhelming emphasis on [[concept_of_real_vs_fake | realism]] as the goal for film acting, with Oscars frequently awarded to actors portraying real people who "disappear" into their roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:41:41">[05:41:41]</a>.

### Critique of Realism's Dominance

Cinema scholar Carole Zucker argues that equating good film acting with "gestural and psychological verisimilitude (i.e. realness/trueness) suggests a limited vision of performance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:56:56">[05:56:56]</a>. The prevailing dominance of [[concept_of_real_vs_fake | realism]] in acting may have narrowed the perception of what acting can be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:09:09">[06:09:09]</a>.

In 1988's *Vampire's Kiss*, [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Cage]] found a "laboratory" for his new ideas in a low-stakes indie setting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:01:01">[07:01:01]</a>. This film showcased his emerging style, characterized by:

Many of his famously "ridiculous moments" are meticulously planned, such as his alphabet recitation scene in *Vampire's Kiss*, where every movement, sound, and gesture was carefully thought out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:19:19">[09:19:19]</a>.

### Why Realism Dominates Film Acting (and why [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Cage]] challenges it)

Film, with its close-ups and point-of-view shots, is considered an intimate art form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:55:55">[13:55:55]</a>. This intimacy often leads audiences to expect performers to behave in a way that approximates how they themselves would react <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:02:02">[14:02:02]</a>. [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Cage's]] refusal to conform to this expectation, combined with often working with actors who do not share his acting philosophy, confuses audience expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:10:10">[14:10:10]</a>. His "wild reactions and bug-eyed expressions" become particularly susceptible to being isolated as memes, further stripping them of context and making them appear ridiculous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:27:27">[14:27:27]</a>.

However, there can be merits to this "out-there" performance style <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:40:40">[14:40:40]</a>. For example, in *Vampire's Kiss*, [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Cage's]] character Peter is so inaccessible and performative that the audience cannot sympathize with him, unlike Christian Bale's Patrick Bateman in *American Psycho*, who, despite his villainy, is often revered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:43:43">[14:43:43]</a>.

Ultimately, while subjective, [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Nicolas Cage's]] defiance of [[concept_of_real_vs_fake | realism]] in acting is compelling, different, and controversial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:30:30">[15:30:30]</a>. His approach suggests that Hollywood could become more interesting if more actors challenged the strict benchmark of [[concept_of_real_vs_fake | realism]] in performance, much as other art forms like music and literature embrace abstraction and rule-breaking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:41:41">[15:41:41]</a>.
