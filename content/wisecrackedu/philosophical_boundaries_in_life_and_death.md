## What Does it Mean to Live?

The terror inspired by zombies offers deep insights into human perception of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. Zombies are unsettling because they are "like us, but not quite us" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:12">[00:02:12]</a>. The show continually asks what constitutes "life" and "living" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:32">[00:02:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>. The title itself, *The Walking Dead*, refers not only to the zombies but also to the survivors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:36">[00:02:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>.

### Blurring Lines Between Walker and Human

A pivotal moment occurs early in the show at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) when Dr. Jenner reveals that everyone is infected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>. This revelation blurs the line between "walker" and human, as the constant proximity to death makes the distinction seem to "melt away" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.

From a biological perspective, walkers are arguably alive, as brain scans show a small section of their brain still lights up, enabling movement, groaning, and eating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:20">[00:03:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>. This contrasts with common assumptions, leading to differing views among characters, such as Lizzie's assertion that they are "just different" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:29">[00:03:29]</a>.

### The Philosophical Question of Suicide

The question of whether a life devoid of meaning is worth living is explored through the lens of [[the_philosophy_of_nihilism_and_existentialism | Albert Camus]], who famously stated, "there is but one truly philosophical problem, and that is suicide" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>. At the CDC, some characters like Dr. Jenner, Andrea, and Jacqui consider ending their lives due to the misery and lack of meaning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:59">[00:05:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>. However, Camus argued that only life offers freedom, suggesting that suicide is not liberating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>. Rick's group, by contrast, clings to a notion of freedom and persists <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>.

## What Makes Us Human?

Another central philosophical question is: "What makes us human? Or, put the other way, what makes us not a zombie?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:24">[00:06:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27">[00:06:27]</a>. ### Reason and Morality

In the zombie world, losing the ability to reason and speak is often equated with no longer being human <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>.

The show suggests that morality is a key differentiator between humans and animals (or zombies) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>.
