## Beyond Realism in Film Performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>

To understand Cage's distinctive style, it's helpful to examine the [[historical_context_of_film_performance_techniques | history of film performance]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a>.

Early silent films drew from theatrical genres like melodrama and vaudeville, which emphasized large, sweeping gestures easily understood by an audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>. Actors relied on exaggerated facial expressions and body language due to the lack of verbal communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>. This acting was not, and did not aim to be, "realistic" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.

The advent of "talkie" films in the late 1920s forced actors to rethink their craft <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>. Around the same time, America was introduced to Konstantin Stanislavsky's techniques, which eventually evolved into [[role_of_method_acting_in_cinema | method acting]], an approach focused on channeling "emotional truth" from within <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. As these techniques migrated to film, actors began to create fully-rounded characters, sometimes even briefly living as them, exemplified by Daniel Day-Lewis's immersive preparation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:51">[00:04:51]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. This emphasis on realism, combined with sound film, swept Hollywood in the 1940s and 50s, making actors like Marlon Brando famous for "disappearing" into their roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>.

Despite other acting styles existing, an overwhelming emphasis on realism has persisted as the goal for film acting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. Oscars are typically awarded to actors who embody real people and "disappear" into their roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>. However, cinema scholar Carole Zucker argues that adhering rigidly to gestural and psychological verisimilitude limits the vision of performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. This raises the question of whether the dominance of realism has narrowed our perception of what acting can be, and if Cage's performances appear "weird" because they fall outside this view <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>.
