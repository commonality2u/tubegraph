## Historical Context of Film Performance

The [[historical_context_of_film_performance_techniques | historical context of film performance techniques]] reveals a significant shift in acting styles:

### Early Cinema and Theatrical Roots
When silent narrative films emerged, they borrowed heavily from popular theatrical forms like melodrama and vaudeville <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>. These forms required large, sweeping gestures and exaggerated expressions for comprehension by a wide audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>. Actors relied solely on their faces and bodies, resulting in "wide eyes, ugly crying and and berserk-o gestures" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>. This early style was not, and did not try to be, realistic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.

### The Rise of Sound and Realism
The advent of "talkie" films with synchronized sound in the late 1920s forced actors to re-evaluate their craft <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>. Simultaneously, the groundbreaking techniques of Russian acting coach Constantine Stanislavsky were introduced in America <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>. Stanislavsky's ideas emphasized realism and laid the foundation for [[role_of_method_acting_in_cinema | method acting]], which aimed to help actors channel "emotional truth" from within <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. As these techniques migrated from theatre to film, actors began portraying fully-rounded characters, sometimes even briefly living as those characters for their roles, as seen with Daniel Day Lewis in *My Left Foot* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:51">[00:04:51]</a>.

This emphasis on realism, combined with the enhanced realism offered by sound film, solidified in the 1940s and 1950s, permeating Hollywood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a>. Actors like Marlon Brando became renowned for "disappearing into a character" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>. While other styles, such as the campy performances in John Waters films, have existed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a>, an "overwhelming emphasis on realism as the goal of acting for film" has persisted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. Oscars are frequently awarded to actors who embody realism, often by portraying real people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.

### Critiquing the Dogma of Realism
Cinema scholar Carole Zucker argues that adhering to a strict standard of "gestural and psychological verisimilitude" (realness/trueness) for film acting "suggests a limited vision of performance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. This raises the question: has the dominance of realism in acting narrowed our understanding of what acting can be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a>?

## [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Nicolas Cage's Unique Approach]]

[[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Nicolas Cage]] (nephew of director Francis Ford Coppola), despite being one of Hollywood's most prolific actors with over 73 films <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>, is often seen as an anomaly. His refusal to be typecast, shifting between "stinkers, weirdly-great indie films, big action films and everything in between," makes him difficult to define <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.

### The "Laboratory" of *Vampire's Kiss*
Initially, Cage embraced [[role_of_method_acting_in_cinema | method acting]], even having two teeth removed for his role in 1984's *Birdy* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27">[00:06:27]</a>. However, he quickly grew tired of the style, seeking an acting form that went "beyond" literalism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>.

Cage considers the 1988 film *Vampire's Kiss* to be his "laboratory" for these new ideas, using it as a low-stakes independent setting to experiment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>. In this film, his highly stylized and meme-generating performance stood out against otherwise normal acting and cinematography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>. He made "bold daring choices that take advantage of all aspects of his physicality," including "patently goofy facial expressions" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>.

### "Western Kabuki" and Other Influences
Cage's approach is unapologetically performative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>. While confusing in naturalistic films like *Vampire's Kiss*, his style tends to work well in movies that "push the limits of reality," creating surreal worlds that accommodate his bold choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>. Examples include his bombastic role in *Moonstruck*, fitting for a film preoccupied with opera <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>; his unrestrained performance in the LSD-fueled *Mandy* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>; and his outrageous, Elvis-crooning Sailor in David Lynchâ€™s dreamlike *Wild At Heart* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:27">[00:08:27]</a>.

Cage's "outrageous antics" are often criticized as "out of control" or "over the top" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>. However, he is described as "incredibly thoughtful and incredibly well-versed in the craft of acting" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>. Many of his famously "ridiculous moments" are meticulously planned, as seen in his alphabet recitation in *Vampire's Kiss*, where every movement, hand clap, and holler was carefully thought out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:29">[00:09:29]</a>.

Cage draws inspiration from various sources:
*   **Kabuki:** A Japanese avant-garde performance art with extreme stylization, which informs his tendency to ad-lib made-up lyrics and adopt a different voice for each character <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>. He describes his vocal experimentation as an attempt to use his voice in a "heavy metal or operatic or baroque way" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>.
*   **Physicality:** His "insane attention to the details of body movement" allowed him to portray two distinct characters in *Adaptation* by adjusting his spine for rigid versus relaxed movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:39">[00:10:39]</a>.
*   **German Expressionism:** He has described watching classic German Expressionist films like *Nosferatu* and *Metropolis* as a child, influencing specific hand motions and character physicality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:19">[00:11:19]</a>.
*   **"Nouveau Shamanism":** For his widely panned role in *Ghost Rider*, Cage adopted "nouveau shamanism," integrating Afro-Caribbean techniques, sewing "thousand-year-old Egyptian artifacts into his clothing," and painting his face like a voodoo priest to trick himself into believing he was a character from another dimension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>.

These varied influences demonstrate Cage's commitment to pushing boundaries rather than simply "phoning in" performances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:42">[00:12:42]</a>.

## Why the Resistance to Non-Realism in Acting?

Nicolas Cage "defiantly refuses to conform to realism," questioning why it remains the benchmark for good acting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>. While other art forms, such as music (e.g., "Yellow Submarine," Arnold Schoenberg) and literature (e.g., Virginia Woolf's abstract novels), are celebrated for their departure from realism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:26">[00:13:26]</a>, film acting often faces criticism for similar deviations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:48">[00:13:48]</a>.

One theory suggests that film, with its close-ups and point-of-view shots, is an intimate art form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>. Audiences tend to identify closely with central characters and expect performers to behave "approximately the way that we would" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:02">[00:14:02]</a>. Cage's "wild reactions and bug-eyed expressions" confound these expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:27">[00:14:27]</a>, and when taken out of context in memes, his performances can appear inherently ridiculous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:31">[00:14:31]</a>. Furthermore, Cage often works with collaborators who do not share his acting philosophy, creating a clash of styles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:20">[00:14:20]</a>.

### [[Moral ambiguity in cinema | Moral Ambiguity]] and Character Perception
A non-realistic acting style, like Cage's, can prevent audiences from sympathizing with morally ambiguous characters. For example, his Peter in *Vampire's Kiss* is so "inaccessible" and "clearly performative" that he avoids the risk of becoming sympathetic to the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:11">[00:15:11]</a>. This contrasts with Christian Bale's charismatic but murderous Patrick Bateman in *American Psycho*, who, despite being a satirical poke at 80s yuppie culture, became an "iconic antihero" admired for his "social-status obsessiveness" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:43">[00:14:43]</a>.

Ultimately, the value of non-realistic acting is subjective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:34">[00:15:34]</a>. While some find [[Nicolas Cages unique acting style | Nicolas Cage's style]] ridiculous, others consider it "totally awesome" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:36">[00:15:36]</a>. Regardless of individual preference, his compelling, different, and controversial approach makes Hollywood "a lot more interesting" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:30">[00:15:30]</a>.
