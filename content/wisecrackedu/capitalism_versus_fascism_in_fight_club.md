### Rationalization and Loss of Individuality
*Fight Club* extensively features the sociological concept of "rationalization," which involves organizing society for efficiency rather than tradition, custom, or individual desires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>. This means factory-produced goods replace artisanal ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>, and social life is optimized for large numbers of people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.

The narrator's life is a prime example of this: his possessions are mass-produced and predictable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>, and he views his own home as a "condo on the 15th floor of a filing cabinet for widows and young professionals," reducing his existence to efficient storage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:46">[00:02:46]</a>. The film highlights the downside of this efficiency, where "efficient" often means "efficiently consuming" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>, leading to the adage, "The things you own, end up owning you" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>.

The narrator describes everything as a "copy of a copy of a copy," indicating that both products and consumers become indistinguishable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:04">[00:03:04]</a>. Director David Fincher emphasized this by placing Starbucks cups in nearly every scene <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>.

[[philosophical_influences_in_fight_club | Philosophers Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer]] argued that this trend of indistinguishable commodities is a direct result of rationalization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>. They believed that standardized products with fake differences are designed to be cost-effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. For them, this commodification makes life dull <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a> and "kind of [[starship_troopers_and_its_depiction_of_fascism | fascist]]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>. Living in a rationalized world dehumanizes people, turning them into "lonely, alienated drones at the behest of [[consumerism_and_rationalization_in_fight_club | consumer capitalism]]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>.

The narrator's "single-serving lifestyle" on business trips further illustrates this dehumanization, where even human connections are small and disposable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. He realizes he has reduced people to statistics in his reports <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. Adorno and Horkheimer also posited that "amusement" under modern capitalism becomes a "prolongation of work," serving only to rejuvenate individuals for their next work shift, and remaining controlled by the system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:12">[00:05:12]</a>.

Adorno went as far as comparing mass consumption to [[starship_troopers_and_its_depiction_of_fascism | fascism]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>. He believed the same rationalization mechanism that makes people blindly spend money on large corporations like Starbucks is the same one that leads them to embrace [[starship_troopers_and_its_depiction_of_fascism | fascist]] ideology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>. Just as citizens mobilize under a totalitarian dictator, modern society mobilizes under capitalism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>.

### Project Mayhem: A New Form of Rationalization and Fascism

Under Tyler's leadership, Project Mayhem mirrors the very rationalized world it opposes.
