## Critiquing Celebrity Culture: "Britney's New Look"

The episode "Britney's New Look" is among the more gruesome [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] episodes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>. In it, Britney Spears is hounded by paparazzi to the point of "Kobaining herself" and being photographed to death by the townspeople <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>.

This episode directly references Shirley Jackson's 1948 short story, "The Lottery" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:59">[00:00:59]</a>. In Jackson's story, a small town draws lots to determine who is stoned to death as a sacrifice for a good harvest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>. "The Lottery" itself is a commentary on mob psychology, tradition, and scapegoating, interrogating how easily tradition can justify unspeakable cruelty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>.

[[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] substitutes the lottery winner Tessie Hutchinson with Britney Spears <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>. The show juxtaposes the typical lottery narrative of instantaneous riches with the dark reality that "celebrity" can be a curse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>. This highlights the cruel, sacrificial economy of celebrity reporting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>. People who achieve fame face the possibility of being symbolically "stoned in the tabloids," with camera flashes and comments about physical flaws replacing literal stones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>.

The episode satirizes tabloid media's insatiable appetite and ruthless disregard for a celebrity's humanity in pursuit of profit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. "The Lottery" exposes the shallow justification for human sacrifice and public complicity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>. Similarly, "Britney's New Look" suggests that Britney's publicist, music producers, MTV awards, and the paparazzi are all responsible for her "public execution" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>. The episode "exposes the cult of celebrity as a ruthless economy built on voyeuristic consumption of celebrity dysfunction" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:45">[00:02:45]</a>.

## Ads, AI, and PC Culture: Season 19 Finale

In the final three episodes of Season 19, Jimmy discovers that ads have become indistinguishable from reality and even other people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] develops this idea with nods to sci-fi classics about artificial intelligence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>.

*   **_Ex Machina_ and _Blade Runner_ References**: Jimmy's interrogation of Leslie mirrors interview scenes in *Ex-Machina* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>. His question, "Does she know she’s an ad?" parallels the *Blade Runner* question, "Does she know she’s a replicant?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>. These sci-fi works explore the line between humanity and machine, asking "what does it mean to be human?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:26">[00:03:26]</a>. [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] replaces replicants with advertisements that are so well-adapted they are indistinguishable from humans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>, critiquing sponsored content and the blurring lines between entertainment and advertising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>.
*   **The Voight-Kampff Test and [[political_correctness_and_its_critique_in_south_park | PC Culture]]**: The final episode depicts PC Principal massacring ads, similar to Deckard retiring replicants in *Blade Runner*, even administering a quick Voight-Kampff test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>. While the original test gauges empathy, in [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]], it determines if someone is "PC," implying that if you don't care about being [[political_correctness_and_its_critique_in_south_park | PC]], "you aren’t human" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>.
*   **Femme Fatale Trope**: [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] also borrows the Femme Fatale trope from *Ex Machina* and *Blade Runner*, with Kyle and Jimmy falling for the "sizzling Ad Leslie," paralleling Deckard's love for Rachael and Caleb's for Ava <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>.
*   **Critique of Cultural Capitalism**: The show's genius lies in placing PC Principal in the same existential crisis as Deckard (is he human or something else?) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>. Beyond humanity, [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] introduces a criticism of [[south_park_and_capitalism | cultural capitalism]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:57">[00:04:57]</a>. PC Principal becomes akin to a lifestyle brand, and advertising sells people on a "way of living," where "being PC is just one of them" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>.

## Performative Charity: "Scause for Applause"

The episode "Scause for Applause" targets childhood heroes and the nature of charity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:29">[00:05:29]</a>. It uses Jesus and Stan as stand-ins for Lance Armstrong, twisting the moral center of Dr. Seuss stories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:32">[00:05:32]</a>.

When it's revealed that Jesus used pain killers and HGH, the people of [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] cut off their "What Would Jesus Do" bracelets, parodying the Lance Armstrong doping scandal and the abandonment of the Livestrong bandwagon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:40">[00:05:40]</a>.

Instead of just criticizing Lance Armstrong, [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] shifts its focus to critique the general population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>. The animation even changes to a "Seuss mode" halfway through <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.

*   **Dr. Seuss's "The Sneeches" Reference**: When Stan and Jesus want to make new bracelets, they visit Doctor P.F. Pityet Bracelets and Factory, a reference to Sylvester McMonkey McBean from Dr. Seuss's "The Sneeches" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>. The Sneeches, with or without stars on their bellies, are preyed upon by McBean, who profits from their desire to feel special <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>. Similarly, P.F. Pityet removes and creates new "scause bands," his name clearly referencing an economics that preys on "pity and self-congratulations" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:58">[00:06:58]</a>.
*   **Critique of "Raising Awareness"**: "Raising awareness" is ridiculed as a form of "sick pity," where the giver gains more self-gratification than the receiver of aid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:16">[00:07:16]</a>. While Dr. Seuss preaches kindness and commonalities, [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] suggests that today's kindness and "love of difference" are often no more than a way for people to build their "cultural capital" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:35">[00:07:35]</a>.
*   **Superficiality of Charity**: The episode lampoons individuals like Cartman who wear charities as a fashion statement, highlighting how people prefer causes that require little effort over "the hard work of helping others" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>. [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] strips the Dr. Seuss narrative of its moralistic content, revealing the "perverted narcissism that pervades these acts of charity" within the "non-controversial act of 'consciousness raising'" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>.

## Media Fearmongering and Disaster Films

Disaster films are a common target for [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>. Episodes like "Pee" (parodying *2012*), "Pandemic" and "Pandemic 2: The Startling" (*Cloverfield*), and "Two Days After the Day After Tomorrow" (*The Day After Tomorrow*) use over-the-top hyperbole to challenge media fearmongering and doomsday warnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>.

In these episodes, tiny events spark disproportionate calamities:
*   Too many people peeing in a water park leads to a tsunami in "Pee" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a>.
*   A single boating accident causes the town to fear an ice age in "Two Days After the Day After Tomorrow" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>.
*   The head of homeland security attempting to stop Peruvian pan pipe bands leads to a giant guinea attack in "Pandemic" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>.

Each episode riffs on the "reactionary nature of the media, panic, and subsequent news coverage" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>. For example, in "Two Days After," news reports inflate Beaverton casualties from hundreds to hundreds of millions, a direct reference to inflated death tolls during Hurricane Katrina <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:33">[00:09:33]</a>.

The show's portrayal of news reports detailing looting, rape, and cannibalism "without any actual reporting" is a scathing indictment of journalistic practices that circulate news without fact-checking or independent verification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:08">[00:10:08]</a>.

[[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] also critiques the tendency to instantly seek a cause or person responsible for catastrophic events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>. The townspeople are less concerned with helping and more interested in playing the "blame game," ignoring Stan's admission that he "broke the Damn" because it doesn't fit their narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>.

By pushing the tropes of disaster films—thinly connected plots, sketchy science, shaky cameras, and ridiculous catastrophes—to their extreme, [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] exposes the "inanity of both the disaster genre and contemporary 'journalism'" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:05">[00:11:05]</a>.

## Conclusion

Following in the traditions of Jonathan Swift, O'Henry, and Mark Twain, Trey Parker and Matt Stone paint a bleak picture of society, filled with "sexy robots, politically correct bullies, foul mouthed children, sentient whole foods, and an endless number of clueless celebrities" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>. [[south_parks_approach_to_social_satire | South Park]] "disembowels popular culture and feeds the audience the intestinal spillage," cannibalizing tropes and references from American discourse and re-presenting them to create a new understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>.
