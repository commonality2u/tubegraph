## Case Study: The Wolf of Wall Street

The closing shot of *The Wolf of Wall Street*, characterized by a simple push-in and crane-up, is presented as transformative to the film's overall meaning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>.

### Shifting Tones and Narrative Ambiguity
Throughout most of its runtime, *The Wolf of Wall Street* utilizes poppy, upbeat musical hits to emphasize the debauchery of Jordan Belfort's lifestyle, which includes his yacht, women, drugs, and parties, all funded by defrauding investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. This use of music impacts the narrative, as detailed in [[Influence of music and media on the films narrative]]. However, the film's ending introduces a solemn, meditative music cue, drowning out a client's voice describing "the pen" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:41">[00:01:41]</a>.

Unlike similar films where corrupt figures face definitive justice, *The Wolf of Wall Street* lacks overt moralizing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>. Jordan Belfort's prison sentence is depicted as a "cakewalk," and he doesn't serve his full term, implying that "nothing has changed" as he continues to sell and people continue to buy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:19">[00:02:19]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>. This approach challenges audience perception, which is also a theme in [[The films satire on militarism and audience perception]].

### Interpretations of the Final Shot

The final shot of *The Wolf of Wall Street* offers multiple interpretations that serve as a broader cultural critique:

1.  **A Mirror to the Audience:** The backlight in the seminar scene evokes a projector shining on an audience, suggesting that the moral blame for the depicted depravity extends beyond Jordan Belfort to the culture that permits and encourages his actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. Belfort himself notes that his scam thrives because people desire to "get rich quickly" and want "something for nothing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:11">[00:03:11]</a>. The film implicates the audience, who, despite not engaging in Belfort's extreme behaviors, share his desire for instant wealth and often live vicariously through his exploits, finding amusement in his actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. This commentary on societal values can also be found in [[Cultural and political commentary in Idiocracy]].

2.  **Worship of Money:** The scene, featuring people intently staring at Jordan Belfort, can be likened to a religious congregation. Director Martin Scorsese, a devout Catholic known for exploring faith in his films, appears to suggest that characters in *The Wolf of Wall Street* worship money instead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:05">[00:04:05]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>. The camera focuses on the "painfully average faces" of these desperate, plain-looking individuals seeking meaning and elevation from their mundane existence through money, cynically likening it to the transformative power of faith, albeit without the promise of easy wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>.

3.  **Enjoyment of Exploitation:** By solemnly depicting a crowd that reveres Jordan despite his "detestable actions," Scorsese hints at a darker truth: that people might actually enjoy being taken advantage of <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:51">[00:04:51]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:55">[00:04:55]</a>. This idea is supported by French philosopher Jean-Francois Lyotard, who suggested that exploited individuals, like the English unemployed, found a "hysterical, masochistic" enjoyment in their harsh conditions and the dissolution of their personal identity and communities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>. Applied to the film, this interpretation suggests that the closing image signifies the "voluntary submission of the masses," who not only tolerate white-collar criminals stealing their money but find a "perverse enjoyment" in their mundane existence under the influence of figures like Jordan Belfort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>.

The singular final shot is emblematic of *The Wolf of Wall Street*'s complexity, positioning it as a "totem of our culture of excess" that defies easy answers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. This type of [[social_commentary_in_horror_films|social commentary]] is also seen in other genres. The film's critical approach extends to [[celebrity_culture_and_media_critique]] through its depiction of Belfort's lifestyle and how it is consumed by the audience.
