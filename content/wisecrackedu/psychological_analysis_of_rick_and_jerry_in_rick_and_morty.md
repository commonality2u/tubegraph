## The Problem of Intelligence and Misery

The question arises: Is Rick destined to be miserable because of his intelligence, and if so, is it better to be a [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]]? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>

Nineteenth-century author Fyodor Dostoevsky would likely disagree, stating in *The Idiot*: “It is better to be unhappy and know the worst, than to be happy in a fool’s paradise” <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:46">[00:00:46]</a>. This statement aptly describes the dynamic between [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]] and Rick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>. Rick's despondency is fueled by his knowledge that "nothing matters," while a "fool's paradise" could refer to situations like Jerryboree, the Shymaliens simulation, Pluto, or the Titanic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:53">[00:00:53]</a>.

### Rick Sanchez: The Tortured Genius

Despite being the smartest man in the universe, Rick is surprisingly relatable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>. Creator Dan Harmon notes that people can identify with Rick's feeling of being misunderstood and overwhelmed by their daily lives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. Many can also relate to Rick's self-loathing and self-destructive behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:36">[00:01:36]</a>.

Though practically omnipotent, with the power to invent, transform, and destroy to get what he wants, Rick is miserable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. His inner turmoil manifests in subtle and overt ways, such as his catchphrase "wubba lubba dub dub" meaning "I am in great pain, please help me," and his suicide attempt in Season 2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>.

Rick attributes his misery to his intelligence, stating: "Am I evil? Worse. You're smart. When you know nothing matters, the universe is yours. And I’ve never met a universe that was into it... smart people get a chance to climb on top, take reality for a ride, but it’ll never stop trying to throw you. And, eventually, it will. There’s no other way off" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>. He is also bored senseless by mundane life, finding temporary reprieve only through his adventures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:39">[00:06:39]</a>.

### [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]] Smith: The Discombobulated Everyman

In contrast, [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]] is portrayed as less intelligent and often discombobulated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>. He functions best when he doesn't overthink, as his attempts at deep thought often worsen situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>. Even in difficult circumstances, [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]] doesn't appear to suffer to the same degree as Rick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>. He doesn't resort to alcohol or question his existence, and his threshold for happiness seems low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>.

[[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]] lacks social, survival, or general skills, often relying on pity to navigate life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>. Rick highlights this: "You act like prey, but you’re a predator! You use pity to lure in your victims! That’s how you survive! I survive because I know everything... and you survive because people think, "Oh, this poor piece of [bleep]. He never gets a break. I can’t stand the deafening silent wails of his wilting soul. I guess I’ll hire him or marry him"" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.

## Intelligence and [[themes_of_mental_health_in_rick_and_morty | Mental Health]]

The stereotype of the "tortured genius" is prevalent in pop culture, often making it seem as though depression is a prerequisite for success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:02:56]</a>. However, many brilliant individuals do not suffer from [[themes_of_mental_health_in_rick_and_morty | mental illness]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>.

### Research on Intelligence and Depression

A 2017 study by Dr. Ruth Karpinski surveyed 3,700 Mensa members, finding that over a third reported being depressed, which is five times the national average <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. However, 10% of those self-reporting depression had not received a medical diagnosis, highlighting the imperfection of self-diagnosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>.

Karpinski proposed that individuals with IQs of 130 or more have "hyper brains," allowing them to comprehend the world's problems, uncertainties, and possibilities more easily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>. This concept is based on psychiatrist Dr. Kazimierz Dabrowski's work, who observed that exceptionally intelligent patients tended to over-contemplate everything, especially negative aspects, and experienced deeper depression compared to intellectually average individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.

### Dabrowski's Overexcitability

Dabrowski termed these traits "overexcitability," which he viewed not as negative, but as predictive of potential for self-actualization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>. He described five forms of overexcitability:
*   Excessive energy or nervousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>
*   Deep appreciation for arts or music <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>
*   Extreme curiosity or an active mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>
*   Vivid imagination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>
*   Extreme and complex emotional experiences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>

Rick exemplifies all these characteristics: he is energetic, freestyles music, is overanalytical, capable of inventing worlds, and has complex feelings for his family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

### Existential Depression

Psychologists suggest that people who think with "hyper brains" are more prone to existential depression, characterized by feelings of hopelessness stemming from the belief that life is meaningless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>. Dr. James Webb, a psychologist studying gifted children, notes that while this can occur during a crisis for anyone, it is more likely to happen spontaneously to those with above-average intelligence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>. Rick's worldview aligns with this, constantly confronting the futility of existence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>.

[[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]], in contrast, does not exhibit traits associated with a "hyper brain," unless enthusiasm for rare coins, mobile games, or the Titanic counts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.

### Intelligence, Wealth, and Happiness

While some studies link intelligence to depression, others suggest that intelligence can unlock opportunities that lead to greater health and happiness, particularly financial success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>. Being smarter is associated with greater wealth and, consequently, better health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a>. Some research indicates a positive correlation between higher IQ and self-reported happiness, challenging the idea that genius inevitably leads to misery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>.

## Social Isolation and [[critiques_of_individualism_and_conformity_in_rick_and_morty | Conformity]]

Geniuses are often associated with eccentricity, and Rick's intensity can overwhelm others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>. This eccentricity, a hallmark of high IQ individuals, can lead to social isolation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>. Attempting to fit in with "normal-minded" people may require minimizing this intensity, potentially resulting in a "crisis of self" where [[critiques_of_individualism_and_conformity_in_rick_and_morty | conformity]] makes individuals feel lost about their true identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>.

Rick can be seen as being in a perpetual crisis of self. On Earth, he remains an oddball, though this doesn't seem to bother him much <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:03">[00:09:03]</a>. However, he does compromise his nature for his family, such as attending therapy, which he finds mundane and boring <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>.

## Conclusion: Rick vs. [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]]

Some research suggests that the "hyper brains" of highly intelligent people make them more prone to depression due to their unique perception of the world and intensity, which can be off-putting to others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:26">[00:10:26]</a>. Conversely, their intelligence can lead to high-paying jobs, reducing stress from poverty and allowing access to better healthcare, which may contribute to overall happiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>.

The question of whether it's better to be unhappy with knowledge (a Rick) or happy in ignorance (a [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]]) remains subjective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>. While psychology does not offer a definitive answer, limited evidence suggests that while one might be less susceptible to existential depression as a [[character_analysis_of_jerry_in_rick_and_morty | Jerry]], a "Rick" might experience a better life overall due to leveraging their intelligence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>.
