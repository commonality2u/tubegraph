## The Wolf of Wall Street and Audience Complicity

For much of its three-hour runtime, *The Wolf of Wall Street* portrays Jordan Belfort's lavish lifestyle of excess, funded by defrauding investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>. Unlike films that depict corrupt figures receiving clear justice, *The Wolf of Wall Street* avoids overt moralizing; Belfort's prison sentence is depicted as a "cakewalk," and he doesn't serve his full term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>. This narrative choice sets the stage for a deeper examination of societal responsibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:19">[00:02:19]</a>.

### The Mirror Effect of the Final Shot

The film's closing shot features a simple push-in and crane-up on an audience, with a backlight evoking a projector shining on the back of viewers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>. This visual metaphor suggests that the film is holding up a mirror to the moviegoers themselves, implying that blame for the moral depravity depicted in the film lies not solely with Jordan Belfort, but also with "us, and the culture that allows and encourages his behavior" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>.

Jordan Belfort himself states that his scam succeeded because people "want to get rich and they want to get rich quickly. They all want something for nothing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>. This highlights the audience's shared desire for quick wealth, suggesting that Belfort's scheme would not have worked without a culture susceptible to his allure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>.

Director Martin Scorsese is interpreted as "calling us out" for having spent three hours "partying with Jordan, laughing and reveling in his exploits" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>. The film suggests that the audience [[analysis_of_audiences_complicity_and_enjoyment_in_excess | revels in everything he does]], living vicariously through him and wishing they could live his life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. This "reveling" is seen as a key aspect of the [[analysis_of_audiences_complicity_and_enjoyment_in_excess | analysis of audience's complicity and enjoyment in excess]].

### Voluntary Submission and Perverse Enjoyment

The closing image further suggests a more sinister cultural phenomenon: that society might "actually like being taken advantage of" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:55">[00:04:55]</a>. This idea is supported by French philosopher Jean-Francois Lyotard, who famously posited that individuals can find enjoyment in suffering and destruction, even in their own exploitation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>.

> "The English unemployed did not become workers to survive, they… enjoyed the hysterical, masochistic, whatever exhaustion it was of hanging on the mines, in the foundries, in the factories, in hell, they enjoyed it… enjoyed the mad destruction of their organic bodies… the decomposition of their personal identity… enjoyed the dissolution of their families and villages, and enjoyed the new monstrous anonymity of the suburbs and the pubs in the morning and evening." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a>

Applying this philosophy to *The Wolf of Wall Street*, the final shot may indicate "the voluntary submission of the masses, who not only don’t mind white collar criminals stealing their money, but find a perverse enjoyment in their banal existence under the heel of the Jordan Belforts of the world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.

Ultimately, this single shot highlights the complexity of *The Wolf of Wall Street* as a critique of cultural excess, suggesting that the film defies easy answers about accountability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.
