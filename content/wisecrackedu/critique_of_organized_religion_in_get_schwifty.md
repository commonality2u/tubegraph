The *Rick and Morty* episode "Get Schwifty" is highlighted for its complex take on religion, going beyond typical trivialization to explore the human tendency to rationalize beliefs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>. This analysis forms part of a broader [[philosophy_in_rick_and_morty_episode_get_schwifty | Wisecrack Edition on the philosophy of "Get Schwifty"]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>.

## Initial Critique of Organized Religion

Upon the appearance of a giant floating head demanding humanity "SHOW ME WHAT YOU GOT!" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a> and causing global catastrophes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>, the episode quickly satirizes the opportunistic nature of organized religion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>. The church, facing world-ending events, is depicted as being preoccupied with needing more faith, more money, and a new church organ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>.

> "If sanity has been restored to this assembly, I'd like to talk about the cost of repairing our new organ." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>

## The Headism Cult and Arbitrary Faith

The episode introduces "Headism," a cult that emerges from a cosmic problem of perspective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>. Lacking understanding of the Cromulons' true purpose (filming a reality show) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>, people naturally form a cult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. This satirizes the arbitrary nature of faith and its ceremonies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. For example, the Headists' "Ascension Day," where "unwantables" are sent to the gods, draws a striking resemblance to the crucifixion of Christ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>.

## Exposing Logical Fallacies

"Get Schwifty" goes deeper than a simple indictment of religion, instead targeting the reasoning behind belief structures themselves and exposing fallacies in what people believe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>. The episode specifically mocks two common logical fallacies that conflate correlation with causation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>:

*   **Cum Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**: This fallacy, where simultaneous events are mistakenly believed to be causally related <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>, is central to Headism's formation. Principal Vagina's prayer coincides with the abatement of cataclysmic events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>, leading followers to believe his prayer caused it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. In reality, the Heads were pleased by Rick's impromptu performance of "Get Schwifty," a song about bodily functions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>. This fallacy is reiterated when the Heads' booing during Rick's performance without Morty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a> is misinterpreted by Headists as anger over Principal Vagina trying to ascend Beth and Jerry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>.
*   **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**: This fallacy incorrectly assumes that because one thing precedes another, it is the cause <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>. While not directly tied to religious practice, the President's belief that naming people causes their death illustrates this broader cognitive flaw <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>.

Characters with a limited perspective, unaware of the reality show, consistently believe their ceremonies influence the Heads, a point acknowledged by Beth and Jerry who observe the correlation without causation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.

## The Human Brain and Confirmation Bias

The episode posits that people do not simply fall into silly beliefs out of irrationality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:36">[00:04:36]</a>. Instead, they "over rationalize their thinking to the point of absurdity" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:40">[00:04:40]</a>. Cognitive psychologists suggest that pseudo-scientific beliefs, like doomsday cults or conspiracy theories, are resilient because they are sustained by the structure of the human brain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>.

Belief systems persist and spread not through trickery or mass stupidity, but through appeals to rationality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:57">[00:04:57]</a>. The human brain is "hard-wired to search for logical conclusions and easy explanations" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>. People tend to believe what aligns with their worldview <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>. This is explained by:

*   **Confirmation Bias**: The tendency to seek out and interpret information that confirms one's existing beliefs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:29">[00:05:29]</a>. This bias allows people to overlook glaring flaws in their belief systems for personal benefit, as seen with Summer's parents who ignore the Headists' practices as long as Summer is "thriving" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:35">[00:05:35]</a>.

## Broader Satire Beyond Religion

While "Rick and Morty" enjoys "shitting on religion" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>, the episode ultimately argues that "post hoc rationalization is an inevitability of our cause and effect thought process" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>. Even the audience is fooled by this tendency, as demonstrated by Rick's snake and wristwatch trick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>, which plays on the brain's desire to attribute event A as the cause of event B <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>.

The episode's critique goes beyond religious institutions, challenging the ability to make predictions and understand the complexity of existence itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>. This depth distinguishes *Rick and Morty* from contemporary satire, as "Nothing is safe. Not even belief" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
