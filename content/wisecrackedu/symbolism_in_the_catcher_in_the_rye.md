J.D. Salinger's *The Catcher in the Rye* tells the story of [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden Caulfield]], a 16-year-old boy reflecting on events that led to his stay in a mental hospital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>. The narrative explores [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] struggle to find stability and acceptance in a "broken society" where people are often "faking" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. When he comes up empty-handed, he feels compelled to "buck the system" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>. The novel employs several key symbols to convey [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] internal state and his critique of the adult world.

## Main Symbols

### The Red Hunting Hat
The red hunting hat is a significant symbol in the novel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>. By turning his hunting hat around, [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] indicates that his values are the opposite of the rest of society's <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>. This manner of wearing the hat is also similar to how baseball catchers wear theirs, foreshadowing [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] eventual role as the "Catcher in the Rye" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>.

### The Catcher in the Rye
The central symbol of the novel is derived from a dream [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] shares with his sister Phoebe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a>. [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] envisions himself in a large field of rye filled with children, standing at a cliff to catch any "little homies" who are about to fall off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>.

> On the literal, [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] be saving kids from fallin off a cliff, but figuratively he saving them from the all the bent-ass sh%# of adulthood. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>

Figuratively, [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] aims to protect children from the "bent-ass sh%#" of adulthood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>. Although [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden]] cannot prevent the pure world of children from transitioning into the "fake-ass world of adults," he intends to resist this change for himself and others for as long as possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>.

### The Museum's Glass Cases
[[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] thoughts on the glass cases in the museum symbolize his desire to freeze time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>. He expresses a reluctance to join the ranks of "fake-ass adults," wishing instead to remain unchanged like the "plastic playas behind the glass" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.

### The Ducks in Central Park Lake
Despite [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] tough talk, he possesses a gentle soul <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>. This is evident in his recurring question about what the ducks in the Central Park lake do once the water freezes over <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>. This concern highlights his underlying innocence and vulnerability.

In the end, [[holden_caulfields_struggles_with_society | Holden's]] sensitivity in a world of deceit ultimately leads him to spend his days in a psych ward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>.
