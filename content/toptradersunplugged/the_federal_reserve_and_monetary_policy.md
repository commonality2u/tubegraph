## The Evolving Role of the Federal Reserve

The [[role of central banks and monetary policy | Federal Reserve's role]] is often misunderstood by the public, who tend to see it as simply "the guy in the suit behind the desk" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:19">[00:18:19]</a>. While the [[role of central banks and monetary policy | Federal Reserve]] was originally established as a lender of last resort, offering punitive rates of interest for borrowing, its function has become corrupted over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:31">[00:18:31]</a>. The institution was created to respond to financial crises and smooth the business cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:55">[00:16:55]</a>.

Instead of smoothing, the objective has shifted to preventing the downward half of the business cycle entirely, primarily by "throwing money at it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:04">[00:19:04]</a>. The current fiat system has given the [[Federal Reserve monetary policy and interest rates | Federal Reserve]] "enormous latitude" to print money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:11">[00:19:11]</a>.

> "When you corrupt the price of money, you corrupt every single transaction that takes place on a daily basis everywhere. And if you have no solid foundation, if there's no truth in the price of money, then you can't have truth in society because everything in society, in one way or another, comes back to trust in money." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:12">[00:20:12]</a>

### Consequences of Monetary Policy

The prolonged period of nearly free capital for the past three decades has corrupted the price of money, impacting every transaction and undermining societal trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:03">[00:20:03]</a>. This policy has led to:

*   **Increased Inequality:** Massive asset price inflation in equity, bond, and real estate markets has enriched a specific class of people, while the average person who doesn't own large portfolios or investment properties has not benefited <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:57">[00:25:57]</a>.
*   **Suppression of Recessions:** While more frequent, shallower recessions would typically "clean out the dead wood," the [[Federal Reserve Policies and Economic Impact | Federal Reserve's actions]] have prevented these necessary corrections, leading to a situation where a serious recession could cause everything to decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:39">[00:19:39]</a>.
*   **Market Dependence:** The stock and bond markets have become dependent on the [[Federal Reserve Policies and Economic Impact | Federal Reserve]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:53">[00:19:53]</a>.
*   **Political Problems:** The belief that printing money has no ill consequences has encouraged fiscal errors by politicians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:47">[00:20:47]</a>. Central bankers knew this would ultimately lead to problems, but their "hubris" led them to believe they could control it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:11">[00:21:11]</a>.

### Monetary vs. Fiscal Policy

A "broad misconception" exists regarding monetary policy and the creation of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:54">[00:56:54]</a>. The argument is that while [[Federal Reserve monetary policy and interest rates | monetary policy]] (like Quantitative Easing) is often seen as inflationary, the money created often does not enter the broader economy, resulting in zero or even negative velocity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:31">[00:55:31]</a>. This money has fueled technological advancements and globalization, which are largely deflationary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:00">[00:56:00]</a>.

However, recent "helicopter money" or [[federal_reserve_policies_and_economic_impact | fiscal policy]] — real dollars with a velocity of one — *has* entered the economy. The US, for example, saw an order of magnitude greater fiscal stimulus in the last two years than ever before, in real inflation-adjusted terms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:16">[00:56:16]</a>. This direct injection led to 8% inflation within 18 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:32">[00:57:32]</a>.

### Loss of Confidence in Institutions

A key feature of the "Fourth Turning," a cyclical period described by Neil Howe and Bill Strauss, is the loss of confidence in institutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:36">[00:32:36]</a>. This erosion of trust has been evident since the 2008 Global Financial Crisis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:57">[00:32:57]</a>. Ironically, confidence in [[central_banks_and_monetary_policy | central banks]] should be highest when [[Federal Reserves interest rate policies | interest rates]] are high, as it indicates proactive measures to address societal problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:07">[00:32:07]</a>. Yet, confidence has been highest when [[Federal Reserves interest rate policies | interest rates]] were at their lowest, a period marked by "abject" policy handling of crises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:23">[00:32:23]</a>.

The decline in trust extends to other institutions like the CDC and WHO, particularly following the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:24">[00:33:24]</a>. This loss of faith is necessary for the "tearing down of old institutions and the rebuilding in the light of a new generation" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:32">[00:34:32]</a>.

## Central Banking 2.0 and the Yen's Role

The Bank of Japan (BOJ) has been a pioneer in "yield curve control," effectively pegging the 10-year JGB (Japanese Government Bond) at zero <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:19">[00:45:19]</a>. They currently own 67% of all JGBs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:33">[00:44:33]</a> and could potentially own the rest within 50 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:45">[00:44:45]</a>.

The Australian Central Bank (RBA) also attempted yield curve control but "capitulated" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:21">[00:46:21]</a>. While Japan's domestic ownership of JGBs was once thought to insulate it, the yen has recently weakened dramatically, moving 20% in a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:50">[00:46:50]</a>.

The critical question is what happens if a [[central_banks_and_monetary_policy | central bank]] owns all its government's bonds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:50">[00:44:50]</a>. Possible outcomes include a swap between the [[Central banks and monetary policy | central bank]] and the Ministry of Finance, or the issuance of a perpetual zero-coupon bond, effectively writing off the debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:33">[00:47:33]</a>. This would be an unprecedented "experiment" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:52">[00:48:52]</a>.

The recent shift in the yen's stability signals a new macro environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:47">[00:49:47]</a>. Central bankers globally are showing vulnerability:

*   The RBA governor apologized for their "hapless performance" on yield curve control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:09">[00:50:09]</a>.
*   The Bank of England governor expressed shock at surging food prices and helplessness against inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:23">[00:50:23]</a>.
*   Jerome Powell stated, "with the benefit of hindsight, we maybe should have acted sooner" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:47">[00:50:47]</a>.

This vulnerability suggests that [[central_banks_and_monetary_policy | central banks]] are "about to be tested" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:30">[00:51:30]</a>. The lack of stress tests for [[central_banks_and_monetary_policy | central banks]] themselves, compared to financial institutions, is also noted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:48">[00:51:48]</a>.

### Future Outlook

The current period is likened to the decade of the 1970s, where [[Interest rates and monetary policy | interest rates]] eventually rose sharply after a period of stagnation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:47">[01:11:47]</a>. The need for [[Federal Reserves interest rate policies | interest rates]] to go higher is driven by the need to compensate savers, choke off [[Inflation trends and Federal Reserve policies | inflation]], and address market excesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:31">[01:11:31]</a>. This rise in [[Interest rates and monetary policy | interest rates]] will accompany rising commodity prices, marking a shift from an "age of virtual" to an "age of virtuous" where real assets are valued over digital or financial constructs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:50">[01:08:50]</a>.
