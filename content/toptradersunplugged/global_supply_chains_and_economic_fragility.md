## The Interplay of Low Interest Rates and Globalization

The relationship between interest rates and globalization is not new <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:27">[00:53:27]</a>. The first major phase of globalization, from the mid-19th century up to World War I, was characterized by declining interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:38">[00:53:38]</a>. This era saw a very long bull market in bonds, with falling long-term rates in the US and UK between 1850 and 1900 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:55">[00:53:55]</a>. This period of rampant globalization was linked to:
*   Increased trade with the rest of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:22">[00:54:22]</a>.
*   Access to cheap labor globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:24">[00:54:24]</a>.
*   A disinflationary or deflationary impact on the cost of traded goods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:32">[00:54:32]</a>.
*   A dampening of the bargaining power of the domestic labor force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:46">[00:54:46]</a>.

This combination ultimately induced a decline in interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:04">[00:55:04]</a>.

In more recent times, [[global_economic_and_financial_challenges | global trade]] is largely financed in US dollars, and [[global_energy_crisis_and_supply_chain_issues | global supply chains]] are predominantly dollar-funded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:22">[00:55:22]</a>. As interest rates fell, the cost of dollar funding became cheaper, incentivizing companies to expand their supply chains further around the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:53">[00:55:53]</a>.

## The "Roundabout Production Methods" and Fragility

The concept of "roundabout production methods" from the Austrian School of economics is relevant here <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:06">[00:56:06]</a>. This refers to the amount of capital tied up for a period of time in production processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:08">[00:56:08]</a>. When the cost of funding a supply chain is very low, it encourages the creation of very long and complex supply chains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:16">[00:56:16]</a>. However, the longer and more complex these supply chains become, the more potentially fragile they are <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:23">[00:56:23]</a>.

This fragility is exposed by:
*   **Rising Interest Rates** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:30">[00:56:30]</a>: Higher rates make long supply chains more expensive to maintain.
*   **External Shocks** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:35">[00:56:35]</a>: Events like the COVID-19 pandemic and associated supply shocks highlight the vulnerability of these extended networks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:41">[00:56:41]</a>.

## Political and Economic Consequences

Beyond economic fragility, there is also a political backlash against globalization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:49">[00:56:49]</a>. When workers experience stagnant wages for decades, they question the outsourcing of jobs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:54">[00:56:54]</a>. This links low interest rates and globalization to broader political discontent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:26">[00:57:26]</a>.

The dynamic interplay between low interest rates, [[global_economic_and_geopolitical_risks | global economic]] trends, and political outcomes creates a complex system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:49">[00:57:49]</a>.

> "The very low interest rates are like sort of fertilizer and they over fertilize the environment and lead to sort of in a way weak and fragile a weak and fragile ecosystem" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:10">[00:59:10]</a>

This analogy suggests that while low rates may seem beneficial in the short term, they can lead to an over-fertilized, yet weak and fragile, economic ecosystem, similar to how preventing forest fires can lead to more severe fires later on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:43">[00:59:43]</a>. Central banks, by preventing "creative destruction," contribute to this fragility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:38">[00:59:38]</a>.

This deepens the understanding of how monetary policy can inadvertently contribute to [[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | economic challenges of deglobalization]] and [[global_financial_stability_risks | global financial stability risks]] by fostering unsustainable [[supply_and_demand_dynamics_in_natural_resources | supply and demand dynamics]] and undermining long-term stability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:06">[00:53:06]</a>.
