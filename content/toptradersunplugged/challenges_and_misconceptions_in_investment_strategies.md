Jerry Parker, founder and chairman of Chesapeake Capital, discusses the enduring philosophy of Trend Following and addresses common challenges and misconceptions within [[Investment Challenges and Opportunities | investment strategies]]. ## The Foundation of Trend Following Philosophy

Parker's investment philosophy is deeply rooted in the "eternal truths" or "Ten Commandments" taught by Richard Dennis in the Turtle program <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:58">[00:08:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. He maintains that trading should inherently be difficult and that skepticism should be applied to methods promising smoother, easier returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:02">[00:14:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:09">[00:25:09]</a>.

Key tenets of this philosophy include:
*   **Taking Small Losses and Letting Profits Run**: A fundamental rule for managing trades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:32">[00:10:32]</a>.
*   **Massive Diversification**: Spreading risk across many markets is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>.
*   **Simple Execution**: Using one entry, one exit, and a stop loss for clear, disciplined trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>.
*   **Volatility is Not Risk**: This concept, drilled into him, suggests that market volatility doesn't inherently equate to capital risk if managed correctly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43">[00:10:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:44">[00:15:44]</a>.
*   **Sample Size**: The importance of a large sample size in trading decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:57">[00:29:57]</a>.

Parker acknowledges that he took these "eternal truths" more seriously than most of his peers from the Turtle program, who often deviated into broader [[Systematic investment strategies | systematic investment strategies]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:09">[00:09:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:07">[00:10:07]</a>.

## Evolution and Consistency at Chesapeake Capital

Chesapeake Capital was formed in 1988, benefiting from the immediate credibility gained through the Turtle program's track record <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a>. The firm initially experienced many years of managing billions of dollars, especially from public funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>.

### Detours and Returns to Core Principles
Around 2006-2007, Chesapeake Capital experimented with modifications like profit objectives, smoothing techniques, dynamic position sizing, and correlation/volatility management, aiming to improve returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>. However, this led to unexpected performance not seen in backtests, prompting a return to the classic Trend Following method of one entry, one exit, and a stop loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:16">[00:12:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:25">[00:12:25]</a>. This experience reinforced Parker's commitment to the foundational rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:50">[00:12:50]</a>.

### Long-Term Look Back and Market Expansion
A significant change in the firm's approach was shifting from a short-term 20-day look-back to hundreds of days, becoming very long-term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:07">[00:18:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:11">[00:18:11]</a>. This change was prompted by poor performance in the late 1990s and observing large, multi-year trends on weekly charts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:03">[00:20:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:10">[00:20:10]</a>. Backtesting confirmed that this longer-term approach was consistently better, particularly from a "risk one dollar to make three" perspective, rather than focusing on Sharpe ratio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:42">[00:21:42]</a>.

Chesapeake also aggressively expanded into a diverse range of markets, including single stocks, commodities, currencies, interest rates, ETFs, mortgage-backed securities, TIPS, and corporate and municipal bonds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:48">[00:18:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:30">[00:19:30]</a>.

## Key Decisions and Misconceptions in [[Investing strategies and risk management | Investing Strategies and Risk Management]]

Parker highlights two major mistakes he has made over his 39-year career, both of which Richard Dennis warned about in 1983 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:54">[00:22:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:58">[00:22:58]</a>:
1.  **Trading Too Large**: This is considered the most important factor, as it impedes the ability to stay in the game and follow the system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:05">[00:23:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:34">[00:23:34]</a>.
2.  **Lack of Discipline / Not Following Rules**: An average system followed 100% of the time can outperform a better system that is not consistently followed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:18">[00:23:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:25">[00:23:25]</a>.

### The Role of Volatility and Sharpe Ratio
Parker is largely dismissive of the Sharpe ratio as a primary metric for Trend Following, arguing it unfairly and inaccurately measures risk by penalizing positive skew and preventing the core principle of letting profits run <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:41">[00:35:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:56">[00:35:56]</a>. He believes that for Trend Following, consistency in annual or trailing 12-month returns is a better measure of success than a high Sharpe ratio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:33">[00:37:33]</a>.

Regarding volatility, Parker uses it at the point of trade entry to normalize risk across markets but does not dynamically adjust positions based on ongoing volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:02">[00:42:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:07">[00:42:07]</a>. While acknowledging anecdotal evidence of increased volatility around turning points, he cautions against over-optimizing for such events due to small sample sizes and the risk of being whipsawed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:51">[00:42:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:40">[00:43:40]</a>.

### The Debate on Stops and Correlation
Parker believes that keeping losses small or optimal is key, and his backtests suggest that an optimized stop-loss level results in the same average loss as having no stops at all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:04">[00:55:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:10">[00:55:10]</a>. This allows for the use of stops without hurting performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:28">[00:55:28]</a>.

He also dismisses the importance of correlations in ongoing dynamic positioning. For Parker, the goal is to apply simple rules consistently across different market situations, accepting higher or lower risks at different times based on capital, not open trade equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:40">[01:00:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:57">[01:00:57]</a>. This approach of taking small losses and not incorporating open trade profits into sizing decisions helps protect capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:47">[01:02:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:55">[01:02:55]</a>.

## Client Expectations and the Future of Trend Following

A central theme is the conflict between adhering to a strict Trend Following philosophy and meeting client demands for smoothed returns or high Sharpe ratios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:15">[00:45:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:18">[00:45:18]</a>. Parker argues that clients often "want" smoothness but "need" the consistency and resilience offered by pure Trend Following, even if it entails uncomfortable drawdowns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:22">[00:37:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:13">[00:38:13]</a>.

Parker is confident that "100% Trend Following" with a diversified portfolio, including equities, is a superior portfolio strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:11">[01:05:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:41">[01:05:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:24">[01:06:24]</a>. He expects a return to a period where Trend Following strategies will be superior, especially given the unpredictable nature of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:17">[01:07:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:34">[01:07:34]</a>. He believes that people underestimate the dangers and "crazy things" that can happen in the market, making Trend Following one of the best solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:09">[01:08:09]</a>.
