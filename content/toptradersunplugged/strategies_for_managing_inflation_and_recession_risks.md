## The Federal Reserve and Economic Myths

Discussions around the Federal Reserve (FED) and its actions often circulate around three common myths:
1.  **FED Proactivity**: The belief that the FED is proactive in setting interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:48">[02:44:48]</a>. In reality, the FED is 100% reactive, consistently lagging rate changes in T-bills set by the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:08">[02:48:08]</a>. They are constantly "chasing the new thing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:48">[04:26:48]</a>, as exemplified by their "transitory inflation" stance earlier this year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:35:10">[04:35:10]</a>.
2.  **Policy Outcomes**: The idea that the FED's interest rate policy produces specific outcomes, like accelerating or braking the economy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:04">[03:06:04]</a>. It is questionable whether their policies produce outcomes that wouldn't have occurred naturally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:07">[03:19:07]</a>. While the FED's narrative tools are powerful and can influence markets, they have largely been playing from behind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:08:35">[05:08:35]</a>.
3.  **Good/Bad Job**: The assessment that the FED is doing a good or bad job in producing these outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:15">[03:26:15]</a>. What might appear as slowing the economy can lead to recession <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:06">[03:36:06]</a>. The FED is currently "stuck in a box" and lacks the same liquidity to draw upon as in previous periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:05:07">[06:05:07]</a>. Populist forces and fiscal policies aimed at addressing inequality drive inflation, creating a "vicious cycle" where the FED is constrained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:23:45">[06:23:45]</a>. The FED's cyclical responses are short-sighted and may even exacerbate structural inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:02:18">[08:02:18]</a>.

The FED's primary influence has shifted towards the behavior of speculators <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:04">[08:42:04]</a>.

### Policy Errors

A notable policy error was the FED's failure to finance itself for more than a few years when interest rates were at historical lows, instead of issuing 20 or 30-year bonds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:59:00">[09:59:00]</a>. This oversight will likely result in increased costs for taxpayers in the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:14:04">[10:14:04]</a>.

## Market Dynamics and Liquidity

Recent market actions, such as significant S&P, NASDAQ, and Dow Jones movements on November 30th and December 13th, highlight the impact of **illiquidity** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:59:16">[11:59:16]</a>. Top-of-book depth in Futures and total depth in options markets have thinned dramatically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:53:07">[13:53:07]</a>. This is exacerbated by the seasonally illiquid holiday period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:05:27">[14:05:27]</a>, leading to a market unprepared for large news-driven flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:14:00">[14:14:00]</a>.

### Seasonality and Structural Flows
Key factors influencing year-end market behavior include:
*   **December Opex**: The longest-standing expiration (three years old), with high open interest and structured products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:52:43">[14:52:43]</a>.
*   **Holidays**: Increased days off reduce market activity and volume <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:19:16">[15:19:16]</a>.
*   **Volume Weighted Time**: The effective trading time during this period is almost 50% of other periods, leading to faster decay of time and volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:47:09">[15:47:09]</a>.
*   **Skew**: As December Opex comes down, risk premium across the market decreases, creating natural structural flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:58:34">[15:58:34]</a>. These flows are larger than normal due to less liquidity to absorb them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:10:04">[16:10:04]</a>.
*   **Year-end positioning**: Short interest and chasing into the year-end contribute to a structurally positive environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:16:04">[16:16:04]</a>. This year, it's more positive than usual due to higher volatility and potential energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:31:02">[16:31:02]</a>.
*   **January Effect Reversal**: Typically, markets appreciate, leading to money flowing back into the market in January <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:50:09">[16:50:09]</a>. This year, the market is down, and factors like private equity, venture capital, and real estate lagging will lead to a "drop in collateral" and deleveraging early next year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:54:33">[17:54:33]</a>. This means the second part of the positive flow is "flowing in reverse" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:55:04">[17:55:04]</a>.
*   **Tax Loss Selling**: This also adds to negative flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:29:16">[19:29:16]</a>.

This combination of factors creates a volatile, illiquid market, where a 4% rally in the S&P can be followed by a 4% decline in hours, demonstrating the "leverage in the market" and how little flow it takes to move it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:30:23">[20:30:23]</a>. The majority of market value is passive and not actively moving the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:07:07">[21:07:07]</a>.

### Zero DTE Options and Speculation
Almost half of options volume (44% in S&P options this quarter) is in zero-days-to-expiration (0DTE) options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="48:00:23">[48:00:23]</a>. This trend is driven by:
*   **Underperformance of Implied Volatility**: Implied volatility hasn't performed well, so traders are more keen to bet on realized volatility with short-dated options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="48:32:00">[48:32:00]</a>.
*   **Speculative Activity**: This activity forces dealers into short gamma positions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:02:18">[49:02:18]</a>.
*   **Margin Constraints**: 0DTE options typically don't show up on daily margin calls from clearing houses, allowing traders to sell tails massively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:09:12">[51:09:12]</a>. This is a "very dangerous game" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:26:50">[51:26:50]</a>.

This concentration of trading in 0DTE options is "changing the distribution of outcomes" and creating a more "schizophrenic Market" with less liquidity and greater gamma effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:37:37">[52:37:37]</a>.

## Recession Debate and Market Outcomes

The common debate about whether a recession will occur and how it will impact the market is often oversimplified <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:06:05">[25:06:05]</a>. The economy is not the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:35:05">[25:35:05]</a>. For instance, from 1968-1982, the market went nowhere for 14 years, despite the economy growing above trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:38:00">[25:38:00]</a>. During that period, there were four recessions, yet overall growth was strong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:10:48">[26:10:48]</a>.

In the current environment, the FED has less control and the "fed put is softer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:35:09">[26:35:09]</a>. A **stronger economy or a shallower recession** might actually be the "worst thing for the market" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:00:36">[29:00:36]</a>. This is because it might not be enough for the FED to pause or pivot meaningfully, allowing secular inflation to build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:10:29">[29:10:29]</a>. The more pull-down and problems the economy faces, the more likely there will be a populist fiscal response, exacerbating market issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:52:16">[29:52:16]</a>.

> "The worse the recession, ironically, that might be the better thing for the market here." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:06:40">[30:06:40]</a>

The key driver is the liquidity provided by the FED, which is now being withdrawn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:42:04">[29:42:04]</a>.

## [[Investment Strategies During Inflationary Periods | Investment Strategies]]

The focus should be on building portfolios that are resilient to inflation and economic data points, leveraging non-correlated return streams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:16:16">[31:16:16]</a>.

### The "Why Hey" Effect (Reverse Tina Effect)
With interest rates no longer at zero, there is an alternative to equities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:07:05">[27:07:05]</a>. Money that rushed from bonds into equities is now aggressively flowing back into bonds, where investors can earn 5% interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:18:29">[27:18:29]</a>. This "Reverse Tina Effect" (There Is No Alternative) is a significant flow that is under-discussed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:32:00">[27:32:00]</a>. This also means less money to absorb liquidity and an increased risk premium in the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:00:03">[28:00:03]</a>.

### Volatility and Asset Class Opportunities
*   **Treasury Volatility**: Treasury volatility has become "fairly priced" given the inflationary environment and the reduced FED put <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:56:06">[39:56:06]</a>.
*   **FX Volatility**: FX volatility is also tied to the FED's strength and control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:34:04">[40:34:04]</a>.
*   **Commodity Volatility (Oil Put)**: In times of resource scarcity and inflation, commodity volatility historically shows less two-sided movement, with an uptrend and "ball compression to the downside" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:00:41">[41:00:41]</a>. OPEC+ or controlling entities can underpin prices, creating a "new put" on oil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:12:00">[41:12:00]</a>. The US government's explicit plan to buy oil at $70-$72 a barrel acts as a visible "oil put" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:42:26">[41:42:26]</a>. This suggests that long-term [[Investment strategies during periods of upheaval | volatility compression]] in the commodity space is a strong bet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:28:16">[42:28:16]</a>.
*   **Gold Volatility**: Gold, often seen as an [[Inflation and Economic Policies | inflation hedge]], has behaved differently this year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:15:37">[45:15:37]</a>. This is partly due to positioning; it was a "crowded trade" where everyone was waiting for it to work, leading to short-term underperformance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:17:29">[46:17:29]</a>. Gold also doesn't pay a yield, making it less attractive when interest rates rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:05:07">[47:05:07]</a>.
    *   **Strategic Opportunity**: Longer-term, there's an opportunity for an [[Investment strategies during periods of upheaval | inflationary hedge]] by buying cheap calls in gold, while offsetting volatility exposure by selling puts in oil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:46:12">[43:46:12]</a>.
*   **Equity Volatility**: Equity volatility and gold volatility are currently "cheap" compared to everything else <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:39:59">[44:39:59]</a>. A "second move phenomena" is expected to increase equity volatility and correlations to one, likely in Q1 or Q2 of next year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:53:11">[44:53:11]</a>.

### Shorting the Market
A "counter-trend strong rally" is anticipated in January-February, potentially around the February Opex period (Feb 14th) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:30:26">[35:30:26]</a>. This could be the "last rug pull" before a continuation of the market's return to liquidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:46:08">[35:46:08]</a>. This aligns with macro trends, such as the FED potentially pausing after another 50 basis point hike <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:14:04">[35:14:04]</a>.

## Geopolitical Factors and De-globalization

A significant surprise of 2022 was the acceleration of geopolitical issues, particularly Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and the bifurcation between China and the US <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:20:00">[54:20:00]</a>. These changes are structurally important for the next 5-10 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:46:08">[54:46:08]</a>.

The past 24 years of globalization, driven by integrating China and India into the global economy, acted as a significant deflationary force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:04:36">[55:04:36]</a>. This allowed the FED to print money and push liquidity into the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:23:44">[55:23:44]</a>. However, these structurally deflationary forces are slowing and coming to a more abrupt halt than anticipated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:33:04">[55:33:04]</a>.

The West's economies are not built to operate in an adversarial relationship with China <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:08:35">[59:08:35]</a>. While production can shift to other countries like Mexico or Vietnam, the overall capacity is dramatically reducing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:25:21">[59:25:21]</a>. This requires the West to onboard a tremendous amount of production domestically, which will lead to "structurally really inflationary effects" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:49:00">[59:49:00]</a>.

> "De-globalization is the biggest kind of thing that we need to be thinking about." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:05">[01:00:05]</a>

The continued relationship between China and the US, coupled with resource scarcity (labor, commodities, currency funding), is accelerating these changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:15">[00:56:15]</a>. If the "Taiwan thing does come to pass," the situation will likely worsen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:56:03">[56:56:03]</a>. These "second-order effects" of de-globalization will have even greater impacts on economies and markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.
