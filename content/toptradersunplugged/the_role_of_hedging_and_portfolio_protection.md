## Evolution of Portfolio Construction

Historically, valuing assets involved either predicting buyer/seller demand or analyzing cash flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:07">[00:10:07]</a>. However, a significant shift in financial thought occurred around the year 2000 with the emergence of [[risk_parity_and_portfolio_construction | risk parity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:15">[00:12:15]</a>. This concept, deemed as important for finance as the theory of relativity was for physics, posits that bonds are not a displacement for stocks but rather an enhancement to a stock portfolio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>.

Instead of holding only stocks, investors can use stocks as collateral to own bond futures, leading to a portfolio that is both safer and more profitable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>. This approach dramatically improves portfolios by leveraging the negative correlation between bonds and stocks, especially on a large scale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:49">[00:13:49]</a>. Thus, bonds are viewed as protecting other investments while still generating returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:05">[00:14:05]</a>.

## Treasuries as a Core Protective Asset

Treasuries are often considered a key component of [[hedging_strategies_and_portfolio_diversification | hedging strategies]].
The notion that treasuries are perpetually "overvalued" has been a consistent, yet often incorrect, sentiment over decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>. The concept of "term premium" in fixed income markets is dismissed as "smoke and mirrors" that adds no value to the discussion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:57">[00:08:57]</a>.

For portfolio protection, treasuries are viewed as:
*   **Cash Equivalents:** They are liquid investments usable as collateral <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:29">[00:14:29]</a>.
*   **Risk-Free Assets (Default):** While they have duration risk, their default risk is negligible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:54">[00:14:54]</a>.
*   **Convexity:** Due to convexity, low yields can actually benefit a bond's price more significantly if yields drop further <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:53">[00:15:53]</a>. For example, a 30-year bond rallying from 3% to 0% yield would see its price increase from 100 to 119 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:46">[00:15:46]</a>. At around 3% yield, the risk-reward for a 30-year bond is symmetric even accounting for a complete default scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:12">[00:16:12]</a>.
*   **Collateral in Lending:** The post-2008 emphasis on collateralized lending has increased the importance of bonds, particularly treasuries, as funding for repo and other funding markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:41">[00:16:41]</a>. The global growth in debt markets and transactions fuels an increasing demand for safe collateral <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:16">[00:17:16]</a>.

## Strategic Approaches to Hedging

[[hedging_with_options_and_derivatives | Hedging with options and derivatives]] can be complex. While options offer convexity, they often involve higher bid-offer spreads and multiple ways to be wrong (e.g., incorrect timing, speed of movement) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:06">[00:20:06]</a>. A preferred strategy is to find convex trades that also offer positive carry, meaning one gets paid to hold them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:26">[00:21:26]</a>. Treasury bonds are a prime example of this type of trade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:30">[00:21:30]</a>.

Opportunities for "convex trades" can also arise from:
*   **Esoteric Products:** Using specialized products, such as municipal index swaps or basis index swaps, where liquidity is lower, can present value opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. These products, despite being esoteric, are affected by macro factors and can exhibit hidden convexity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.
*   **Currency Differentials:** Being long a currency with positive carry (e.g., USD) can create convexity by looking at its historical range and identifying situations with more upside than downside <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:24">[00:22:24]</a>.
*   **Specific Examples from March 2020:**
    *   **Eurodollar Options:** The distribution of outcomes for Fed policy was seen as non-normal, with two peaks: one near zero yield and another assuming no major event, creating opportunities for "dirt cheap" options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:40">[00:18:40]</a>.
    *   **Dividend Futures:** These were seen as a way to "exclude speculation" beyond a return to normal, as the world in which companies stop paying dividends entirely would be extremely unusual <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:00">[00:29:00]</a>. Their prices in Europe were crushed, trading 40-50% down from tops <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:43">[00:29:43]</a>.
    *   **Oil:** The strategy was based on oil prices eventually returning to normal, rather than speculating on immediate delivery contracts which could go negative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:49">[00:30:49]</a>.
    *   **TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities):** In March 2020, TIPS were considered "totally insane prices" and one of the "most mispriced greatest assets" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:14">[00:31:14]</a>. Structuring asset swaps showed that the implied inflation and carry made no economic sense, suggesting a strong likelihood of future inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:30">[00:31:30]</a>.

## The Fed's Role in Market Risk Management

The Federal Reserve's actions, particularly in injecting liquidity, are pivotal in market stability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:23">[00:28:23]</a>. The Fed is seen as consistently surprising markets by injecting more liquidity than anticipated, rather than disappointing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:27">[00:28:27]</a>. While the Fed tends to be a leader in its actions, its influence makes it impossible for other central banks to be immune to its decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:28">[00:43:28]</a>. The typical duration and size of Fed hiking cycles are also observed to be shrinking over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:12">[00:46:12]</a>.

## The Continuous Challenge of Risk Management

[[risk_management_in_trading | Risk management in trading]] is an ongoing battle, not a series of isolated sessions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:27">[00:27:27]</a>. Unlike a poker game where one can simply leave the table <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:07">[00:27:07]</a>, a portfolio is always active <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:28">[00:27:28]</a>.

### Portfolio Risk vs. Position Risk
A key aspect of [[risk_management_in_trading_strategies | risk management in trading strategies]] is prioritizing portfolio risk management over individual position risk management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:38">[00:55:38]</a>. Over-focusing on a single trade can lead to losing the overall balance of the portfolio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:26">[00:56:26]</a>. It's crucial to maintain a balanced portfolio and avoid cutting trades that are actually providing a "lifeline" during adverse conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:48">[00:56:48]</a>.

### Psychological Pressures
Trading involves immense psychological pressure, as a money manager's personal identity is often tied to their performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:51">[00:36:51]</a>. Underperforming can be a significant blow to ego and sense of self <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:00">[00:37:00]</a>. This emphasizes the need for psychological resilience and the ability to move on from mistakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:04">[00:26:04]</a>.
