This has led to questions about whether the transmission mechanism of [[impact_of_monetary_policy_on_markets | monetary policy]] has changed or if there are simply long and variable lags at play <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>.

## Current Economic Outlook and Recession Debate

There is an ongoing debate among economists regarding the likelihood of a [[potential_us_recession_predictions | US recession]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>. This discussion often boils down to a "flow versus stock" argument <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>.

*   **Flow Perspective**: Looking at the rate of change, US M2 (broad money supply) growth has been negative year-on-year, something not seen before, suggesting an impending economic implosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>.
*   **Stock Perspective**: In absolute terms and against its long-term growth trend, M2 remains significantly above historical levels due to the massive [[impact_of_fiscal_dominance_and_central_bank_policies | fiscal stimulus]] and [[impact_of_monetary_policy_and_qe_on_markets | monetary stimulus]] injected between 2020 and mid-2022 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:16">[00:07:16]</a>. This suggests the economy is still digesting prior stimulus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>.

A key factor in the US economy's resilience is that during the period of extremely low interest rates, American consumers and corporations made smart financial decisions by locking in long-duration debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>. Consumers secured mortgages for 20 years at low rates, and corporations extended the duration of their debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. This means their balance sheets are generally not in bad shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>.

In contrast, the **US government's balance sheet is in poor shape** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. The government significantly shortened the duration of its debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>. Historically, recessions serve to clear excesses from the previous cycle by impacting those who overextended themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>. Logically, the government should be tightening its belt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. However, the government is moving in the opposite direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>.

The US is currently running a budget deficit of around six percent of GDP while experiencing full employment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:25">[00:09:25]</a>. This situation was considered inconceivable just a few years ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>. Furthermore, the government plans to add another $4 trillion to the national debt over the next two years, moving it from $31 trillion to $35 trillion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>. This includes significant spending through the Inflation Reduction Act and infrastructure projects, all while employment is full <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:55">[00:09:55]</a>.

Given this massive expansion in government debt, a significant contraction in consumer and corporate spending would be required to offset it and trigger a recession <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>. The continued presence of excess savings in the system further supports consumer spending, potentially through the end of the year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>.

### Defining Recession

A recession, in terms of its impact on markets and people, is typically defined as a period where unemployment rises meaningfully, leading to a negative spiral of reduced consumption and further job losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:49">[00:11:49]</a>. While the US had two negative quarters of GDP growth in 2022, there was no significant increase in unemployment, rendering it a "so what" event for the markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>. Currently, unemployment remains strong, wages are rising (especially at the low end), and consumption is robust, largely due to ongoing government spending at both federal and state levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:11">[00:12:11]</a>.

## Inflation Outlook

Despite the narrative that inflation is steadily declining, some argue that the absence of [[impact_of_interest_rates_on_fiscal_policy_and_global_debt | fiscal consolidation]] means inflation remains a concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:45">[00:13:45]</a>. Running large budget deficits with full employment is not conducive to controlling inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>.

Inflation tends to be a volatile variable, often rising and falling before settling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:13">[00:14:13]</a>. While headline inflation has come down, core inflation remains high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>. Much of the recent inflation reduction stemmed from a fall in energy prices, which began to roll off in the third quarter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:57">[00:14:57]</a>.

Potential inflationary forces include:
*   **Strikes**: An anticipated "summer of strikes" in the US involving dockers on the west coast and Teamsters (truck drivers), exacerbated by low inventories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:22">[00:16:22]</a>. This could lead to supply chain dislocations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:48">[00:17:48]</a>.
*   **El Niño**: A strong El Niño event could lead to bad harvests, particularly in the dry Midwest and Asia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:56">[00:17:56]</a>.
*   **Europe's Energy Issues**: Europe's energy situation remains unresolved, with a mild winter having provided temporary relief <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:13">[00:18:13]</a>.
*   **De-sinification**: The shift of production out of China, driven by political imperatives rather than cost, is an inflationary force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:04">[00:20:04]</a>. China's workforce is no longer growing, ending its role as a major deflationary force on global manufacturing wages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:59">[00:20:59]</a>.

## Monetary and Fiscal Policy Dynamics

The current [[us_economic_policies_and_potential_recession_risks | US economic policies]] are characterized by a tight [[impact_of_easy_monetary_policy | monetary policy]] (high interest rates) and loose [[impact_of_fiscal_dominance_and_central_bank_policies | fiscal policy]] (large budget deficits) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:07">[00:47:07]</a>. This combination raises questions about its impact on the dollar, as a similar situation in the early 1980s led to a significant dollar rally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:20">[00:47:20]</a>.

The US government's plan to issue $4 trillion in new debt over the next two years, in addition to rolling over roughly half of its existing debt over the next three years, poses a critical question: Who will buy this debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:47">[00:48:47]</a>?

*   **Foreign Central Banks**: Foreign central banks may be less inclined to hold US dollars as reserves, given the increasing ability to conduct commodity trade in other currencies (Renminbi, Indian Rupees, Thai Baht) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:15">[00:49:15]</a>. This "de-dollarization of the commodity trade" is a major macro consequence, potentially leading to an "Emerging Market boom" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:43">[00:38:43]</a>.
*   **US Commercial Banks**: Currently, the inverted yield curve makes it unattractive for US commercial banks to buy long-term government debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:34">[00:49:34]</a>.

This situation could lead to two main scenarios:
1.  **Fed Monetization**: The [[Federal Reserve Policies and Economic Impact | Federal Reserve]] may eventually have to step in to buy a substantial portion of this debt. If this occurs, the US dollar would likely depreciate, while bonds would fall, and commodities and gold would rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:50">[00:49:50]</a>.
2.  **Bond Market Meltdown**: If the Fed refuses to monetize the debt, it could lead to a spike in the US dollar and a significant bond market meltdown, resulting in a much steeper yield curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:11">[00:50:11]</a>.

Unlike European countries that extended the duration of their debt when interest rates were low, the US government significantly shortened the duration of its debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:30">[00:51:30]</a>. Half of the US outstanding debt is below two years, with very little issued in the 10-15 year range <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:02">[00:52:02]</a>. This unusual term structure contributes to the inverted yield curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:17">[00:52:17]</a>. As the US government issues more debt, it might shift towards issuing longer-term bonds if it determines that 10-year rates at 3.8% are more favorable than 1-year rates at 5% given the Fed's stance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:46">[00:52:46]</a>. This could help normalize the yield curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:02">[00:53:02]</a>.

While the Fed aims to fight inflation, it also has a mandate to maintain financial stability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:20">[00:53:20]</a>. A positively sloped yield curve is generally seen as healthy for the financial system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:19">[00:53:19]</a>. If inflation cools, the Fed might welcome a steepening of the yield curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:43">[00:53:43]</a>. However, decisions about debt issuance rest with the US Treasury, not the Fed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:54">[00:53:54]</a>.

In conclusion, the current [[impact_of_fiscal_dominance_and_central_bank_policies | US economic policies]], characterized by ongoing fiscal expansion amidst high interest rates, challenge traditional recession predictions. The resilience of consumer and corporate balance sheets, coupled with the government's continued spending and debt issuance, suggest a different economic trajectory than a conventional downturn. The ultimate impact on the dollar and bond markets will depend on who absorbs this massive debt and the [[Federal Reserve Policies and Economic Impact | Federal Reserve's]] response.
