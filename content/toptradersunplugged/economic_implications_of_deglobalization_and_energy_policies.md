## Impact of Deglobalization on the Dollar Standard

The foundations that established the dollar standard are currently being reversed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a>. This reversal suggests a move towards a multi-polar world on the international trading circuit, rather than a decline of the dollar within America itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. This does not imply that currencies like the yuan, ruble, or bitcoin will surpass the dollar, but rather that the neutral clearing point it provided will be lost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>.

The era of intensive [[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | globalization]] has produced significant wealth over the last 30 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:38">[01:14:38]</a>. However, transitioning to a [[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | multipolar world]] may lead to economic regression, impacting the stability of the financial system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:41">[01:14:41]</a>.

### Erosion of Dollar Neutrality
Historically, the dollar served as a neutral medium of exchange in the offshore eurodollar market, accessible to anyone regardless of their political alignment with America <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:08">[00:40:08]</a>. The US even welcomed adversaries using the dollar, viewing it as a mechanism to open closed systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:43">[00:40:43]</a>.

However, current US policy aims to restrict dollar access even to enemies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:18">[00:41:18]</a>. This stance is seen as counter-intuitive, as a go-to reserve standard requires neutrality, even turning a blind eye to "bad practice" to remain the currency of commerce <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>. The Afghan asset confiscation is cited as a significant precedent where a central bank could no longer trust its foreign exchange deposits would be available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:27">[00:46:27]</a>. This shift undermines the dollar's "exorbitant privilege" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:47">[00:43:47]</a>.

The US's energy independence, partly due to the shale revolution, has allowed it to become more isolationist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:42">[00:56:42]</a>. While this strengthens America, an isolationist stance ultimately undermines the dollar as a global standard because it reduces the continuous flow of dollars abroad and compromises its perceived neutrality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:29">[00:59:29]</a>. The dollar standard was built on an investment in global interdependence, such as the Lend-Lease Act and post-WWII investment in Europe, which were not isolationist perspectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:05">[01:01:05]</a>.

A multi-polar world will likely lead to continuous bouts of shortages and market imbalances, feeding inflation because a common valuation mechanism is lost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:27">[01:02:27]</a>. Traders will demand premiums for the increased risk of lacking a neutral clearing point <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>.

## [[global_energy_crisis_and_supply_chain_issues | Global Energy Crisis]] and Policy Failures

Current energy policies, particularly the "net zero" framing, are criticized as naive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:00">[01:14:00]</a>. The architects of these policies appear to lack understanding of basic supply and demand principles and economic stability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:10">[01:14:10]</a>. The transition to renewables should have been funded by an interim period of natural gas consumption, as only fossil fuels can sustain base load requirements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:28">[01:14:28]</a>. The technology for renewables to meet these needs is not yet sufficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:48">[01:14:48]</a>.

Underinvestment in natural gas development and critical storage facilities has created vulnerabilities, mirroring past financial crises where dependence on wholesale markets proved unsustainable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:08">[01:16:08]</a>. This policy has given countries that prioritize power over climate change, like Russia, significant international leverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:22">[01:17:22]</a>. Russia might have even funded environmental movements to weaken Western energy resilience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:39">[01:17:39]</a>.

There is a growing concern about the upcoming winters due to energy shortages, which could lead to fatalities from lack of heating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:24">[01:18:24]</a>.

### Sanctions and Economic Warfare
The West's sanctions against Russia are viewed as a "self-made embargo" that will be difficult to enforce and will lead to severe consequences for Western populations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:33">[00:52:33]</a>. While Russia's GDP is smaller than G7 nations, its role as a top exporter of commodities and food products is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:49">[00:54:49]</a>. Commodities are the "oxygen" that allows the financial system ("lifeblood") to flow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:30">[00:55:30]</a>. Removing this "oxygen" can lead to the collapse of the entire system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:38">[00:55:38]</a>. The expectation is that after initial "virtue signaling," "sanction fatigue" will set in, leading to a turning a blind eye to violations as the quality of life declines and electorates become discontent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a>.

The US's energy independence gives it a practical advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:12">[00:57:12]</a>. However, even with military strength, a country needs raw materials and rare resources to power its military-industrial complex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:57">[01:05:57]</a>. The West faces a shortage of noble gases like helium and neon, essential for semiconductors and ballistic missiles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:43">[01:06:43]</a>.

### The Role of Nuclear Power
Nuclear power is highlighted as a potential solution to energy shortfalls. While it takes about 10 years to build conventional plants, innovations in micro-nuclear generation and battery-like models offer promising technologies with reduced meltdown risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:19">[01:19:19]</a>. These technologies, sidelined for ideological reasons connected to anti-war and anti-nuclear proliferation movements, merit further investigation, especially for countries lacking consistent sun or wind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:57">[01:21:57]</a>. Overcoming "not in my backyard" mentalities and streamlining bureaucracy are necessary for these plants to be built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:33">[01:23:33]</a>.

## Broader Cultural and Political Shifts

The current global shifts are not just economic but cultural, with profound economic implications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:50">[00:31:50]</a>. The "culture wars" are increasingly polarizing the field of economics, making previously legitimate schools of thought, like neoliberalism or laissez-faire, taboo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:37">[00:32:37]</a>. This risks creating "one-sided economics," akin to the Soviet Union's Marxist-based economic education, which was deemed a "joke degree" in free market economies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:38">[00:33:38]</a>. The exclusion of diverse economic theories from debate can have negative real-world impacts, leading to economic misallocation and self-implosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:04">[00:36:04]</a>.

The Federal Reserve's actions have "made it easy for government," relieving pressure on them to address underlying problems, which has contributed to rising inequality over the last 40 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:2:8:00">[00:2:8:00]</a>. The current political pendulum is swinging "erratically" and "extremely" between market-driven and government-dominant approaches, leading to greater instability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:50">[00:38:50]</a>.

### Media and Groupthink
The COVID-19 pandemic revealed how readily society can adopt collective fear and policy compliance, with questioning becoming taboo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:51">[01:17:51]</a>. This "groupthink" phenomenon, also seen in financial markets, is dangerous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:07">[02:10:07]</a>. The internet and social media have broken down traditional media segmentation, forcing even financial publications to cater to the "lowest common denominator," leading to a "dumbing down" of news content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:42">[00:28:42]</a>. This has resulted in a censorious environment, prompting a return to "regating" information through independent media platforms to allow for "truth speaking" within informed communities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:02">[02:09:02]</a>.

## Adapting to a Changing World

In a [[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | deglobalizing world]], the impact on passive investing and index funds depends on factors like [[impact_of_esg_policies_on_energy_investment | ESG]] policies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:18">[01:08:18]</a>. While global growth-focused ETFs may suffer, domestic tech companies could continue to do well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:30">[01:09:30]</a>. Stock picking may make a comeback, focusing on companies that benefit from "reshoring" or "friend-shoring" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:52">[01:09:52]</a>. The overall effect on index funds will primarily be through the price level, as equity markets tend to be favorable environments during periods of uncontrolled inflation, making them a hedge against inflation risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:06">[01:10:06]</a>.

Ultimately, navigating current and future crises requires a shift from individual "prepping" to building robust social networks. While personal preparedness can offer short-term resilience, genuine survival and long-term adaptation depend on community-led, trust-based interactions and collective problem-solving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:07">[01:26:07]</a>.
