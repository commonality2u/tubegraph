This overarching theme profoundly affects supply, investment, and pricing across the commodity complex.

## Supply Response and Investment Distortions

[[structural_changes_in_commodity_markets | Decarbonization]] makes it challenging for the commodity sector to generate the typical supply response that would occur under high pricing conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:29">[00:05:29]</a>. Publicly traded companies, often undervalued, are economically incentivized to perform share buybacks with their cash flow rather than investing in new production, such as drilling for oil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>.

This phenomenon is not limited to the oil sector but is observed across all commodities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>. The "old economy," responsible for 80% of global emissions, is perceived as "dirty," while the "new economy" is considered clean <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:14">[00:06:14]</a>. This perception causes investors, seeking to avoid the [[impact_of_esg_and_geopolitical_factors_on_natural_resource_markets | decarbonization]] debate, to ignore the old economy, leading to severe constraints on capital flowing into the commodity space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>.

Despite a 60% increase in oil stock prices in the past year, this represents merely a "dead cat bounce" that does not correct the long-term decoupling between oil equities and oil prices observed since 2017 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. This disconnect is evident across oil, metals, miners, and agriculture producers, where capital attraction lags behind underlying commodity prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>.

The distinguishing factor in the current inflationary period, compared to 2008 or the 1970s, is the constrained ability of these sectors to respond to higher prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>.

### Distortions vs. Carbon Tax

The current investment distortions result in higher prices than a direct carbon tax would create, and critically, the tax revenues are not collected by governments in major energy-consuming regions like the U.S. or Europe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>. This issue needs policy-makers to address it to ensure a rational approach to [[impact_of_esg_and_geopolitical_factors_on_natural_resource_markets | decarbonization]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>.

## Impact on Specific Commodities

### Oil and Energy

The lack of long-term [[investment_strategies_in_commodities | investments]] in projects like deep water offshore platforms off the coast of Nigeria has halted that type of production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:02">[00:32:02]</a>. Going forward, the primary engines of growth in oil production are fast-cycle sources: U.S. Shale, the Middle East, and Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:17">[00:32:17]</a>. With Russia's output constrained due to [[geopolitical_factors_influencing_commodity_markets | geopolitical factors]] and U.S. Shale facing capital attraction difficulties, the Middle East (specifically Saudi Arabia and UAE, which have maintained or increased reinvestment rates since 2019) remains the dominant engine of growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:50">[00:31:50]</a>. This dynamic gives core OPEC (Gulf countries) unprecedented market power due to the inelastic supply curves of other producers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:49">[00:32:49]</a>.

### Metals

Aluminum is projected to be one of the most bullish commodities over the next 5-10 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:35">[00:40:35]</a>. This is due to what's termed the "climate paradox": aluminum production generates significant emissions but is crucial for climate change solutions as a key "green metal" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:48">[00:40:48]</a>. Supply curtailments, driven by energy crises in Europe and China's domestic energy policy (e.g., export tariffs on aluminum to keep energy domestic), suppress its production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:05">[00:41:05]</a>. Russia, a low-cost producer with clean hydropower, could provide "green metal" aluminum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:08">[00:42:08]</a>.

Titanium and other smaller rare earth type metals are also expected to face significant issues due to Russia's large market share <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:30">[00:43:30]</a>.

### Agriculture

Agriculture markets are characterized as tight due to "the three C's":
*   **Climate:** Yields in traditional "Bread Baskets" like the U.S. and Brazil are constrained by weather shocks, necessitating a shift to more Northern climates or areas with better yields, which takes time and creates supply restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:39">[00:51:39]</a>.
*   **Conflict:** Geopolitical conflicts, as seen with Ukraine, continue to pose serious issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:20">[00:52:20]</a>.

## Rationality of [[impact_of_esg_and_geopolitical_factors_on_natural_resource_markets | Decarbonization]] Approaches

The debate around [[impact_of_esg_and_geopolitical_factors_on_natural_resource_markets | decarbonization]] highlights a concern about addressing climate change in a rational manner that yields results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:06">[00:56:06]</a>. Despite trillions invested in renewables over the last decade (e.g., $3.8 trillion), hydrocarbon consumption only shifted from 82% to 81% of primary energy sources before the Russian conflict <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:32">[00:56:32]</a>.

There is a suggestion that renewables may not be the most effective solution for rapid [[impact_of_esg_and_geopolitical_factors_on_natural_resource_markets | decarbonization]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:55">[00:56:55]</a>. Nuclear power is presented as a highly effective, zero-carbon option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:36">[00:57:36]</a>. Conversely, renewables, with their lower energy density, require vast land areas (e.g., large wind and solar farms) and generate significant environmental "destruction," despite having no carbon emissions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:02">[00:58:02]</a>. This contrasts with more energy-dense sources like nuclear, where waste is minimal, or even coal, which has higher associated mortality rates but is being burned more (525,000 tons more this year) leading to increased emissions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:44">[00:58:44]</a>.

A more direct and efficient approach to reducing carbon emissions could involve replacing coal with cleaner-burning natural gas and expanding nuclear power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:15">[00:59:15]</a>. The fundamental economic principle is that if consumption is not stopping and investment is constrained, the gap between supply and demand will grow, leading to significant [[volatility_in_commodity_markets | volatility]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:45">[01:01:45]</a>.
