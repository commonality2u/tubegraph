## Cyclical vs. Secular Inflation

A key distinction in macroeconomics is between cycles and trends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>.
*   **Long-term Trend:** The probability of inflation being structurally higher over the next 20 years is not negligible, potentially averaging 3-4% instead of the 1.5% seen in the last two decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:09">[00:15:09]</a>. This trend is influenced by factors such as:
    *   Global supply chain vulnerabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:27">[00:15:27]</a>.
    *   Shrinking availability of cheap labor globally, with the Chinese workforce projected to shrink by 30% over the next 30 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:16">[00:16:16]</a>.
    *   Onshoring efforts, leading to higher labor costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:53">[00:15:53]</a>.
*   **Cyclical Fluctuations:** While the long-term trend may be higher, inflation could still temporarily drop to zero in a given cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:48">[00:16:48]</a>. Historically, recessions have reduced inflation by an average of 6.8 percentage points from their peak within 16 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:28">[00:17:28]</a>. There has been no disinflationary recession in the last 100 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:42">[00:17:42]</a>.

## Historical Parallels (1970s vs. Today)

Comparing the current economic climate to past inflationary periods, specifically 1968-1982, reveals both similarities and differences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>.

### The 1970s Economy:
*   Highly industrialized and labor-intensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>.
*   Wage growth was a significant driver of nominal spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.
*   Labor held more importance than capital for economic growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>.
*   Strong union power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>.
*   Post-WWII demographic boom led to solid labor force growth and productivity, allowing for structural growth of 3.5% per annum without significant leverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:04">[00:07:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</a>.
*   Inflationary episodes were rooted in these structural factors, leading to wage-price spirals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>.

### Today's Economy:
*   More capital-intensive, with capital outweighing labor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:48">[00:08:48]</a>.
*   Technology plays a larger role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>.
*   A U.S. company needs about one-tenth of the employees today to generate the same sales as in the 1970s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>.
*   Labor's importance is significantly rising over the last 5-10 years, particularly in service-driven economies (75% of the economy in the US and Europe) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>.
*   **Deglobalization:** A key similarity to 1965, driven by factors like fiscal spending, resource scarcity, and protectionism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>. This constrains labor supply, increasing inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>.
*   **Demographics:** The millennial generation is the largest demographic bubble since the Baby Boomers and is currently entering key household formation years, driving demand for goods and services globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a>. Political pressure to support these households can lead to continued fiscal stimulus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:56">[00:12:56]</a>.

## Monetary and Fiscal Policy Impacts

The current environment is characterized by tight [[Impact of monetary policy on markets | monetary policy]] and [[Impact of government policy on markets | fiscal policy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>.

*   **Fed's Stance:** The Federal Reserve aims to keep policy tighter for longer, with fed funds above equilibrium, until core inflation trends down to 2% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:58">[00:17:58]</a>. Some anticipate the Fed will be forced to cut interest rates to 0-1% in 2024 due to this tightening, expecting an accident to occur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>.
*   **Fiscal Policy:** There's an expectation of a much tighter fiscal policy ahead of elections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:28">[00:03:28]</a>. However, if an economic pullback occurs in a 2024 election year, more fiscal stimulus is highly likely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:18">[00:13:18]</a>.

### Banking System Stability
Recent banking issues (e.g., Silicon Valley Bank, First Republic Bank) are viewed as idiosyncratic liquidity and risk management problems, not systemic credit crises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:50">[00:29:50]</a>. Central banks have tools to backstop the value of collateral (like treasuries and mortgage-backed securities) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:55">[00:29:55]</a>. Regulations post-2008, such as Basel III and the Supervisory Outlier Test in Europe, have made banks more resilient to interest rate risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:12">[00:30:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:44">[00:31:44]</a>.

However, a different risk for European banks is their exposure to commercial real estate, which is experiencing severe drawdowns due to structural problems (e.g., office vacancies). This could become a credit problem that central banks cannot easily backstop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:33">[00:34:33]</a>.

## Market Dynamics and Volatility

### Bond Market Behavior
The inverted yield curve in overnight index swaps since March 2022 indicates that long-term bond investors anticipate weaker growth and inflation due to the short-term tightening cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:54">[00:21:54]</a>. The term premium in the bond market is virtually non-existent, meaning investors are not compensated for taking duration risk compared to rolling T-bills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:09">[00:23:09]</a>.

### Bond Volatility
Bond market volatility, especially at the front end, has been extremely high. This significantly impacts overall risk-taking as fixed income is a systemically important part of institutional portfolios, reducing its diversification properties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:29">[00:25:29]</a>. Interestingly, long-dated bond volatility did not explode during the banking crisis, suggesting investors do not price in structural inflation at the long end <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:11">[00:26:11]</a>.

### Equity Market
Downside protection in the S&P 500 (e.g., 25 Delta put with a three-month expiry) is currently relatively cheap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:20">[00:39:20]</a>. However, the absence of "jump risk" (sudden, large moves) has made buying put options an expensive way to express a bearish view in recent years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:37">[00:39:37]</a>. There has been a complete abandonment of "vol hedges" because they haven't worked as expected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:02">[00:44:02]</a>.

## The US Dollar and De-Dollarization

The future trajectory of the U.S. dollar is complex and depends on the nature of any recession <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:29">[01:01:29]</a>:
*   If a recession occurs without a major credit deleveraging event, the dollar may not appreciate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:57">[01:00:57]</a>.
*   However, any deleveraging event in the dollar-denominated global debt system will mechanically lead to dollar appreciation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:47">[01:00:47]</a>.

The idea of de-dollarization is gaining traction, with figures like Stanley Druckenmiller reportedly shorting the dollar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:04">[00:57:04]</a>. The dollar's role as the global reserve currency is due to its unparalleled liquidity, depth, and the rule of law in its underlying market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:08">[01:04:08]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:42">[01:05:42]</a>. Alternatives like the Euro, Japanese Yen, or Chinese Yuan cannot provide the same deep, liquid, and repo-backed markets necessary to recycle vast amounts of global savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:15">[01:06:15]</a>. Transitioning away from a system with $12 trillion of dollar-denominated liabilities outside the U.S. would be extremely chaotic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:15">[01:07:15]</a>.

However, [[Impact of inflationary shocks on markets | volatility]] in the dollar and FX tends to trend significantly higher during periods of inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:52">[01:01:52]</a>.

### Japan and the Yen
Japan, as the largest exporter of capital, holds significant global influence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:14">[00:47:14]</a>. Japanese investors tend to be risk-averse and repatriate capital during periods of uncertainty, which historically benefits the Yen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:54">[00:47:54]</a>. Coupled with recent wage growth (4%) and services inflation (4%)—the first in 30 years—and a new Bank of Japan governor, potential changes in monetary policy (e.g., yield curve control) could lead to higher domestic interest rates, making repatriation more attractive and further strengthening the Yen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:00">[00:49:00]</a>.

### Carry Trades
The current environment has seen renewed interest in carry trades, particularly in Emerging Market currencies like the Brazilian Real, Mexican Peso, Polish Zloty, and Hungarian Forint <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:23">[00:52:23]</a>. These offer significant double-digit yearly carry with relatively low realized volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:49">[00:52:49]</a>. However, this trend may be getting extreme, as investors become complacent about the risks associated with these currencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>.

## Commodities and Geopolitics

### Role of Commodities
In an environment where [[Impact of central bank policies on markets | central banks]] face conflicting mandates (price stability vs. full employment) and reduced control due to rising inflation, other entities (like OPEC) begin to assert their influence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:45">[01:08:45]</a>. Commodities, especially industrial ones, have a crucial role in macro portfolios during periods of high and rising inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:39">[01:11:39]</a>. This is because they can underpin prices and potentially reduce long-term volatility in industrial commodities as powerful entities "put" on these markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:47">[01:10:47]</a>.

### Geopolitical Landscape
The world is becoming increasingly bipolar, with Western powers aligning against a growing challenge from countries like China and Brazil, who are questioning the dollar's dominance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:10">[01:14:10]</a>. This shift is tied to global populism, which is inherently local and driven by fiscal policies that prioritize domestic concerns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:14">[01:16:14]</a>. The move from monetary to fiscal policy, and from capitalism to populism, suggests a period of increased global conflict in all its forms (economic, resource, and political), creating asymmetric investment opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:16">[01:17:16]</a>.
