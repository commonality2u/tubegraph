## The Dollar as the Denominator of Global Assets
The [[US Dollar and Global Currency Movements | US dollar]] is the denominator for most global assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:12">[00:08:12]</a>. If the value of the denominating currency is destroyed, a tangible asset like copper or oil should rise in that currency's terms, assuming demand doesn't shift globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>. Even for portfolio managers in countries like Germany with a dollar benchmark, the dollar's effect can be enormous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.

The power of the reserve central bank (the Fed) is disproportionate compared to other [[Central bank policies and their influence on global markets | central banks]] like the ECB, BOJ, or Bank of England <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. The Fed, as the reserve currency issuer, can literally create reflation and ultimately inflation by debasing or lowering the dollar's value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>. This dictates which asset classes to consider and how to allocate portfolios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:55">[00:09:55]</a>.

## The Dollar Cycle
Dollar cycles have shown remarkable consistency in both length and amplitude over the last 50 years of freely floating exchange rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:33">[00:10:33]</a>. Cyclically, the dollar should be in the process of peaking, as the current cycle from 1991 looks almost identical to the 1976-2002 cycle, which was the prior dollar high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>.

Key dollar cycles mentioned include:
*   1972-1979: Up into the Plaza Accord <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:14">[00:11:14]</a>.
*   Post-Plaza Accord: Down into Sakakibara's intervention, then up to the 2002 high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:20">[00:11:20]</a>.
*   Post-2002: Down into the Great Financial Crisis (GFC) and then up again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:26">[00:11:26]</a>.

### Factors Extending the Cycle
The recent dollar cycle has been extended by:
*   **Quantitative Tightening (QT):** Janet Yellen's claim that QT would be "like watching paint dry" was incorrect; the dollar strengthened significantly from 106 to 118 on the broad trade-weighted index when the Fed began shrinking its balance sheet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>.
*   **COVID-19 Funding Squeeze:** This further propelled the "napalm run," a risk-off dollar rally where risk assets and stocks fall, bonds rally, emerging markets struggle, and the dollar rises, exacerbating the risk-off event <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>. The Fed intervened aggressively and quickly, using swap lines and a new repo facility, effectively arresting this "napalm run" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:05">[00:13:05]</a>.

Typically, the end of a dollar cycle occurs with a risk-off dollar rally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:53">[00:17:53]</a>, as seen in crises like the Latin American debt crisis (1980s), the Asian crisis (1990s), or China's devaluation (2015-2016) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:04">[00:18:04]</a>.

## Drivers of Potential Dollar Weakness
Several factors suggest the dollar cycle may be at its top:
*   **Relative US Economic Weakness:** The US is starting from a deeper economic hole than many peers, evidenced by post-WWII (or even Great Depression) unemployment highs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:27">[00:19:27]</a>. The US's "super flexible labor market" is an "Achilles heel" at times like this, as unemployment issues could persist for at least another 18 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:35">[00:19:35]</a>.
*   **Weakening Domestic Oil Industry:** The growth of the US shale oil industry since 2011 correlated well with relative US industrial production outperformance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:40">[00:20:40]</a>. However, the shale sector is now shrinking, and a robust comeback is unlikely, which will weigh heavily on US relative industrial production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:08">[00:21:08]</a>.
*   **Budget and Current Account Deficits:** The US budget deficit is expected to grow, straining the treasury market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:35">[00:22:35]</a>. The US is not running a current account deficit large enough to support its reserve currency status, partly due to expanded domestic oil production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:53">[00:15:53]</a>. This has been filled by capital account outflows, which are "fickle" and can create systemic fragility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:26">[00:16:26]</a>. The Fed's expanded balance sheet and swap lines have mitigated this funding shortfall for now <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:17">[00:17:17]</a>.
*   **Narrowing Interest Rate Differentials:** While currencies can diverge from interest rate differentials, these divergences were often a function of [[Central bank policies and their influence on global markets | central bank intervention]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:52">[00:21:52]</a>. Current interest rate differentials and relative current account deficits suggest a euro-dollar exchange rate of 1.30 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:22">[00:22:22]</a>. If the yield curve is not allowed to price in necessary interest rates, the currency will take the strain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:36">[00:23:36]</a>.
*   **End of "US Exceptionalism":** The concept of US exceptionalism as a "self-reinforcing reflexive cycle" has historically encouraged foreign investment in US dollar assets, leading to stock buybacks, equity market appreciation, and further inflows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:25">[00:24:25]</a>. If the dollar starts to break, the US becomes less attractive, and this virtuous cycle can turn vicious or simply crumble <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>.

### Dollar's Relationship with Commodities
A classic example of the dollar's influence on assets is seen between 2002 and 2008 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:46">[00:27:46]</a>. During this period, the dollar dropped 30% in broad trade-weighted terms, while the CRB index (a commodity price index) rose threefold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:57">[00:27:57]</a>. This highlights how a weakening dollar can lead to rising commodity prices.

## The Fed's Role and Monetary Policy
Loretta Mester's comments suggest that the Fed's quantitative easing (QE) so far was merely to stabilize financial markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:53">[00:28:53]</a>. "Old-fashioned QE" with potentially another $80 billion a month or trillion a year, possibly masked as yield curve control, is expected when the economy recovers, especially given the US Treasury's debt issuance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:03">[00:29:03]</a>.

There is a noticeable, increasingly negative short-term correlation between the S&P 500 and the [[US Dollar and Global Currency Movements | US dollar]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:37">[00:34:37]</a>. This indicates that when the S&P 500 is up, the dollar is down, and vice versa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:46">[00:34:46]</a>. This correlation might be related to "US exceptionalism" trade flows, where foreign investors buy US assets (like S&P futures) as the dollar strengthens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:39">[00:35:39]</a>.

### Fed's Independence and Fiscal Dominance
The Fed has become "independent within government rather than independent of government," a concept first coined by William Martin in the mid-1960s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:01">[01:00:01]</a>. This means the Fed supports the government's will, even if it leads to inflationary pressures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:15">[01:00:15]</a>. This complicit behavior, contrasting with Paul Volcker-era tendencies, suggests that the Fed believes it can control inflation if it happens, a belief that proved "bloody wrong" in the 1960s when inflation expectations became unanchored <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:23">[01:01:23]</a>. This marks a shift from traditional monetary policy, with central banks now acting as a supporting act to fiscal authorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:31">[00:50:31]</a>.

## Investment Implications
Given the potential for dollar decline and the Fed's policies:
*   **Precious Metals:** Gold and especially silver are favored as protections against dollar collapse and inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:21">[00:40:21]</a>. Silver, historically a "high beta" play, tends to outperform in inflationary/reflationary environments and its ratio to gold mirrors 5-year break-evens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:00">[00:41:00]</a>. A strong dollar decline could make silver a "10-bagger" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:04">[00:42:04]</a>.
*   **Bonds:** The bond market is not considered a place for long-term exposure, as financial repression is expected to destroy real wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:50">[00:43:50]</a>. This involves pegging the long end of the curve and allowing nominal GDP (with its inflation component) to rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:18">[00:43:18]</a>. This leads to a redistribution of wealth from the rich to younger generations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:05">[00:45:05]</a>.
*   **Equities:** Equities must be part of the mix, but investors will need to accept higher volatility or use overlay products to manage risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:09">[00:56:09]</a>. Growth stocks may underperform in a higher inflationary environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:56">[00:56:56]</a>.
*   **Emerging Markets:** Opportunities for dollar-based investors may arise in emerging markets if the dollar begins to roll <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:53">[00:55:53]</a>.
*   **Commodities:** Agricultural commodities are considered interesting, especially after being "crushed" during COVID-19 despite continued consumption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:31">[00:54:31]</a>. Trading commodity markets, including backwardations and contangos, will become more relevant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:01">[01:05:01]</a>.
*   **Active Management:** The current environment favors active management over passive investment, which is seen as having peaked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:05">[00:53:05]</a>. Finding quality trading funds and paying for performance will be crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:19">[00:53:19]</a>.
