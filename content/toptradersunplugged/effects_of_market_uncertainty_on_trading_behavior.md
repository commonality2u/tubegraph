## Market Dynamics During Crises and Booms

Historically, overall correlations between asset classes may appear low or uncorrelated over long periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:39">[00:37:39]</a>. However, when examining specific periods of market stress or expansion, conditional correlations become apparent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:28">[00:45:28]</a>.

### Trend Following During Adversity
During sustained periods of adversity, such as:
*   The tech collapse (2002-2003) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:16">[00:38:16]</a>
*   The 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:38">[00:38:38]</a>
*   The Trump trade wars (2018) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:05">[00:39:05]</a>
*   The COVID-19 decline (2020) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:30">[00:39:30]</a>
*   The current market decline linked to the Ukraine war and rising inflation (2022) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:44">[00:39:44]</a>

Trend-following strategies, represented by the TTU Trend Following Index, have shown clear negative correlation to equity markets, often rising sharply as equities decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:28">[00:38:28]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:22">[00:40:22]</a>. This provides a "protective cushioning" effect for portfolios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:23">[00:43:23]</a>.

### Trend Following During Boom Periods
Conversely, during market boom periods, such as:
*   Post-tech bubble collapse (2003-2008) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:21">[00:41:21]</a>
*   Resurgence after the GFC (2011) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:50">[00:41:50]</a>
*   Significant growth periods (e.g., 2014-2015, 2020-2021) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:59">[00:41:59]</a>

Trend-following programs also experience significant growth, exhibiting a positive correlation with equity markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:31">[00:41:31]</a>. This demonstrates the "lifting power" of trend following within a portfolio, providing greater upside potential compared to traditional assets like bonds, which primarily offer a cushioning effect without significant lifting power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:22">[00:44:22]</a>.

## The Role of Market Structure and Behavioral Tendencies

The ability of trend-following to provide both a cushion and a lift is rooted in the fundamental nature of financial markets. [[Volatility and Market Dynamics | Markets are not normally distributed]]; instead, they exhibit a leptokurtic signature, characterized by a peaked center and "fat tails" (more extreme events than a normal distribution would predict) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:30">[01:03:30]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:07">[01:03:07]</a>. This non-normal distribution implies causal correlation in market data, unlike random events like coin flips <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:47">[01:04:47]</a>.

### Convergent vs. Divergent Trading Strategies
There are two broad approaches to extracting edge from price data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:51">[01:05:51]</a>:

*   **Convergent Techniques:** These are based on the premise that prices revert to an equilibrium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:52">[01:06:52]</a>. The majority of market participants (around 90%) employ convergent strategies, such as:
    *   **Fundamental investing:** Calculating intrinsic price and assuming market price will revert to it (e.g., value investing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:06">[01:07:06]</a>.
    *   **Technical analysis:** Based on repeating patterns that emerge from market predictability and oscillations around equilibrium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:57">[01:07:57]</a>.
    *   **High-frequency trading (HFT):** Often mean-reverting, exploiting arbitrage from predictable market behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:47">[01:08:47]</a>.
    *   **Central bank interventions:** Aim to stabilize markets and suppress [[Volatility in trading strategies | volatility]], encouraging convergent models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:03">[01:09:03]</a>.
    These methods exert a "convergent force" on the market, pushing prices towards an equilibrium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:52">[01:09:52]</a>.

*   **Divergent Techniques:** These are non-predictive, price-following, and reactive in nature, targeting the "tail regions" of market distributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:48">[01:10:48]</a>. Only a small percentage of participants (around 10%) specialize in these, including:
    *   Option buyers (calls and puts) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:58">[01:10:58]</a>
    *   Diversified systematic trend followers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:05">[01:11:05]</a>

### Shift in Trading Behavior During Uncertainty
During periods of market uncertainty or unpredictability, convergent models begin to fail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:38">[01:11:38]</a>. This forces convergent participants to exit their positions, which, ironically, amplifies existing trends, effectively turning their convergent strategy into a divergent impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:01">[01:12:01]</a>. Some traders may also refuse to exit, attempting to "average down" or increase leverage, leading to margin calls that further amplify trends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:17">[01:12:17]</a>.

This process results in a "wealth flow" from convergent to divergent participants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:12">[01:13:12]</a>. Trend-following programs, designed to cut losses short and allow profits to run without profit targets, are ideally configured to capture these amplified directional trends and outliers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:18">[01:14:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:03">[00:52:03]</a>. Their inherent design generates positive skew in trade distribution, with many small losses offset by a few significant winners <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:25">[00:53:25]</a>.

Furthermore, when predictive models fail, traders often revert to [[Behavioral finance in trading | behavioral tendencies]] like fear and greed, which further drive and amplify market trends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:32">[01:15:32]</a>. This shift in aggregate trading behavior is the fundamental reason why trend following excels during both boom and bust periods, making it a powerful component for portfolio diversification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:02">[01:15:02]</a>.

## Diversification and Outliers
Diversification is crucial for trend followers, as it brings together uncorrelated return streams, smoothing the overall portfolio equity curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:21">[00:55:21]</a>. While outliers (the few large winning trades) are infrequent in individual markets, a diversified portfolio across many markets increases the *frequency* of capturing these outliers across the total portfolio time series <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:01">[00:57:01]</a>. This increased frequency of non-linear anomalies directly improves the compound annual growth rate of a diversified portfolio, providing significant "lifting power" beyond simply dampening [[Impact of market volatility on investment returns | volatility]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:15">[01:02:15]</a>. This strategic advantage highlights [[the_impact_of_market_conditions_on_trading_strategies | the impact of market conditions on trading strategies]] and how [[financial_market_behaviors_during_crises | financial market behaviors during crises]] can be capitalized upon by specific approaches.
