## Early Foundations: The Turtle Program
Jerry Parker's journey into [[Systematic trading strategies | systematic trading]] began in 1983 when he was selected for Richard Dennis's Turtle program <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:17">[01:17:17]</a>. This program was a unique experiment where participants were trained in proprietary trading concepts and then traded solely for Mr. Dennis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:11:03">[05:11:03]</a>.

Before joining, Parker had already been interested in the stock market and trading, and had come across literature concerning [[Trend following strategy and its evolution | trend following]], finding that it "made a lot of sense" to him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:37:40">[03:37:40]</a>. He was drawn to the idea of a [[Simplicity in trading strategies | systematic approach]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:33">[03:57:33]</a>, influenced by televised discussions on price trends, risk control, small losses, and diversification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:16:30">[04:16:30]</a>.

The Turtle training, lasting only two or three weeks, focused on:
*   The philosophy of [[Systematic trading strategies | trading systems]] and [[Trend following strategy and its evolution | trend following]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:43:40">[18:43:40]</a>.
*   Specific entry and exit signals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:51:33">[18:51:33]</a>.
*   A mindset to approach markets, recognizing that parameters might need adjustment over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:04:14">[19:04:14]</a>.

A core principle taught was to "love your system," including its less desirable characteristics like less than 50% winning trades, many small losses, big profits turning into small profits or even losses, and drawdowns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:22:15">[26:22:15]</a>. The importance was on following the system and not being evaluated solely on performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:59:15">[19:59:15]</a>.

Initially, Parker found it challenging to "pull the trigger" on trades due to the fear of losing money and also struggled with getting out of trades too quickly to preserve profits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:48:09">[24:48:09]</a>. He acknowledged that during the program, his creativity was limited as he focused on pleasing Richard Dennis and following rules exactly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:14:11">[22:14:11]</a>.

### Initial Adjustments and Research

Parker started Chesapeake with a managed account, immediately deciding to trade with "a lot less leverage and risk" compared to the Turtle program's aggressive 150-200% annual returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:45:04">[36:45:04]</a>. *   **Diversification**: Adding as many markets as possible, including non-US markets and later single stock futures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:50:37">[41:50:37]</a>. ### Challenges and Refinements

Parker emphasizes that his [[Evolution of Systems Trading | evolution of systems trading]] has been consistent in its core philosophy:
*   **Staying True to Trend Following**: He has never been interested in adding "counter-trend" or "mean reversion" components to his strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:44:03">[43:44:03]</a>. He classifies himself as someone who adheres to the "original system philosophy as much as is humanly possible" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:19:19">[44:19:19]</a>.
*   **Avoiding Over-optimization**: In the early 2000s, his systems became "a little bit too optimized" and included "unfortunate non-trend following components" that he later removed around 2007 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:11:06">[41:11:06]</a>. This highlights the [[Challenges and strategies in improving trading systems without overoptimization | challenges and strategies in improving trading systems without overoptimization]]. He believes adding features that cut profits short often do not improve future performance, even if they look good in backtests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="48:28:30">[48:28:30]</a>.
*   **Robustness and Simplicity**: Parker prioritizes robust systems and being conservative, avoiding additions that would weaken reliability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:02:40">[45:02:40]</a>. He asserts that resisting the temptation to reduce drawdowns or capture profits more frequently, and instead creating systems with fewer parameters, increases their reliability and ability to consistently make money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:49:03">[45:49:03]</a>.

### Trend Following as the "Least Risky" Strategy

Parker holds a strong conviction that [[Systematic trading and trend following strategies | trend following]] is the "least risky investment strategy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:04:06">[49:04:06]</a>. He attributes this to its core components:
*   **Diversification**: Trading many different markets and taking both long and short positions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:56:07">[49:56:07]</a>.
*   **Systematic Approach**: Backtesting rules to ensure they work and following them consistently across thousands of trades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:14:02">[50:14:02]</a>.
*   **Long-Term Nature**: Staying in trends for as long as necessary to capture significant moves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:39:10">[50:39:10]</a>.
*   **Risk Control**: Taking small losses, letting profits run, and using trailing stops based on market prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:13:30">[51:13:30]</a>.

He acknowledges that the industry has sometimes "mishandled" this approach through excessive leverage, high fees, and commissions, which can overwhelm the inherent benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:50:31">[51:50:31]</a>.

### Industry Pressures and Lessons

As Chesapeake Capital grew, Parker experienced pressure to conform to investor preferences. He believes that a low-leveraged program aiming for about 10% annual returns would have been a better business idea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:23:07">[53:23:07]</a>. He resisted attempts to implement "portfolio management" strategies (like adjusting positions based on volatility fluctuations) that he felt would "destroy the core system and the robustness of the core of the trading" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:10:04">[54:10:04]</a>.

Performance fluctuations, such as his firm's underperformance in 2008 followed by overperformance in 2009, illustrate the "seesaw" nature of systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:51:24">[57:51:24]</a>. He warns against "tinkering with systems" after periods of underperformance, as new research might lead to worse results than older, robust systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="58:11:15">[58:11:15]</a>.

He notes that recent challenges (around 2014) for managed futures were often due to heavy allocation to commodities, which had been flatlining, rather than a fundamental flaw in [[Systematic trading and trend following strategies | trend following]] itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:24">[01:00:24]</a>. When applied to trending markets like stocks, the same systems performed well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:57">[01:00:57]</a>.

Regarding losses, Parker shifted from saying "we take a small loss" to "we take an optimal loss" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:25">[01:02:25]</a>. This is because a loss that is too small can lead to excessive whipsawing and a very low winning percentage, while one that is too large is obviously detrimental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:57">[01:02:57]</a>. The "optimal stop-loss placement" aims for a winning percentage in the low 40s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:07">[01:03:07]</a>.

## Evaluating Trading Systems

When evaluating traders, Parker believes that [[history and evolution of trading programs | historical track records]] are meaningful, especially their longevity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:35">[01:04:35]</a>. However, he cautions against trying to pinpoint the "top five" traders, as many [[Systematic trading strategies | systematic trading strategies]] tend to converge to similar long-term performance over 10 or 20 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:21">[01:05:21]</a>. Investors should focus on hiring a diverse group of competent traders who have been able to stay in business and withstand various market environments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:02">[01:05:02]</a>. He concludes that the public often struggles to appreciate [[Systematic trading and trend following strategies | systematic trend following strategies]] because they don't understand the necessary characteristics of such systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:37">[01:06:37]</a>.
