## Socionomics: Mood and Decision-Making

Peter Atwater's work in socionomics focuses on the relationship between mood and decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27">[00:06:27]</a>. This field explores how collective mood influences various aspects of society, from economic and financial choices to political and cultural decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>. Examples often cited in "socionomics light" include the hemline index (hemlines rise when people feel frisky, fall when mood is bad) and the skyscraper index (tall buildings go up during periods of high confidence) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:39">[00:06:39]</a>.

During periods of low confidence, behaviors become highly emotional and impulsive across financial markets, politics, and culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:23">[00:07:23]</a>. Conversely, as confidence rises, society tends to become more collegial, consensus-building, and peaceful, embracing abstract and different ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>.

### Mood as a Leading Indicator

Research has shown a tangible link between social mood and market movements. Sentiment expressed on platforms like Twitter, based on language analysis, tends to roll into the markets with a 24 to 48-hour lag, barring exogenous events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. The media often acts as a mirror of the prevailing mood, not a leader or a follower <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>. Media companies prioritize resonance with the current mood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>.

### COVID-19 and the "K-Shaped Recovery"

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted a stark disparity in confidence, leading to what Peter Atwater termed the "K-shaped recovery" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:26">[00:12:26]</a>. This phenomenon had nothing to do with economics, but everything to do with confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>.
*   Those able to work from home and isolate experienced a dramatic rebound in confidence due to self-containment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:41">[00:12:41]</a>. The "Downton Abbey" system of delivery made isolation easy for the wealthy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:55">[00:12:55]</a>.
*   Conversely, those who had to go out for work (e.g., hospital, supermarket, delivery) did not experience such a recovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:11">[00:13:11]</a>.

This period also saw a nihilistic response among young people, manifesting in phenomena like the Reddit-driven trading surges (e.g., GameStop) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:36">[00:13:36]</a>. At the market's peak in early 2021, there was a concurrent bubble of extraordinary confidence among the financial elite paired with this "what do I have to lose" mentality at the bottom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>.

## Confidence in Financial Systems

Confidence in central banks, such as the Federal Reserve, is linked to the level of interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>.
*   In the late 1970s and early 1980s, as interest rates spiked, confidence in the Fed evaporated, with no belief that inflation could be controlled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:10">[00:16:10]</a>.
*   Higher rates and lower bond prices correlate with less confidence in central banks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:35">[00:16:35]</a>.
*   The belief that quantitative easing (QE) could solve all problems and even negative interest rates were expressions of overconfidence in central bankers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:55">[00:16:55]</a>.
*   Since then, confidence in central bankers has been waning as interest rates rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:18">[00:17:18]</a>.

### Populism, Inequality, and [[impact of ai on the economy and investing | Investment]]

Populism has been building over the last decade, driven by monetary policy, supply-side economics, and the resulting inequality fostered by the Federal Reserve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:04">[00:19:04]</a>. COVID-19 unleashed significant fiscal policy ($12 trillion), which can be seen as a populist revolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:01">[00:24:01]</a>.

Cryptocurrency embodied this zeitgeist, reflecting a distrust of government by the younger labor class and a belief in [[technological_advancements_effect_on_the_economy | technology's ability]] to solve all problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:00">[00:24:00]</a>. However, a significant downturn in crypto could lead to immense anger and be seen as an "enormous betrayal" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:01">[00:26:01]</a>. This could trigger a "griftization" cycle, where the financialization of self-promotion, seen at both high (Elizabeth Holmes) and low ends (cultist behavior), comes to light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:17">[00:26:17]</a>.

A major risk is the high level of borrowing undertaken by those at the top since 2009 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:24">[00:32:24]</a>. Banks like JP Morgan have more outstanding in private banking than in their entire credit card portfolios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:35">[00:32:35]</a>. This growth of leverage among the "invulnerable" elite makes them vulnerable to a bubble burst that they and their entourage are unprepared for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:28">[00:33:28]</a>. Unlike 2008, there will likely be no sympathy for their failures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:41">[00:33:41]</a>.

The underlying tension is the "up and down" divide, not just political ideology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:53">[00:27:53]</a>. Those at the bottom, feeling uncertainty and powerlessness, desire to regain certainty and control in their lives, and may take pleasure in the downfall of those who previously held extreme certainty and control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:39">[00:34:39]</a>.

## Investment Strategy in a Volatile World

In environments of declining social mood, there's a retreat from abstraction in investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:39">[01:01:39]</a>. This suggests a shift towards:
*   **Cash:** People want physical cash, especially in times of low market confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:50">[01:01:50]</a>.
*   **Commodities:** Real assets become highly valued. This includes grains, energy inputs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:33">[01:02:33]</a>. The concentration of these resources in the center of the US, while over half the population is on the coasts, has significant implications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:49">[01:02:49]</a>.
*   **Value over Abstraction:** A pilgrimage from "long-duration equities" to value-based investments occurs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:10">[01:02:10]</a>.

For investors, it is crucial to recognize that perceptions of the future are a function of one's own confidence level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:25">[01:13:25]</a>. If confident, the imagined future is brighter than when not confident <a class="yt-timestamp" data=t="01:13:31">[01:13:31]</a>. Investors are encouraged to be more ambivalent about imagined outcomes, and to consider the absolute opposite scenario before making a trade. The less willing one is to believe an opposite scenario can happen, the more likely it is to occur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:36">[01:14:36]</a>.
