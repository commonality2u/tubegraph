## Historical Dollar Constraints for Emerging Markets

Historically, the biggest constraint for most Emerging Markets (EMs) has been the difficulty in accessing [[significance_of_the_us_dollar_in_global_markets | US dollars]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:55">[00:38:55]</a>. This constraint not only impacted immediate needs but also future planning, making countries reluctant to embark on large-scale [[structural_changes_in_commodity_markets | infrastructure spending]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. For example, India might hesitate to build a railway if it feared running out of dollars to buy necessary iron ore <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:09">[00:39:09]</a>.

## The Game Changer: Local Currency Commodity Trade

The ongoing [[impact_of_geopolitical_tensions_on_the_us_dollar_and_global_trade | Ukraine war]] has inadvertently provided a "silver lining" for Emerging Markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:51">[00:37:51]</a>. It has opened up opportunities for these nations to access commodities at prices they never had before, primarily by trading in their own local currencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:01">[00:38:01]</a>.

This means that countries like India no longer have to first earn [[significance_of_the_us_dollar_in_global_markets | US dollars]] to fund their massive commodity needs. For instance, Russia is now willing to accept Indian Rupees for oil, a "huge game changer" for India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:29">[00:38:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:33">[00:38:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:36">[00:38:36]</a>. Similar dynamics apply to Indonesia, Brazil, and other nations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:38">[00:38:38]</a>. This removal of the dollar constraint liberates Emerging Markets to pursue ambitious [[structural_changes_in_commodity_markets | infrastructure projects]] without the previous financial uncertainty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:32">[00:33:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:01">[00:39:01]</a>.

## Economic Implications for Emerging Markets

The de-dollarization trend is expected to fuel significant growth in a broad range of Emerging Markets, moving the focus of global growth away from the traditional U.S. and China narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:09">[00:33:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:01">[00:35:01]</a>.

Key indicators of this shift include:
*   **Bond Market Performance:** Over the past 18 months, Brazilian government bonds have outperformed [[us_dollar_and_global_currency_movements | US treasuries]] by 50%, and Indian government bonds by 25%. Similar trends are seen in Indonesia and other markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:57">[00:33:57]</a>.
*   **"Triple Merit Scenario":** A proper bull market, lasting a decade or more, is characterized by gains in currency, bonds, and equities, driven by falling real rates, rising exchange rates, and increasing asset prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:22">[00:34:22]</a>. This scenario is currently playing out in countries like Brazil, India, Indonesia, and Mexico <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:36">[00:34:36]</a>.
*   **Infrastructure Boom:** In the region stretching from Istanbul to Jakarta, home to 3.5 billion people, incomes are growing at 5% annually, and [[structural_changes_in_commodity_markets | infrastructure spending]] is "going absolutely bananas" due to newfound access to commodities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:43">[00:34:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:52">[00:34:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:04">[00:35:04]</a>.

This shift presents a challenge to Emerging Markets to emulate China's past infrastructure achievements, spurred by increased visibility of development via social media <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:12">[00:35:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:25">[00:35:25]</a>.

The de-dollarization of commodity trade has significant implications for the [[us_dollar_and_global_currency_ आंदोलनों | US dollar]]. ## Categorization of Emerging Markets

Not all Emerging Markets will benefit equally from this de-dollarization. A split is emerging between:
*   **Beneficiaries:** Large Emerging Markets where countries like Russia are willing to accept local currencies (e.g., China, India, Thailand, due to tourism ties) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:26">[00:39:26]</a>.
*   **Frontier Markets:** Smaller or more unstable markets (e.g., Egypt, Sri Lanka, Pakistan) where their currencies are not widely accepted in international trade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:48">[00:39:48]</a>.

This [[monetary_regime_change_and_its_impact_on_commodities | shift]] represents a fundamental change, empowering major Emerging Markets with a new level of economic autonomy and potential for sustained growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:06">[00:40:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:11">[00:40:11]</a>.
