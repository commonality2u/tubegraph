## Roy Neerhofer's Background and Firm

Roy Neerhofer, founder and president of R.G. Neerhofer Capital Management, describes his firm's strategy as having a specific intent: to combine interesting standalone returns with consistent downside protection for portfolios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>. Their goal is to maintain a consistent negative correlation to equities, performing better when equities and traditional portfolios face difficulties or high volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>. The strategy is tuned not to maximize their own Sharp ratio, but to maximize the risk-adjusted return of their clients <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>.

Neerhofer's path to trading began with an early interest in computers and programming at 13, leading him to teach himself to program and start a small software company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:43">[00:05:43]</a>. This experience instilled efficient programming skills due to severe memory limitations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. During this time, he also interned for his brother, Victor, an early entrant into the hedge fund space who was present for the first Equity Futures trade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:24">[00:07:24]</a>. Victor observed and quantified patterns in intraday price data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>.

Before graduating from Harvard in 1987, Roy studied neuroscience, particularly the differences between musicians' and non-musicians' brains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. His key insight from neuroscience—that human behavior is a product of our hardware—was directly applied to his trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>.

After college, he worked for his brother's firm, which had an "incredible team" including successful short-term traders like Monroe Trout, Steve Wisdom, and Paul Bey, providing a "tremendous mentoring experience" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>. In 1992, Neerhofer left to form R.G. Neerhofer Capital Management, starting trading in July 1993 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>. The firm's original pitchbook from 1993 remains largely consistent with its current approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>.

### Core Strategy and Philosophy

R.G. Neerhofer Capital Management's core strategy employs a variety of timeframes (from minutes to weeks) and utilizes both momentum and counter-trend contrarian signals, all systematically executed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:31">[00:56:31]</a>. The system, comprising 60 to 70 individual strategies grouped into "style buckets" or "families," runs "essentially automatically" like an aircraft on autopilot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:51">[00:56:51]</a>.

Their flagship "Diversified" program is tuned to maintain a negative correlation of -0.3 to -0.4 to equity markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:33">[00:57:33]</a>. They also offer:
*   **Optimal Alpha program**: Aims for zero correlation to the equity market, often outperforming the Diversified program in "risk-on" environments while producing similar alpha <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:14">[00:58:14]</a>.
*   **IHedge program**: Shifts emphasis to commodities and selling fixed income, designed for an environment of rising interest rates and commodity prices, with short-term managers being less susceptible to negative carry on the short side <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:01">[01:00:01]</a>.

Clients can switch between these funds with short notice, allowing them to express individual portfolio views or even request custom correlations through a Separately Managed Account (SMA), ranging from as low as -0.6 or -0.7 to as high as +0.3 (like a 50/50 mix with equities) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:39">[01:00:39]</a>.

## Cognitive Biases and Systematic Trading

Human beings are often "terrible investors" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:23">[01:12:23]</a> if they follow their instincts, which are optimized for survival on the African Savannah, not for profitable trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:44">[01:12:44]</a>. Neerhofer highlights several cognitive biases:
*   **Risk Aversion**: People "hate to lose more than they love to win" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:21">[01:13:21]</a>. This leads to strategies that appear appealing but may not be optimal, and drives incentives to avoid losses, pushing firms to be short volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:02">[01:18:02]</a>.
*   **Endowment Bias**: Something owned is typically valued more highly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:59">[01:13:59]</a>.
*   **Consensus Bias**: The tendency to "Stampede with the herd," making it emotionally difficult to be a contrarian <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:29">[01:14:29]</a>.
*   **Recency Bias**: Recent information is remembered more vividly than past information, which is helpful in nature but not in financial markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:41">[01:14:41]</a>.
*   **Visual Pattern Recognition**: The brain's tendency to see patterns in data, even when they aren't statistically significant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:18">[01:15:18]</a>. Roy's brother, Victor, was a "tremendous opponent" of charting for this reason <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:29">[01:15:29]</a>.

Even [[building_and_evaluating_systematic_trading_strategies | systematic trading strategies]] are not immune to these biases. Developers and investors tend to overuse recent performance when evaluating strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:58">[01:16:58]</a>. Research shows that the last two years of Sharp ratio are "completely unpredictive" of future performance for CTAs, yet it's the most common evaluation tool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:24">[01:17:24]</a>.

## Evolution of Trading Systems and Strategies

### Industry Challenges in a Changing Rate Environment
The greatest contributor to CTA success over the last 25 years has been long fixed income, driven by declining interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:00">[00:31:00]</a>. A significant portion of these profits (two-thirds) came from the positive roll yield due to a positively sloping yield curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:50">[00:31:50]</a>. If interest rates rise, this roll yield becomes negative for short positions, destroying the ability to [[trend_following_strategy_and_its_evolution | trend follow]] in fixed income on the short side <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:35">[00:33:35]</a>.

In a rising rate environment, CTAs would need to find profits elsewhere:
*   **Commodities**: Too small to support the current size of the [[evolution_of_systems_trading | CTA industry]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:08">[00:35:08]</a>.
*   **Equities**: Larger managers are pushed towards long equity exposure to support industry growth, leading to increased correlation of the CTA index to equities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:55">[00:35:55]</a>.
*   **FX Markets**: Potentially the main driver of returns for CTAs due to sufficient liquidity, volatility, and diversity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:04">[00:36:04]</a>.

This implies a disturbing trend where investors prefer large managers, yet smaller managers might be better positioned to gain meaningful allocations in non-fixed-income markets like commodities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:18">[00:37:18]</a>. The protective quality of CTAs, historically seen in events like Long-Term Capital Management's demise or 9/11, largely came from being long fixed income during a secular decline in interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:49">[00:38:49]</a>. In a rising rate environment, CTAs may spend more time short fixed income, leading to a secular shift in their correlation to equities and less protection during equity sell-offs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:18">[00:40:18]</a>.

### Volatility and Market Cycles
Quantitative easing (QE) has had a tremendous suppressing effect on realized volatility across all markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:04">[00:54:04]</a>. With the end of QE, volatility is expected to rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:22">[00:54:22]</a>. This will challenge portfolios designed for a low or declining volatility environment, highlighting the need for proactive portfolio re-evaluation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:50">[00:54:50]</a>.

## Evaluating a Long-Term Track Record

### The Importance of Track Records and How to Evaluate Them
A longer track record indicates survival, whether by luck or skill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:04">[01:13:04]</a>. A key evaluation point is consistency to message and mission. Neerhofer's firm consistently communicated its mission to be negatively correlated to other assets, not [[trend_following_strategy and its evolution | trend following]], and to be beneficial to portfolios over big market cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:08">[01:14:08]</a>.

Despite periods of underperformance (e.g., 2009-2012 due to underestimating QE's impact), the firm refined its strategy, improving execution for lower costs and increasing model diversity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:07">[01:15:07]</a>. Quantitatively, they continue to provide downside protection while improving returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:22">[01:15:22]</a>.

### Beyond Performance Metrics
Neerhofer strongly believes that past performance is "completely unindicative of future performance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:15">[01:16:15]</a>. Instead of focusing on mean return or Sharpe ratio, investors should look at:
*   **Correlation**: Is the manager consistently delivering the stated correlation profile? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:40">[01:16:40]</a>.
*   **Performance in Shock Events**: How does the manager perform during market sell-offs or specific shock events? This reveals the underlying factors driving the program <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:43">[01:16:43]</a>.
*   **Longevity, Process, and Cost**: These provide more valuable insights into a manager's potential future success than historical returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:11">[01:17:11]</a>.

Picking managers is "a really, really hard job" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:36">[01:17:36]</a>, and studies show that the last two years of Sharp ratio are "worse than chance" at predicting future performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:04">[01:18:04]</a>.

## Organizational Structure and Culture

R.G. Neerhofer Capital Management's infrastructure has evolved over 22 years, with all code developed in-house, primarily in C++ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:28">[01:02:28]</a>. This includes everything from top-down allocation tools to execution algorithms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:45">[01:02:45]</a>. The firm operates three shifts to maintain 24/7 coverage during market hours, with about two dozen staff, mostly on the investment side (13-14 "quants" who are also programmers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:59">[01:02:59]</a>. Most of the team focuses on improving the strategy, risk management, and exploring new ideas, operating like a "scientific research lab" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:15">[01:04:15]</a>.

The firm prioritizes hiring "creative out-of-the-box thinkers" who can produce negatively or zero-correlated strategies and offer "world-changing insights," rather than just incremental improvements or fitting data better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:11">[01:06:11]</a>. The interview process focuses on creativity, looking for people who are willing to "think differently" and challenge the status quo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:10">[01:07:10]</a>.

The firm fosters an "open culture" where all trading room staff see real-time P&L and positions, enabling them to understand and question models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:59">[01:10:59]</a>. This transparency, combined with a willingness to educate each other, builds confidence for individuals to ask "dumb questions" and constructively challenge existing paradigms, rather than fearing being "shot down" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:42">[01:11:42]</a>.
