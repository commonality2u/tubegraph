## Socionomics and Social Mood

Peter Atwater, a prominent expert in socionomics, social mood, and human behavior, defines socionomics as the study of the relationship between mood and decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>. Common "socionomics light" phenomena include the hemline index (hemline up when people feel frisky, down when in a bad mood) and the skyscraper index (tall buildings rise with confidence) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. These illustrate how confidence manifests in financial, political, and cultural decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:53">[00:06:53]</a>. Low confidence is associated with highly emotional and impulsive behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:23">[00:07:23]</a>.

Mood can act as a leading indicator for financial markets; sentiment on Twitter, for instance, influences markets with a 24 to 48-hour lag, barring exogenous events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. The media serves as a mirror of public mood, prioritizing resonance with current sentiment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>.

Technological advancements, such as social media, accelerate and broaden the spread of mood across society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:19">[00:10:19]</a>. This rapid spread of information can cause financial crises to unfold in days and weeks rather than longer periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:52">[00:10:52]</a>. Policy makers often "woefully under appreciate" how trading technology, available 24/7, increases the potential for rapid market closures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>.

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted a significant disparity in confidence, leading to a K-shaped recovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:26">[00:12:26]</a>. Those who could work from home experienced a dramatic rebound in confidence due to self-containment, while those who had to go out did not recover <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:41">[00:12:41]</a>. The period also saw a "nihilistic response" among young people, manifested in phenomena like the Reddit-driven trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:29">[00:13:29]</a>. This created a concurrent bubble of extraordinary confidence among the financial elite paired with a "what the f***" mentality among those at the bottom, sweeping into assets like SPACs and crypto <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:50">[00:13:50]</a>.

Confidence in central banks, such as the Federal Reserve, is inversely linked to interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>. During the late 1970s and early 1980s, spiking interest rates led to a complete evaporation of confidence in the Fed's ability to control inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:13">[00:16:13]</a>. Conversely, negative interest rates can be seen as an expression of overconfidence in central bankers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:00">[00:17:00]</a>.

## The Rise of Populism and Its Consequences

[[populism_and_economic_implications | Populism]] has been building over the last decade, stemming from monetary policy and the resulting inequality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:52">[00:18:52]</a>. COVID-19 unleashed a "populist revolution" through a massive increase in fiscal policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:04">[00:24:04]</a>.

The distrust of government, particularly among the younger, laboring class, coupled with a belief in technology's ability to solve problems, has fueled phenomena like crypto <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:15">[00:24:15]</a>. If crypto experiences another significant downturn, it risks becoming an "enormous betrayal" and a "triggering point" for the realization of a "griftization" cycle, characterized by the financialization of self-promotion and blind followership in fantastical promotions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:01">[00:26:01]</a>. This anger, especially among young people who had nothing to lose and invested in crypto, could lead to dramatic change, echoing the historical pairing of youth and revolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:16">[00:27:16]</a>.

The fundamental conflict is shifting from political ideology (left vs. right) to a "more up and down" divide, focusing on the resolution of power and money disparities between those at the top and those at the bottom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:56">[00:27:56]</a>. While natural systems may tend towards inequality, human beliefs in equality and justice, when exerted upon these systems, can create "big booms and busts" like [[populism_and_economic_implications | populism]] and revolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:27">[00:29:27]</a>.

[[populism_and_economic_implications | Populism]] drives fiscal policy, which in turn drives inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:43">[00:24:43]</a>. This creates a feedback loop where inflation further exacerbates [[populism_and_economic_implications | populism]], potentially leading to price controls and reduced market liquidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>.

## Globalization, Deglobalization, and Geopolitics

Globalization is viewed as a consequence of rising confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:25">[00:52:25]</a>. The concept of "horizon preference" suggests that under threat, focus narrows to "me here now," while rising confidence broadens perspectives to "us everywhere forever," fostering collegiality, peace, and acceptance of abstract and different concepts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:32">[00:52:32]</a>.

[[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | Deglobalization]] is expected to be a consequence of falling mood, leading to natural balkanization and division <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:48">[00:53:48]</a>. In such an environment, large supra-international bodies like the World Health Organization, the UN, and NATO become too abstract and irrelevant because the perceived crisis is immediate and personal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:10">[00:54:10]</a>. A preview of this was seen with COVID-19, where national interests became paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:26">[00:54:26]</a>.

The overconfidence that precedes a downturn rarely has a single tipping point, but rather a subtle deterioration in mood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:07">[00:55:07]</a>. The collapse of Lehman Brothers, for example, marked a bottom in economic confidence, indicating the onset of hopelessness and despair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:26">[00:55:26]</a>.

The shift towards [[fragmentation_and_deglobalization_trends | deglobalization]] is rooted in a move away from equilibrium due to factors like inequality driven by monetary policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:24">[00:56:24]</a>. For 40 years, globalization flourished as corporations increased profits by lowering costs through a "cooperation game" at the top, but this resulted in growing inequality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:46">[00:56:46]</a>. This inequality and the resulting [[populism_and_economic_implications | populism]] have "pricked that bubble," leading to a shift towards nationalism and competition over commodities and scarce resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:18">[00:57:18]</a>. This is intertwined with [[geopolitical_tensions_and_global_conflicts | geopolitical tensions and global conflicts]] like the war in Ukraine and China's stance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:38">[00:57:38]</a>.

When confidence is low, societies tend to become "naturally xenophobic" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:58">[00:57:58]</a>. Turkey is identified as an "oversized canary in the coal mine" for a national crisis, with its unmooring from the West indicative of shifting tides of power during [[geopolitical_tensions_and_global_conflicts | geopolitical stress]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:09">[01:07:09]</a>. Russia's aggression, particularly under Vladimir Putin, is linked to rising oil prices; the higher oil prices go, the more empowered and aggressive Russia is likely to become <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:53">[01:09:53]</a>.

The EU and the Euro could face a confidence crisis significant enough to cause their breakup, depending on how low social mood goes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:26">[01:06:26]</a>.

## Economic and Investment Implications

Inflation is viewed more as a psychological phenomenon than purely economic, tied to feelings of scarcity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:18">[00:39:18]</a>. Unexpected and wildly swinging inflation creates "enormous feelings of vulnerability" because it destroys predictability, forcing difficult cognitive choices, particularly for low-end consumers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:35">[00:39:35]</a>.

The current "mark to market economy" allows investors to speculate on essential goods like gas and food, leading to "huge spikes up and down" that can cause unexpected price collapses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:50">[00:40:50]</a>.

Regarding policy responses, traditional monetary policy tends to increase inequality by benefiting those who can borrow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:27">[00:35:27]</a>. While fiscal policy aims to address inequality, its effectiveness is limited when the cost of money rises, making it more difficult to provide additional stimulus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:59">[00:36:59]</a>. Governments may resort to greater taxation to fund economic malaise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:12">[00:37:12]</a>. Policymakers are likely to be more reticent to over-respond to the next crisis due to current inflation concerns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:44">[00:37:44]</a>.

In a low-confidence environment, investment themes shift from abstract "long duration equity" to a "retreat from abstraction" towards "real assets" like cash and commodities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:39">[01:01:39]</a>. At market lows, people seek physical cash or real goods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:50">[01:01:50]</a>. If reality becomes based on physical goods like grains and energy, this has significant [[geopolitical_tensions_and_global_conflicts | geopolitical implications]] and "staggering implications" for the United States, as the supply of these essential commodities is concentrated in the center of the country, while over half the population resides on the coasts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:33">[01:02:33]</a>. This internal dynamic could create "existential risks" to the US political system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:07">[01:04:07]</a>.

Investors should be ambivalent about imagined outcomes, as their imagination is a function of their own confidence level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:25">[01:13:25]</a>. Strong certainty in an outcome, especially if one cannot consider the absolute opposite scenario, increases the likelihood of being wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:35">[01:14:35]</a>.
