## Case Study: Asia's Accumulator Products (June 2021)

A notable example of negative asymmetry playing out disastrously was the surge of private banks in Asia offering accumulator products in June 2021 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>. These products, focused on major China tech companies like Alibaba, Tencent, and Ping An, were adopted by every private bank in Singapore and Hong Kong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>. Within three months, the entire structure collapsed, leading to rumors of billions of dollars in unrecognized losses across numerous banks within six months, despite individual ticket sizes being modest ($50,000 to $100,000) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>. The sheer scale of this exposure was phenomenal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>.

## Systemic Fragility and Mismeasured Risk

The financial system exhibits significant fragility due to uncapitalized or mismeasured risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a>. A major flow position and uncapitalized risk in the market stem from how the regulated financial system treats government bonds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:18">[00:05:18]</a>. They are often considered "riskless" or "risk-reducing," requiring no capital to hold or even reducing capital requirements for other assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>. This perception allows for unlimited leverage to be applied to them without an apparent increase in risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>, which inevitably leads to systemic issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

### The "Immaculate Recession" Narrative and its Aftermath

The market reacted strongly to the anticipation of a knee-jerk reversal of Fed policy, assuming a "Pavlovian response" where the Fed would always slash rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. Instead, the Fed engaged in *de facto* financial repression, subsidizing regional banks to hold government bonds, providing more incentives to retain already hidden, accumulated unrealized losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.

The "immaculate recession" narrative, which suggests a smooth economic downturn without significant market disruption, is seen as too good to be true <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:20">[00:10:20]</a>. While forward yield curves and equity markets priced in a recession with falling rates, many credit markets were not similarly priced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:07">[00:10:07]</a>. The systemic risk lies in the lack of capital supporting leveraged fixed income in regulated financial institutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>. If bonds plummet, institutions like banks, pension funds, and insurance companies face collapse; however, if only equities decline and equity holders lose money, it is not considered systemically risky given current market structures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. Banks typically fail by leveraging assets perceived as not risky, rather than by taking obvious risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>.

## The Power of Convexity and Antifragility

A key strategy to navigate market volatility and capitalize on divergences is to embrace convexity or antifragility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a>. This means building [[investment_strategies_for_volatility | portfolios]] that benefit from unexpected outcomes and large movements, rather than being optimized for the "mean" expectation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>.

### Compounding Returns and Divergences

Over a 40-year period (480 months) for the S&P 500, the 10 worst months contribute over 40% of the long-term compounded return, and the 10 best months contribute over 30% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:05">[00:16:05]</a>. The middle 460 months only account for 25% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:21">[00:16:21]</a>. Yet, traditional financial models often ignore these "two percentile wings" and focus on the expected center of outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:37">[00:16:37]</a>. This highlights that 75% of returns are driven by these extreme events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:47">[00:16:47]</a>.

A compounding portfolio is one that benefits from divergences from the mean <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:55">[00:16:55]</a>. The magnitude of monthly returns, not their frequency, is what truly matters for compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:53">[00:18:53]</a>. The financial industry's focus on average returns and metrics like Value at Risk (VaR) can ignore or even destroy the wealth accumulation through geometric compounding, effectively transferring wealth from savers to the financial industry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:17">[00:19:17]</a>.

### The Football Pitch Analogy

An effective analogy for [[Investing strategies and risk management | investment strategies]] is a football (soccer) pitch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:16">[00:17:16]</a>. In a match, 96% of the time, the ball is between the two penalty boxes, but the entire outcome is determined within those 2% of the time spent inside the penalty boxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:26">[00:17:26]</a>. Similarly, in portfolios, the "midfield passing" or daily activity (460 months) catches the eye but has minimal impact on the long-term compounding path. All significant impact comes from the "big numbers" in the "wings" or tail events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:05">[00:18:05]</a>.

A "convex barbell portfolio" (long equities, long volatility) was highlighted as having an "absolutely sensational year" and a "sensational" five-year history, significantly outperforming traditional 60/40 portfolios that assume constant correlations and volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:40">[00:20:40]</a>.

## Historical Context of Volatility and Inflation

The period from 1997 to 2016 was unique, characterized by incredibly low inflation and low inflation volatility, relative to 800 years of historical data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:15">[00:25:15]</a>. This period of low inflation and stability was linked to the ability of central banks, like the Federal Reserve, to apply unlimited monetary policy due to a preceding deflationary regime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:38">[00:31:38]</a>.

### Shift to Structural Inflation and Volatility

There's a growing belief that the "genie is out of the bottle" and the era of unprecedented [[market_volatility_and_volatility_strategies | volatility suppression]] is ending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:43">[00:25:43]</a>. A structural inflationary pressure is emerging due to the rebalancing of inequality and political pressures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>. This means a more difficult situation where liquidity is removed to combat inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:45">[00:12:45]</a>.

The current economic climate, with falling prices, lower inflation, strong employment, and lower rates, might appear as a "Goldilocks" scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>. However, this "best of both worlds" can quickly turn into the "worst of both things," moving towards stagflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:57">[00:11:57]</a>. The shift from maximizing mean outcomes (GDP) to maximizing median outcomes (distributing money to people) implies that demand-side economics now matters more, altering the monetary policy landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:53">[00:32:53]</a>.

The Fed's policy under Alan Greenspan (from 1996) involved removing assets from the measurement of price stability, then using monetary policy to inflate those assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:32">[00:37:32]</a>. This created a "wealth effect" of growth without appearing to cause inflation, making the dual objectives seem complementary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:40">[00:37:40]</a>. This approach, however, built up "massive asset inflation," leading to wealth segregation and social fragility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:52">[00:29:52]</a>.

The current situation is also compared to the 1970s, but with a crucial difference: Paul Volcker tackled inflation with 20% debt to GDP, whereas Jerome Powell is dealing with 120% debt to GDP, not just in the US but globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:06">[00:39:06]</a>. This high debt level raises concerns about fiscal dominance and the inability of central banks to act without being forced to buy bonds again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:39">[00:39:39]</a>.

## The Role of Japan and China in Global Liquidity and Volatility

Japan is highlighted as a country worth watching. The Bank of Japan has engineered a unique situation by taking bonds out of banks' hands, something the West is now grappling with <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:32">[00:43:32]</a>. They stepped away from Yield Curve Control (YCC) by gradually allowing pressure to release, much like "firing bullets slowly" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:30">[00:45:30]</a>. Higher interest rates generally benefit Japan's private sector as corporates and consumers are not heavily leveraged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:11">[00:46:11]</a>. Japan's corporate sector has focused on efficiency and margin profitability, not financialization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:39">[00:46:39]</a>.

However, Japan remains the "most dangerous peg in the world" not for itself, but for the rest of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:51">[00:48:51]</a>. Its private sector is the largest net foreign investor and creditor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:56">[00:48:56]</a>. A reversal of these flows, if Japanese investors bring money back home, could have significant implications as Japan is the last major provider of liquidity in the "Hunger Games" of global finance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:16">[00:49:16]</a>. This could impact dollar-yen exchange rates and global funding markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:30">[00:59:30]</a>.

China also plays a critical role. It was the singular credit impulse creator for a decade after the Global Financial Crisis (GFC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:02">[00:51:02]</a>. Now, China is experiencing its own post-excess hangover, with stocks like the Hang Seng Index back to 1997 levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:10">[00:51:10]</a>. Despite efforts to manipulate and hold its currency steady to attract inflows, China registered its first net outflow of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) in recent history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:55">[00:51:55]</a>.

## Structured Products and Volatility Suppression

The phenomenon of structured products has become crucial to volatility compression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:30">[01:06:30]</a>. These products, particularly in the US, are driven by higher interest rates, which allow for a value proposition where investors can stack structuring products on top of higher yields <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:06">[01:07:06]</a>. This demand, while seemingly logical, leads to selling volatility and compressing central indexes, creating a "donut" effect where liquidity is pulled out of the system, increasing volatility in "non-Vol centers" but pinning the core <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:17">[01:08:17]</a>.

Historically, in Asia, as volatility and real yields decreased, structured products became increasingly complex, long-dated, and embedded with more "leverage" (non-recourse leverage) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:30">[01:10:30]</a>. The true risk in a big unwind is not merely Delta or Gamma exposure but having to buy back this non-recourse leverage, which includes third and fourth moment risks like Vol of Vol and convexity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:10">[01:11:10]</a>. This led to multi-year structures where the biggest payoffs came from owning deeply out-of-the-money convexity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:01">[01:13:01]</a>.

In such environments, the aim is not to project a probability distribution but a *possibility* distribution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:18">[01:14:18]</a>. The enormous asymmetric impact on pricing comes from the possibility of a "Bayesian conditional shifting of the existing probability distribution" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:40">[01:14:40]</a>.

### Global Implications

The scale of retail-driven structured product activity, particularly in Asia, has historically influenced global market volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:49">[01:15:49]</a>. For instance, Korean retail traders' autocallable products drove volatility not just in Korea but also in Nikkei, Hang Seng, Euro Stoxx, and S&P <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:52">[01:15:52]</a>. The implicit negative asymmetry being taken by these groups is phenomenal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:10">[01:16:10]</a>.

The relationship between interest rate volatility and equity volatility is also counterintuitive; despite a blow-up in interest rates and interest rate volatility, equity volatility has not necessarily gone higher <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:56">[01:19:56]</a>. This highlights a significant bifurcation of volatility across markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:47">[01:19:47]</a>. Understanding these [[understanding_volatility_dynamics_in_investment_strategies | volatility dynamics]] and [[Investment Strategy Divergence | divergences]] is crucial for investors in the current global macro-driven world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:06">[01:21:06]</a>.
