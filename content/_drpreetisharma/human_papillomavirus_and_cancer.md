## Pathogenesis of HPV-Induced Neoplasia

The primary mechanism by which [[hpv_mechanism_and_its_role_in_cancer_development | HPV]] contributes to neoplasia is through the inactivation of tumor suppressor genes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:51:00">[08:51:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:38:00">[13:38:00]</a>.

### Key Viral Proteins

Two crucial [[hpv_mechanism_and_its_role_in_cancer_development | HPV]] proteins are responsible for this oncogenic effect:
*   **E6:** This protein inactivates the p53 tumor suppressor gene, often referred to as the "policeman" of the body <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:11:00">[12:11:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:50:00">[12:50:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:25:00">[13:25:00]</a>.
*   **E7:** This protein inactivates the retinoblastoma (RB) gene, known as the "governor" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:11:00">[12:11:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:50:00">[12:50:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:25:00">[13:25:00]</a>.

The inactivation of these tumor suppressor genes allows for uncontrolled cell proliferation, leading to the development of cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:43:00">[13:43:00]</a>.

### High-Risk HPV Types

Certain [[hpv_mechanism_and_its_role_in_cancer_development | HPV]] types are considered high-risk due to their strong association with cancer development. These include:
*   **HPV 16** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:00:00">[11:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:03:00">[11:03:00]</a>
*   **HPV 18** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:00:00">[11:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:03:00">[11:03:00]</a>

These high-risk types have a greater likelihood of causing [[genital_system_tumors_and_lesions | squamous cell carcinoma]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:12:00">[11:12:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:15:00">[11:15:00]</a>.

## Histological Features

A characteristic cytological finding associated with [[hpv_mechanism_and_its_role_in_cancer_development | HPV]] infection is the **koilocyte** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:04:00">[09:04:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:56:00">[09:56:00]</a>.

### Koilocyte Characteristics
*   **Raisinoid nucleus:** The nucleus appears shrunken and wrinkled, similar to a raisin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:22:00">[09:22:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:51:00">[09:51:00]</a>.
*   **Perinuclear halo:** A distinct clear or white area surrounds the nucleus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:38:00">[09:38:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:43:00">[09:43:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:51:00">[09:51:00]</a>.

The viral proteins E4 and E5 are associated with the morphological changes that characterize koilocytes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:48:00">[11:48:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:56:00">[11:56:00]</a>.

### Differential Diagnosis for Raisinoid Nucleus with Perinuclear Halo
While koilocytes are characteristic of [[hpv_mechanism_and_its_role_in_cancer_development | HPV]], a similar histological appearance (raisinoid nucleus with perinuclear halo) can also be seen in **Chromophobe [[renal_cell_carcinoma_diagnosis_and_pathology | Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)]]** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:05:00">[10:05:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:16:00">[10:16:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:24:00">[10:24:00]</a>.
