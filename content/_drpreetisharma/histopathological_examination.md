Histopathological examination involves the microscopic study of tissues to diagnose disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. It encompasses various techniques, including staining, immunofluorescence, and electron microscopy, to identify characteristic cellular and tissue changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>.

## Types of Necrosis

Different patterns of necrosis offer clues to specific conditions:
*   **Coagulative Necrosis:** Characterized by "ghost cells" and a "tombstone appearance," often seen in myocardial infarction, kidney, or liver tissues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>. A "row of tombstone appearance" can also be observed in pemphigus vulgaris <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **Liquefactive Necrosis:** Commonly seen in the brain following a stroke, presenting as a liquid or fluid-filled area <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>.
*   **Caseous Necrosis:** Appears "cheese-like" and is characteristic of tuberculosis (TB), but can also be seen in fungal infections, such as those caused by *Histoplasma* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:04">[00:03:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.
*   **Fat Necrosis:** Presents as "fatty yellow" areas, often in organs like the breast or omentum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>. It can appear as a "chalky white area" due to calcification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>.
*   **Fibrinoid Necrosis:** Observed in blood vessels as a "pinkish" change. It is associated with conditions like polyarteritis nodosa, rheumatic heart disease, SLE, and hypertension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>.

## Cellular Changes and Inclusions

### Pigments and Deposits
*   **Lipofuscin:** An "old age pigment" appearing as a brown, perinuclear pigment around the nucleus, typically in elderly patients (e.g., an 83-year-old man) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>.
*   **Hemosiderin:** Associated with bleeding, it indicates iron presence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>. The specific stain for iron is Pearls of Prussian Blue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>. Iron overload is known as hemochromatosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>.
*   **Lipids:** Stains include Oil Red O (red) and Sudan Black B (black) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>.
*   **Calcium:** Stains include Von Kossa (black) and Alizarin Red S (red) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>. Alizarin Red S is more sensitive for minute amounts of calcium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>.

### Giant Cells and Granulomas
*   **Langhans Giant Cell:** Horseshoe-shaped nuclei, characteristic of tuberculosis (TB) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:59">[00:06:59]</a>. It is not the same as Langerhans cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.
*   **Touton Giant Cell:** Round nuclei, seen in xanthomas (lipid problems) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.
*   **Foreign Body Giant Cell:** Nuclei are "all here and there," with a history of foreign material like talc powder or sutures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>.
*   **Finkeldey Cell:** Characterized by intranuclear and intracytoplasmic inclusions, seen in measles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:40">[00:07:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a>.
*   **Reed-Sternberg Cells:** Show "red and pinkish eyes" (binucleated or multinucleated appearance), found in Hodgkin's lymphoma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:48">[01:14:48]</a>. Variants include:
    *   **Lacunar RS Cell:** Surrounded by a lacuna (clear space), seen in nodular sclerosis Hodgkin's lymphoma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:55">[01:14:55]</a>.
    *   **Popcorn RS Cell:** Irregularly shaped, seen in nodular lymphocyte predominant Hodgkin's lymphoma (NLPHL) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:07">[01:15:07]</a>.
*   **Owl's Eye Inclusions:** Blue-colored eyes, seen in Cytomegalovirus (CMV) infections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.
*   **Stellate Granuloma:** Star-shaped, seen in cat scratch disease and lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>. LGV can present with painful buboes and a "groove sign" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.
*   **Dürck Granuloma:** Parallel lines, seen in cerebral malaria caused by *Plasmodium falciparum* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:31">[00:09:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.

### Specific Cell Types and Bodies
*   **Gaucher Cells:** Characterized by "crumpled tissue paper appearance," seen in Gaucher's disease (beta-glucocerebrosidase deficiency) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:50">[01:00:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>. Patients may have bone pain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:17">[01:01:17]</a>.
*   **Onion Skin Pattern:** Round, seen in Tay-Sachs disease (alpha-hexosaminidase A deficiency) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:50">[01:04:50]</a>. Tay-Sachs also presents with a "cherry red spot" in the macula <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:19">[01:05:19]</a>.
*   **Zebra Bodies:** Black and white striped bodies, seen in Niemann-Pick disease (sphingomyelinase deficiency) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:25">[01:05:25]</a>. Niemann-Pick disease typically involves splenomegaly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:51">[01:05:51]</a>.
*   **Ninja Star Nucleus:** Distinctive appearance, seen in dilated cardiomyopathy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:45">[01:24:45]</a>.
*   **Helter-Skelter Cardiac Fibers:** Disorganized cardiac fibers, characteristic of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:53">[01:25:53]</a>.
*   **Anitschkow Cells:** Resemble "caterpillars" or "owls," found within Aschoff bodies in rheumatic heart disease (RHD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:51">[01:26:51]</a>.
*   **Spider Cells:** Resemble spider webs, found in cardiac rhabdomyomas (often in the left ventricle) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:26">[01:29:26]</a>.
*   **Signet Ring Cells:** Nucleus pushed to the periphery, characteristic of diffuse gastric cancer (linitis plastica) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:19">[01:32:19]</a>. Also seen in Krukenberg tumors of the ovary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:30">[01:32:30]</a>.
*   **Foamy Cells:** Can indicate intestinal TB (positive ZN stain) or Whipple's disease (PAS-positive in lamina propria) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:05">[01:33:05]</a>.
*   **Rosenthal Fibers:** Pinkish, hair-like fibers, seen in pilocytic astrocytoma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:12">[01:40:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:17">[01:40:17]</a>.
*   **Verocay Bodies:** Areas of palisading nuclei with intervening anucleated zones (Antony A area), seen in schwannomas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:53">[01:43:53]</a>.
*   **Neurofibrillary Tangles:** "Flame cells" made of tau protein, seen in Alzheimer's disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:07">[01:45:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:12">[01:45:12]</a>.
*   **Lewy Bodies:** Round bodies made of alpha-synuclein, seen in Parkinson's disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:47">[01:45:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:51">[01:45:51]</a>.
*   **Bunina Bodies:** Seen in amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:17">[01:46:17]</a>.
*   **Asbestos Bodies:** Dumbbell-shaped, blue (due to iron coating), also called "ferruginous bodies," seen in asbestosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:34">[01:46:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:45">[01:46:45]</a>. Stained with Pearls of Prussian Blue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:56">[01:46:56]</a>.
*   **Kératin Pearls:** Seen in squamous cell carcinoma of the lung <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:07">[01:48:07]</a>.
*   **Azzopardi Effect:** Blue color around blood vessels due to DNA precipitation, seen in small cell carcinoma of the lung <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:09">[01:50:09]</a>.
*   **Reticulocytes:** Immature RBCs with blue dots (RNA remnants), stained with supra-vital stains like New Methylene Blue or Brilliant Cresyl Blue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:50">[01:50:50]</a>.
*   **Pencil-shaped Cells:** Seen in iron deficiency anemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:29">[01:50:29]</a>.
*   **Target Cells (Codocytes):** Cells with a central dot, seen in thalassemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:01">[01:58:01]</a>.
*   **Howell-Jolly Bodies:** Single dots (DNA remnants), seen in megaloblastic anemia or post-splenectomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:52">[01:58:52]</a>.
*   **Cabot Rings:** Figure-of-eight rings, seen in megaloblastic anemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:20">[01:59:20]</a>.
*   **Hypersegmented Neutrophils:** Neutrophils with many lobes, seen in megaloblastic anemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:23">[01:59:23]</a>.
*   **Spherocytes:** Round red blood cells with no central pallor, commonly caused by autoimmune hemolytic anemia (IHA) or hereditary spherocytosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:04:42">[02:04:42]</a>.
*   **Bite Cells:** Cells with a "bite" taken out, often in clusters, seen in G6PD deficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:19">[02:07:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:22">[02:07:22]</a>.
*   **Heinz Bodies:** Inclusions within RBCs, seen in G6PD deficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:24">[02:07:24]</a>. Stained with Crystal Violet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:36">[02:07:36]</a>.
*   **Schistocytes (Fragmented RBCs):** Irregularly shaped, triangular, or helmet-shaped, resulting from fragmentation of RBCs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:03">[02:08:03]</a>.
*   **Auer Rods:** Rod-like inclusions, seen in myeloblasts, positive for myeloperoxidase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:30">[02:13:30]</a>.
*   **PAS (Pink) Blocks:** Pink-colored blocks, seen in lymphoblasts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:49">[02:12:49]</a>. Lymphoblasts are PAS positive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:09">[02:13:09]</a>.
*   **Smudge Cells:** Broken cells, seen in CLL <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:20">[02:13:20]</a>.
*   **Rouleaux Formation:** RBCs stacked like coins, seen in multiple myeloma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:13">[02:17:13]</a>.
*   **Russell Bodies:** Bodies in the cytoplasm of plasma cells, seen in multiple myeloma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:57">[02:17:57]</a>.
*   **Dutcher Bodies:** Bodies inside the nucleus of plasma cells, seen in multiple myeloma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:02">[02:18:02]</a>.
*   **Mott Cells (Mulberry Cells):** Cells with bodies everywhere, seen in multiple myeloma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:06">[02:18:06]</a>.
*   **Flame Cells:** Cells that appear to have "caught fire," contain IgA, seen in multiple myeloma (specifically IgA myeloma) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:11">[02:18:11]</a>.
*   **Rackage Crystalloids:** Seen in Leydig cell tumors or Leydig cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:36">[02:37:36]</a>.
*   **Call-Exner Body:** Tumor cells arranged around pink material, seen in granulosa cell tumors (CD99 positive, coffee bean appearance) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:53">[02:39:53]</a>.
*   **Koilocytes:** Dark nucleus with a perinuclear halo, characteristic of Human Papillomavirus (HPV) infection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:28">[02:06:28]</a>.

## Stains and Microscopy Techniques

*   **Routine Stains:**
    *   **Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E):** Standard stain.
    *   **Perls' Prussian Blue:** Used for iron (hemosiderin, asbestos bodies) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:56">[01:46:56]</a>.
    *   **Oil Red O and Sudan Black B:** Used for lipids <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.
    *   **Von Kossa and Alizarin Red S:** Used for calcium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>.
    *   **Congo Red:** Used for amyloid deposits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:30">[01:07:30]</a>. On a polarizing microscope, it shows an "apple green birefringence" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:41">[01:07:41]</a>. On light microscopy, it appears "salmon pink" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:48">[01:07:48]</a>.
    *   **Verhoeff-Van Gieson (VVG) Stain:** Used for elastic fibers, seen in temporal arteritis where there is fragmentation of the internal elastic lamina <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:43">[01:08:43]</a>.
    *   **Alcian Blue:** Used to stain acidic mucin, found in goblet cells of intestinal columnar epithelium, characteristic of Barrett's esophagus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:58">[01:30:58]</a>.
    *   **Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS) Stain:** Used for glycogen and mucin. Positive for Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesions in diabetic nephropathy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:15">[01:52:15]</a>.
    *   **Crystal Violet:** Used to stain Heinz bodies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:36">[02:07:36]</a>.
    *   **India Ink / Nigrosin:** Negative stains used for the capsule of *Cryptococcus neoformans* in CSF samples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:55">[01:38:55]</a>.
    *   **Muz Carmine:** Used to stain *Cryptococcus neoformans* in biopsy samples (e.g., brain tissue post-mortem), giving a reddish color <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:09">[01:39:09]</a>.
    *   **Nigella Agar:** Culture media for *Cryptococcus neoformans*, producing brown colonies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:14">[01:39:14]</a>.
    *   **Gomori Methenamine Silver (GMS):** Black stain used for fungus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:53">[02:08:53]</a>.
    *   **PAS Stain (Fungus):** Pink stain used for fungus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:12">[02:09:12]</a>.
    *   **Calcofluor White:** Fluorescent stain for fungus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:17">[02:09:17]</a>.
    *   **Lactophenol Cotton Blue (LPCB):** Used for fungal morphology from agar culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:35">[02:09:35]</a>.
    *   **Flocks tttt stain:** Used for Dursan patterned bodies in molluscum contagiosum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:04">[02:06:04]</a>.
    *   **Seller's Stain:** Special stain for Negri bodies in rabies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:56">[01:59:56]</a>.

*   **Microscopy Types:**
    *   **Light Microscope:** Uses G-banding (Giemsa) for karyotyping <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:33">[01:12:33]</a>. Fixative is 10% Neutral Buffered Formalin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:47">[01:13:47]</a>.
    *   **Fluorescent Microscope:** Uses Q-banding (Quinacrine) for karyotyping <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:43">[01:12:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:40">[01:24:40]</a>. Used for [[Immunofluorescence and Pathology Imaging Techniques | immunofluorescence]] patterns like ANA (Homogenous, Peripheral, Nucleolar, Speckled, Centromeric) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:46">[01:46:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:47">[01:49:47]</a>.
    *   **Electron Microscope (EM):** Used for detailed ultrastructural analysis, like amyloid fibrils (7.5-10 nm diameter) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:57">[01:17:57]</a>. Fixative is 2-2.5% glutaraldehyde <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:53">[01:13:53]</a>.
    *   **Dark Field Microscope:** Used for thin, hard-to-see organisms like spirochetes (e.g., *Treponema pallidum* in syphilis) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:17">[01:14:17]</a>.

## Genetic and Molecular Techniques
*   **Karyotyping:**
    *   Chromosomes are arrested in metaphase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:26">[01:12:26]</a>.
    *   **G-banding (Giemsa):** For light microscopy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:36">[01:12:36]</a>.
    *   **Q-banding (Quinacrine):** For fluorescent microscopy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:47">[01:12:47]</a>.
    *   Identifies trisomies: Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome), Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:07">[01:12:07]</a>.
*   **Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH):** Used to detect chromosomal disorders like translocations (e.g., BCR-ABL translocation in CML, t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:16">[01:13:16]</a>. It detects monosomies and translocations but *not* point mutations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:49">[01:13:49]</a>.
*   **Comparative Genomic Hybridization (CGH):** Detects gains (red color) and losses (green/below color) in genetic material, with yellow indicating normal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:05">[01:14:05]</a>.

## Organ-Specific Histopathology

### Cardiovascular System
*   **Myocardial Infarction (MI):**
    *   **Light Microscopy:** Earliest changes include wavy cardiac fibers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:29">[01:29:29]</a>.
    *   **Gross Diagnosis:** Tetrazolium chloride (TPTC) stain shows a pale area for MI, while normal heart tissue is brick red <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:39">[01:29:39]</a>. TPTC stains lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:02">[01:30:02]</a>.
    *   **Reperfusion Injury:** Characterized by "contraction band necrosis" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:18">[01:31:18]</a>.
*   **Cardiomyopathies:**
    *   **Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM):** Features "Ninja star nucleus" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:45">[01:24:45]</a>. Most common cause is idiopathic, followed by alcohol <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:57">[01:24:57]</a>. Genetic cause: titin gene mutation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:07">[01:25:07]</a>.
    *   **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM):** Associated with "banana-shaped heart" on gross exam and "helter-skelter cardiac fibers" on microscopy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:41">[01:25:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:56">[01:25:56]</a>. Often seen in sudden death of young athletes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:38">[01:25:38]</a>.
    *   **Restrictive Cardiomyopathy:** Most common cause is amyloidosis, characterized by "pink material outside the myocytes" which is Congo red positive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:07">[01:26:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:21">[01:26:21]</a>.
*   **Endocarditis:**
    *   **Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD):** Presents with small, "warty vegetations," Aschoff bodies, and Anitschkow cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:33">[01:26:33]</a>. Chronic RHD can cause a "fish mouth mitral valve" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:24">[01:27:24]</a>.
    *   **Infective Endocarditis:** Characterized by "large and bulky vegetations" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:54">[01:27:54]</a>.
    *   **Non-Bacterial Thrombotic Endocarditis (NBTE):** Small, friable vegetations prone to embolization, often associated with hypercoagulable states (e.g., adenocarcinoma) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:01">[01:28:01]</a>.
    *   **Libman-Sacks Endocarditis:** Seen in SLE, characterized by vegetations on both upper and lower valve surfaces, predominantly on the lower surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:34">[01:28:34]</a>.
*   **Cardiac Tumors:**
    *   **Myxoma:** Most common primary cardiac tumor in the left atrium, characterized by "lepidic cells" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:08">[01:29:08]</a>.
    *   **Rhabdomyoma:** Most common primary cardiac tumor in children, often in the left ventricle, characterized by "spider cells" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:16">[01:29:16]</a>.

### Gastrointestinal System
*   **Achalasia Cardia:** Radiologically presents as a "bird beak appearance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:06">[01:30:06]</a>. Associated with *Trypanosoma cruzi* infection (Chagas disease), causing megaesophagus and megacolon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:10">[01:30:10]</a>.
*   **Barrett's Esophagus:** Metaplasia from squamous epithelium to intestinal columnar epithelium (with goblet cells) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:35">[01:30:35]</a>. Goblet cells contain acidic mucin, stained by Alcian Blue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:49">[01:30:49]</a>. It is a precursor to adenocarcinoma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:02">[01:31:02]</a>.
*   **Menetrier's Disease:** Grossly, the stomach appears "brain-like" (gyral pattern) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:31">[01:31:31]</a>. More common in males, caused by increased TGF-alpha <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:38">[01:31:38]</a>.
*   **Linitis Plastica (Diffuse Gastric Cancer):** Stomach appears like a "leather bottle," characterized by signet ring cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:05">[01:32:05]</a>. Associated with loss of E-cadherin (CDH1 gene mutation) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:46">[01:32:46]</a>.
*   **Whipple's Disease:** Characterized by PAS-positive foamy cells in the lamina propria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:35">[01:33:35]</a>. (Differential: intestinal TB with ZN-positive foamy cells) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:18">[01:33:18]</a>.
*   **Celiac Disease:** Associated with HLA-DQ2 and DQ8 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:10">[01:34:10]</a>.
