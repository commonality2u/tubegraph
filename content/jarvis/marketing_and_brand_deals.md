## Unusual Brand Partnerships
A notable example of an unconventional [[brands_seeking_clout_and_attention | brand seeking clout and attention]] involved KFC partnering with Lifetime for a movie titled "A Recipe for Seduction" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>. This collaboration was described as "the weirdest brand deal of all time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>, presenting an origin story for Colonel Sanders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. The initial movie poster went viral, leading to widespread discussion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:53">[00:00:53]</a>.

Unlike KFC's previous ill-fated venture into a gaming console that also cooked chicken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:08">[00:01:08]</a>, this Lifetime movie was actually produced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>. Despite its seemingly absurd premise, the movie was "very beautifully shot" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:54">[00:24:54]</a>, and functioned as a 16-minute commercial for KFC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:54">[00:24:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:56">[00:24:56]</a>.

## The Impact of Brand Antics on Consumer Perception
The explicit goal behind such campaigns is to generate conversation and attention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:06">[00:00:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:08">[00:00:08]</a>. The "Recipe for Seduction" movie successfully became a widely discussed topic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:01">[00:25:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>.

Despite the widespread discussion, the success of these tactics in directly influencing consumer purchasing behavior can be ambiguous. In the case of the KFC movie, it was acknowledged that the campaign won in terms of getting people to talk about KFC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:07">[00:25:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:11">[00:25:11]</a>. However, whether it made viewers want to eat KFC chicken was uncertain, especially given that the only character explicitly shown eating chicken in the movie was a "crazy murderer man" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:11">[00:25:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:13">[00:25:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:21">[00:25:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:23">[00:25:23]</a>. This highlights the complex [[the_impact_of_brand_antics_on_consumer_perception | impact of brand antics on consumer perception]] and the potential for unintended associations.

Ultimately, while unconventional brand deals like the KFC/Lifetime movie may not always translate directly into increased sales of the core product, they are often successful as a "marketing ploy" for generating widespread discussion and content, achieving the goal of making people talk about the brand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:01">[00:25:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:05">[00:25:05]</a>.
