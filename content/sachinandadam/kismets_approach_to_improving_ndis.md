## The Challenges of the NDIS

The [[national_disability_insurance_scheme_in_australia | NDIS]], established to support Australia's most vulnerable citizens, is reportedly being affected by around $4 billion in [[fraud_and_inefficiencies_in_the_ndis | fraud annually]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>. Key issues include:
*   **Widespread fraud and misuse of funds** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>.
*   **Low patient satisfaction** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>.
*   **Bureaucratic system** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.
*   **Lack of adequate funding for participants** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
*   **Inefficient payment processing**: The NDIS makes between 300,000 to 500,000 payments daily, second only to Medicare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.

## A Personal Perspective on Disability and the NDIS

The importance of fixing the [[national_disability_insurance_scheme_in_australia | NDIS]] is underscored by personal stories. Aran, who was born with cerebral palsy 22 years ago, highlights the critical role of disability services in people's quality of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:36">[00:01:36]</a>. Aran describes two main challenges of growing up with a disability in Australia:
1.  **Stigmatization from the community** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>.
2.  **Infrastructure challenges**: Inaccessible houses, buildings, and areas that others take for granted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:06">[00:03:06]</a>.

When the [[national_disability_insurance_scheme_in_australia | NDIS]] was introduced, Aran was hopeful for a centralized, government-funded system to acquire necessary equipment for increased mobility and independence, such as his standing wheelchair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>. Despite the NDIS doing "amazing things" for many, it remains a bureaucratic system with communication issues and insufficient funding for many clients <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:03">[00:04:03]</a>.

## Mark Woodland's Journey to Kismet

Mark Woodland, the founder of Kismet, has a diverse background, including 10 years in the Army and founding a childcare software company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:57">[00:04:57]</a>. His motivation for Kismet stems from a deep personal history, including the death of his brother who was killed in a car crash after joining the army <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. This tragedy influenced his decision to join the army himself, completing his brother's path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.

After selling his previous company, Xplor, for nearly $100 million, Mark initially attempted to retire and become a farmer in rural Melbourne <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>. However, a phone call from his mother-in-law, seeking advice on utilizing her mother's aged care budget, revealed a significant administrative problem similar to education but with subtle nuances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:44">[00:10:44]</a>. Realizing he could "connect people with the ones they love while reducing that admin," he decided to launch Kismet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:05">[00:11:05]</a>.

Kismet's inception was also influenced by a broader vision to solve the "biggest aging care population problem," which is how people survive without families and the burden on the "sandwich generation" who care for both older and younger family members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>.

## Kismet's Technological Solution

Kismet operates within the [[national_disability_insurance_scheme_in_australia | NDIS]] ecosystem by addressing administrative burdens for key players:
*   **Participants**: Individuals seeking care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a>.
*   **Support Coordinators**: Organize care for participants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:15">[00:12:15]</a>.
*   **Service Providers**: Offer services like physio or speech therapy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a>.
*   **Plan Managers**: Act as bookkeepers, organizing invoice payments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:33">[00:12:33]</a>.

Kismet's primary goal is to improve healthcare accessibility by reducing administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>. Support coordinators often spend significant time finding available and suitable providers for participants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:57">[00:12:57]</a>. Kismet's system automates this process: support coordinators use a free platform to submit inquiries, and within 24 to 48 hours, they receive a shortlist of providers with both availability and suitability for specific care requirements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:07">[00:13:07]</a>.

According to a Kismet user and support coordinator, Bian from Healthy Emporium, the tool is highly time-effective, reducing hours of searching for providers for participants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>.

## The Role of Technology vs. Policy

While technology can significantly streamline processes, it cannot solve all problems within the [[national_disability_insurance_scheme_in_australia | NDIS]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:44">[0:14:44]</a>. Mark Woodland believes technology, particularly Kismet, can address the "biggest problem," which is administrative burden and lack of connection in care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:46">[00:14:46]</a>. By freeing up individuals from administrative tasks, technology allows them to focus on other areas that require human intervention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:55">[00:14:55]</a>.

There is a call for more [[innovation_in_australian_technology | technology companies]] like Kismet to build solutions in regulated industries, especially healthcare, due to the significant challenges present across the sector <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:07">[00:14:07]</a>. Being a first-mover in such markets offers considerable advantages for growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>.

However, the role of government and policy remains crucial. Technology optimists sometimes overstate technology's capabilities, but policy and regulators play an equally, if not more, important role in areas like:
*   **Allocating funding effectively** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:07">[00:16:07]</a>.
*   **Fixing [[fraud_and_inefficiencies_in_the_ndis | fraud]]** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:09">[00:16:09]</a>.
*   **Ensuring the quality of service providers** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:10">[00:16:10]</a>.
*   **Guaranteeing taxpayer money genuinely improves patient outcomes** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:16">[00:16:16]</a>.
