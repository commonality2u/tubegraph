## The Origins of Grameen Bank and Microfinance
Grameen Bank was established in Bangladesh by Professor Muhammad Yunus, who, along with Grameen Bank, was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006 for his work in [[Microfinance and its impact | microfinance]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:31">[00:31:31]</a>. The core problem Grameen Bank sought to address was the pervasive poverty in Bangladesh, where individuals lacked jobs and couldn't initiate businesses because their impoverished status meant they didn't possess asset collateral to secure traditional loans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:48">[00:31:48]</a>.

### The "Social Collateral" Model
Professor Yunus ingeniously reimagined the lending process, creating a system where five borrowers would form a group, and Grameen Bank would lend to them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:41">[00:32:41]</a>. This model introduced "social collateral," meaning each member was collectively responsible for repaying their individual loans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:43">[00:32:43]</a>. These groups would meet weekly to discuss issues and crowdsource solutions, fostering a supportive environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:00">[00:33:00]</a>. This approach incentivized repayment by building trust and community, rather than relying on punitive measures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:26">[00:32:26]</a>.

## Success and Impact of Grameen Bank
The results of this model were remarkable: women borrowers not only repaid their loans at nearly 100% with interest, but they also became significantly more self-sustaining <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:07">[00:33:07]</a>. This sustainable model allowed the business to fund its own expansion through the interest generated, distinguishing it from a traditional charity reliant solely on grants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:25">[00:33:25]</a>.

*   In Bangladesh, Grameen Bank has issued over $28 billion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:36">[00:33:36]</a> to more than 10 million women <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:38">[00:33:38]</a>, impacting over 44 million people, or about 25% of the country's population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:55">[00:36:55]</a>.
*   The repayment rate is an impressive 97% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:41">[00:33:41]</a>.
*   This model has been replicated in over 50 countries globally, including the United States <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:47">[00:33:47]</a>.
*   In the US, over the past decade, Grameen has issued $1.3 billion in [[Microfinance and its impact | microfinance]] to over 130,000 women, with a 99% repayment rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:10">[00:39:10]</a>.

The speaker notes that [[Microfinance and its impact | microfinance]] has significantly contributed to reducing poverty and positively impacting household income levels in Bangladesh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:30">[00:36:30]</a>.
