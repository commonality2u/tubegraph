Word documents attached to meetings, often containing sensitive information, become accessible through AI, even if users don't realize they were "open" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:00">[03:00:00]</a>.

These issues lead to organizations churning from [[AI and its implications for society | AI]] solutions or not rolling them out at all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:28:00">[03:28:00]</a>. ### Anticipated Security Breaches

The biggest threat vector, especially in countries sensitive to national security, is **insider risk** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:01:00">[05:01:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:11:00">[05:11:00]</a>. This involves contractors or employees, potentially from non-friendly countries, attempting to access and exfiltrate sensitive files <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:02:00">[05:02:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:50:00">[05:50:00]</a>.

The process often involves:
1.  A person becoming a contractor with limited access <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:43:00">[05:43:00]</a>.
2.  Access rights gradually increasing over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:47:00">[05:47:00]</a>.
3.  The individual gaining access to a group that has permissions to sensitive files <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:51:00">[05:51:00]</a>.
4.  Utilizing internal [[AI and its implications for society | AI]] tools to find and then transmit those sensitive files to an international actor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:06:00">[05:06:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:21:00">[05:21:00]</a>.

Governments, particularly in defense, recognize the productivity benefits of [[AI and its implications for society | AI]] but operate with a zero-risk tolerance, leading them to be slower to adopt but willing to pay for robust [[AI security and data permissions challenges | AI security]] solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:42:00">[05:42:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:51:00">[05:51:00]</a>.

## Moats in [[AI security and data permissions challenges | AI Security]]

Traditional "moats" like brand or UX are becoming less relevant in the rapidly evolving [[AI and its implications for society | AI]] landscape, where copying UI/UX is easier than ever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:39:00">[03:39:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:31:00">[04:31:00]</a>.

For Redactive, key "moats" include:
*   **Execution Capability**: The ability to execute more effectively than competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:53:00">[04:53:00]</a>.
*   **Enterprise Understanding & Service**: Deep understanding of enterprise needs and the ability to service them at scale, especially handling large amounts of data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:59:00">[04:59:00]</a>. Scanning millions of documents is a difficult problem in itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:11:00">[04:11:00]</a>.
*   **Intellectual Property**: Proprietary algorithms for computing data abnormalities, which leverage LLMs and embedding models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:00">[04:37:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:00">[04:57:00]</a>.

Valente believes that "moats are a thing of the past" and that success is increasingly about **execution** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:00">[04:47:00]</a>. This is exemplified by the competition between GitHub Co-pilot (owned by Microsoft, which has all traditional moats) and Cursor (a "rapper" product), where Cursor is currently winning by better understanding and supercharging the developer workflow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:31:00">[04:31:00]</a>.
