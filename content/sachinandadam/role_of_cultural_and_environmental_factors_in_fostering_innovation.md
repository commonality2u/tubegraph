From: [[sachinandadam]] <br/> 

Anand Apique, formerly of Sequoia Asia and now managing director at Peak XV, highlights how cultural and environmental factors significantly influence drive, creativity, and success in entrepreneurship and investment. His experiences, from his childhood in India to his career in Silicon Valley and Southeast Asia, illustrate the profound impact of these elements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>.

## Personal Drive and Problem Solving
Apique's core motivation has always been understanding how things work and breaking down complex problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>. This drive, rooted in his computer science background, extends to investing, which he sees as a blend of analytical thinking and creativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>. He believes the best founders also embody this combination of creativity and insatiable curiosity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:18">[00:05:18]</a>.

### Influence of Childhood Environment
Growing up in a relatively poor India, education was the primary path to social mobility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>. This environment of resource constraints fostered creativity and a strong urge to build a better life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. Apique views this early struggle as a "biggest gift" that instilled a drive to achieve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:12">[00:08:12]</a>.

### The Role of Immigrant Mentality
Apique observed in San Francisco that many successful founders were immigrants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>. While not a prerequisite for success, immigrants often bring a powerful drive stemming from a background of not having much and seeking a better life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:52">[00:07:52]</a>. This resonates with his own upbringing and highlights how challenging environments can cultivate strong entrepreneurial spirit.

### India's IIT System
The Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs) are renowned engineering programs in India known for their meritocratic selection process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>. This system creates a community where successful alumni, including leading tech CEOs, act as role models, fostering a "virtuous cycle" of aspiration and ambition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>. This illustrates how a structured educational environment and strong community can significantly influence career trajectories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:26">[00:10:26]</a>.

## Cultivating "Manufactured Luck"
Apique believes that luck can be "manufactured" by positioning oneself in environments with high activity and surrounding oneself with smart, motivated people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>.

### The Bay Area as an Epicenter
San Francisco, particularly the Bay Area, is described as the "epicenter of the startup world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:19">[00:15:19]</a>. Being in this environment facilitates serendipitous connections and the flow of information, which can lead to significant opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:21">[00:15:21]</a>. This exemplifies [[the_influence_of_environment_on_personal_change | how environment can influence personal change]] and success.

### Culture of Creativity and Risk-Taking
The Bay Area fostered a culture of creativity, a "hacker mindset" and "builder mindset" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:07">[00:19:07]</a>. This environment encourages individuals to believe they are "only limited by your own creativity and ambition" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:28">[00:19:28]</a>. Furthermore, being in environments where one feels comfortable taking risks is crucial for success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:50">[00:15:50]</a>. This aligns with [[balancing_risk_and_innovation_in_business_strategy | balancing risk and innovation]] in business strategy. At Stanford, there was a cultural expectation that students would start companies after graduation, reinforcing entrepreneurship as a path to outsized impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:00">[00:20:00]</a>. The absence of such a culture, even with other "raw ingredients" like talent and capital, can hinder output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:21">[00:20:21]</a>.

## Organizational Culture and Long-Term Vision
Apique found that the culture at Sequoia (now Peak XV) was instrumental in his unexpected enjoyment of investing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:48">[00:25:48]</a>.

### Enduring Success and Long-Term Play
The firm's longevity and enduring success are attributed to a culture focused on playing the long game <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:05">[00:26:05]</a>. Decisions are made with a long-term mission in mind, prioritizing long-term value creation over short-term optimization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:30">[00:26:30]</a>. This long-term perspective is reinforced by their non-profit limited partners (LPs), many of whom have existed for centuries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:12">[00:27:12]</a>.

### Fostering Innovation and Growth
The culture at Peak XV embraces "innovating for the long run," "trying new things," and "not being afraid to fail" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:40">[00:27:40]</a>. It emphasizes investing in people for the very long term and believes in compounding knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:45">[00:27:45]</a>. This supportive culture provides a privilege for young professionals to learn from experienced investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:59">[00:27:59]</a>.

### Training Young Talent
The firm's approach to hiring and developing talent is rooted in a long-term perspective, aiming for individuals to view it as their "last job" rather than a temporary one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:44">[00:38:44]</a>. This long-term view fosters an environment where people are given "the ability to make mistakes," learn, and grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:46">[00:39:46]</a>. For accelerated young talent, the key is focus: becoming an expert in a specific area before expanding their career <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:05">[00:41:05]</a>. This strategy mirrors how startups begin with a "wedge" product before expanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:14">[00:41:14]</a>.

## Global Ambitions and Market Dynamics
Founders globally are increasingly ambitious, aiming to build global or regional businesses rather than just being the largest in their local market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:42">[00:34:42]</a>. Peak XV's thesis is to build a venture franchise that supports these global ambitions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:07">[00:35:07]</a>.

### Regional Expansion and Experimentation
The expansion into Southeast Asia by Sequoia (now Peak XV) was considered an "experiment" with the hypothesis that a vibrant entrepreneurial ecosystem existed beyond China and India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:14">[00:33:14]</a>. Singapore was identified as a key hub for this expansion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:30">[00:33:30]</a>.

### Disparity and Opportunity in Fintech
In sectors like fintech, there are significant disparities in service availability and sophistication across markets like Australia and India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:18">[00:45:18]</a>. This disparity presents an "incredible opportunity" for investors who can identify these "white spaces" with a global perspective, bringing markets up to par in terms of access to services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:43">[00:45:43]</a>.
