## Reimagining Path: A Minimalist, Private Social Network

The first major social network idea is a modern take on the defunct social network, Path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:54">[00:21:54]</a>. The core premise is a minimalist social network focused on close friends and real-time location sharing, addressing a perceived gap in today's social media landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:47">[00:19:47]</a>.

### The Problem with Current Social Media & The Inspiration from Path
Current dominant social media platforms like Instagram, Twitter (now X), and Facebook have evolved into broad, public-facing networks that often overcomplicate interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:04">[00:22:04]</a>. The speaker notes that Instagram's status updates are often used by people just to share what city they're in, indicating a desire among friends to know each other's whereabouts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:31">[00:20:31]</a>. While Apple's Find My Friends offers some location sharing, it lacks depth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:05">[00:21:05]</a>.

Path, launched 10-12 years prior, was a private, closed social network designed for a user's closest friends and family, typically limiting connections to around 75 to 150 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:07">[00:22:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:48">[00:24:48]</a>. This limit was based on Dunbar's Number, a sociology theory suggesting humans can only maintain about 150 meaningful connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:00">[00:25:00]</a>. Path allowed users to share their lives through photos, videos, status updates, and locations within this trusted circle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:27">[00:22:27]</a>. Path was considered the "anti-version" of the current state of social networks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:27">[00:25:27]</a> and was eventually sold for around $100 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:33">[00:25:33]</a>.

### The "Dunbar" App Concept
The proposed new social network, tentatively named "Dunbar," would reinvent Path for today's user <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:37">[00:25:37]</a>.

*   **Core Functionality**: A simple feed that automatically updates a user's city location with a single button push <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:21">[00:21:21]</a>. The feed would display friends based on proximity, facilitating impromptu meetups when friends overlap in the same city <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:28">[00:21:28]</a>.
*   **Minimalist Design**: It would be a "micro social network" doing "one thing really well" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:50">[00:19:50]</a>.
*   **Focus on Close Connections**: Like Path, it would be a closed-off network for true friends, addressing the desire for privacy in an increasingly public digital world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:07">[00:22:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:05">[00:32:05]</a>.
*   **Location Features**: Beyond just city, it could leverage more granular location information, like Path's ability to know the user's neighborhood (e.g., "Good morning from Hayes Valley") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:57">[00:28:57]</a>. This concept aligns with [[building_a_minimalist_social_network_based_on_realtime_location_sharing | building a minimalist social network based on realtime location sharing]].
*   **Content Generation**: The app should encourage users to create information or spark conversations, moving away from a passive "lurking" experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:19">[00:28:19]</a>.

### Business Model & Challenges
While [[building_a_social_app_with_ai | building a social app with AI]] is faster and cheaper now than in the past, consumer social startups are still challenging due to the need for network effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:12">[00:24:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:20">[00:27:20]</a>. However, a "Dunbar" app could potentially be monetized via a subscription model (e.g., $20-30/year) or by selling digital sticker packs, similar to how Path once monetized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:42">[00:31:42]</a>. The narrative around a private, anti-mainstream social network could generate significant PR and attract users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:13">[00:26:13]</a>.

### Potential Future Monetization: Personal Data
A more speculative idea suggests a future monetization model where users pay for premium subscriptions by committing anonymized personal data to train large language models (LLMs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:55">[00:32:55]</a>. This data would be anonymized and could be used by companies like Facebook to train their models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:15">[00:33:15]</a>.

## Time Capsules of Shared Memories

A separate, yet related, social idea focuses on sharing "time capsules of shared memories" with friends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:44">[00:34:44]</a>. This idea is inspired by the common experience of losing track of shared photos in digital albums and the powerful human desire for nostalgia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:34">[00:34:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:27">[00:39:27]</a>.

### The Problem: Memories Go to Die
Currently, shared photo albums often become digital graveyards for memories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:34">[00:34:34]</a>. Users find themselves scrolling through old photos for extended periods, trying to rediscover specific moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:25">[00:25:25]</a>. While sharing old photos in group chats can bring joy, these too often become buried <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:01">[00:40:01]</a>.

### The Clout Precedent
The concept of monetizing nostalgia was explored by Clout, a social network from around 2013 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:37">[00:36:37]</a>. Clout connected to users' other social media accounts (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram) and sent reminders of past memories, predating Facebook's built-in memory feature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:49">[00:36:49]</a>. Clout went viral because people loved reliving and sharing these nostalgic moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:50">[00:37:50]</a>. However, it failed to sustain its own network effects, as users primarily visited Clout to find memories and then shared them on other platforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:09">[00:38:09]</a>.

### The Time Capsule Solution
The proposed "time capsule" would be a curated, personal, and ephemeral gift of shared memories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:50">[00:34:50]</a>.

*   **Curated Content**: The sender would curate a collection of photos and videos from a shared experience (e.g., a trip, a dinner party) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:55">[00:34:55]</a>.
*   **Ephemeral Nature**: The time capsule would be designed to be ephemeral, preventing users from feeling obligated to hold onto too many digital gifts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:52">[00:35:52]</a>.
*   **Personal Touch**: This would be a "very personal, high touch" gift, creating a deeper connection than a simple text or digital photo album <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:46">[00:35:46]</a>.
*   **Physical or Digital**: The concept envisions something between a text photo and a physical photo album, like photos printed on a notecard, nicely packaged, and shipped <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:45">[00:38:45]</a>, or a digital equivalent that revives the memory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:39">[00:39:39]</a>.

This idea taps into the consistent human need for nostalgia and connection, suggesting that while products change, fundamental human desires do not <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:09">[00:39:09]</a>.
