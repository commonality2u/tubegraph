## Scaling with the Triple Triple Double Double

A key framework for achieving significant growth is the "triple triple double double" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:12">[00:00:12]</a>. This involves tripling revenue three times, then doubling it twice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. For example, a $3 million business can grow to $9 million, then $27 million, then $54 million, and finally $108 million in five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>. At this stage, a company gains significant optionality, whether for a $100 million exit or continued growth and market domination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>. Aman, an expert in scaling, took AppSumo from $3 million to $84 million using similar strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>. His "sweet spot" is the scale-up phase, from mid-seven figures to $100 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.

## The Nine Steps to Nine Figures Framework

Founders often misunderstand the requirements for scaling a business beyond seven figures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>. The shift involves transitioning from a product-focused founder to someone building a comprehensive company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. The "Nine Steps to Nine Figures" framework outlines three distinct phases:

1.  **Startup Phase**: This phase focuses on achieving product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>. It involves identifying the right **Persona** (who to sell to), defining the **Product** (what to sell), and establishing **Promotion** (how to sell at scale) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>. Product-market fit is achieved when there are more leads than the business can handle, often around mid-seven figures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>.
2.  **Scaleup Phase**: Once product-market fit is established, the focus shifts to company creation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>. This involves:
    *   **Process**: Optimizing the business to be highly automated and software-driven, ideally leveraging [[Impact of AI on Education and Entrepreneurship | AI]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>.
    *   **People**: Building a small team of "A players" who are fun to work with <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>. The idea that "it's people first" is often misleading; a well-optimized business reduces the need for excessive personnel and bureaucracy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.
    *   **Performance**: Managing the team's performance by providing clear "scoreboards," incentives, and compensation packages, acting as a coach to draw up the next play <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:48">[00:06:48]</a>.
3.  **Growup Phase**: The final phase focuses on legacy protection and continued growth beyond $100 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:12">[00:03:12]</a>.

### Incentivizing Performance

The principle "show me the incentive, I'll show you the outcome" highlights the importance of proper compensation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>.

-   **Avoiding Common Mistakes**: Incentivizing solely on profit can lead to excessive cost-cutting without growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>. Conversely, incentivizing only on revenue can lead to spending without profitability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.
-   **Ideal Structure**: A 50/50 split for bonuses, where half is based on top-line revenue and half on bottom-line profitability, creates founder alignment and encourages individuals to consider both growth and profit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>.
-   **Equity vs. Cash**: Harvard research suggests no marked increase in performance from granting equity, largely because employees often struggle to value it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>. A more effective approach is to define a total compensation package (e.g., $400K) and allow the employee to decide how much of the bonus portion (e.g., $300K) they want in cash versus equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. This approach makes the equity feel like a real investment, aligning their interests with the company's long-term success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>.

### Strategic Foundations and Avoiding Churn

Ignoring foundational aspects in the early stages is akin to building a skyscraper on a sandy foundation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:44">[00:14:44]</a>. Companies that scale to mid-seven figures without proper fundamentals often face issues like high churn and increased competition, leading to founder burnout or company collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:05">[00:15:05]</a>.

-   **Loyalty Over Churn**: Instead of obsessing over churn, focus on fostering customer loyalty, measured by **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:31">[00:16:31]</a>. NRR reflects how much existing customer cohorts spend over time, including expansion revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:06">[00:17:06]</a>. Every 3% increase in NRR can double a company's valuation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:43">[00:16:43]</a>.
-   **Retention First**: It's crucial to focus on retention and customer loyalty *before* aggressively pursuing growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:05">[00:18:05]</a>. Growing a business with incredible customer loyalty makes it easier to "throw gasoline on that fire" later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:10">[00:18:10]</a>. Neglecting retention leads to massive churn and unprofitable growth, like trying to fill a leaky ship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:24">[00:18:24]</a>.
-   **Early Customer Feedback**: Your first few customers are often the most loyal and provide the most valuable feedback, making it easier to refine your product and build brand equity before mass promotion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:35">[00:19:35]</a>.

### Growth Channels and Strategic Investment

When considering growth, particularly once retention is solid, a portfolio approach to marketing channels is advisable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:17">[00:21:17]</a>.

-   **80/20 Rule for Marketing**: Allocate 80% of resources to proven marketing channels that consistently produce results (like "index funds") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:31">[00:21:31]</a>.
-   **20% for Experimentation**: Dedicate 20% to exploring new channels or "rocket ships" with high potential (like "Nvidia" or "Bitcoin" in investments) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:44">[00:21:44]</a>. This diversification ensures the business isn't entirely reliant on one channel that might eventually decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:02">[00:22:02]</a>.
-   **Test Then Invest**: For new initiatives, start with small, manual experiments to validate hypotheses before investing heavily in software or scaling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:07">[00:39:07]</a>. For example, AppSumo tested a manual affiliate program with their top sharers before building a full platform, which eventually generated multiple eight figures in revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:42">[00:40:42]</a>.

### Evaluating Acquisitions

Strategic acquisitions require careful consideration using an "Impact vs. Effort Matrix" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:45">[00:24:45]</a>.

-   **Prioritizing Opportunities**: Rank potential acquisitions or initiatives on a scale of 1 to 5 for both effort and potential impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:21">[00:25:21]</a>. Aim for "10 out of 10 ideas" – those requiring low effort (e.g., a 1) but promising massive impact (e.g., a 5) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:26">[00:25:26]</a>.
-   **Avoiding Shiny Object Syndrome**: Founders often chase new, exciting opportunities while neglecting high-ROI improvements within their existing business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:53">[00:25:53]</a>.
-   **Realistic Expectations**: Recognize that high-impact acquisitions often involve significant effort, including due diligence that can take 18 months and still fall apart <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:48">[00:28:48]</a>. Post-acquisition, the real work of integration and growth begins, often involving fixing pre-existing issues like churn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:07">[00:29:07]</a>.

### The Power of Focus and Specialization

Instead of building "strip malls" of multiple, disparate businesses, focus on building "skyscrapers"—deepening loyalty and expanding within a core, winning model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:40">[00:33:40]</a>.

-   **Be the Best**: In most markets, the majority of value flows to the "winner" or "gold medalist" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:13">[00:36:13]</a>. Before entering a market or committing to a focus, ask if you can genuinely be the best in that specific arena <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:00">[00:37:00]</a>.
-   **Deepening Loyalty**: For AppSumo, identifying marketing agencies as their most profitable customer segment led to hyper-niching on software deals for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:22">[00:38:22]</a>. This focus not only increased loyalty and referrals from ideal customers but also indirectly benefited other customer segments by allowing for better deal negotiation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:28">[00:39:28]</a>.
-   **Founders vs. CEOs**: Founders are artists who discover "flight" (product-market fit) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:01">[00:32:01]</a>. CEOs are managers who scale that discovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:29">[00:47:29]</a>. A common mistake is for founders to abandon a successful venture to start the next thing without successfully handing off the baton <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:20">[00:32:20]</a>.
-   **Hiring Strategically**: Founders should hire executives in areas where they have expertise first, like hiring a sales executive if sales is their "zone of genius" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:08">[00:43:08]</a>. This allows them to properly train, evaluate, and ensure greatness in that function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:24">[00:43:24]</a>. The CEO's ultimate goal is to delegate everything off their plate, becoming "the laziest person at the company" by thinking several quarters ahead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:13">[00:46:13]</a>.

### Identifying Your Zone of Genius

To identify your "zone of genius," reflect on where your energy and motivation naturally flow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:41">[00:44:41]</a>.

-   **Calendar Test**: What excites you on your calendar? What tasks do you consistently push off? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:43">[00:44:43]</a>
-   **Tinkering Interests**: What topics do you find yourself researching or "tinkering" with in the morning before meetings, even with an empty calendar? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:51">[00:44:51]</a>
-   **Energy In vs. Energy Out**: Create a simple list of five things that give you energy and five things that drain it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:27">[00:45:27]</a>.

Knowing your zone of genius helps in effective delegation and ensures you spend your time on the most impactful tasks for your business. For early-stage companies (seven figures), the focus should remain on hitting monthly numbers and improving the core business, not on long-term, multi-decade visions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:35">[00:48:35]</a>.
