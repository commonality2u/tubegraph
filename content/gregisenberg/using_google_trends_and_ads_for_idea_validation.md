Validating a startup idea before investing heavily in building a product can significantly de-risk the venture. Justin Mayers, founder of Kettle and Fire, a multi-million dollar bone broth company, shares a framework for how he validated his business idea with minimal investment [00:05:05]. His approach centered on understanding market demand and willingness to pay, utilizing tools like Google Trends and online advertising.

## Kettle and Fire: An Origin Story <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>

In 2014, while working at Rackspace, Justin Mayers sought to start his own company [01:32:00]. Having heard about bone broth's health benefits from friends in CrossFit and his own research into gut, skin, and joint health, he identified a personal need for the product [01:40:00]. At the time, bone broth was not widely discussed or easily found online [01:46:00].

His core questions were: How many people actually want this product, and are there enough of them willing to spend sufficient money to build a meaningful business? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:00">[02:01:00]</a>

## Phase 1: Validating Market Demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:00">[02:17:00]</a>

To assess demand, Justin conducted research at both micro and macro levels:

*   **Micro-level Research** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:30:19">[02:30:19]</a>
    He investigated online communities where his target audience (paleo and CrossFit enthusiasts) congregated, such as Mark's Daily Apple, Paleo Hacks, and [[Using AI tools like Gummy Search to find startup ideas from Reddit | Reddit]] [02:39:00]. He analyzed traffic and engagement on threads discussing bone broth's benefits and preparation methods [02:43:00]. The high level of engagement indicated a small but passionate group of potential customers [02:53:00].
*   **Macro-level Research** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a>
    Justin then utilized [[Leveraging Google Trends for Business Ideas | Google Trends]] to observe the overall interest in bone broth. He noted that the term was "starting to take off," indicating a growing trend [03:19:00].

Combining these insights, he concluded that at least several thousand people were interested in bone broth and looking for the product [03:30:00]. His initial goal was modest: to build a business that could generate $200,000 a year, allowing him and his brother to earn $10,000 each per month [03:42:00]. This would only require a couple of thousand customers spending a few hundred dollars annually [03:55:00].

Justin emphasizes that while data tools are useful, the idea stemmed from solving his own problem and discussing it within his passionate community [04:17:00]. [[Using data and trends to identify startup opportunities | Google Trends]] served as a validation tool to ensure the idea had broader potential beyond a personal side project [04:43:00].

## Phase 2: Validating Willingness to Pay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:02:00">[06:02:00]</a>

The next, crucial step was to determine if people would pay enough to make the business viable [06:05:07]. Justin created a minimal viable test:

1.  **Low-Cost Setup** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:11:02">[06:11:02]</a>
    He purchased the cheapest bone broth domain name he could find for $9.99 [06:11:02] and paid $5 on Fiverr for a basic logo and renders [06:35:00].
2.  **Landing Page Development** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:47:00">[06:47:00]</a>
    Using Unbounce (a tool that existed in 2015), he created a landing page that highlighted value propositions identified from his community research, such as:
    *   Healing leaky gut (a key concern in the paleo community) [07:07:00]
    *   Convenience (addressing the 24-48 hour cooking time for homemade bone broth) [07:11:00]
    *   Quality ingredients (organic, 100% grass-fed bones) [07:22:00]
3.  **Pricing Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:37:00">[07:37:00]</a>
    Despite having no product or production plan, he set a price of $29.99 for 16 ounces, significantly higher than typical grocery store broth ($5 for 16 ounces) [07:47:00]. The logic was that if people paid this premium price for a non-existent product, he could confidently figure out the economics later [07:57:00].
4.  **"Sketchy" Checkout Process** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:09:00">[08:09:00]</a>
    The "Order Bone Broth Now" button redirected customers to his personal PayPal account, asking them to send $29.99 [08:20:23]. This intentionally rudimentary setup was designed to gauge true willingness to pay [08:37:00].
5.  **Advertising with Bing Ads** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:49:00">[08:49:00]</a>
    Justin spent approximately $100 on Bing Ads (which was cheaper than Google Ads at the time) to drive traffic to his landing page [08:51:00].
6.  **Results and Validation** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:18:00">[09:18:00]</a>
    Over a two-week test, the campaign achieved a 30% conversion rate for clicking the order button. Despite the "super sketchy checkout flow" and no actual product, he generated nearly $500 in revenue [09:18:00]. Spending about $250 on ads, this validated the hypothesis that a real product could generate around $100,000 in annual sales [09:39:00].

## Post-Validation and Building the Business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:07:00">[10:07:00]</a>

After validating demand and willingness to pay, Justin took the following steps:

*   **Customer Communication** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:04:02">[11:04:02]</a>
    He emailed every person who had placed an order, explaining there was no product yet. He offered a full refund or a 50% discount if they were willing to wait a few weeks for the product to be developed [11:19:00]. Most customers chose the discount, demonstrating strong interest [11:40:09].
*   **Product Development** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:08:00">[12:08:00]</a>
    It took Justin eight months to figure out how to actually make, scale, and deliver the bone broth product [12:09:00]. This validation process was the foundation for building Kettle and Fire into a "9 figure annual business" [01:18:00].
