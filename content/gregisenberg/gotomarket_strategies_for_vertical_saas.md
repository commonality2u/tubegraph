## Analyzing Go-to-Market for Vertical SaaS
To develop an effective strategy, it's beneficial to study how successful [[vertical_saas_business_model_and_its_opportunities | vertical SaaS]] companies in a specific space have scaled their operations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:51">[00:35:51]</a>. This includes researching how they acquired their first 10, 100, and 1,000 customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:12">[00:36:12]</a>. AI tools like Claude or ChatGPT can assist in this research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:01">[00:36:01]</a>.

Go-to-market can be challenging in many industries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:17">[00:36:17]</a>. Historically, many vertical SaaS industries have required heavy outside sales models, involving "country clubby," sit-down, relationship-building approaches, rather than typical product-led motions where users are acquired through platforms like Product Hunt or email <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:25">[00:36:25]</a>. If founders prefer not to engage in such sales-led models, the go-to-market strategy must be a critical part of their scorecard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:53">[00:36:53]</a>.

Venture capitalists (VCs) generally favor companies with product-led or [[marketing_strategies_for_saas_startups | marketing-led customer acquisition]] due to their faster growth potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:09">[00:37:09]</a>. While sales-led publicly traded companies can achieve significant scale, they typically require substantial average contract values (ACVs) to build large businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:20">[00:37:20]</a>. For faster growth, founders should seek industries where customers can be acquired through product-led or marketing-led approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:40">[00:37:40]</a>.

## Wedge Products as a Go-to-Market Strategy
A "wedge product" is a crucial "get-in-the-door" product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:25">[00:40:25]</a>. The ultimate goal in vertical SaaS is often to become the all-in-one, one-stop solution, but no company starts there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:37">[00:40:37]</a>. Attempting to build an all-in-one solution initially can be viewed as unrealistic by investors, as it requires extensive time and capital, and risks providing only a shallow feature set <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:50">[00:41:08]</a>.

The typical and most effective approach is to identify an area within the industry where operations are currently manual (e.g., paper-based processes) or served by poor existing competition, but are highly important to the customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:17">[00:41:17]</a>. A wedge product should be:
*   **Creative:** Offer a novel solution to an existing problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:27">[00:41:27]</a>.
*   **Easy to Implement:** It should be quick to deploy, avoiding lengthy (e.g., 12-month) implementations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:35">[00:41:35]</a>.
*   **Reputation Prover:** It establishes credibility and trust with the customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:40">[00:41:40]</a>. Most wedge products fail because this aspect is not thoroughly considered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:45">[00:41:45]</a>.

### Case Study: Roofr.com
Roofr.com, a software company for roofing businesses, exemplifies an effective wedge product strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:01">[00:42:01]</a>. Recognizing the fragmented roofing market, they did not begin by building an all-in-one solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:08">[00:42:08]</a>. Instead, they started with a proposal tool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:30">[00:42:30]</a>.

This tool utilized Google Maps to generate real-time, digital roofing quotes, eliminating the need for awkward, in-person estimates with clipboards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:56">[00:42:56]</a>. The tool was free for roofing companies, with Roofr.com taking a percentage of each booked job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:28">[00:43:28]</a>. This allowed them to acquire customers via [[marketing_strategies_for_saas_startups | digital marketing]] (e.g., Instagram, Facebook ads), which is unconventional for home services industries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:40">[00:43:40]</a>.

The success of the proposal tool built trust and spread rapidly among roofing companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>. This established reputation made it significantly easier to sell additional products, such as a CRM, to existing customers, who were then willing to pay a monthly SaaS fee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:56">[00:45:14]</a>. The strategy focused on "following the money" by helping close deals, increasing trust, and then expanding into a full suite of services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:37">[00:46:12]</a>.

## Incorporating Payments for Scale
A crucial strategy for scaling vertical SaaS companies is [[incorporating_payments_and_pricing_strategies_in_vertical_saas | integrating payments]] into the solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:31">[00:46:31]</a>. All major industry-specific software companies eventually own the transaction process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:44">[00:46:50]</a>.

For example, Toast, a restaurant software company, significantly expanded its market size by integrating payments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:38">[00:47:46]</a>. While charging $10/month per restaurant (1 million restaurants in the US) would result in a relatively small annual software market of $120 million, by taking a percentage of the trillion dollars in revenue that restaurants process annually, their addressable market becomes exponentially larger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:01">[00:48:03]</a>.

If a wedge product can be built either before or after the transaction, it positions the company to eventually capture the transaction itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:14">[00:48:21]</a>. This allows vertical SaaS companies to scale immensely quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:09">[00:48:11]</a>.
