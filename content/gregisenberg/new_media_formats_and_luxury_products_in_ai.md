## New Media Formats with AI

Dan Chipper is focused on the future of media, particularly "media that you talk to" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:00">[02:39:00]</a>. He believes that [[Using AI in Content Creation | AI]] creates opportunities for new media formats by making storytelling significantly cheaper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:32:00">[05:32:00]</a>. This doesn't replace professional storytellers but enables storytelling in contexts where it was previously too expensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:40:00">[05:40:00]</a>.

### Advanced Voice Notes
An initial concept involves "advanced voice notes," expanding on tools like ChatGPT's voice mode <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:01">[01:01:01]</a>. The idea is to have an AI-powered voice note that can generate a text summary and allow recipients to interact with the full transcript of the conversation, asking follow-up questions to gain richer context without direct interaction with the sender <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:00">[01:53:00]</a>.

### Interactive Audio Content
This concept extends to interactive audio content. For example, an internal incubation called "tldr" takes meeting recordings and converts them into concise podcast summaries, enabling users to quickly grasp key takeaways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:01:01">[03:01:01]</a>. A prototype of this idea allows listeners to interrupt a podcast and have a conversational AI host respond to questions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:30:00">[03:30:00]</a>. The speaker suggests that listeners should consider how the media they consume can be transformed into two-way interactions with AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:28:00">[04:28:00]</a>.

### Voice-First Interfaces
Voice is becoming a natural interface, especially for younger generations (e.g., 3 to 12-year-olds), who use devices like Apple Watches and are accustomed to interacting with voice assistants like Siri <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:28:00">[28:28:00]</a>. This trend suggests a new "oral culture" where interaction with computers and the environment will increasingly be voice-based <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:31:00">[29:31:00]</a>.

This presents a significant startup opportunity for "voice-first interfaces" or "voice-first versions" of existing services, such as a voice-first Expedia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:35:00">[30:35:00]</a>. Startups have an advantage over larger companies in developing these new AI paradigms because they can take more risks with interfaces without alienating existing users, allowing them to cater to early adopters and gradually scale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:47:00">[33:47:00]</a>.

## Luxury Products in the Age of AI

AI's ability to drastically reduce the cost of content creation can enable a shift towards luxury products. The speaker suggests that when technology makes things cheaper, those things often become luxury status goods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:56:00">[22:56:00]</a>.

### Reimagining Classic Literature
An example of this concept is "new great books," which involves using AI to create new versions of out-of-copyright classic literature (like Plato or Marcus Aurelius) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:11:00">[16:11:00]</a>. These new versions could feature:
*   **Tailored Translations**: Re-translating classic works to be highly engaging and easy to read for specific modern audiences (e.g., a 30-year-old tech professional) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:03:00">[17:03:00]</a>.
*   **Multimodal Formats**: Consuming these stories in audio and video formats, potentially with AI-generated elements and editorial oversight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:56:00">[17:56:00]</a>.
*   **Interactive Features**: Adding "advanced voice mode interactivity" to engage with the content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:24:00">[18:24:00]</a>.

This approach could appeal to students seeking modern alternatives to traditional study aids like SparkNotes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:36:00">[18:36:00]</a>, as well as business and intellectual communities aspiring to engage with the Western Canon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:51:00">[18:51:00]</a>.

### Monetization of Luxury Content
Monetization for these products could involve:
*   One-off access or subscription models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:20:00">[19:20:00]</a>.
*   Creating high-priced, "beautiful physical versions" of books, complemented by digital versions with commentary, audio, or video <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:27:00">[19:27:00]</a>. This suggests a return to books as well-made, luxury goods, rather than just cheap forms of information dispersal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:59:00">[19:59:00]</a>.

The broader principle is to identify what AI is making cheaper and then consider how that thing can become a more expensive, luxury status good for a smaller percentage of the population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:40:00">[23:40:00]</a>. In this context, "human-made" and "handmade" qualities will gain status over [[Impact of AI on Creative Industries | AI-generated]] or mass-produced items <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:09:00">[25:09:00]</a>. This aligns with a growing "anti-AI" sentiment among some demographics, such as college students <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:42:00">[26:42:00]</a>.

## Monetization and Distribution Strategies for AI Products

### Monetization
[[Strategies for monetizing content with AI | Monetization strategies]] for AI products can move beyond traditional subscriptions. One approach is to charge based on the value derived, such as the number of downloads, plays, or listens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:33:00">[07:33:00]</a>. Another emerging trend in AI pricing is charging "per successfully completed task" rather than a monthly fee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:26:00">[08:26:00]</a>.

### Distribution
For [[Business opportunities with AI in video production | distributing AI apps]], a "distribution-first" approach is recommended <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:34:00">[36:34:00]</a>. This involves:
*   **Leveraging Existing Audiences**: Using an established media business or newsletter to reach initial users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:57:00">[34:57:00]</a>. This allows for immediate access to an engaged audience interested in the content or topics covered by the media outlet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:07:00">[35:07:00]</a>.
*   **Building for Oneself**: Developing products that solve personal needs, knowing that a like-minded audience will likely also want them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:16:00">[36:16:00]</a>.
*   **Product-Led Growth**: Once initial users are acquired, strategies like incentivizing sharing (e.g., free access, early feature access) can drive further growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:41:00">[36:41:00]</a>.
*   **Media Distribution Arm**: It is suggested that startups should consider creating their own media arm. While a blog or newsletter might have been effective in the past, a YouTube channel is recommended as a primary growth channel today, funneling viewers into a newsletter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:20:00">[37:20:00]</a>.
*   **Audience Segmentation**: Tools like ConvertKit allow for segmenting audiences (e.g., by video watched or origin) to send targeted app invitations, ensuring the right users are reached to maximize feedback and community building <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:39:00">[39:39:00]</a>.

Ultimately, the goal is not just to acquire users, but to acquire the *right* users who will provide valuable insights and contribute to a strong community <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:51:00">[39:51:00]</a>.
