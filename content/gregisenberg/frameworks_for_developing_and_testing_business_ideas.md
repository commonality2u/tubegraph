## Origin Story of Kettle & Fire

In 2014, Justin Mayers, working at Rackspace, sought to start his own company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>. He had heard about bone broth's benefits from friends in the CrossFit community and through reading, noting its advantages for skin, gut, and joint health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>. At the time, bone broth was not widely discussed or available online <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:46">[00:01:46]</a>. His main questions were:
*   How many people truly want this product? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>
*   Are there enough people willing to spend enough on it to create a meaningful business? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>

## Validating Market Demand

Justin's approach to [[validating_startup_ideas_without_building_them | validating demand]] involved both micro and macro-level research:

### Micro-Validation: Community Engagement
He explored online communities where his target audience (Paleo and CrossFit communities) congregated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. He monitored platforms like Mark's Daily Apple, Paleo Hacks, and Reddit to gauge traffic and engagement on threads discussing bone broth's health benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>. The high engagement, with people discussing recipes, sourcing bones, and cooking times, indicated a "small but fanatic and interested" group <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:53">[00:02:53]</a>.

### Macro-Validation: Google Trends
At the macro level, Justin used [[tools_for_finding_and_analyzing_business_opportunities | Google Trends]] to observe the search popularity of "bone broth." He noticed it was "starting to take off" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>. This combination of micro and macro data suggested that "several thousand people" were interested in the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>.

He clarifies that while some advise against relying on Google Trends, his use of it was to validate a problem he personally experienced and was passionate about, rather than randomly seeking a trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>.

> "Then I just use Google Trends to validate that this would be a decent business as opposed to just like Justin's side project or something like this that doesn't actually go anywhere." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>

## Validating Willingness to Pay

The next crucial step was determining if people would pay enough to make the business viable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>. Justin executed a low-cost, minimal viable product (MVP) validation:

1.  **Fake Product and Landing Page**:
    *   He purchased the cheapest relevant domain, "Bonebroths.com" for $9.99 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>.
    *   A logo was designed for $5 on Fiverr <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>.
    *   A landing page was built using Unbounce (a tool from 2015), highlighting key value propositions like convenience, healing leaky gut, and organic ingredients, derived from Paleo forums <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>.

2.  **Pricing Strategy**:
    *   He set a price of $29.99 for 16 ounces, significantly higher than typical grocery store broth ($5 for 16 ounces) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>. This high price point was designed to ensure profitability if demand existed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>.

3.  **Advertising and "Pre-Sales"**:
    *   He spent about $100 on Bing Ads, which was cheaper than Google Ads at the time, to drive traffic to the landing page <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.
    *   The "Order Bone Broth Now" button directed users to his personal PayPal account for payment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>. This "super sketchy checkout flow" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a> still resulted in conversions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>.

4.  **Results and Post-Purchase Communication**:
    *   Over a two-week test, the site achieved a 30% conversion rate (clicks on "order now") and nearly $500 in revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>. This indicated strong demand even for a non-existent product with a poor user experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:53">[00:09:53]</a>.
    *   After the test, Justin emailed every customer, explaining the product wasn't in stock. He offered a full refund or a 50% discount for those willing to wait for the product (which took about eight months to develop) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>. Most chose the discount, validating their strong interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>.

## Modernizing the Validation Process (2025 Perspective)

If starting Kettle & Fire today, Justin notes several advancements, particularly with [[ai_tools_for_identifying_validated_startup_concepts | AI]]:

*   **Improved Design and Development**: Landing pages and logos could be "much better" using [[ai_tools_for_identifying_validated_startup_concepts | AI tools]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:43">[00:12:43]</a>.
*   **Enhanced Market Research**: [[tools_for_finding_and_analyzing_business_opportunities | AI tools]] can quickly "scrape and get a sense of like sentiment analysis across Reddit," forums, and Twitter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:57">[00:12:57]</a>. This allows for rapid distillation of customer pain points and value propositions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:16">[00:19:16]</a>.
*   **Core Principle Remains**: The fundamental idea of trying to sell a product *before* it exists, even if it means direct messaging potential customers or emailing friends, is still paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:18">[00:13:18]</a>. The goal is to get 10 people to pay, which "will be massively validating" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:31">[00:13:31]</a>.

## Opportunity for Entrepreneurs

When considering [[entrepreneurship_and_startup_ideas | startup ideas]], Justin advises focusing on areas of personal passion and "being on the frontier" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:11">[00:14:11]</a>. For him, this was human health and solving the chronic disease crisis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:48">[00:14:48]</a>.
*   Entrepreneurs should identify big problems with "relatively few people focused on them" or where "more energy moving in that direction" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:07">[00:17:07]</a>.
*   Spending time in an area of genuine interest increases the likelihood of generating "new and interesting and different" ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>.

### Recommended Tools for Today's Entrepreneurs
Justin emphasizes the necessity of leveraging [[ai_startup_ideas_and_incubation | AI tools]] for building and scaling companies, as those who don't will be "left behind" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:15">[00:16:15]</a>.

He mentions:
*   **Bolt.new**: A tool for quickly building landing pages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:00">[00:16:00]</a>.
*   **Rok**: Another tool in this category <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:34">[00:16:34]</a>.
*   **Frontier Models**: Playing with models like Claude, GPT, and Gemini is crucial for becoming a "top 1% user," which offers "high leverage" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:42">[00:16:42]</a>.

The cost of these tools is minimal (e.g., $20/month for advanced GPT features), allowing entrepreneurs to test "dozens of startups a month" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:55">[00:21:55]</a>.

### Impact of AI on Startup Building
[[AI startup ideas | AI's impact]] is profound, lowering barriers to entry. A single individual ("one guy in a basement") can now build products comparable to those that previously required large teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:03">[00:20:03]</a>. This is particularly advantageous for individuals with strong visions, as AI tools enable direct execution of that vision without the coordination challenges of a team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:36">[00:19:36]</a>.

The discussion concludes with an example of using GPT-4o's image generation capabilities to redesign Kettle & Fire as a "functional beverage" product, demonstrating how AI can rapidly generate high-quality visual concepts from minimal prompts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:57">[00:20:57]</a>. This capability drastically reduces the need for expensive design agencies, collapsing costs from tens of thousands of dollars to potentially a few hundred dollars a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:24">[00:24:24]</a>.
