## The Nine Steps to Nine Figures Framework

[[scaling_a_business_beyond_seven_figures | Scaling a business beyond seven figures]] requires a fundamental shift in a founder's role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:53">[02:51:53]</a>. It's a transition from being a product-led founder to building a comprehensive company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:13">[02:57:13]</a>. This framework outlines three distinct phases:

1.  **Startup**: This phase focuses on achieving product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:05:07">[03:05:07]</a>. It involves defining the "Persona" (who you're selling to), the "Product" (what you're selling), and "Promotion" (how you're selling at scale) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:21:49">[03:21:49]</a>. Product-market fit is achieved when you have more leads than you know what to do with <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:44:11">[03:44:11]</a>.
2.  **Scale-up**: This is the phase of company creation, typically occurring around mid-seven figures in revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:08:14">[03:08:14]</a>. It's focused on "Process," "People," and "Performance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37:16">[05:37:16]</a>.
3.  **Grow-up**: The final phase, centered around "Legacy Protection" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:46">[03:11:46]</a>.

### Scaling Growth: The Triple Triple Double Double Rule

For those aiming for a $100 million business in five years, the "triple triple double double" rule is a powerful [[growth_strategies_for_startups | growth strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:14">[00:12:14]</a>. This means tripling revenue for three consecutive years, then doubling it for the next two years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:56">[00:27:56]</a>.

*   Start with a $3 million business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:57">[00:33:57]</a>.
*   Year 1: Triple from $3M to $9M <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:05">[00:35:05]</a>.
*   Year 2: Triple from $9M to $27M <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:25">[00:37:25]</a>.
*   Year 3: Triple from $27M to $81M (The speaker mentions 27 to 54, but mathematically this would be 81 before doubling to 108) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:25">[00:37:25]</a>.
*   Year 4: Double from $27M to $54M (This is where the speaker deviates from a pure triple, triple, double, double sequence to reach 108M) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:59">[00:37:59]</a>.
*   Year 5: Double from $54M to $108M <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:48">[00:40:48]</a>.

This strategy suggests that a $3 million business can reach $108 million in five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:08">[00:43:08]</a>.

## Key Focus Areas for CEOs

### 1. Prioritizing Process Over People

Contrary to common advice, the "people first" mentality can lead to bureaucratic businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:50:33">[05:50:33]</a>. A more effective approach is to build a highly optimized, automated, and software-driven business, leveraging tools like [[ai_startup_incubation_and_strategy | AI]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:04:19">[06:04:19]</a>. The goal is to have a handful of "A players" rather than a large, unwieldy team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:14:48">[06:14:48]</a>.

### 2. Managing Performance Through Incentives

A CEO's role is to provide the "scoreboard" and appropriate incentives for their team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:01:21">[07:01:21]</a>.

*   **Bonus Structure**: An ideal bonus structure is a 50/50 split between top-line revenue growth and bottom-line profitability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:34:52">[09:34:52]</a>. This ensures alignment with the founder's goals, preventing an overemphasis on either cost-cutting or unchecked growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:41:45">[09:41:45]</a>.
*   **Equity vs. Cash**: While equity has been a traditional incentive in venture-backed startups, it often doesn't significantly increase performance because employees struggle to value it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:16:11">[11:16:11]</a>. A more effective approach is to offer a total compensation package and allow the employee to decide how much is cash bonus versus equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:46:11">[11:46:11]</a>. This creates "skin in the game" as the employee actively chooses to "invest" in the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:14:04">[14:14:04]</a>.

### 3. Prioritizing Loyalty (Net Revenue Retention) Over Churn

Many founders focus on preventing churn, but the real focus should be on building loyalty, measured by net revenue retention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:31:39">[16:31:39]</a>.

*   **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: This metric measures not just avoiding churn but how much more revenue you gain from existing customers in a cohort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:38:52">[16:38:52]</a>. Every 3% increase in net revenue retention can double a company's valuation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:45:51">[16:45:51]</a>.
*   **Retention First, Then Growth**: It is crucial to focus on customer retention and loyalty first, before aggressively pursuing growth strategies like promotion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:04:36">[18:04:36]</a>. Trying to grow a "leaky ship" with high churn is unsustainable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:55:01">[18:55:01]</a>. Nailing retention upfront helps build brand equity and creates a movement of loyal customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:50:23">[19:50:23]</a>.

## Strategic Decision Making: Impact vs. Effort

A key skill for a CEO is making effective decisions, particularly regarding opportunities like acquisitions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:36:20">[24:36:20]</a>. The "Shield versus Sword" framework (Impact vs. Effort Matrix) helps in this process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:44:03">[24:44:03]</a>.

*   **Low-hanging Fruit**: Prioritize opportunities that require low effort but have a massive potential impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:10:04">[25:10:04]</a>.
*   **Evaluate All Opportunities**: Compare potential acquisitions or new ventures against existing opportunities within your business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:08:44">[26:08:44]</a>. New, shiny objects can be distractions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:53:36">[25:53:36]</a>.
*   **Due Diligence**: Be wary of complex, high-effort acquisitions that might fall apart or require significant fixing post-purchase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:47:38">[28:47:38]</a>. Many acquired companies have underlying issues like churn that need addressing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:17:34">[29:17:34]</a>.

## Building and Optimizing the Executive Team

There are only two core functions in any business: sales and delivery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:15:39">[42:15:39]</a>. A CEO must identify bottlenecks in these areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:22:36">[42:22:36]</a>.

*   **Identify Your Zone of Genius**: A CEO should hire executives who align with their own "zone of genius" first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:00:54">[43:00:54]</a>. This allows the CEO to properly train and assess their performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:28:49">[43:28:49]</a>. Your zone of genius is where you get excited, where you tinker, and what you naturally gravitate towards when your calendar is empty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:42:37">[44:42:37]</a>.
*   **Strategic Flywheel**: Ensure you have the right executives at each corner point of your strategic flywheel to drive sales and ensure proper delivery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:51:24">[43:51:24]</a>.

## The Ultimate Goal of a CEO: Laziness

The founder is often the hardest-working person in the room, involved in every aspect of the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:09:07">[46:09:07]</a>. However, the ultimate goal for a CEO is to become the "laziest" person at the company, meaning having nothing on their plate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:13:08">[46:13:08]</a>. This allows the CEO to think two, three, four, or five quarters ahead and focus on long-term vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:36:13">[46:36:13]</a>.

### Focus and Domination

It takes courage to focus, especially when [[ai_cofounder_services_for_entrepreneurs | AI]] makes building so simple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:20:23">[35:20:23]</a>. Businesses should strive to be the best in their chosen market or niche <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:10:48">[36:10:48]</a>.

*   **Deep Dive, Not Broad**: Instead of launching multiple new business units (like "strip malls"), focus on expanding deeper within your existing successful business (building "skyscrapers") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:40:08">[33:40:08]</a>. This involves analyzing customer data to identify the most profitable segments and doubling down on what they want <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:11:39">[34:11:39]</a>.
*   **Test Then Invest**: When exploring new initiatives, dedicate about 20% of your money, time, and energy to experiments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:18:24">[40:18:24]</a>. If an experiment shows a "marketed increase," then invest in scaling it with proper resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:07:34">[41:07:34]</a>. The founder's job is to "discover" revenue, and then hire to "scale" it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:40:37">[41:40:37]</a>.

## Why This Transition Matters Early On

Ignoring the principles of company building early in the startup phase is akin to building a skyscraper on a sandy foundation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:48:07">[14:48:07]</a>. Companies that fail to consider these fundamentals often experience issues like churn and increased competition around mid-seven figures, leading to founder burnout or company collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:05:07">[15:05:07]</a>. Even in the early stages, understanding the future phases of growth is crucial for sustainable success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:37:46">[15:37:46]</a>.
