## Introduction to Secured Bonds

For retail investors, options in fixed income have historically been limited, primarily consisting of fixed deposits, corporate bonds, and [[debt_mutual_funds_and_safety_considerations | debt mutual funds]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>. These typically offer yields ranging from 6% to a maximum of 9% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:10">[00:00:10]</a>. The removal of indexation benefits for debt investors spurred a search for alternatives offering higher pre-tax yields, ideally 10-12%, within acceptable risk levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. This search led to an increased interest in [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:46">[00:00:46]</a>.

A bond is essentially a loan, meaning that when an investor subscribes to a company's bond, they effectively become a lender to that company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>. Bonds are broadly classified as either secured or unsecured <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>.

### What are Secured Bonds?

[[introduction_to_senior_secured_bonds | Secured bonds]] are distinct because they are backed by specific collateral pledged by the issuer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>. This collateral is typically a tangible asset, such as property, equipment, inventory, or receivables <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>. It can also be based on future revenues like toll collections or airport fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. For example, a company might secure a bond issuance with a portion of its existing asset fleet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>. This collateral provides an additional layer of security for bondholders, ensuring that in the event of liquidation (e.g., bankruptcy), these pledged assets can be sold to repay this specific group of bondholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>.

The term "senior" in [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] refers to their position in the repayment hierarchy during bankruptcy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:59">[00:02:59]</a>. When a company goes bankrupt, several stakeholders claim their dues, but not all have equal rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>. The repayment order typically prioritizes government dues, followed by secured lenders, then unpaid employee salaries, and finally unsecured creditors, with equity shareholders usually receiving nothing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>. Within secured lenders, [[senior_secured_bonds | senior bondholders]] receive higher priority, meaning their claims are settled first, offering an added layer of protection and a greater chance of recovering their investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>.

## Benefits of Secured Bonds

[[senior_secured_bonds | Senior secured bonds]] offer significant benefits by providing two additional layers of protection compared to unsecured bonds, which rely solely on the issuer's promise to pay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>. These protections are:
1.  **Collateral Backing**: The bond is secured by specific assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>.
2.  **Higher Priority in Liquidation**: In case of default, senior bondholders are prioritized in receiving repayment from the company's assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.

This combination significantly helps in reducing the bondholder's potential loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. Despite carrying lower risk compared to unsecured bonds, [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] can offer attractive yields, currently ranging from 11% to 12% over short tenures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>.

It is important to note that credit ratings are typically assigned to the issuer of the bonds, not individual bonds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>. This means a company's overall rating (e.g., Double B) remains the same whether it issues a secured or unsecured bond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. However, a [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bond]], for practical purposes, behaves more like a lower-risk, higher-rated instrument, even if this isn't explicitly reflected in official ratings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. This disparity presents a "reward-risk arbitrage" opportunity that platforms like Wintealth aim to uncover through their deep understanding of [[risk_management_in_investing | risk]], assets, collateral, and liquidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

## Risk Management in Investing

While [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] offer significant advantages, it's crucial to understand the broader context of [[risk_management_in_investing | risk management]] in bond investments. Returns and [[risk_management_in_investing | risk]] are inherently linked in the evaluation of any debt instrument <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:01">[00:12:01]</a>.

### Role of Online Bond Platforms (OBPs)

Online bond platforms (OBPs) have emerged to make high-yielding fixed income instruments, previously accessible primarily to High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs), available to retail investors at much smaller ticket sizes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:48:00">[07:48:00]</a>. HNIs traditionally have access to options like Category 2 AIFs (10% returns), performing credit funds (13-15%), venture debt (16-17%), and special situation debt (20-22%), among others, often requiring investments in lakhs or crores of rupees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>. OBPs address the limited awareness, lack of information, and restricted access faced by smaller investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>.

Key features of OBPs and their role in [[risk_management_in_investing | risk]]:

*   **Curated Platforms**: OBPs are not open marketplaces. Platforms like Wintealth meticulously evaluate each bond before offering it to investors, resulting in a curated selection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:25">[00:08:25]</a>.
*   **Regulatory Oversight**: All OBPs are registered as stockbrokers within the debt segment, making them subject to SEBI's (Securities and Exchange Board of India) regulatory framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>. This mandates that only listed bonds can be offered, providing an extra layer of security through credit ratings and higher governance standards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:45">[00:08:45]</a>.
*   **Strategic Collateral Selection**: Platforms like Wintealth not only select bond issuers but also "cherry-pick" the collateral pool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. For example, they might specifically choose housing loans, vehicle loans, or gold loans as collateral from an NBFC, aiming to keep the collateral quality high to maximize recovery in case of default <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>.
*   **Co-investment**: Some platforms, like Wintealth, co-invest their own money (around 2% of the issue size) in each bond offered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:48">[00:12:48]</a>. This "skin in the game" adds an extra layer of security for investors, as the platform also bears a loss if the issuer defaults <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:59">[00:12:59]</a>.
*   **Accessibility**: The minimum investment amount on these platforms is typically Rs. 10,000, a significant reduction from the Rs. 1 lakh minimum as recently as 2022, making corporate bond markets more accessible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:42">[00:09:42]</a>.

### Evolving Regulatory Framework

Regulations for online bond platforms have evolved to keep pace with innovation and strengthen investor protection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>.
*   **2022**: SEBI mandated all OBPs to register as stockbrokers, ensuring greater oversight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:10">[00:11:10]</a>.
*   **2023**: SEBI rolled out reforms including mandatory KYC for investors, grievance redressal through the SCORES platform, routing of all orders through stock exchange RFQ platforms, and a complete ban on unlisted debt securities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:20">[00:11:20]</a>.
*   **2024**: SEBI further slashed the minimum investment in debt securities from Rs. 1 lakh to Rs. 10,000 to encourage retail participation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>.

### Specific Bond Risks and Mitigation

While [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] are riskier than bank fixed deposits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:49">[00:13:49]</a>, several strategies can mitigate common bond risks:

*   **Credit Risk (Issuer Default)**: This is the risk that the issuer might default on its obligations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:03">[00:14:03]</a>. To tackle this, investors should opt for bonds structured with both seniority and security, such as [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:09">[00:14:09]</a>.
*   **Interest Rate Risk**: As interest rates rise, the price of existing bonds falls, leading to lower returns if sold before maturity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:16">[00:14:16]</a>. This risk is often seen in [[debt_mutual_funds_and_safety_considerations | debt mutual funds]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>. For individual bonds, this can be avoided by holding them until maturity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>.
*   **Liquidity Risk**: The risk of not being able to find a buyer for the bond when needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:30">[00:14:30]</a>. While platforms like Wintealth generally contend that a buyer can be found and assist investors with liquidation, unforeseen events (like COVID) can impact liquidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:32">[00:14:32]</a>.
*   **Sector Exposure Risk**: If all bond issuers belong to the same sector, a downturn in that sector can affect all investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:44">[00:14:44]</a>. While some platforms might have issuers primarily from a single mega-sector (e.g., NBFCs), these issuers often have diverse underlying businesses (e.g., MSME lending, personal loans, student loans, microfinance) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:58">[00:14:58]</a>.

### Diversification Strategies

A key [[strategies_for_diversifying_bond_investment_risk | strategy for diversifying bond investment risk]] is to **not invest the entire capital into a single bond** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:21">[00:15:21]</a>. Instead, it is advisable to invest across five, six, or even ten different [[senior_secured_bonds | senior secured bonds]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:33">[00:15:33]</a>. This diversification mitigates the risk of losing all money during a default and improves liquidity, as it's unlikely that all bonds would be illiquid simultaneously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:37">[00:15:37]</a>. Developing a deeper understanding of these inherent risks and the [[strategies_for_diversifying_bond_investment_risk | strategies to mitigate]] them is key to building a structured and effective debt investment strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:55">[00:15:55]</a>.
