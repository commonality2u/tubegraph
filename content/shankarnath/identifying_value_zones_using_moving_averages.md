## What is a Moving Average?

A moving average is a continuously calculated average of a data set, such as a stock price or index value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:00">[00:28:00]</a>. Unlike a static average, a moving average changes as new data points are added, with the oldest data point being removed from the calculation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>.

For example, a 5-day moving average for a stock like HDFC Bank would be the average of its prices over the last five trading sessions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. As of September 22, 2023, this value was 1,587 rupees 49 paise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:00">[00:14:00]</a>. Plotting a series of these averages over time creates a smoother line compared to the raw price movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:00">[01:28:00]</a>.

Common moving average periods include 15-day, 20-day, 30-day, 50-day, 100-day, and 200-day moving averages (DMA) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:00">[01:35:00]</a>.

## Why are Moving Averages Used?

The primary reason [[moving_averages_and_their_application_in_stock_markets | moving averages]] are used is that they support the concept of mean reversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. This principle suggests that if an asset's current market price is significantly higher or lower than its moving average (e.g., 50-day or 200-day moving average), it is presumed that the price will eventually revert back to the mean value of these averages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:00">[02:07:00]</a>. This allows investors to potentially take advantage of market euphoria or pessimism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>.

## Identifying Potential Value Zones

A "potential value zone" refers to a specific price range around a [[50_day_moving_average_strategy | 50-day moving average]] where an index value or stock price frequently finds support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:16:00">[03:16:00]</a>.

Historically, observing the Nifty50 index, periods like 2008-2009, 2002-2003, 2012, 2016, and 2020 showed the index dipping into this value zone, which subsequently led to excellent returns for investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:00">[03:25:00]</a>. Similar opportunities have been observed in individual stocks like HDFC Life Insurance, Wipro Limited, and Hero Motorcorp <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:00">[03:54:00]</a>.

### Choosing the Right Moving Average Duration
While a 50-day moving average may only offer a few potential value zones over a long period (e.g., five over 25 years for Nifty50), shorter durations like a 15-day moving average can identify more opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:21:00">[04:21:00]</a>. However, shorter durations come with increased volatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:45:00">[04:45:00]</a>. For indices, it's generally preferred to stay with mid-range durations, while for individual stocks, a 20-day or 30-day moving average might be more suitable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:49:00">[04:49:00]</a>. Different investors and traders customize their approach based on experimentation and testing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:01:00">[05:01:00]</a>.

## Golden Crossover Strategy

The Golden Crossover is a technical analysis strategy that uses two specific [[moving_averages_and_their_application_in_stock_markets | moving averages]]: typically the [[50_day_moving_average_strategy | 50-day moving average]] and the 200-day moving average <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:26:00">[09:26:00]</a>. The objective is to identify potential shifts in a stock's price trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:42:00">[09:42:00]</a>.

A Golden Crossover occurs when the [[50_day_moving_average_strategy | 50-day moving average]] crosses above the 200-day moving average <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:49:00">[09:49:00]</a>. This transition indicates that the short-term price trend is becoming more positive, signaling increased buying interest and a bullish trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:57:00">[09:57:00]</a>. For an investor, this presents a good opportunity to invest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:07:00">[10:07:00]</a>.

**Example**: Infosys has experienced multiple golden crossovers.
*   One in 2018 led to a 54% increase in stock price over two years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:12:00">[10:12:00]</a>.
*   Another in July 2020 resulted in a 150% jump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:23:00">[10:23:00]</a>.

Similar to the 50-DMA strategy, these [[moving_averages_and_their_application_in_stock_markets | moving average strategies]] perform better with individual stocks than with indices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:36:00">[10:36:00]</a>. When applied to the Nifty50, the Golden Crossover strategy yielded an XIRR of 12% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:40:00">[10:40:00]</a>.
