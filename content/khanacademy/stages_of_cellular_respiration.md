[[overview_of_cellular_respiration | Cellular respiration]] is considered the most important biochemical reaction, especially for humans, as it is the primary way we derive energy from the food we consume, particularly glucose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:13">[00:00:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. Most carbohydrates are eventually converted into glucose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. This complex process converts glucose into usable energy for the body, along with some byproducts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>.

## Chemical Equation for Cellular Respiration

The overall chemical reaction for [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] can be summarized as:
Glucose (C6H12O6) + 6 O2 â†’ 6 CO2 + 6 H2O + Energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>.

Specifically, one mole of glucose (C6H12O6) reacts with six moles of molecular oxygen (O2) inside the cell <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. Through this process, it produces six moles of carbon dioxide (CO2), six moles of water (H2O), and significant energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:54">[00:01:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>. This energy powers essential bodily functions like muscle contraction, nerve impulses, growth, and cellular division <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:20">[00:02:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>.

## Energy Production: ATP

The energy produced by [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] is primarily packaged into [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATP (adenosine triphosphate)]] molecules, which serve as the main energy currency for biological systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>. While the process directly produces some energy, this energy is then used to synthesize [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATP]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>.

[[atp_production_through_cellular_respiration | Cellular respiration]] also generates heat as a byproduct, which helps warm the cell and maintain the body's temperature for optimal cellular function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.

Textbooks often state that [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] yields 38 [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] per mole of glucose under ideal conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:20">[00:03:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. However, in reality, the efficiency of the cell means that around 29 to 30 [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] are typically produced, although this number can vary and is still an area of ongoing study <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:05">[00:04:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.

## Three Main Stages of Cellular Respiration

[[overview_of_cellular_respiration | Cellular respiration]] proceeds in three main stages: [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]], the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.

### 1. Glycolysis

[[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | Glycolysis]], meaning "breaking up glucose," is the first stage of [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:36">[00:04:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>. The term "glyco" refers to glucose, and "lysis" means to break up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>.

[[glycolysis_phases_and_process | Glycolysis]] breaks down a 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon molecules called pyruvate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.

[[atp_investment_and_payoff_phases_in_glycolysis | Glycolysis]] involves an initial investment of two [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] but generates four [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]], resulting in a net gain of two [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:32">[00:07:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Oxygen Requirement
> [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | Glycolysis]] is an [[anaerobic_vs_aerobic_processes_in_cellular_respiration | anaerobic process]], meaning it does not require oxygen to occur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>.

In addition to [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATP]], [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]] also produces NADH, which plays a crucial role in later stages of [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:16">[00:12:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>.

### 2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

Following [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]], the pyruvate byproducts are re-engineered and enter the Krebs cycle (also known as the citric acid cycle) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:56">[00:07:56]</a>.

The Krebs cycle directly generates another two [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Oxygen Requirement
> The Krebs cycle is an [[anaerobic_vs_aerobic_processes_in_cellular_respiration | aerobic process]], meaning it requires the presence of oxygen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:38">[00:09:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>.

Like [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]], the Krebs cycle also produces NADH and another similar molecule, FADH, which are crucial for the final stage of [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:16">[00:12:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:07">[00:13:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:12">[00:13:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>.

### 3. Electron Transport Chain

The electron transport chain is the final and most significant stage for [[atp_production_through_cellular_respiration | ATP production]] in [[overview_of_cellular_respiration | cellular respiration]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>. This stage is responsible for producing the vast majority of [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]], typically credited with 34 [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:19">[00:10:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Oxygen Requirement
> The electron transport chain is also an [[anaerobic_vs_aerobic_processes_in_cellular_respiration | aerobic process]], requiring oxygen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>. If oxygen is not available, this chain cannot proceed efficiently, significantly limiting [[atp_production_through_cellular_respiration | energy production]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:26">[00:10:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:34">[00:10:34]</a>.

The NADH and FADH molecules produced in [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]] and the Krebs cycle are what primarily drive the electron transport chain to produce a large amount of [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATP]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:40">[00:12:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:06">[00:13:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:06">[00:14:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:09">[00:14:09]</a>.

## Anaerobic Processes and Fermentation

When there isn't enough oxygen, the products of [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]] cannot enter the aerobic Krebs cycle and electron transport chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:35">[00:10:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:39">[00:10:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:42">[00:10:42]</a>. Instead, they are shunted into a side process called fermentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>.

*   **Alcohol Fermentation**: In some organisms like yeast, fermentation produces alcohol from the byproducts of [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:58">[00:10:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:02">[00:11:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a>.
*   **Lactic Acid Fermentation**: Human muscles, when deprived of sufficient oxygen (e.g., during intense sprinting), undergo lactic acid fermentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:12">[00:11:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:13">[00:11:13]</a>. The buildup of lactic acid can cause muscle soreness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:19">[00:11:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:35">[00:11:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:41">[00:11:41]</a>.

While fermentation provides a small amount of [[atp_production_through_cellular_respiration | energy]] (the two net [[role_of_atp_in_cellular_respiration | ATPs]] from [[glycolysis_and_its_role_in_cellular_respiration | glycolysis]]), it is nowhere near as efficient as [[anaerobic_vs_aerobic_processes_in_cellular_respiration | aerobic respiration]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>. If oxygen is available, the body prefers to continue through the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain to maximize [[atp_production_through_cellular_respiration | ATP production]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:49">[00:11:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a>.
