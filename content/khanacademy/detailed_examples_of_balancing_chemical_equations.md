[[Balancing chemical equations | Balancing chemical equations]] is a fundamental concept taught in first-year chemistry classes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>. While it can be challenging for many students, it is a fairly straightforward process with a bit of an "art" to it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:09">[00:00:09]</a>.

## Understanding Chemical Equations
A [[chemical_reactions_and_equations | chemical equation]] essentially describes a [[chemical_reactions_and_equations | chemical reaction]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>. It shows the [[the_role_of_various_chemical_components_in_equations | reactants]], which are the substances that react, and the products, which are what is formed by the reaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>.

For example, consider the reaction of aluminum with oxygen gas to form aluminum oxide:
Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:24">[00:00:24]</a>

### The Problem: Unbalanced Equations
In the example above, the equation is not balanced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>.
*   On the left side (reactants), there is one atom of aluminum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:45">[00:00:45]</a>.
*   On the right side (products), there are two atoms of aluminum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>.
*   For oxygen, there are two oxygen atoms on the left <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>.
*   On the right, there are three oxygen atoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>.

[[Balancing chemical equations | Balancing equations]] involves adjusting the coefficients (numbers in front of molecules) so that the number of atoms for each element is the same on both sides of the equation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>. This adheres to the law of conservation of mass, meaning atoms are neither created nor destroyed.

## Detailed Examples of Balancing Chemical Equations

### Example 1: Aluminum and Oxygen
**Unbalanced Equation:**
Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃

1.  **Balance Aluminum (Al):**
    *   Left side: 1 Al <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>
    *   Right side: 2 Al <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>
    *   To balance, place a '2' in front of Al on the left:
        **2**Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:36">[00:01:36]</a>

2.  **Balance Oxygen (O):**
    *   Left side: 2 O (in O₂) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>
    *   Right side: 3 O (in Al₂O₃) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>
    *   To get 3 oxygens on the left, multiply O₂ by 1.5 (1.5 x 2 = 3):
        2Al + **1.5**O₂ → Al₂O₃ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>

3.  **Eliminate Fractions ([[Whole number convention in chemical equations | Whole Number Convention]]):**
    *   Chemical equations should not have fractions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. Multiply the entire equation by the smallest whole number that clears the fraction (in this case, 2):
        (2Al + 1.5O₂ → Al₂O₃) * 2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:20">[00:02:20]</a>
        **4**Al + **3**O₂ → **2**Al₂O₃ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>

> **Important Note:** You cannot change the subscripts within a molecule (e.g., changing Al₂O₃ to Al₂O₂). This would change the chemical identity of the substance. Only the coefficients (numbers in front of molecules) can be changed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>.

### Example 2: Methane Combustion
**Unbalanced Equation:**
C₂H₄(g) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + H₂O(l) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>

*   **Strategy**: Balance the more complicated molecules first, and leave single-atom molecules (like O₂) for last, as they are easier to adjust without affecting other elements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.

1.  **Balance Carbon (C):**
    *   Left side (C₂H₄): 2 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:39">[00:04:39]</a>
    *   Right side (CO₂): 1 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>
    *   Place a '2' in front of CO₂:
        C₂H₄ + O₂ → **2**CO₂ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>

2.  **Balance Hydrogen (H):**
    *   Left side (C₂H₄): 4 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>
    *   Right side (H₂O): 2 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:18">[00:05:18]</a>
    *   Place a '2' in front of H₂O:
        C₂H₄ + O₂ → 2CO₂ + **2**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>

3.  **Balance Oxygen (O):**
    *   Left side (O₂): 2 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>
    *   Right side:
        *   From 2CO₂: 2 * 2 = 4 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>
        *   From 2H₂O: 2 * 1 = 2 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:44">[00:05:44]</a>
        *   Total right side: 4 + 2 = 6 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>
    *   To get 6 oxygens on the left, place a '3' in front of O₂:
        C₂H₄ + **3**O₂ → 2CO₂ + 2H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>

**Balanced Equation:**
C₂H₄(g) + 3O₂(g) → 2CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>

### Example 3: Ethane Combustion
**Unbalanced Equation:**
C₂H₆ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>

1.  **Balance Carbon (C):**
    *   Left side (C₂H₆): 2 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>
    *   Right side (CO₂): 1 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>
    *   Place a '2' in front of CO₂:
        C₂H₆ + O₂ → **2**CO₂ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:09">[00:07:09]</a>

2.  **Balance Hydrogen (H):**
    *   Left side (C₂H₆): 6 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:18">[00:07:18]</a>
    *   Right side (H₂O): 2 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>
    *   Place a '3' in front of H₂O:
        C₂H₆ + O₂ → 2CO₂ + **3**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:23">[00:07:23]</a>

3.  **Balance Oxygen (O):**
    *   Left side (O₂): 2 O
    *   Right side:
        *   From 2CO₂: 2 * 2 = 4 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:40">[00:07:40]</a>
        *   From 3H₂O: 3 * 1 = 3 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:52">[00:07:52]</a>
        *   Total right side: 4 + 3 = 7 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>
    *   To get 7 oxygens on the left, multiply O₂ by 3.5 (3.5 x 2 = 7):
        C₂H₆ + **3.5**O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>

4.  **Eliminate Fractions:**
    *   Multiply the entire equation by 2 to remove the 3.5 coefficient:
        (C₂H₆ + 3.5O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O) * 2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>
        **2**C₂H₆ + **7**O₂ → **4**CO₂ + **6**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>

**Balanced Equation Check:**
*   **Carbon:** Left: 2 * 2 = 4 C. Right: 4 * 1 = 4 C. (Balanced) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:23">[00:09:23]</a>
*   **Hydrogen:** Left: 2 * 6 = 12 H. Right: 6 * 2 = 12 H. (Balanced) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:29">[00:09:29]</a>
*   **Oxygen:** Left: 7 * 2 = 14 O. Right: (4 * 2) + (6 * 1) = 8 + 6 = 14 O. (Balanced) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>

### Example 4: Iron Oxide and Sulfuric Acid (Using Substitution)
**Unbalanced Equation:**
Fe₂O₃ + H₂SO₄ → Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>

*   **Strategy**: When a polyatomic ion (like SO₄) remains intact on both sides of the equation, you can treat it as a single unit or "pseudo-atom" to simplify the balancing process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>. Let's substitute SO₄ with 'X' <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:44">[00:10:44]</a>.

**Equation with Substitution:**
Fe₂O₃ + H₂X → Fe₂X₃ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>

1.  **Balance Iron (Fe):**
    *   Left side (Fe₂O₃): 2 Fe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:36">[00:11:36]</a>
    *   Right side (Fe₂X₃): 2 Fe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>
    *   Iron is already balanced.

2.  **Balance Oxygen (O) - *outside* the sulfate group:**
    *   Left side (Fe₂O₃): 3 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:47">[00:11:47]</a>
    *   Right side (H₂O): 1 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>
    *   Place a '3' in front of H₂O:
        Fe₂O₃ + H₂X → Fe₂X₃ + **3**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a>

3.  **Balance Hydrogen (H):**
    *   Left side (H₂X): 2 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>
    *   Right side (3H₂O): 3 * 2 = 6 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:37">[00:12:37]</a>
    *   Place a '3' in front of H₂X:
        Fe₂O₃ + **3**H₂X → Fe₂X₃ + 3H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>

4.  **Balance Sulfate (X):**
    *   Left side (3H₂X): 3 X <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:52">[00:12:52]</a>
    *   Right side (Fe₂X₃): 3 X <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:57">[00:12:57]</a>
    *   Sulfate is already balanced.

**Un-substitute 'X' back to SO₄:**
**1**Fe₂O₃ + **3**H₂SO₄ → **1**Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + **3**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>

### Example 5: Carbon Dioxide and Hydrogen
**Unbalanced Equation:**
CO₂ + H₂ → CH₄ + H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:43">[00:13:43]</a>

1.  **Balance Carbon (C):**
    *   Left side (CO₂): 1 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:47">[00:13:47]</a>
    *   Right side (CH₄): 1 C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:51">[00:13:51]</a>
    *   Carbon is already balanced.

2.  **Balance Oxygen (O):**
    *   Left side (CO₂): 2 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>
    *   Right side (H₂O): 1 O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:56">[00:13:56]</a>
    *   Place a '2' in front of H₂O:
        CO₂ + H₂ → CH₄ + **2**H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:58">[00:13:58]</a>

3.  **Balance Hydrogen (H):**
    *   Left side (H₂): 2 H
    *   Right side:
        *   From CH₄: 4 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:11">[00:14:11]</a>
        *   From 2H₂O: 2 * 2 = 4 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:11">[00:14:11]</a>
        *   Total right side: 4 + 4 = 8 H <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:14">[00:14:14]</a>
    *   To get 8 hydrogens on the left, place a '4' in front of H₂:
        CO₂ + **4**H₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:18">[00:14:18]</a>

**Balanced Equation:**
CO₂ + 4H₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>
