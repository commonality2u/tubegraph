## Economic Repercussions

The pandemic has resulted in significant [[economic_repercussions_and_inequality_from_covid19 | economic repercussions]] worldwide. The OECD projected a fall in world GDP of 4.5% in 2020 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:57">[00:13:57]</a>. This contraction is "unbelievable" and will take years to recover <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:42">[00:27:42]</a>.

Beyond GDP, the crisis has:
*   Led to people losing wages and jobs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:56">[00:06:56]</a>.
*   Caused "enormous budgetary deficits" due to extraordinary spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:51">[00:27:51]</a>. Governments have directed trillions of dollars towards supporting households and companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>. Serbia, for example, invested about 13% of its GDP to support the economy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:01">[00:31:01]</a>.
*   Exacerbated underlying issues like inequalities and economic insecurity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:51">[00:15:51]</a>. There is concern that the gap between rich and poor countries, and within societies, will further increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:27">[00:33:27]</a>. Labor-intensive, investment-driven economies will suffer more, while [[innovation_strategies_during_covid19_recovery | innovation]]-based, knowledge-based societies will suffer less <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:25">[00:34:25]</a>.
*   Disrupted global value chains for essential goods, raising issues of supply security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:55">[00:18:55]</a>. Countries will want to move production closer to home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:07">[00:34:07]</a>.

## Government Responses and Recovery Strategies

### Key Lessons from the Crisis
*   **Speed of Reaction** Prompt action was crucial during the first wave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>. Latvia reacted quickly, implementing restrictions before its first COVID-19 death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>.
*   **Societal Cooperation** Getting society to work *with* government, not against it, is vital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. Governments cannot go faster than society is willing to follow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>. This cooperation is built on trust, competence, integrity, openness, and fairness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:05">[00:18:05]</a>.
*   **Adaptation and Flexibility** Governments have shown it's possible to work in more flexible ways and try new things under difficult circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:30">[00:15:30]</a>.

### Strategies for the Future
To be effective for a [[postpandemic_government_challenges_and_strategies | post-COVID-19 world]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>, governments need to build a more flexible, anticipatory, and [[resilience_and_innovation_in_pandemic_response | resilient]] state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>. This means the recovery must be:
*   **Green and Sustainable** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:54">[00:53:54]</a>. The crisis has also highlighted the urgency of combating climate change, which is the "most important intergenerational responsibility" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:44">[00:16:44]</a>.
*   **Inclusive** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:03">[00:54:03]</a>, putting people first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:06">[00:54:06]</a>.
*   **Resilient** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:13">[00:54:13]</a>, more resistant to future shocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:16">[00:54:16]</a>.

### The Role of Digitalization and Innovation
A key strength for governments is to "go digital" and [[innovation_strategies_during_covid19_recovery | innovate]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:32">[00:44:32]</a>. Digitalization increases efficiency, transparency, and accessibility of services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:54">[00:29:54]</a>. Serbia's experience demonstrates this:
*   **E-Government** Serbia prioritized e-government, providing many services fully electronically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:06">[00:30:06]</a>. This ensures services never stopped during the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:59">[00:29:59]</a>.
*   **Digital Education** Investments in digital education meant that schools physically closed but online schooling began immediately, preventing any loss of schooling days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:24">[00:30:24]</a>.
*   **Economic Resilience** The ICT sector grew to 6% of Serbia's GDP and did not fall during the crisis, contributing to a projected GDP decline of only -1% (or -0.7%) for 2020 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:03">[00:32:03]</a>. Unemployment did not increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:14">[00:32:14]</a>.
*   **Combating Corruption and Improving Efficiency** Digitalization can fight corruption by cutting physical contacts, making processes transparent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:49">[00:47:49]</a>. For example, Serbia moved from a difficult and corrupt construction permitting process to being among the top 10 countries globally for speed and efficiency through full electronic implementation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:26">[00:48:26]</a>.
*   **Environmental Benefits** Digital government can save paper, trees, water, and electricity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:27">[00:50:27]</a>. Since June 2017, Serbia saved 15,000 trees, 65 million liters of water, and 5,700 megawatts of electricity through digitalization efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:21">[00:50:21]</a>.
*   **"My First Salary" Program** This program addresses youth unemployment by providing government-paid salary support for companies hiring young people for their first job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:10">[00:51:10]</a>. The program is fully digital, end-to-end, and uses artificial intelligence for matchmaking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:47">[00:51:47]</a>.

Governments need to consider:
*   **Vaccine Distribution** While promising news on vaccines exists, logistical difficulties, such as cold chain requirements (-70Â°C storage), present huge challenges for widespread distribution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>. The challenge also lies in ensuring people are willing to be vaccinated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:06">[01:00:06]</a>.
*   **Global Cooperation** As a global crisis, the pandemic necessitates global approaches, international cooperation, and multilateral collaboration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:38">[00:41:38]</a>. Vaccines should be treated as a public good and made available based on needs worldwide <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:32">[00:42:32]</a>.
*   **Individual Freedoms and Civil Liberties** The pandemic has profoundly changed the perception and understanding of individual freedoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:06">[00:58:06]</a>. Governments must carefully navigate mandatory measures like mask-wearing, social distancing, and contact tracing, balancing public health with civil liberties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:30">[01:00:30]</a>. This balance will remain a worldwide problem [[postcrisis_strategies_and_future_preparedness | post-pandemic]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:12">[01:01:12]</a>.
*   **Use of Artificial Intelligence in Government** AI can automate repetitive bureaucratic tasks (e.g., issuing permits), increasing productivity and efficiency, and facilitating 24/7 service provision without physical contact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:17">[00:46:17]</a>.
