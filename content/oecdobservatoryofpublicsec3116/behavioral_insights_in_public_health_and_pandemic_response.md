The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the critical role of human behavior in slowing the spread of the virus. Adaptations such as hand washing, mask-wearing, and working from home became essential new behaviors in 2020 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>. This reliance on behavioral change brought [[behavioral_insights | behavioral scientists]] to the forefront, working alongside epidemiologists and public health specialists <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>.

This article explores the lessons learned during the crisis and how it will reshape the influence of [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] on [[behavioral_public_policy | public policy]], particularly in rebuilding and shaping post-pandemic recovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.

## Current State of Behavioral Science in Different Countries

The global panel provided insights into their respective regions:

*   **South Africa**: Currently at lockdown level one, with most of the economy open and a curfew of midnight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:12">[00:05:12]</a>. The country has seen around 750,000 cases since March, with a good recovery rate of nearly 93% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>. While the Western Cape experienced a long, flat peak, other provinces had a high, sharp peak, straining the healthcare system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>. [[behavioral_insights | Behavioral science]] is a relatively new field in South Africa, but the Western Cape government has been applying it for almost eight years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:48">[00:14:48]</a>. The pandemic deliberately pushed [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] to the forefront due to the necessity of behavior change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:01">[00:15:01]</a>.
*   **Australia**: The situation is currently very good, with under 100 active cases in a country of 25 million, mostly from returning citizens in quarantine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>. Restrictions are minimal, with large public gatherings occurring <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>. Australia has an active and growing community of behavioral scientists, including about a dozen teams in the federal government, like BETA (Behavioral Economics Team of the Australian Government) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>. The application of [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] has been seen across various policy areas, and teams have been busier than ever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.
*   **France**: Currently in a second lockdown since October 30th, expected to last until December 1st <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>. This lockdown is less stringent than the first, with schools open and some people going to work. There are around 24,000 new cases and 400 casualties per day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>. The French [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] team was heavily involved in communication and clarifying information during the first lockdown <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>.
*   **Qatar and Lebanon**: These two countries present distinct case studies due to differing institutional capacities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>.
    *   **Qatar**: Possessed resources for effective tracing and isolation, even without imposing a curfew, leading to declining numbers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>. Qatar was the first in the region to announce a large number of infections, triggering a wave of transparency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>.
    *   **Lebanon**: Experiencing a "total disintegration of government role," with NGOs and IGOs filling the gap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:06">[00:12:06]</a>. The country is in a two-week lockdown amid a second wave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:22">[00:12:22]</a>.

### Key Insights from the Region:
A recent study in the region revealed three main insights regarding compliance with preventive measures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>:
1.  **Risk Perceptions**: Higher perception of COVID-19 risk leads to greater compliance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:50">[00:12:50]</a>.
2.  **Hard-Handed Measures**: Most effective when implemented fully and over a short period, rather than gradually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a>.
3.  **Fatigue**: The longer people uphold preventive measures, the less likely they are to fully comply, contributing to second waves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:12">[00:13:12]</a>.

A poll showed an overwhelming response that the crisis is making [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] more important <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>.
*   **Amara Martinez** believes this is because COVID-19 is fundamentally about changing behavior, bringing [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] to the forefront <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:12">[00:14:12]</a>. While not always explicitly named, behavior change principles are used in many facets of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:19">[00:15:19]</a>. The potential of [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] is significant, particularly in large-scale events like pandemics, similar to its role in tackling Cape Town's water crisis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:17">[00:16:17]</a>.
*   **Simon Gordon** argues that [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] was always important, as it has been a key method for involving the public in [[behavioral_public_policy | public policy]] and testing what works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:52">[00:16:52]</a>. However, the *perception* and *prevalence* of [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] have clearly shifted due to COVID-19, with increased public awareness and interest in understanding behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:28">[00:17:28]</a>. This has led to greater application and higher expectations for the field <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:46">[00:17:46]</a>.

## Lessons Learned from the Crisis for Behavioral Scientists

### What Worked Well:
*   **Clarifying Information**: The focus on making complex, evolving information as clear as possible was crucial. This involved careful timing, appropriate institutions, and trusted messengers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:21">[00:20:21]</a>.
*   **Cross-Sector Collaboration**: The level of collaboration between academics, government, and sometimes the private sector was "amazing" and unprecedented, driven by a shared focus on combating the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:10">[00:25:10]</a>. This is seen as a lasting legacy for [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] teams in government worldwide <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:22">[00:25:22]</a>.
*   **Rapid Learning and Flexibility**: Teams had to quickly grasp unfamiliar concepts and pivot from traditional, long-term methods (like 18-month RCTs) to providing advice in an hour <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:57">[00:25:57]</a>. [[behavioral_insights | Behavioral insights]] teams demonstrated strong flexibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:13">[00:26:13]</a>.
*   **Strong Research Networks**: Close ties with researchers allowed for quick mobilization of experts and sharing of insights, which is vital as BI teams need significant support in delivering content to policymakers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:58">[00:22:58]</a>.

### What Needs to Be Left Behind:
*   **Fixed Mindsets and Risk Aversion**: People expressed a desire to move beyond rigid thinking and embrace more adaptable approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:04">[00:19:04]</a>.
*   **Delaying for Ideal Conditions**: The pandemic showed that waiting for "perfect conditions" for experiments or interventions limits relevance and timeliness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:12">[00:43:12]</a>. Policymakers need quick, evidence-based answers, not results in 3-12 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:52">[00:43:52]</a>.

### What Needs to Be Done Differently:
*   **Resource Allocation**: Small teams, like in France, had all resources dedicated to immediate, pragmatic operational requests. It's suggested to split resources, with one responsive, agile team and another anticipating long-term problems and working on experimentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:56">[00:23:56]</a>.
*   **Balancing Information Load**: While transparency is crucial, there's a paradox: citizens need information to act, but too much information can lead to cognitive overload <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:52">[00:20:52]</a>. Better use of infographics and visual information is needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:13">[00:21:13]</a>.
*   **Improving Scientific Communication**: Scientific communication needs to be clearer, especially concerning uncertainty. People need to accept and understand that scientific findings evolve with new evidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:13">[00:22:13]</a>.

## The Evolving Role of Behavioral Science in Recovery

Initially, [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] focused on encouraging isolation and confinement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:17">[00:28:17]</a>. As economies reopen and a "new normal" emerges, the role has shifted to informing safe reopening and helping people survive while staying safe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:54">[00:28:54]</a>. This has led to "amazing innovation in nudges" like ringtones, messenger effects, and in-supermarket designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:13">[00:29:13]</a>.

The field is now better positioned as a *facilitator* for implementing expert-driven solutions, rather than overstepping into health science recommendations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:55">[00:29:55]</a>. Its core role is to make habits stick or desist using evidence, experimentation, and a [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] lens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:18">[00:30:18]</a>.

### Areas for Impact in Recovery:
*   **Beyond Economic Recovery**: Focus on "social fabric recovery," addressing mental health, well-being, dignity, food security, early childhood development, and gender-based violence, which are often secondary impacts of the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:15">[00:32:15]</a>.
*   **Mainstreaming Behavioral Science**: Integrate [[behavioral_insights | behavioral science]] into how policy problems are tackled, not just for specific behaviors. It should become a "behavioral lens" for government work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:50">[00:35:50]</a>.
*   **Complex Policy Problems**: [[behavioral_insights | Behavioral science]] can be applied to complex issues like gender-based violence and parental engagement, not just simple nudges like queueing in supermarkets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:57">[00:34:57]</a>.

## Empirical Evidence and New Methods

Running rigorous experiments like Randomized Control Trials (RCTs) is challenging during a crisis due to limited time and resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:08">[00:38:08]</a>.

*   **Adapting Experimentation**: Settle for smaller pilots and quasi-experimental evaluation methods for rapid assessment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:14">[00:38:14]</a>.
*   **Experimental Surveys**: Regularly conduct surveys (e.g., knowledge, attitudes, perception surveys) but add an experimental angle to test interventions without affecting survey validity. For example, testing how dynamic social norms can increase trust in online mental health services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:28">[00:40:28]</a>. These can inform communication strategies in real-time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:22">[00:41:22]</a>.
*   **Avoiding "Old-School" Limitations**:
    *   Do not wait for ideal conditions for experiments; timeliness and relevance are crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:23">[00:43:23]</a>.
    *   Understand that behavioral interventions are one tool among many, alongside policy responses or new regulations. The trick is to know which problem warrants which intervention at a given time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:30">[00:44:30]</a>.
    *   Adopt a multi-pronged approach to problem-solving, acknowledging that one problem rarely has a single answer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:08">[00:46:08]</a>.

### Wish List for International Collaboration (e.g., OECD):
*   **Networks and Communities**: Continue supporting groups where [[behavioral_insights | behavioral insights]] practitioners can exchange best practices and share difficulties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:19">[00:49:19]</a>.
*   **"What Works" Center**: Build a resource center for best practices during crises, covering compliance to health measures, communication strategies, and trusted messengers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:51">[00:49:51]</a>.
*   **Q&A Platforms**: Create platforms where practitioners can ask questions and receive answers from experts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:12">[00:50:12]</a>.
*   **Evidence Labeling/Indicators**: Develop a scoring system for the quality of evidence supporting recommendations (e.g., based on surveys, interviews, RCTs). This would set clear expectations for policymakers on the rigor and robustness of advice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:28">[00:51:28]</a>.
*   **Pre-registration of Trials**: Encourage the BI community to pre-register trials to assess the accuracy of intuitions and predictions regarding intervention outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:26">[00:52:26]</a>.

*   **Capacity Building**: Building bottom-up capacity within government, NGOs, and with implementation partners, particularly in developing countries, will be a major focus, with academia playing a key role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:01">[00:56:01]</a>.
*   **Integration with New Technologies**: Incorporating artificial intelligence and big data will elevate the field, but ethical considerations, including pre-registration and ethical standards, are paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:51">[00:56:51]</a>.

## Tackling Fatigue and Misinformation

### Combating Fatigue:
*   **Constant Reminders**: Utilize consistent reminders, salient messages, and effective messengers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:16">[01:00:16]</a>.
*   **Emphasize Harm to Others**: Communicate not only the harm to oneself but also to loved ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:26">[01:00:26]</a>.
*   **Provide Options**: Give people clear options for what they *can* do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:36">[01:00:36]</a>.
*   **Rapid Pilot Testing**: Continuously innovate and quickly pilot interventions to see what works and what doesn't, given that behaviors are constantly changing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:26">[01:01:26]</a>.

### Fighting Misinformation and Conspiracy Theories:
*   **Scientific Literacy**: Tackle the root of conspiracy theories by teaching scientific literacy in schools <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:46">[01:02:46]</a>.
*   **Understanding Persuasion Asymmetry**: Recognize that planting a seed of doubt for a conspiracy theory requires little effort, while countering it with certainty requires much greater effort and active behavior from the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:07">[01:04:07]</a>.
*   **Brandolini's Law**: Be aware that the effort needed to counter wrong information is significantly greater than the effort to create it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:41">[01:04:41]</a>.
*   **Clear Explanations**: Spend more time explaining things clearly, using trusted sources like scientists and doctors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:13">[01:05:13]</a>.
*   **Inoculation Theory**: Work on "psychological vaccines" against fake news and vaccine hesitancy, potentially through games <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:13">[01:06:13]</a>.
