## The OECD "Mind Shift Green Lift" Report

The OECD report "Mind Shift Green Lift: Six Behavioral Science Trends for Environmental Policy" reflects the organization's commitment to assisting governments in tackling climate challenges more effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. The report highlights how [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] can help governments in shaping better policies by understanding how people make decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>. This approach is particularly relevant for [[behavioral science and demandside policies | demand-side strategies]], which are gaining increasing importance in policy agendas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>.

### Key Insights from OECD Work

*   **Human Behavior is Central:** Achieving a green, low-carbon future requires changing the behaviors of individuals, businesses, and governments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>. [[behavioral science in environmental policy | Behavioral science]] offers valuable insights into encouraging these changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>.
*   **Emissions Reduction Potential:** Shifting behaviors in key areas like transport, food consumption, and energy use could reduce greenhouse gas emissions by up to 70% by 2050 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.
*   **Overcoming Barriers:** The OECD survey on Environmental Policy and Individual Behavior (EPIC) reveals that availability, affordability, and convenience of sustainable options are crucial drivers of sustainable behaviors at the household level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>. For instance, policies promoting electric vehicles need to be accompanied by infrastructure investments like accessible charging stations and affordable public transport alternatives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.
*   **Integration into Policy:** [[incorporating_behavioral_science_in_government_and_industry | Behavioral science must be integrated into policymaking]] to design more effective strategies that drive lasting change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>.

### OECD Net Zero Plus Project

The OECD is undertaking a multi-disciplinary project called "Net Zero Plus" to address complex climate challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>. This project aims to deliver a rapid, just, and resilient transition to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions while enhancing resilience to climate impacts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>.

Key policy papers under this project include:
*   **Demand-side policies for climate change mitigation:** This paper draws on work from the IEA and OECD, offering insights into [[behavioral science and demandside policies | demand-side policies]] in sectors such as energy use in buildings, transport, waste, and diets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>. Tools like green defaults, social influences, and choice architecture have proven effective in fostering sustainable behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.
*   **Role of the government-citizen interface in driving climate action:** This paper explores how governments can engage citizens more effectively by drawing on OECD's work on trusting government, citizen participation, public communication, and [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
*   **Positive Tipping Points:** This work stream investigates how social, economic, and political tipping points, when triggered, can lead to systemic shifts and accelerate emission reductions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>. Behavioral mechanisms, such as choice architecture influencing consumer/producer behavior and social norms amplifying change, play a pivotal role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:56">[00:07:56]</a>.

## Six [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | Behavioral Science Trends]] for Environmental Policy

The "Mind Shift Green Lift" report identifies six key trends illustrating how governments are harnessing [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to accelerate the green transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:18">[00:15:18]</a>. These trends are based on an analysis of over 100 case studies from more than 30 countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:45">[00:12:45]</a>.

### 1. Informing Policies at Every Stage of the Policy Cycle
Traditionally, [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] was often applied late in the policy process, mainly to fine-tune existing policies or improve communications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:29">[00:18:29]</a>. However, analysis shows that policymakers are now using [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] at every stage, including at the very start, to better understand behavioral challenges and design smarter, more effective policies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:47">[00:18:47]</a>.

*   **Example (France):** The French interministerial directorate for public transformation developed a repairability index for electronic products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:11">[00:19:11]</a>. They applied [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to analyze why 62% of consumers discarded broken devices without considering repair, and then designed and tested labels to empower consumers to make informed choices about repairability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:46">[00:19:46]</a>. This led to consumers opting for higher repairability scores and manufacturers introducing more repairable laptops <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:11">[00:20:11]</a>.

### 2. Driving Climate Adaptation Efforts
While [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] traditionally focused on climate mitigation, it is now playing a larger role in [[role_of_behavioral_science_in_climate_adaptation | climate adaptation]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:56">[00:20:56]</a>. Adaptation involves adjusting behaviors to deal with immediate and long-term effects of climate change, such as preventing wildfires or guiding actions during floods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:11">[00:21:11]</a>. Effective adaptation policies must be grounded in how people actually behave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a>.

*   **Example (Australia):** During a severe drought (2017-2019), the Australian government's Behavioral Economics Team (BETA) identified barriers to farmers seeking assistance, such as complicated application processes and stigma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:11">[00:22:11]</a>. BETA recommended reframing support with [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to encourage farmers to seek help before crisis points, and establishing trusted local points of contact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:45">[00:23:45]</a>.

### 3. Predicting and Accounting for Public Acceptability of Green Reforms
Governments are increasingly using [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to predict and account for public acceptability of green reforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:04">[00:24:04]</a>. Understanding how people perceive and respond to policies is critical for building trust and designing clearer, more accessible policies aligned with public values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:30">[00:24:30]</a>.

*   **Example (Netherlands):** The Dutch Ministry of Economic Affairs and Climate Policy conducted research (surveys, focus groups, interviews, online experiments) to understand behavioral drivers and barriers affecting small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in investing in sustainability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:21">[00:25:21]</a>. Findings revealed that while 64% of SMEs were inclined to be sustainable, only 5-18% took feasible steps due to perceived high costs and other priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:44">[00:26:44]</a>. This informed concrete, tailored policy approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:04">[00:27:04]</a>.

### 4. Boosting Citizen Participation in Environmental Policies
Governments and civic society actors are using [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to proactively and inclusively engage citizens in shaping and implementing green policies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:29">[00:27:29]</a>. This helps mobilize diverse groups and create more opportunities for public involvement, addressing the "trust gap" identified by OECD surveys <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:09">[00:28:09]</a>.

*   **Example (Ireland):** The National Dialogue on Climate Action in Ireland, led by the Department of Environment, Climate and Communications, engages citizens directly to raise awareness, motivate action, and empower sustainable behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:58">[00:28:58]</a>. Since 2021, over 12,000 citizens have participated in annual climate conversations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:10">[00:29:10]</a>. This program's research supports studies addressing climate challenges and has led to a behavioral insights dashboard for policy design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:54">[00:29:54]</a>.

### 5. Increasing Demand for Sustainable Goods, Services, and Technologies
Governments are increasingly using [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to drive demand for sustainable goods, services, and technologies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:35">[00:30:35]</a>. [[behavioral science and demandside policies | Demand-side policies]] are essential, with potential to reduce emissions by as much as 70% by 2050 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:47">[00:30:47]</a>. This includes designing labels, removing barriers to green services (e.g., public transport), and encouraging adoption of green technologies (e.g., heat pumps) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:26">[00:31:26]</a>.

*   **Example (Denmark):** The Danish Competition and Consumer Authority tested two types of climate labels for food to guide consumers toward sustainable choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:54">[00:31:54]</a>. While a "scale label" improved consumers' ability to identify climate-friendly alternatives, it did not significantly change overall purchasing behavior, suggesting the need for clearer, more specific information and broader consumer engagement/education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:44">[00:33:44]</a>.

### 6. Building Resilience to Climate Misinformation
Governments are leveraging [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] to build resilience against and counter the spread of climate misinformation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:58">[00:33:58]</a>. This involves understanding susceptibility to misinformation and developing strategies to counter its impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:18">[00:34:18]</a>.

*   **Example (Canada):** Canada's PARKA initiative (Program of Applied Research on Climate Action) conducts surveys and studies to assess the public's susceptibility to climate misinformation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:55">[00:34:55]</a>. A randomized controlled trial tested interventions to boost resilience, finding that enhancing digital media literacy and providing active preemptive refutations of false claims were most effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:16">[00:36:16]</a>. Interestingly, passive information about finding misinformation made people more skeptical and less accurate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:49">[00:36:49]</a>.

## Moving from Insights to Action

To accelerate the green transition, the report encourages governments to move from insights to action by:
1.  **Harnessing behavioral science to trigger positive social tipping points:** Identifying critical moments and behaviors that can spark large-scale, lasting change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:50">[00:37:50]</a>.
2.  **Driving demand for sustainable choices:** Using [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] tools like default options and dynamic social norms to drive widespread adoption of sustainable behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:51">[00:38:51]</a>.
3.  **Engaging citizens as active participants:** Empowering people as agents of change by reducing participation barriers, building trust, and aligning policies with public expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:15">[00:39:15]</a>.
4.  **Strengthening decision-making within governments:** Integrating [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] into governmental decision-making processes for environmental policies to reduce biases and improve effectiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:33">[00:39:33]</a>.

### Important Caveats
*   **Shared Responsibility:** The burden of responsibility should not be placed entirely on individuals. Instead, consumers, users, and citizens should be empowered as active participants in the green transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:12">[00:40:12]</a>.
*   **Not a Silver Bullet:** [[behavioral science in environmental policy | Behavioral science]] is not a single solution. While behavioral change is vital, it is one piece of a broader strategy that also includes technology and systemic changes for meaningful progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:35">[00:40:35]</a>.
*   **Broad Definition of Demand-Side Policies:** In this context, the demand-side approach is not just about individual choices; it includes the power of governments and industries to drive systematic change, such as through procurement efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:20">[00:41:20]</a>. Real impacts demand collaboration across all sectors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:12">[00:41:12]</a>.

## Discussion on Adaptation vs. Mitigation

There is a concern that more attention and financial investment are shifting towards [[role of behavioral science in climate adaptation | climate adaptation]] rather than mitigation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:50">[01:24:50]</a>. Adaptation policies may be easier to gain support for due to their local, quicker, and more visible effects, appealing to politicians focused on immediate constituent needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:17">[01:25:17]</a>. This raises concerns about whether focusing on adaptation might divert resources and efforts from crucial mitigation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:37">[01:25:37]</a>.

While adaptation is often seen as more acceptable, even by skeptical politicians, it is crucial for the [[behavioral science in environmental policy | behavioral science]] community to examine potential backfiring effects of adaptation-prompting behaviors on mitigation efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:08">[01:27:08]</a>. There should be a clear hierarchy: first avoiding emissions (mitigation), then adapting to unavoidable changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:09">[01:28:09]</a>.
