## Overview
The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the critical need for effective data governance and the strategic use of data by governments to strengthen resilience and design sustainable recovery strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:12">[01:11:12]</a>. While governments have shown an ability to act faster than previously thought, the full potential of data as a key asset has not always been realized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:08">[01:05:08]</a>. Achieving better outcomes requires a whole ecosystem approach, bringing together different powers of the state, representatives from the economy, society, and academia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:18">[02:18:18]</a>. This emphasizes the importance of [[publicprivate_sector_collaboration | public-private sector collaboration]] and [[partnerships_between_public_sector_and_private_sector_for_innovation | partnerships between the public sector and private sector for innovation]] to leverage data for public good <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:30:00">[05:30:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:33:14">[05:33:14]</a>.

## Lessons from the Pandemic
During the initial months of the pandemic, governments globally demonstrated remarkable digital transformation, moving at a rapid rate to harness data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:20:00">[06:20:00]</a>. This included understanding health hotspots, tracking medical supply needs, and deploying first responders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:36:00">[06:36:00]</a>. The shift to vaccine distribution and management has further intensified [[partnerships in public private innovation | public-private partnerships]] involving the third sector and research institutions to unleash healthcare innovation at an unprecedented rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:48:00">[06:48:00]</a>.

Key successes noted include:
*   **Rapid Development**: Governments proved they could develop solutions in weeks, not months or years, when faced with urgent imperatives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:30:00">[09:30:00]</a>.
*   **Increased Collaboration**: The crisis fostered more collaboration and use of data across sectors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:18:00">[07:18:00]</a>.
*   **Streamlined Data Sharing**: Processes for data sharing agreements, which typically take months, were accelerated to weeks for critical services like shielding vulnerable citizens, while still adhering to data protection and ethics frameworks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:15:00">[15:15:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:53:00">[15:53:00]</a>. This demonstrated that with a clear sense of purpose, shared objectives, and understanding of the legal and ethical landscape, work can proceed at pace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:22:00">[16:22:00]</a>.

### Singapore's Experience
Singapore's government, having invested in critical digital infrastructure five to seven years prior, was well-positioned to respond to the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This foresight included establishing a government data architecture focused on single sources of truth, cleaning data, and creating "trusted centers" for data preservation and maintenance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:52:00">[09:52:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:56:00">[09:56:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>.
*   **API Gateway**: The "Apex" data exchange, built four years ago, served as a data gateway with nearly 3,000 datasets and 600 million calls per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This infrastructure allowed for rapid data pulling from different agencies when needed during the pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>.
*   **Contact Tracing**: Singapore developed the TraceTogether app in four weeks, which, along with a physical token, achieved 60% population coverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:27:00">[09:27:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:12:00">[10:12:00]</a>. This significantly reduced contact tracing time from five to six days to less than two days, allowing for quick isolation and cutting transmission chains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:32:00">[10:32:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:38:00">[10:38:00]</a>.
*   **Data Pervasiveness and Privacy**: The success hinged on the pervasiveness of data, while ensuring data privacy by storing data on devices and only downloading it upon positive COVID-19 contact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:54:00">[10:54:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:24:00">[11:24:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:32:00">[11:32:00]</a>.

## Challenges and Solutions
Despite successes, several challenges hinder effective [[publicprivate_partnerships_in_technology_and_innovation | public-private partnerships in technology and innovation]] for data sharing:
*   **Cultural and Administrative Barriers**: In some countries, governments faced cultural or administrative barriers to releasing open data on health issues and adopting civil society proposals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:06:00">[19:06:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:09:00">[20:09:00]</a>.
*   **Lack of Trust**: A lack of transparent data and contradictory information can lead to public mistrust and the spread of misinformation and conspiracy theories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:22:00">[22:22:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:50:00">[22:50:00]</a>.
*   **Data Quality and Silos**: Many government departments collect duplicate and inconsistent data, making it difficult to identify a single source of truth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:37:00">[36:37:00]</a>. Bureaucracy and siloed operations remain significant issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>.
*   **Human Infrastructure**: A critical missing element is a "human infrastructure" or a professional role within the private sector capable of responding to data solicitations from the public sector and navigating the complexities of data sharing agreements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:10:00">[55:10:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:40:00">[55:40:00]</a>.

To overcome these:
*   **Formulating Clear Questions**: Governments need to become more sophisticated in formulating specific questions for which data could provide answers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:00:00">[31:00:00]</a>. This clarity helps engage data holders from the private sector and civil society in a more focused manner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:21:00">[31:21:00]</a>.
*   **Public Engagement and Trust**: Engaging the public in conversations around data reuse, possibly through citizens' assemblies, can help establish [[building_trust_in_publicprivate_partnerships | trust]] and encourage citizens to become data providers when necessary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:06:00">[33:06:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:01:00">[34:01:00]</a>.
*   **Standardization and Interoperability**: Focusing on data standards and quality, along with the interoperability of systems, allows data to flow appropriately across different government entities and sectors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:35:00">[42:35:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:46:00">[42:46:00]</a>.
*   **Professionalization of Data Stewardship**: Creating roles like "Chief Data Stewards" within the private sector, distinct from Chief Data Officers, could accelerate [[public_sector_data_use_and_transformation | data collaboration]] by managing external data relationships and ensuring responsible data reuse for public interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:54:00">[56:54:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:12:00">[57:12:00]</a>. Training civil servants on how to use data and ask the right questions is also crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="38:56:00">[38:56:00]</a>.
