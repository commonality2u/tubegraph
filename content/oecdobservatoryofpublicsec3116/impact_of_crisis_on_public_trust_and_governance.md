## Public Trust and its Role in Crisis Response

A critical element influencing the ability to drive and sustain change is [[Public trust and governance | public trust]] in government <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>. The question of whether citizens will [[Public trust and governance | trust government]], especially a larger government, is paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>. Following the 2008 financial crisis, [[Public trust and governance | trust in government]] institutions significantly declined across many developed nations, though there has been a steady recovery in some countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:34">[00:12:34]</a>. The long-term impact on [[Public trust and governance | citizen perceptions]] of government performance due to the COVID-19 response remains to be seen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:48">[00:12:48]</a>. The level of [[Public trust and governance | public trust]] directly affects the government's capacity to respond to future crises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:07">[00:49:07]</a>.

## Historical Barriers to Public Sector Innovation

Prior to the pandemic, several long-standing barriers hindered innovation in the public sector:
*   **Bandwidth Constraints** Large, hierarchical public organizations often lack the capacity to adopt new technologies or implement consistent reform programs, being frequently bombarded with immediate demands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:17">[00:19:17]</a>. This is partly a function of the political environment, which makes it difficult to focus on key priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:50">[00:19:50]</a>.
*   **Vested Interests** Reform often involves challenging existing ways of doing things, potentially affecting the economic interests of certain groups or making life more difficult for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:01">[00:20:01]</a>. These groups can resist necessary changes that would benefit the broader public <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:10">[00:20:10]</a>.
*   **Risk Aversion** There is a pervasive risk aversion in the public service, partly due to the public scrutiny and accountability mechanisms (e.g., parliamentary committees, inquiries) that can result from mistakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:36">[00:20:36]</a>. This often fosters a "gotcha" culture rather than a learning culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:05">[00:21:05]</a>.
*   **Lack of Entrepreneurial Mindset** Pre-crisis, a bureaucratic mindset often meant that if something couldn't be done, it wasn't questioned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:20">[00:24:20]</a>. The crisis revealed that many perceived barriers were merely habits, not legal restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:32">[00:24:32]</a>.
*   **Slow-Moving Communication and Silos** Traditional public administration relied on slow, territorial communication and reporting, and operated in silos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>.
*   **Lack of Sustained Attention** Even with an understanding of the need for advanced planning and risk management, sustaining interest and funding for contingency planning over long periods is challenging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:25">[00:28:25]</a>.

## Crisis as a Catalyst for Change and Innovation

The COVID-19 crisis, though devastating, acted as a catalyst, pushing many long-called-for changes over the line <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>.
*   **Breaking Down Barriers** The crisis enabled the removal of many barriers and resistors to reform <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:29">[00:21:29]</a>.
*   **Political and Leadership Push** In countries like Finland, a strong push from government and political leadership provided the necessary "kick" to open the floodgates for changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:32">[00:22:32]</a>.
*   **Entrepreneurial Mindset** The crisis fostered an entrepreneurial mindset where bureaucrats questioned "why not" instead of "cannot be done" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:18">[00:24:18]</a>. This led to a cultural shift away from risk aversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:43">[00:24:43]</a>.
*   **Clear Direction and Autonomy** Public servants felt a clearer sense of purpose, receiving clear directions and greater autonomy to make decisions at the front line, which motivated them as part of a "team sport" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:53">[00:24:53]</a>.
*   **Collaboration and Agility** The crisis fostered greater collaboration across policy areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>, broke down silos, and replaced slow, territorial communication with fast decision-making by small, dynamic teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>. Governments reorganized into more agile task forces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:46">[00:25:46]</a>.
*   **Citizen Engagement** The crisis encouraged greater engagement of stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a> and forced a focus on user experience and meeting target populations where they are <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:20">[00:18:20]</a>.
*   **Scaling Pilot Programs** Existing pilot programs and transformations in business processes, which were being developed in small instances, were able to go to scale rapidly due to funding and a breaking down of barriers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:50">[00:28:50]</a>.

## Enduring Transformations Post-Pandemic

Several changes are likely to persist, fundamentally altering how governments operate:
*   **Remote/Blended Working** Widespread remote working or blended remote/office work arrangements are expected to become standard operating procedures, with implications for urban planning, public transport, and office space utilization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:37">[00:30:37]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:56">[00:36:56]</a>. This also prompts reevaluation of classified information handling and performance evaluation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:52">[00:39:52]</a>.
*   **Digitalization and Data-Driven Decisions** The shift towards digital services like telehealth and electronic forms is irreversible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:50">[00:29:50]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:26">[00:33:26]</a>. Data-driven decision-making, which proved crucial during the pandemic, will be maintained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:54">[00:33:54]</a>. The application of AI, such as chatbots for service delivery and workforce augmentation, is gaining speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:45">[00:40:45]</a>.
*   **Increased Agility and Innovation Dynamics** Governments will likely maintain the increased speed of reaction, agility, and incremental innovation, continuously experimenting and trying new approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:20">[00:34:20]</a>.
*   **Enhanced Collaboration and Consultation** Greater collaboration within and between governments, and with society, is expected to continue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:26">[00:35:26]</a>. There's a move towards de-formalizing processes and designing bottom-up solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:04">[00:35:04]</a>.
*   **Higher Health System Capacity** Health systems have had to increase capacity, build test and tracing systems, and develop sophisticated data infrastructure for immunization programs, all of which are expected to remain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:11">[00:38:11]</a>.
*   **Focus on Social and Racial Justice** In the United States, there is a growing appreciation for the impact of social and racial justice on government programs, leading to efforts to adjust program structures for more equitable outcomes, particularly at state and local levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:48">[00:41:48]</a>.
*   **Recognition of Essential Childcare** The shift to remote work has highlighted the absolute essential nature of reliable and affordable childcare, forcing a different perspective on social structures around work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:27">[00:40:27]</a>.

## Warning Signs and Capacity Constraints for Future Crises

Despite the positive shifts, challenges and warning signs persist:
*   **Societal Digital Literacy and Accessibility** Governments cannot move faster than society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:46">[00:44:46]</a>. Issues like digital literacy and access to technology (e.g., computers, broadband) remain significant hurdles for equitable service delivery and education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:53">[00:44:53]</a>.
*   **Equitable Aid Distribution** In federal systems, the complex flow of aid funds from federal to local levels, and then to individuals, can lead to inefficiencies and unequal distribution of support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:03">[00:47:03]</a>.
*   **Politicization of Response** The politicization of crisis response can undermine public acceptance and compliance with mitigation protocols, impacting the government's ability to respond effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:40">[00:48:40]</a>.
*   **Funding Constraints** The massive public debt incurred during the crisis, combined with pre-existing fiscal challenges (e.g., tax reduction policies, increasing entitlement programs), suggests a future of austerity rather than increased spending flexibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:53">[00:53:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:10">[00:54:10]</a>. While some digitalization reforms may be self-financing in the long run, large-scale policy changes face significant budgetary obstacles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:35">[00:56:35]</a>. Demographic problems will further strain economies and increase age-related costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:23">[00:57:23]</a>.
*   **People and Skills** Sustaining change requires the right people with the right skills and motivation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:53">[00:49:53]</a>. While the crisis has given public servants a sense of purpose and may attract more talent, continuous learning for all, including senior administrators, is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:01">[00:50:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:03">[00:52:03]</a>.
*   **Data Utilization** A significant challenge remains in how governments use, link, and extract value from data to make better decisions and provide improved services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:45">[00:50:45]</a>.
*   **Foresight vs. Risk Management** While risk management (risk registers) is common, governments need to invest more in foresight and scenario planning to anticipate future challenges beyond immediate risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:09">[00:39:09]</a>.

## Administrative Habits to Decommission

To foster future innovation and responsiveness, several administrative habits should be abandoned:
*   **Inability to End Things** Governments are often good at starting initiatives but poor at ending them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:22">[01:04:22]</a>. There is a need to prioritize and decommission outdated projects or practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:15">[01:05:15]</a>. This includes abandoning multi-year, largely outsourced, large IT projects in favor of shorter-term, agile deliverables <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:34">[01:03:34]</a>.
*   **Excessive Risk Aversion** The "gotcha" culture, fueled by media and political discourse, discourages public servants from taking sensible, balanced risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:36">[01:05:36]</a>. A shift towards a learning culture, focusing on understanding what went wrong rather than blame, is needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:18">[01:06:18]</a>.
*   **Long Policy Cycles** The slow, bureaucratic process of policy making and adoption should be replaced with smart, incremental policies that speed up legislation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:06">[01:07:06]</a>.
*   **Stasis Culture of Lifetime Employment** The traditional public service culture of lifetime careers within government can lead to stagnation and risk aversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:33">[01:07:33]</a>. Building a service model that allows for movement between government and private sectors could foster innovation and increase risk tolerance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:56">[01:07:56]</a>.

This period of crisis has vividly illuminated systemic problems within government structures, but also presents an opportunity to remedy these issues and build more resilient, agile, and citizen-centered public services for the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:55">[00:42:55]</a>.
