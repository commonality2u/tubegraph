## Current Policy Challenges

Despite significant progress in clean energy markets, propelled by government policies and market realities leading to $2 trillion USD global investment in renewables and low-carbon technologies last year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>, the momentum for climate action is at risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.

Key [[challenges_in_addressing_climate_change | challenges]] facing climate mitigation policies include:
*   **Slowed Policy Adoption** The pace of climate policy adoption and strengthening has decreased significantly, from 10% annually to just 1-2% per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</a>.
*   **Economic and Geopolitical Strain** Slower global growth, multiple geopolitical tensions, and rising public debt are straining government budgets, making climate action difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.
*   **Shifting Priorities** Government priorities are shifting, reflecting changes in administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:11">[00:05:11]</a>.
*   **Public and Political Pushback** Some sectors, such as agriculture in Europe, show pushback, risking a drop in public and political support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.
*   **Economic Misconceptions** There's a lingering perception that climate action will hinder economic opportunities or that the net-zero transition is not investable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>. However, evidence shows that stronger climate action can provide an economic boost, with a projected net gain of 0.2% in global GDP by 2040 and up to 13% by 2100 when accounting for avoided climate damages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>.

### Innovation and Technology Challenges
While [[Innovation and technology in climate mitigation | innovation]] has driven significant progress, reducing prices for batteries, solar, and wind power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>, and green startups have seen increased investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>:
*   **Insufficient Pace** The current pace of [[Innovation and technology in climate mitigation | innovation]] is inadequate to meet carbon neutrality goals; climate-related patent filings have decreased as a share of all patenting over the past decade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>.
*   **Focus on Incremental Innovation** Current efforts concentrate on incremental [[Innovation and technology in climate mitigation | innovation]] and deploying existing technologies, rather than R&D for entirely new technologies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.

### Governance Challenges from [[Role of climate councils in policy implementation | Climate Councils]]' Perspective
[[Role of climate councils in policy implementation | Climate councils]] report four main [[challenges_faced_by_governments | challenges]] in advising national governments:
*   **Polarization of Climate Policy** Climate change is increasingly politicized, viewed as a left-wing issue rather than science-based, hindering decarbonization progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.
*   **Harder-to-Abate Sectors** While progress has been made in decarbonizing the power sector, less has been done in harder-to-abate sectors like agriculture and food, where a significant disconnect exists between action and potential emissions reductions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>.
*   **Weak Governance** Inadequate channels in government often prevent climate policy from being consistently informed by science and fail to bring the public and stakeholders along <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a>.
*   **Short Time Horizons** Due to short election cycles, government policies can "flip-flop" every four to five years, requiring [[Role of climate councils in policy implementation | climate councils]] to ensure policy consistency across longer time horizons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>.

### Cross-Cutting Governance Challenges
Several core [[challenges_faced_by_governments | challenges]] persist in climate governance, exacerbated by existing structures:
*   **Siloed Responsibility** A perception that climate policy is solely the responsibility of "climate people" rather than a cross-cutting, whole-of-government endeavor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:54">[00:23:54]</a>.
*   **Inadequate Public Engagement** Public consultation and engagement are often insufficient, leading to under-planned impacts on communities and risking backlash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:18">[00:24:18]</a>.
*   **Capacity Constraints and Misalignment** Local-level capacity constraints, narrow KPIs, and siloed budgets hinder collaboration, potentially leading to contradictory actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:40">[00:24:40]</a>.
*   **Bureaucratic Inefficiencies** Despite high-level drives for coordination, bureaucratic processes often impede progress in implementation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:17">[01:15:17]</a>.
*   **Political and Institutional Barriers** Climate policy, especially concerning energy, is highly politically contested, with election cycles and geopolitics (e.g., countries pulling out of the Paris Agreement) acting as barriers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:15">[01:16:15]</a>.
*   **Mismatch in Mission Implementation** Many "missions" are still primarily "innovation missions" led by STI authorities, failing to mobilize a broad policy mix (e.g., regulatory changes, skill development, economic incentives) needed for true systemic transformation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:13">[01:35:13]</a>.

## Solutions and Opportunities through [[Mission governance in achieving climate targets | Mission Governance]]

[[Mission governance in achieving climate targets | Mission governance]] is proposed as a powerful tool to enhance governmental effectiveness and responsiveness in addressing climate change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:30">[01:13:30]</a>.

### Core Principles of [[Mission governance in achieving climate targets | Mission Governance]]
[[Mission governance in achieving climate targets | Mission governance]] offers four key attributes:
1.  **Clear and Measurable Objectives** Establishes explicit objectives that anchor climate commitments within strategic planning, ensuring prioritization at the highest levels, guiding resource allocation, and strengthening cross-sectoral coordination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:44">[01:13:44]</a>.
    *   *Example:* The UK's dedicated Cabinet Committee on Climate Change, chaired by the Prime Minister, ensures unified direction and accountability across government departments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:31">[01:14:31]</a>.
2.  **Capability Building** Emphasizes strengthening the capabilities of public officials and institutions, which is crucial for handling the technical expertise and adaptive policymaking required for climate strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:53">[01:14:53]</a>.
    *   *Example:* Sweden's "Impact Innovation Programs" include an accelerator function to build public sector capacity, providing officials with tools, data, and skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:52">[01:15:52]</a>.
3.  **Evidence-Based Decision Making** Integrates robust data and continuous learning, overcoming the challenge of tracking policy outcomes with accuracy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:16">[01:16:16]</a>. It embeds evaluation, experimentation, and iterative learning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:47">[01:16:47]</a>.
    *   *Example:* Denmark's "innomissions" explicitly integrate experimentation within mission portfolios to refine policies based on evidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:18">[01:17:18]</a>.
4.  **Stronger Stakeholder Engagement** Fosters transparency and equity by incorporating participatory processes at its core, ensuring policy decisions reflect broad societal interests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:36">[01:17:36]</a>.
    *   *Example:* New Zealand's Climate Change Commission prioritizes improving stakeholder engagement to enable greater societal contributions to net-zero emissions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:24">[01:18:24]</a>.

### Role of Centers of Government and [[Role of climate councils in policy implementation | Climate Councils]]
Centers of government have become increasingly important in a complex world, with about 90% of OECD centers taking responsibility for prioritizing key objectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:41">[01:33:41]</a>. They are well-positioned to champion integrated policies, align sectoral actions, and secure political support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:41">[01:34:41]</a>.

*   **Anchoring Missions** Anchoring missions in a high-level body at the center of government (e.g., a climate council, or a dedicated cabinet committee) is crucial for coordination and oversight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:16">[01:16:16]</a>.
    *   *Example:* South Africa's Presidential Climate Change Commission is chaired by the president, providing a central anchoring point to drive a systematic and cross-sectoral approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:30">[01:27:30]</a>.
*   **Overcoming Bureaucracy** Clear mandates and mechanisms for overseeing climate policy implementation are critical to counter bureaucratic inefficiencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:56">[01:15:56]</a>.
*   **Building Trust** [[Mission governance in achieving climate targets | Mission governance]] can help build trust in institutions by grounding actions in credible, impartial, and evidence-based advice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:59">[02:00:59]</a>.

### Mission-Oriented Approach for Climate Targets
The report suggests that integrating targeted, ambitious, and time-bound missions into national climate mitigation commitments can drive significant change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:18">[00:47:18]</a>. Specific sectoral missions are needed for areas like industry or agriculture, with real commitments for delivery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:30">[00:48:30]</a>.

The mission governance framework has five pillars: Structures, Strategic Orientation, Coordination, Execution, and Resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:17">[00:49:17]</a>. When elements within these pillars are missing, action tends to be lacking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:39">[00:49:39]</a>.

**Key Findings on Mission Governance Implementation:**
*   **Strengths:** Climate mitigation frameworks are good at providing framework anchoring, road maps, and access to broad policy mixes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:51">[00:55:51]</a>. Public funding is also relatively good <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:19">[00:56:19]</a>.
*   **Gaps:** Significant gaps exist in private investments, experimentation, mobilization of communities and private sector, coordination actions, and process integration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:27">[00:56:27]</a>.

### Pathways to More Effective Missions
To make [[Innovation and technology in climate mitigation | innovation]] missions "real transition missions," several scenarios are observed:
1.  **Gradual Broadening and Strengthening:** Missions learn and improve by doing, slowly broadening their scope and integrating better over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:35">[01:37:35]</a>.
2.  **Transfer of Leadership:** Leadership of missions transfers from STI authorities to the "challenge owners" (e.g., ministries of water, transport, environment). This is sensitive due to political turf and budget competition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:55">[01:37:55]</a>.
3.  **Dedicated Mission Agencies:** Missions are led by dedicated agencies reporting to several ministries, as seen with Norway's Research Council <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:26">[01:38:26]</a>.
4.  **Elevating Missions to Central Government:** Missions are led by central government bodies, like the Prime Minister's office or powerful transition committees, providing broad political support and a climate agenda <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:12">[01:39:12]</a>.
5.  **Regional/Local Missions:** Operating at regional or local levels, these "micro-missions" can alleviate [[challenges_of_global_governance_systems | governance challenges]] and ensure stronger private stakeholder engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:28">[01:40:28]</a>.

## Conclusion
Governments play a pivotal role in achieving global climate targets, but existing governance structures are often inadequate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:42">[00:44:42]</a>. Integrating [[mission_governance_in_achieving_climate_targets | mission governance]] principles into national climate efforts is crucial for systematic and concrete action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:06">[00:57:06]</a>. This includes developing targeted missions for specific sub-targets with clear commitments and timelines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:12">[00:57:12]</a>. Mission governance can enable and strengthen political support and societal engagement, moving beyond reactive responses to climate crises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:27">[00:57:27]</a>. Furthermore, enhancing [[Innovation and technology in climate mitigation | innovation]] and experimentation in climate policy and integrating them into current actions is encouraged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:52">[00:57:52]</a>.
