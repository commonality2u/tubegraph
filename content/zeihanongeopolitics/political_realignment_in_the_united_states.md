## The Pivotal Role of [[role_of_independents_in_us_politics | Independent Voters]]

For the past 30 years, most elections, especially at the presidential level, have been decided by Americans who self-identify as [[independent_voters_in_american_elections | independent voters]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>. While roughly 30% of the American population identifies as Democrats, and 30% as Republicans, the 40% in the middle call themselves Independents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>. However, over three-quarters of this group consistently vote for one party or the other, often more than 90% of the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. Thus, a thin sliver of only 5 to 10% of voters has served as the balance of power within the American political system for decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a>. This group was instrumental in electing Obama twice, W twice, Biden, and also in electing and then unseating Trump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>.

Ahead of the 2022 midterms, Donald Trump's rhetoric, suggesting that Independents' votes didn't matter and that decisions should be made at the primary level, provoked a strong reaction from "America's True Independents" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a>. In nearly every race endorsed by Donald Trump, the Democrats won <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>. This outcome transformed what was anticipated to be a "red wave" in the midterms into, at best, a "red fizzle" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>.

Given that Independents tend to be fickle, switching sides every two or three elections due to buyer's remorse, it took a significant provocation from Donald Trump to compel them to vote the same way twice in a row <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. With Trump's rhetoric remaining unchanged, the expectation was that Independents would continue their voting pattern from 2020 and 2022 into 2024 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>. Initial exit poll data from the 2024 elections suggests this prediction largely held true, with Independents breaking strongly against Trump for the third consecutive election <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>.

## Unforeseen Shifts in Voter Demographics

Despite the Independent vote aligning with expectations, the overall election outcome shifted because "everyone else voted differently" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>. A significant "bleed over" of support from the traditionally "elite-led" group to Trump was observed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.

The contemporary Democratic alliance has historically rested on three pillars: Coastal, primarily white, college-educated elites; minorities of all backgrounds; and organized labor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. However, in this election, many of these pillars showed cracks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>:
*   Unmarried women, a significant part of the alliance, shifted to Trump by five points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
*   Youth (18 to 29-year-olds), who almost always vote Democrat, broke towards Trump by 6% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
*   Black men shifted to Trump by 7% more than before <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:28">[00:04:28]</a>.
*   Non-white college graduates shifted by 9% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>.
*   Asians shifted by 11% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>.
*   People in lower income brackets (30% to 49%) shifted by 12% for Trump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:39">[00:04:39]</a>.
*   Latinos experienced a dramatic shift of 17%, with Latino men shifting by 22% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.

These significant shifts among groups traditionally associated with the Democratic party mark a substantial change in the [[changing_demographics_and_their_political_impact | political landscape]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>.

## Current State of [[political_volatility_and_realignment_in_the_united_states | Political Volatility and Realignment]]

The current period is characterized by [[political_realignment_in_the_united_states | political realignment]], with party loyalties visibly shifting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:23">[00:05:23]</a>.

### Key Observations

1.  **Voter Participation Decline**: A major difference between 2020 and 2024 was a drop in voter participation by over 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>. This suggests that Trump no longer possesses the same motivating factor, either for or against him, as he once did <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:43">[00:05:43]</a>.

2.  **Emergence of a New Party and Demise of the Democratic Coalition**: With [[political_factions_in_motion | political factions]] in motion, a new party is emerging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. The current alignment that brought Trump to power for his second term is identified as "Maga," distinct from the traditional Republican alliance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>. Conversely, this marks the end of the Democratic Party as it was known <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:17]</a>.
    *   Organized labor is now, at best, a swing vote for Democrats, with over half voting for Trump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>.
    *   The most significant shift is among minorities, particularly Hispanics, the fastest-growing demographic due to immigration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>. Hispanics are, paradoxically, the group most opposed to migration in the United States <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>. Trump's focus on the southern border resonated strongly with those who had previously crossed it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:50">[00:06:50]</a>.
    *   The loss of Hispanic and organized labor support from the Democratic alliance means that areas where these groups are locally significant are now "up for grabs" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:58">[00:06:58]</a>. Without significant self-reflection and alignment shifts, the remaining base of white, coastal, educated elites is insufficient to win federal elections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:11">[00:07:11]</a>.

3.  **Impending Constitutional Crisis**: A constitutional crisis is anticipated in the next few years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>. Donald Trump's perceived decline in cognitive function raises questions about his ability to serve an entire term without losing his mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:46">[00:07:46]</a>. Unlike Joe Biden, who had a support network to offer guidance, Trump's "Maga" party is a cult of personality, purged of anyone who might challenge him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>. His new cabinet is expected to exclude those who previously disagreed with him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. This situation presents a constitutional crisis for the country, as there is no precedent for removing a president who has lost mental capacity but refuses to leave office <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>. It also represents a leadership crisis for the Maga movement, as Trump is synonymous with Maga itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>.

The combination of voter apathy, the demise of the Democratic coalition without a clear replacement, and the potential coming demise of Donald Trump without a clear successor promises a dynamic and challenging political period ahead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>.
