The Chinese military has been undergoing significant structural changes due to ongoing political purges, which have implications for its effectiveness and decision-making capabilities.

## Context of Chinese Military Structure
In China, the Defense Ministry differs significantly from its U.S. counterpart <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>. Unlike the U.S. Defense Secretary who commands forces and reports directly to the president <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>, the Chinese Defense Ministry does not have operational control over the military or policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. Its role is primarily that of a "glorified press secretary" handling military diplomacy and acting as an interface with foreign counterparts, but without real decision-making authority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>. The actual decision-makers sit on the Central Military Commission, which is chaired by [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi Jinping]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>.

## The Purges
[[Military purges in China | Purges]] within the Chinese government have been ongoing for 14 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. These purges are primarily aimed at [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi]] tightening his grip on power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. When [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi]] came to power, [[Political and Social Instability in China | China]] was characterized by significant corruption and a "centrally disassociated" system, where local and regional decision-making was prevalent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>. To centralize authority and combat perceived political competition, [[Impact of Xi Jinpings Leadership on Chinas Governance | Xi]] initiated a series of purges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>. Over 12 years, this process "gutted the system" of individuals with ambition and competence across various sectors, including local and state government, academia, business, and federal bureaucracy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>.

Initially, the military was largely exempt from these purges due to its armed status <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>. However, [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi]] has now begun to target the military, going after political players or those guilty of corruption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>.

### Impact on Military Leadership
Over the past few months, 20 top-level military personnel have been fired, with 12 of these dismissals occurring in just two weeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>. This includes a recently appointed defense minister, who was chosen by [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi]] himself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>. The previous defense secretary vanished for two months before his formal disposal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:27">[00:01:27]</a>, and a new defense minister, Admiral Dong Jun, was appointed in the last couple of weeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

The Central Military Commission, responsible for military planning, is now reportedly "stacked... with incompetent yes-men" or individuals who avoid offering opinions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>.

## Consequences for Effectiveness
The purges are imposing the same "structural gutting" on the military that has affected other parts of [[Chinas political system and public opinion control | China's bureaucracy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>. This has led to a bureaucracy that struggles to function, as individuals are hesitant to transmit information for fear of [[Xi Jinpings influence on the Chinese military | Xi's]] reaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>. This phenomenon is now observed in military activities as well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>.

Previously, China's military preparedness and capability were significantly limited by a lack of expertise and massive corruption, comparable to "Russian-style stealing of the funds" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>. The current purges, while addressing corruption, also lead to a military where a substantial portion of waking hours may be spent on political indoctrination rather than military tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. This shift raises questions about the overall effectiveness of the Chinese military in the [[Future of Chinese leadership decision making | future]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:57">[00:04:57]</a>.
