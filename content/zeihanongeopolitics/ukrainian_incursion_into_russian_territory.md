This marks a notable shift, as past [[ukrainian_counteroffensive_in_the_war | Ukrainian counteroffensives]] on their own territory were often hampered by Russian defensive lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>.

## Details of the Incursion

In the last two weeks, Ukrainian forces have invaded Russia proper, capturing approximately 1,000 square kilometers in Kursk Province <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. This initial territorial gain is larger than the entirety of what the Russian army achieved in the [[russian_assault_on_the_ukrainian_city_in_donbas | Donbas]] over the last 18 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>.

The Ukrainian plan involves taking a substantial portion of the territory, aiming to temporarily occupy about half of Kursk Province, which is roughly 6,000 square miles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>. For scale, the Donbas region where much of the fighting has occurred is about 20,000 square miles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:31">[00:02:31]</a>.

A key tactical move by Ukraine has been the destruction of the three permanent bridges over the Seym River in Kursk Province <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>. This action makes it significantly harder for Russia to reinforce its positions in the area <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>. Ukrainian forces are expanding on at least four different axes: Northwest, Northeast, North, and East <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

With the bridges out, Russian resupply efforts are limited to pontoons, which are vulnerable to shorter-range attacks like artillery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:02:56]</a>. This has curtailed Russia's ability to retaliate effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>.

## Ukrainian Objectives

The primary strategic objective of this incursion is to neutralize the city of Belgorod <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>. Belgorod serves as the main logistical hub for Russian forces in the northern part of Ukraine, concentrating their armies and artillery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:29">[00:03:29]</a>. By taking control of the southern half of Kursk Province, Ukraine aims to disrupt most of the infrastructure feeding into Belgorod, effectively neutralizing the city of a million people without direct urban combat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a>.

This operation has also allowed Ukraine to trigger a "war of movement" on Russian territory, something they struggled to achieve on their own soil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>. Unlike the multi-layered Russian defensive lines, minefields, and artillery placements found in areas like Donbas, Kursk Province lacks these extensive fortifications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. This allows Ukrainian forces to engage in a battlefield that leverages their strengths over Russian ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.

## Russian Response and Challenges

Russia's typical strategy of mass artillery assaults followed by waves of conscripts to pin down defenders has been less effective in Kursk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:12">[00:04:12]</a>. The rapid Ukrainian advance took the last of Ukraine's reserve units and some NATO-trained advanced units <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:39">[00:04:39]</a>, but Russia's ability to counter is severely limited <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>.

Russian forces face significant challenges:
*   **Manpower Shortages:** The pipeline for forcibly drafted men, particularly from occupied minority populations, is depleting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>. Russia historically prefers a 3:1 or even 7:1 manpower superiority when attacking, which they lack in Kursk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
*   **Leadership Deficiencies:** Leadership is also scarce, with reports of Putin assigning a former bodyguard to oversee the Kursk operation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.
*   **Logistical Strain:** For two and a half years, Russia focused logistical efforts on building a pipeline of men and material <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>. Now, with 10,000 Ukrainian personnel in Kursk Province <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>, Russia needs 30,000 to 70,000 troops to retake the territory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>, which they cannot find <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>.
*   **Depleted Reserves:** Russia has mobilized nearly all remaining military forces in the country, stripping security personnel from the Pacific, Baltic, and Arctic theaters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>. Even security forces from the Far East's cosmodrome have been redeployed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>. These forces are often untrained and lightly armed, lacking large-scale artillery support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:39">[00:07:39]</a>.

While Ukraine is likely sustaining casualties in this offensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>, the operation has allowed them to play to their strengths, neutralize Belgorod, and alter the war's narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>.

## Significance of the Incursion: Red Lines and Western Policy

The most significant development from the Kursk operation is Russia's continued non-use of nuclear weapons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>. Throughout the conflict, Russia has repeatedly issued "red lines," threatening nuclear retaliation if certain boundaries were crossed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>. However, these threats have proven to be bluffs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:09">[00:09:09]</a>.

With Ukraine having crossed the international border in force, effectively crippling the Russian military in the area, and poised to neutralize a major city <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>, Russia has not responded with nuclear force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:32">[00:09:32]</a>. This development challenges the long-held Western concern about "escalation management" and the potential for Russian nuclear use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>.

The fact that Russia has shown either an unwillingness or inability to escalate to the nuclear level suggests that the conservatism in Western capitals regarding challenging Russia may dissipate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>. This implies that most Russian threats to date are meaningless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:46">[00:10:46]</a>. While an ultimate red line for nuclear use likely exists, it is now clear that it does not involve population centers, territory, or cities within dozens of miles of Russia's internationally recognized borders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:51">[00:10:51]</a>.

This shift is anticipated to pave the way for new offensive operations with the full backing of Western countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:11">[00:11:11]</a>. This represents the most significant shift in the war to date, with consequences expected in the coming months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.
