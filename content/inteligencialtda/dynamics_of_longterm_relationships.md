## Evolution of Relationships: Past vs. Present

Modern society presents an "absurd menu" of ways to relate, in stark contrast to past generations where options were limited to one's school, neighborhood, or workplace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>. This abundance of choice can be overwhelming, leading to doubt and fear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>.

In the past, social filters for [[human_relationships_and_social_behaviors | interaction]] were more severe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>. For example, contacting someone involved calling a landline and speaking with parents, creating a significant barrier to casual communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. Today, easy access to an almost infinite number of partners, akin to food delivery, removes these social friction barriers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>.

Despite this perceived "abundance," the human brain is naturally programmed for scarcity, capable of forming real affective connections with only 50 to 150 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. This creates a "cognitive dissonance" in a hyper-abundant environment, leading to a flood of undigested information and open conversations that the brain is not designed to handle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:27">[00:11:27]</a>. This dissonance is a major catalyst for modern psychiatric illnesses like depression, anxiety, and bipolarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:28">[00:12:28]</a>.

The Stoic philosophy emphasizes "Memento Mori" – remembering one's death – as a practice to truly live <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:22">[00:20:22]</a>. By regularly contemplating one's mortality, individuals can assess if their actions align with their life's purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:07">[00:21:07]</a>.
