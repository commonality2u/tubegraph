## Arminianism and Calvinism: Doctrines of Salvation

Arminianism and Calvinism are two prominent lines of thought regarding the [[nature_of_sin_and_salvation | doctrine of salvation]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:26">[00:10:26]</a>. While not the only schools of thought, they represent major positions in understanding [[christianity_forgiveness_and_salvation | salvation]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:03">[00:18:03]</a>.

### Historical Context
Arminianism emerged from within Calvinism through Jacob Arminius, who served as director of the University of Leiden in Holland and died in 1609 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:44">[00:10:44]</a>. Arminius, originally a Calvinist, developed difficulties with the more rigorous aspects of [[concept of predestination and election in Christianity | predestination and election]] that characterized 10th-century Protestant scholasticism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>. This period saw Calvinism emphasizing the doctrine of election significantly more than John Calvin himself had in his *Institutes of the Christian Religion* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>.

Arminius reacted to this emphasis, arguing it did not do justice to God's character and justice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:18">[00:12:18]</a>. After Arminius's death, his followers presented a document outlining their disagreements with Calvinism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:30">[00:12:30]</a>. This led to the Synod of Dort (1618-1619), which established the five points of Calvinism, known by the acronym TULIP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:41">[00:12:41]</a>. Those who thought differently were barred from pastoral ministry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:05">[00:13:05]</a>. Arminianism subsequently developed further outside the Netherlands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:24">[00:13:24]</a>.

> [!INFO] The Five Points of Calvinist Soteriology (TULIP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:51">[00:13:51]</a>
> *   **T**otal Depravity
> *   **U**nconditional Election
> *   **L**imited Atonement
> *   **I**rresistible Grace
> *   **P**erseverance of the Saints

### Core Distinctions
Both Arminianism and Calvinism are considered orthodox within Christianity, meaning they are not heresies, and both acknowledge the Bible as the inerrant, infallible Word of God <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>. The unifying factor among believers is faith in Christ as Redeemer and Savior, not adherence to specific doctrinal differences like TULIP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</a>.

#### Atonement: Limited vs. Unlimited
A key point of contention is the scope of Christ's atonement.
*   **Calvinist View (Limited Atonement):** Jesus Christ died specifically for the elect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:28">[00:20:28]</a>. The death of Christ is effective only for those chosen by God before the foundation of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:04">[00:21:04]</a>, making it logical that He died to rescue only these chosen ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:21">[00:29:21]</a>.
*   **Arminian View (Unlimited Atonement):** Jesus Christ died for all human beings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:52">[00:28:52]</a>, offering [[christianity_forgiveness_and_salvation | salvation]] to all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:12">[01:06:12]</a>. This attitude is described as honest and sincere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:05">[02:56:05]</a>. However, it is effective only for those who believe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:09">[02:56:09]</a>. Some argue that Arminianism logically leads to limited atonement because if God foresaw who would believe and persevere, the number of saved individuals is fixed, meaning Christ died for that specific, foreknown group <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:10:59">[03:10:59]</a>.

#### [[concept of predestination and election in Christianity | Election and Predestination]]
*   **Calvinist View:** God has His elect, as evidenced by texts like Acts 13:48, which states that "all who believed had been destined for eternal life" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:43">[02:08:43]</a>. Revelation 17:8 speaks of a "book of life written from the foundation of the world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:08">[02:08:08]</a>. This election is unconditional, based on God's free pleasure, not human actions or foreseen merit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:51">[02:59:51]</a>. The process of [[christianity_forgiveness_and_salvation | salvation]] involves foreknowledge, calling, justification, and glorification, a chain that cannot be broken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:24">[02:39:24]</a>.
*   **Arminian View:** Arminius himself believed in election, but through different mechanisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:26">[02:27:26]</a>. They believe God chose based on His foreknowledge of who would believe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:00">[02:12:00]</a>.

#### God's Love and Wrath
*   **Calvinist View:** God is love, and His love is infinite <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:36">[02:54:36]</a>. However, God also possesses infinite wrath and justice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:55">[02:56:55]</a>. These attributes harmonize in God's mind, though humans may not fully grasp how <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:01">[02:57:01]</a>. Texts like Proverbs 16:4 state that God made "even the wicked for the day of calamity," and Romans 9 refers to "vessels of wrath prepared for perdition" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:05">[02:55:05]</a>. The Calvinist accepts these apparent paradoxes without trying to reinterpret texts to remove tension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:58:36">[02:58:36]</a>.
*   **Arminian View:** God is love, and His essence is love <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:52:15">[02:52:15]</a>. Wrath is not an antonym of love; even someone who loves can get angry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:01">[03:00:01]</a>. God's offer of [[christianity_forgiveness and salvation | salvation]] is sincere to all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:41">[01:06:41]</a>. Rejecting the idea that God doesn't love everyone is central to the Arminian perspective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:53:02">[02:53:02]</a>.

#### [[role of free will in theology | Free Will]] vs. Divine Sovereignty
*   **Calvinist View:** Man is fully responsible for his actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:35">[01:16:35]</a>. However, God determines all things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:43">[01:18:43]</a>. This creates an "insoluble theological tension" or paradox <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:59">[01:16:59]</a>. Adam, before the fall, had total [[role of free will in theology | free will]] because his will was not corrupt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:43">[02:16:43]</a>. After the fall, sinful nature stains the will, leading to an inclination towards evil and disgust for holy things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:55">[02:16:55]</a>. Therefore, no one can come to Jesus unless the Father draws them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:48">[02:17:48]</a>. The Calvinist believes that Adam was free *within* the sphere of God's decree, meaning his freedom was bounded by God's overarching plan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:16">[02:27:16]</a>.
*   **Arminian View:** God calls individuals to participate synergistically in the process of [[christianity_forgiveness_and_salvation | salvation]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:41">[01:11:41]</a>. Man can receive, accept, and extend his hand to [[religious and spiritual beliefs about the afterlife and divine grace | grace]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:21">[01:13:21]</a>. The [[role of free will in theology | free will]] is defined as the freedom to make decisions without coercion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:20">[02:21:20]</a>. For Arminians, a final judgment makes sense because humans are responsible for their choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:06">[01:15:06]</a>.

## The Role of the Holy Spirit
Both Calvinists and Arminians agree that the Holy Spirit opens the heart of man and convicts him of sin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:37">[01:10:37]</a>. The conversion process is monergistic, meaning the work of salvation belongs solely to God; humans cannot save themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:01">[01:11:01]</a>.

*   **Calvinist Perspective:** The Holy Spirit's conviction is applied selectively, working in the elect to convince them of sin, righteousness, and judgment, leading them to believe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:03">[01:09:03]</a>. This is known as irresistible grace in the Calvinist TULIP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:52">[01:08:52]</a>.
*   **Arminian Perspective:** The Holy Spirit works in everyone's heart who hears the Gospel through "prevenient grace," preparing the person to receive it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:48">[02:18:48]</a>. This grace encompasses everyone who hears the gospel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:55">[02:20:55]</a>.

## Perseverance of the Saints
*   **Calvinist View:** There is no loss of salvation in Calvinism. The chain of salvation (foreknowledge, predestination, calling, justification, glorification) cannot be broken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:55">[01:55:55]</a>. If a person commits apostasy, it means they had a "false faith" or "dead faith" and were never truly elected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:36">[01:53:36]</a>. The proof of election is perseverance in faith <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:51">[01:53:51]</a>.
*   **Arminian View:** While not explicitly detailed in the transcript, the emphasis on human decision and acceptance of grace suggests a potential for falling away from faith, though the core belief remains in God's saving power.

## Divine Foreknowledge vs. Foreordination
The nature of God's knowledge of future events is a significant point of divergence.
*   **Calvinist View:** God knows the future because He *wrote* it; He planned and determined all things beforehand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:43:01">[02:43:01]</a>. Texts like Psalm 139:16 suggest that all days are "written" and "shaped" in God's book <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:10">[02:39:10]</a>. God's omniscience is not based on discovering a pre-existing future, but on His having ordained it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:30">[02:25:30]</a>.
*   **Arminian View:** God knows the future because He is omniscient and "saw" it before it happened <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:35">[02:25:35]</a>. His foreknowledge is based on His prescience, not on His pre-determining every event <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:48">[02:25:48]</a>. This preserves human responsibility for choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:01">[02:33:01]</a>.

## Conclusion
The discussion highlights that while both theological camps are considered orthodox, they grapple with interpreting biblical texts that speak of God's infinite love and wrath, His sovereign decree, and human responsibility. Calvinism tends to embrace these as "insoluble theological tensions" or paradoxes that humble human understanding before God's inscrutable mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:59">[03:11:59]</a>. Arminianism, conversely, seeks to reconcile these concepts in a way that emphasizes human agency and God's universal offer of salvation. Both acknowledge that [[christianity_and_redemption | redemption]] is solely from God <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:35">[01:11:35]</a>, yet their [[philosophy and theological interpretations | interpretations]] of the mechanics of [[nature_of_sin_and_redemption_in_christian_theology | sin and redemption]] remain distinct.
