The [[conflict_in_israel_and_palestine | conflict in Israel and Palestine]] is not merely 11 days old, but spans 75 years as a historic stage, originating from a process that is not religious or an age-old fight without solution, which is considered a myth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>. It is argued that the current situation is a massacre, not a war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.

## Origins of the Conflict
The process began with British imperialism, which fostered a dynamic of "divide and conquer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>. This led to intensified migration to the region, influenced by geopolitical decisions, natural resources like oil, and other factors debated at the first Zionist Congress in 1897 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.

Historically, in [[the_historical_significance_of_Jerusalem | Historical Palestine]], populations of Muslims, Christians, and Jews coexisted; for instance, 10% of the population was Christian and 3% was Jewish <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:58">[00:06:58]</a>. The immigration process that followed was considered unfair, supported by international subsidies and an understanding of the historical persecution of the Jewish people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>.

### Zionism and its Character
Criticisms of Zionism must be separated from the legitimacy and dignity of the Jewish people, and antisemitism is condemned as a form of oppression and racism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:32">[00:07:32]</a>. The Zionist ideology from 1897 onwards transformed the character of a people seeking self-determination into a process that claimed "a land without people for a people without land," despite people already living there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. This migration was subsidized by sectors of the European upper bourgeoisie, leading to the expulsion of the native population and Zionist domination of land, labor, and means of production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>.

André argues that Zionism was not created in 1897, as Jews have wished to return to their ancestral land for over 2,000 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:52">[00:34:52]</a>. Political Zionism, as an organized movement, began to address the "Jewish problem" in Europe 60 years before the Holocaust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:07">[00:35:07]</a>. Zionism is described as a national self-determination movement for the Jewish people, not racist, and exists in various forms and views <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:50">[00:35:50]</a>. It is possible to be pro-Palestinian and Zionist simultaneously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:44">[00:36:44]</a>.

Pre-1897 Jewish communities existed in the region, with the first kibbutz and Jewish-majority city (Rishon LeZion) founded before Theodor Herzl's "Jewish State" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:29">[00:35:29]</a>. Jewish immigration also coincided with Arab immigration to the coast of Palestine from 1850 to 1920 due to economic development around ports like Haifa and Jaffa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:14">[00:39:14]</a>.

## Escalation and Wars
Tensions severely increased at the end of World War I <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:36">[00:37:36]</a>. The British Empire, at war with the Ottoman Empire, made conflicting promises: offering the land to the Meccans for a great Arab state and, in the Balfour Declaration, promising a home for the Jewish people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>. This declaration, which did not explicitly deny the existing national state or call for the expulsion of indigenous people, intensified migration and conflicts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.

During the Palestinian rebellion against the British Empire (1936-1939), Zionist militias allied with the British to fight the Palestinian people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>.

After World War II, global shock over the Holocaust increased pressure for the Jewish people to have peace and self-determination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:36">[00:10:36]</a>. However, the British Empire, weakened economically, hesitated to fulfill its promises. This led to the Jewish Uprising, where Zionist militias conducted attacks, including the King David Hotel bombing, which killed 92 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:32">[00:11:32]</a>. These actions, qualified as terrorism, prompted Britain to hand the issue over to the UN <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:44">[00:11:44]</a>. André states that the King David Hotel target was the British Army HQ, not civilians, though civilians died <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:05">[00:33:05]</a>.

### UN Partition Plan and the Nakba
In 1947, the UN Partition Plan (Resolution 181) proposed dividing [[the_historical_significance_of_Jerusalem | Historical Palestine]] into two states: one Jewish and one Palestinian, with Jerusalem as an international city <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:02">[00:12:02]</a>. Israel received the larger portion of the land, including main arable lands, water reserves, and the largest Mediterranean coast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:19">[00:12:19]</a>. The Palestinian territory became a "patchwork quilt" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:30">[00:12:30]</a>.

Upon the end of the British Mandate, Israel declared its creation in 1948, but a Palestinian state was not declared <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:51">[00:12:51]</a>. This event, known as "Al-Nakba" (the great tragedy) by Arabs, involved the expulsion of 750,000 Palestinian families, destruction of 520 villages, and led to ongoing confrontation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:58">[00:12:58]</a>. Palestinians migrated to Gaza and Jordan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:34">[00:13:34]</a>.

André asserts that the Arab League rejected Resolution 181 in 1947, and its leaders, including the Mufti of Jerusalem and Hassan al-Banna (founder of the Muslim Brotherhood), advocated for war and throwing Jews into the sea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:55">[00:48:55]</a>. This rejection, along with the Arab League's subsequent actions, contributed to the Nakba <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:17">[00:49:17]</a>. The War of Independence followed Israel's declaration of independence on May 14th, 1948, as Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, Iraq, and Egypt sought to prevent Israel's founding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:49">[00:52:49]</a>. This war led to a population exodus for both Palestinians and Jews from Arab countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:08">[02:10:08]</a>. Israel won, surviving the war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:59">[00:52:59]</a>. André questions why Egypt and Jordan, which controlled Gaza and West Bank respectively from 1949 to 1967, did not establish a Palestinian state, suggesting their interest was not in a Palestinian state alongside Israel but in its place <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:32">[00:54:32]</a>.

### Arab-Israeli Wars and Military Occupation
The conflict progressed through the Arab-Israeli wars, including the 1956 invasion of Sinai, which was resolved by a UN peacekeeping mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:42">[00:13:42]</a>. Israel, allied with the United States since 1956, continued its objective of territorial expansion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:00">[00:14:00]</a>.

The Six-Day War in 1967 resulted in Israel's total military occupation of the West Bank (Jordan) and Gaza Strip, as well as the Syrian Golan Heights and parts of Southern Lebanon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:02">[00:15:02]</a>. Israel returned the Sinai Peninsula but continued military occupation of the West Bank and Gaza <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:42">[00:15:42]</a>. André states that Israel did not start the Six-Day War but attacked Egypt preventively, as Egypt was preparing to attack Israel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:03">[00:55:03]</a>.

The Yom Kippur War followed in 1973 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:48">[00:15:48]</a>.

### Intifadas and Political Developments
The first Intifada, a revolt by the Palestinian population, began almost 20 years after Israel's military occupation, in 1987 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:53">[00:15:53]</a>. It is described as a dignified revolt, justified by international law which prohibits colonization, military occupation, and forced displacement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:35">[00:16:35]</a>. This revolt was met with immense brutality by the Zionist state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:02">[00:17:02]</a>.

Palestinian political organizations like the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP) advocated for a secular state and an end to oppression, including Marxist organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a>. The consensus among the people was to have self-determination and recovery of territory, at least to the UN Partition Plan borders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:55">[00:17:55]</a>.

The political dispute that began in 1987 saw the emergence of Hamas, an organization rooted in Gaza with ties to the Muslim Brotherhood, which pursued military confrontation strategies against Israel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:22">[00:18:22]</a>. Hamas's emergence is seen as a result of the strangulation and despair of the Palestinian people after agreements failed to yield results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:55">[01:12:55]</a>.

The Oslo Accords (1993) saw Israel commit to returning occupied territories, but with limitations that denied Palestinian sovereignty, armed forces, and territorial integrity, and stipulated a 7-year withdrawal period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:57">[00:19:57]</a>. This generated frustration as it did not guarantee self-determination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:17">[00:20:17]</a>. André mentions that in 2000, at the Camp David summit, Israel (under Prime Minister Ehud Barak) offered a final settlement, including 97% of the West Bank, but Yasser Arafat did not respond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:42">[00:57:42]</a>. In 2008, Prime Minister Ehud Olmert made another peace offer, including 100% of the West Bank and Gaza, and the return of 100,000 refugees, which was also not accepted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:51">[01:00:51]</a>.
