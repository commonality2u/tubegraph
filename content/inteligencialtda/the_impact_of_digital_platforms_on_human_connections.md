## Evolution of Relationships

In the past, social circles were limited to those encountered in person, such as classmates, neighbors, or colleagues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>. Communication was often cumbersome, involving landline calls or leaving messages with neighbors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. Today, [[Impact of technology and modern society on human behavior | digital platforms]] offer an "absurd" and "infinite number of options" for relating, akin to ordering food delivery with a tap of a finger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:07:01">[00:07:01]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:09:17">[00:09:17]</a>.

## Psychological Impact of Hyper-abundance

While this abundance can seem positive, it also presents significant challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>. The sheer volume of choices can lead to increased doubt and anxiety, particularly in romantic relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.

Psychologically, the human brain is wired for scarcity, capable of maintaining only "50 to 150 real affective connections" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:10:31">[0:10:31]</a>. This contrasts sharply with modern [[Digital behavior and social media influence | digital environments]], where individuals may have thousands of "followers" or "friends" but without genuine depth of connection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:10:47">[0:10:47]</a>.

The rapid influx of information and constant open conversations (e.g., "10 conversations open" simultaneously) overloads the brain, which is not designed to process such a high volume of affectionate interactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:11:27">[0:11:27]</a>. This "cognitive dissonance" between a brain programmed for scarcity and a hyper-abundant digital environment is seen as a catalyst for a wide range of psychiatric illnesses, including depression, anxiety, and bipolarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:12:16">[0:12:16]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:12:28">[0:12:28]</a>.

> "So we have a brain based on scarcity, he is programmed in scarcity, only that we are in a hyper environment abundant and this generates a dissonance total cognitive that catalyzes the great most psychiatric illnesses such as depression anxiety bipolarity anxiety schizophrenia psychosis" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:12:19">[0:12:19]</a>

## Modern Relationship Dynamics

### Ephemeral Connections
The ease of access to a multitude of potential partners contributes to the "ephemeral" nature of many modern relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:25:01">[0:25:01]</a>. Relationships are often based on superficial traits like physical appearance or material possessions, leading to quick dissatisfaction and easy replacement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:25:03">[0:25:03]</a>.

### Lack of Depth and Willingness to Sacrifice
A significant issue in modern society is a pervasive lack of depth and an unwillingness to sacrifice for others in relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:24:55">[0:24:55]</a>. This stems from a culture that raises individuals to "have" rather than to "give," leading to selfishness and a sense of entitlement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:26:56">[0:26:56]</a>. When relationships inevitably face challenges, this mentality often results in failure and a nihilistic outlook, where individuals may conclude that all relationships are inherently flawed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:27:31">[0:27:31]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:27:55">[0:27:55]</a>.

### The Problem of Escapism
The excessive use of digital platforms for relating can be a form of escapism, a "neurosis" driven by the fear of real-world confrontation, rejection, or judgment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:30:49">[0:30:49]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:31:09">[0:31:09]</a>. Creating a "character behind a screen" is easier than facing the complexities and vulnerabilities of genuine in-person interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:31:33">[0:31:33]</a>.

## Influence on Mental Health
The over-reliance on digital interactions and the pervasive societal drive to "desire" contributes to a large number of mentally ill individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:32:00">[0:32:00]</a>. This constant pursuit of "something more" prevents contentment and fosters a cycle of unfulfilled desires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:32:13">[0:32:13]</a>.

[[Social Media and Mental Health | Studies on lottery winners]] show that financial gain does not equate to lasting happiness if an individual was already unhappy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:32:27">[0:32:27]</a>. Similarly, the drive for external validation and continuous seeking of novelty in digital interactions can lead to chronic dissatisfaction, as the brain struggles to find balance between pleasure and pain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:32:16">[0:32:16]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:20:20">[0:20:20]</a>.

> "the more pleasure I seek the greater the pain ticket that I have to pay" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:20:20">[0:20:20]</a>

## Conclusion
The [[Impact of technology | impact of digital platforms]] on human connections is multifaceted. While offering unparalleled access and options, they simultaneously contribute to superficiality, a lack of commitment, and a rise in mental health issues due to the inherent mismatch between human psychology and the demands of a hyper-connected, hyper-abundant digital world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:11:10">[0:11:10]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:25:01">[0:25:01]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="0:32:00">[0:32:00]</a>. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for fostering healthier, more meaningful connections in the digital age.
