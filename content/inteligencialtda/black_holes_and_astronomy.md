## The Concept of Black Holes

The history of black holes in [[astrophysics_and_the_universe | astronomy]] is interesting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:57">[03:57:57]</a>. The concept emerged from Albert Einstein's work on relativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:58:04">[03:58:04]</a>.

### Einstein's Theory of Gravity
Einstein's theory of general relativity, introduced in 1915, proposed that mass distorts space-time, which explains gravity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:06">[04:27:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:32:05">[04:32:05]</a>. This was a departure from Newton's explanation of gravity as a force, which Einstein found insufficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:05">[04:20:05]</a>. Einstein conducted thought experiments, like imagining a person falling from a roof, to develop his understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:06">[04:44:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:06">[04:27:06]</a>. He realized that an object falling due to gravity experiences acceleration similar to an accelerated object, leading to the idea that inertial mass and gravitational mass are the same <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:54:05">[04:54:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:54:05">[04:54:05]</a>.

A key prediction of Einstein's theory was that light bends when passing near a large mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:07">[04:56:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:07">[04:57:07]</a>. This effect, known as gravitational lensing, allows for the observation of distant objects distorted and magnified <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:17">[01:00:17]</a>. This was proven in 1919 during a total solar eclipse in Sobral, Brazil, where measurements of star positions near the eclipsed Sun matched Einstein's calculations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:32">[01:00:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:32">[01:00:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:32">[01:00:32]</a>.

### Formation and Properties
The theoretical understanding of black holes began with studying dying stars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:07">[04:57:07]</a>. When stars much more massive than the Sun (e.g., 8-10 times the Sun's mass) die, they undergo a supernova explosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. The remaining core, if its mass exceeds about three times the Sun's mass, collapses in on itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. This creates an object where all its mass is concentrated in an extremely small space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

A black hole is characterized by a gravitational force so immense that its escape velocity is greater than the speed of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. This means not even light can escape, hence the name "black hole" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. This region is known as the event horizon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. Black holes are not "drains" that suck everything in; objects only get captured if they get very close to the event horizon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

Matter approaching a black hole forms an accretion disk, spiraling inwards. This disk can shine intensely, sometimes outshining entire galaxies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. Black holes that are actively feeding at an absurd rate are called Quasars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. The gravitational field around a black hole is so intense that it bends the light from this accretion disk, creating the visible "ring" effect seen in images <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

Small black holes (e.g., house-sized) are more dangerous than supermassive ones because the gravitational difference between two points is extreme, leading to a "spaghettification" effect where objects are pulled apart into atoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. Supermassive black holes have a less drastic gravitational gradient, making entry less destructive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. The Milky Way's central supermassive black hole, Sagittarius A*, is largely inactive, feeding mainly on stellar winds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

## Observing Black Holes
While black holes were mathematically theorized as early as 1918 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>, their experimental verification took decades. The first black holes were detected in the 1950s using radio astronomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. Scientists initially thought their calculations were wrong due to the immense masses detected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

For instance, Quasar 3C 273, located 273 light-years away, was one of the first detected active black holes, shining so brightly it overshadows its host galaxy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>. The name "Quasar" comes from "quasi-stellar radio source" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:07">[04:42:07]</a>.

> "The photo of the black hole that we took is how he is 50 million years ago" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:04">[01:10:04]</a>

This highlights the concept of looking into the past when observing distant astronomical objects due to the finite speed of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:42">[01:08:42]</a>. For example, light from the Sun takes 8 minutes to reach Earth, meaning we always see the Sun as it was 8 minutes ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:54">[01:08:54]</a>.

### Comets
Comets are often described as "the cats of [[astrophysics_and_the_universe | astronomy]]" due to their unpredictable nature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>. They are much larger than most asteroids, starting at 10-20 kilometers in size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>. ### Solar Eclipses
Total solar eclipses occur when the Moon completely blocks the Sun, visible only within a narrow 300 km band <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>. Unlike lunar eclipses, which are visible across an entire hemisphere, solar eclipses require specific geographic positioning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>. Brazil is expected to experience an annular solar eclipse in 2023, where the Moon won't completely cover the Sun, leaving a "ring of fire" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>.
