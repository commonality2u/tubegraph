## The Imposing Nature of the Sea

The deep sea evokes a primal fear, especially when one is far from land and sees only water in every direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a>. This feeling highlights the immense scale of the ocean, which most urban dwellers only encounter at the beach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</a>. Finding even a small object, like a human head, in the high seas is incredibly difficult due to the sheer size of the water mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a>.

Technically, coastal countries have territorial waters extending 12 miles from shore and an economic exploitation zone up to 200 miles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>. Beyond 200 miles lie "international waters," which are considered "no man's land" as they do not belong to any country <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. This legal vacuum means that crimes committed in international waters are typically investigated by the country whose flag the vessel bears <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>. The sea's lack of witnesses and security cameras makes it a "perfect environment for a perfect crime," as bodies often disappear without a trace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:11">[01:18:11]</a>.

Deep-sea environments, especially at depths of 4,000 meters or more, are extremely hostile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:10">[01:19:10]</a>. Pressure can reach 300 atmospheres, a force that can turn thick titanium and carbon fiber into "eggshells" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:59">[00:43:59]</a>. Temperatures are absurdly cold, often comparable to a freezer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:47">[01:14:47]</a>.

### The Titan Submersible Tragedy
The recent [[titanic_and_deepsea_exploration | Titanic]] tourist submersible, named Titan, imploded during a descent to the [[titanic_and_deepsea_exploration | Titanic]] wreckage, killing all five occupants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:17">[00:39:17]</a>. The submersible, unlike a submarine, is not autonomous; it is transported to a site, descends, and then must be rescued <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:12">[00:34:12]</a>. The descent to 4,000 meters takes approximately two hours <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:08">[00:39:08]</a>. Passengers paid $250,000 for the trip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:39">[00:38:39]</a>.

The implosion likely occurred early in the descent on Sunday, June 18, 2023, possibly due to a structural failure, material fatigue, or even the vibrations caused by attempts to signal the surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:53">[00:42:53]</a>. The pressure at that depth is so immense that even a small scratch on the hull could trigger an implosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:52">[00:44:52]</a>. Given the extreme conditions, it is believed no human remains will be recovered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:56">[00:36:56]</a>. The rapid, instantaneous death from implosion is considered the "lesser evil" compared to being trapped alive, slowly running out of air in the freezing darkness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:47">[01:16:47]</a>.

This event has brought to light concerns about "commercial expeditions" to the [[titanic_and_deepsea_exploration | Titanic]], which prioritize profit over stringent scientific safety protocols <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:46">[01:01:46]</a>. The Titan's use of a video game joystick for control and its reliance on Bluetooth connectivity were noted as unusual for such a mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:02">[00:50:02]</a>. The owner of OceanGate, Stockton Rush, who was also a victim, was a major proponent of these expeditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:51">[00:49:51]</a>.

## Treasure Hunts

The sea holds immense "treasures" lost over centuries from countless shipwrecks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:01">[01:42:01]</a>. During the age of exploration, vast quantities of gold, silver, and other valuables were transported by sea from colonized lands like the Americas to Europe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:11">[01:42:11]</a>. Many of these ships sank due to bad weather or pirate attacks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:20">[01:42:20]</a>. It is estimated that only about 5% of these lost treasures have been found <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:34">[01:44:34]</a>.

Laws in most countries, including Brazil, state that anything found on the seabed belongs to the state, discouraging discoverers from reporting finds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:10">[01:43:10]</a>. However, some countries, like the United States and the Bahamas, have more flexible laws allowing finders a share of the treasure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:50">[01:44:50]</a>.

*   **Treasure of Lima**: A famous treasure allegedly looted from Peruvian cathedrals during its independence from Spain, consisting of gold and other artifacts, believed to have been hidden on an island after being attacked by corsairs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:34">[01:47:34]</a>. An expedition is reportedly being assembled to search for it on Trindade Island off the Brazilian coast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:09">[01:49:09]</a>.
*   **Oak Island Treasure**: A well-known legend of a treasure buried on Oak Island off the American coast, with expeditions searching for it for over a century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:55">[01:53:55]</a>.
*   **Coco Islands (Costa Rica)**: Numerous expeditions have searched for treasure on Coco Island, a remote island off Costa Rica <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:42">[01:54:42]</a>.
*   **WWII Treasures**: World War II saw many valuable goods, including art and non-gold assets, transported by sea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:50">[01:50:50]</a>. Frequent attacks on these ships led to significant losses that remain undiscovered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:10">[01:51:10]</a>.

## Other Nautical Phenomena

### Rogue Waves
Also known as "freak waves," these are abnormal, unpredictable giant waves that appear suddenly, even in calm seas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:07:34">[03:07:34]</a>. Unlike typical waves formed by wind, rogue waves are often accumulations of two or more waves that add energy, creating a "mountain of water" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:22">[03:09:22]</a>. They can be found in specific areas like the North Atlantic and off the coast of South Africa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:08:17">[03:08:17]</a>. Historically, sailors' accounts of "monstrous" waves were dismissed until scientific documentation, like the "New Year wave" on a North Sea oil platform, provided proof <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:50">[03:09:50]</a>. Rogue waves can easily capsize and sink even large ships instantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:15">[03:13:15]</a>, explaining why some ships disappear without a trace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:51">[03:13:51]</a>.

### Collision with Whales
Sailboats are more susceptible to colliding with whales than motorboats, as whales avoid engine noise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:50">[01:56:50]</a>. A sleeping whale on the surface may not detect an approaching sailboat, leading to a collision that can damage the vessel like hitting a rock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:00">[01:57:00]</a>. Cases of orcas interacting aggressively with sailboats have also been reported, particularly in the Gibraltar region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:22">[01:57:22]</a>.
