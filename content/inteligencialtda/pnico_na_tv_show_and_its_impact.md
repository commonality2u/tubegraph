Pânico na TV was a highly influential and often controversial Brazilian comedy show that transitioned from radio to television, leaving a significant mark on the country's entertainment landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. Originating from [[pnico_show_and_experiences | Pânico na Rádio]], the show was known for its irreverent humor, boundary-pushing segments, and ability to connect with its audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:54">[00:19:54]</a>.

## From Radio to Television: The Genesis of Pânico na TV

The show's journey began on radio, with personalities like Emílio Surita, Bola, and Bolinha being part of the initial team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:57">[02:49:57]</a>. Marcos Church, known as "Bola," was involved with Pânico from its early days on Jovem Pan radio, joining in February 1993 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:54">[02:24:54]</a>. His entry into the radio world was somewhat accidental, starting with promotional work at Transamérica Radio, then Radio Cidade, before moving to Jovem Pan for similar promotional roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:32">[02:27:32]</a>. His first on-air participation involved giving a "nonsense" opinion during a discussion about marijuana legalization, which led to him becoming a regular contributor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:51">[02:51:51]</a>.

The transition to television was initially not seriously considered by the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:49">[03:29:49]</a>. However, the idea gained traction when the radio show started livestreaming with cameras in the studio, allowing viewers to see the program visually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:03:52">[04:03:52]</a>. This concept, initially a test, eventually led to the creation of Pânico na TV <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:07:07">[04:07:07]</a>. The show's initial move to TV was characterized by a lack of budget, with the director having to buy equipment with his own money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:40:08">[04:40:08]</a>.

## Pânico's Distinctive Humor and Impact

Pânico na TV stood out for its unique brand of humor, which often involved pushing boundaries and interacting directly with the public and celebrities. The show's humor evolved from a spontaneous, "joking around between friends" style on radio to a more structured, yet still unpredictable, television format <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:57">[01:54:57]</a>.

### Breaking Conventions and Audience Engagement

Pânico was instrumental in redefining humor on Brazilian television <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:41:40">[05:41:40]</a>. It broke away from traditional comedy formats, which were often seen as "half the same" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:48:50">[05:48:50]</a>. The show was known for its ability to "take figures and celebrities off their pedestal" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:07">[05:53:07]</a>, approaching them in an unconventional, often provocative, manner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:18">[05:58:18]</a>. This approach allowed the audience to feel like they were part of the group, witnessing backstage antics and inside jokes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:40">[01:57:40]</a>.

A core aspect of Pânico's appeal was its willingness to engage in potentially dangerous or embarrassing stunts for entertainment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:51">[01:06:51]</a>. This included things like eating unusual items, performing physical challenges, and engaging in confrontational interviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:49">[01:53:49]</a>. The show thrived on the unpredictability of these interactions, often leading to genuinely funny and unexpected moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:30">[01:00:30]</a>.

However, this boundary-pushing nature also brought challenges, particularly with the rise of [[impact_of_political_correctness_and_cancel_culture_on_comedy | political correctness and cancel culture]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:10">[01:35:10]</a>. What was considered funny or acceptable in the past became problematic, leading to accusations of insensitivity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:10">[01:35:10]</a>.

### Notable Segments and Characters

Pânico na TV featured a variety of recurring segments and characters that contributed to its popularity:

*   **Pânico Delivery**: A segment where the crew would pick up drunk individuals from parties and drive them home, often with "Panicats" (female assistants) in the car <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:54">[01:04:54]</a>. These segments were unscripted and often led to unpredictable situations, including passengers getting sick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:33">[01:05:33]</a>.
*   **Five Ways (Cinco Maneiras)**: This segment involved performing absurd challenges in five different ways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>. Examples included a "five ways to wax" segment, involving pulling a bag of potatoes with a dog or a Ferrari <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>, and "five ways to give a birthday gift," which included popping a champagne cork into one's forehead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:55">[01:01:55]</a>.
*   **Burial of the Dwarf**: A controversial segment that involved burying a dwarf (a cast member named "Junior" who played a dwarf character) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:02">[00:50:02]</a>. Another version involved throwing him off a trampoline wrapped in paper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:07">[00:50:07]</a>.
*   **Hora da Morte (Time of Death)**: Early successful segment involving risky stunts, which sometimes led to injuries and had to be toned down due to excessive danger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:57">[01:52:57]</a>. One instance involved Marcos Church (Bola) being placed in a coffin that was set on fire, leading to him inhaling smoke <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:19">[01:07:19]</a>.
*   **Jogos Mortais (Deadly Games)**: Inspired by the "Saw" movies, this segment involved various painful challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:13">[01:13:13]</a>. One such challenge involved hula-hooping with razor blades, another involved a paintball shot to the forehead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:58">[01:13:58]</a>.
*   **The Panicat Phenomenon**: The show popularized the "Panicat" role, female assistants known for their beauty and often provocative attire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:35">[01:40:35]</a>. They became a new standard of beauty and were highly sought after, with some even appearing on Playboy covers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:15">[01:41:15]</a>.

## Cast and Collaborations

The show featured a dynamic cast that contributed to its unique style:

*   **Emílio Surita**: The leader and main host of Pânico <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:57">[02:56:57]</a>.
*   **Bola (Marcos Church)**: Known for his spontaneous and often grumpy reactions, he became a central figure due to his willingness to participate in outlandish stunts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:27">[02:57:27]</a>.
*   **Sabrina Sato**: A highly famous personality who joined Pânico after leaving Big Brother Brazil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:30">[01:13:30]</a>. Her presence brought a new dynamic, especially as the show's only prominent female member, and she participated in many challenging segments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:41">[01:14:41]</a>.
*   **Ceará (Wellington Muniz)**: Known for his character work and impressions, he joined Pânico after being discovered in Fortaleza <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:35:46">[02:35:46]</a>.
*   **Carioca (Márvio Lúcio)**: Another key cast member known for his characters and comedic improvisations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:57">[02:49:57]</a>.
*   **Ricardo Barros**: The show's director on TV, known for his direct approach and focus on results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:07">[03:40:07]</a>.

## Network Changes and Evolution

Pânico na TV first gained significant traction on RedeTV!, where it achieved high audience ratings, often competing directly with established shows on major networks like Globo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:50:00">[05:50:00]</a>. The show's success on RedeTV! was phenomenal, sometimes even surpassing the audience of shows like "Fantástico" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:55:04">[05:55:04]</a>.

After a successful run, the show moved to Band (Rede Bandeirantes) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:41">[01:35:41]</a>. This move marked a new phase for Pânico, but also brought challenges related to maintaining audience and content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:10">[01:39:10]</a>. The cast members were employed by the production company (Tutinha's company) rather than directly by the TV networks, providing them with a unique level of autonomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:00">[01:52:00]</a>.

## Legacy and Reflection

Pânico na TV's [[Podcast and Media Influence | influence]] on Brazilian media and comedy is undeniable. It was a pioneer in a more irreverent and direct style of humor, influencing subsequent generations of comedians and shows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:44:00">[05:44:00]</a>.

Despite its success, the show experienced the pressures of maintaining high audience ratings, especially in later years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:22">[02:29:22]</a>. The constant demand for new, shocking content, coupled with increasing public scrutiny and the [[impact_of_political_correctness_and_cancel_culture_on_comedy | evolution of cultural sensibilities]], led to a sense of "wear and tear" within the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:27">[02:29:27]</a>. Many cast members continued to pursue individual careers in comedy and media after the show's conclusion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:21">[02:33:21]</a>.

Pânico na TV remains a significant chapter in the [[the_evolution_of_brazilian_television_and_radio | history of Brazilian television and radio]], remembered for its daring humor, memorable characters, and its ability to connect with a mass audience through unconventional means.
