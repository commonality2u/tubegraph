## Dispelling Myths: Mary Magdalene was NOT a Prostitute

The widespread idea that Mary Magdalene was a prostitute originated with Pope Gregory the Great in 591 AD during a homily, where he confused her with the adulterous woman from Revelation Chapter 8 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:47">[01:57:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:59">[01:59:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>. This confusion led to the popular notion that she was a repentant prostitute, which has become deeply ingrained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:21">[01:19:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:26">[01:21:26]</a>.

It is important to distinguish between an adulterous woman and a prostitute:
*   A **prostitute** is a sex worker who earns money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:52">[01:18:52]</a>.
*   An **adulterous woman** is a married woman who has a lover <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:02">[01:19:02]</a>. A single woman cannot be an adulteress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:07">[01:19:07]</a>.

The woman who anointed Jesus in Luke 7:36-50 was identified as a "sinner" (Greek: hamartei), which implied a difficulty with gravity or a secret sin, not necessarily prostitution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:44">[01:57:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:00">[01:58:00]</a>. The fact that the Pharisee (Simon) needed to consider if Jesus was a prophet to know she was a sinner indicates her sin was not public knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:13">[01:00:13]</a>.

## Mary Magdalene's Historical Role

Mary Magdalene managed to be a prominent woman in a male-dominated (patriarchal) universe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:44">[01:44:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:17">[01:46:17]</a>. Her prominence was endorsed by Jesus of Nazareth, which speaks to the value God and Christ gave to women <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:50">[01:52:50]</a>. She serves as a prototype for how God and the Bible view women, dispelling the notion that the Bible is sexist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:03">[01:06:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:08">[01:06:08]</a>. The biblical view is that woman is the "crown of creation," created after man not as subordinate, but as the polished and finished work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:09">[01:07:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:11">[01:07:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:55">[01:07:55]</a>.

Mary Magdalene achieved financial independence in a male-dominated world, able to negotiate, have a profession, buy things, and have money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:00">[01:08:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:02">[01:08:02]</a>. She was one of Jesus's followers who used her money to finance His ministry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:33">[01:08:33]</a>.

She is the only woman from that antiquity called by her city name (Magdala), a privilege usually reserved for men (e.g., Jesus of Nazareth, Paul of Tarsus, Judas Iscariot) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:30">[01:06:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:38">[01:06:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:37">[01:07:37]</a>. This indicates her recognized importance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:57">[01:07:57]</a>.

Scholars suggest Mary Magdalene was a leader of women and a Christian leadership figure in the early church, inspiring other women to serve and help <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:20">[01:31:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:26">[01:31:26]</a>. She inaugurated a form of female leadership that was uncommon in Judaism of that time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:49">[01:31:49]</a>.

## Biblical Accounts of Mary Magdalene

### The First Anointing and Forgiveness (Luke 7:36-50)
Mary Magdalene is strongly associated with the unnamed "sinful woman" who anoints Jesus' feet at the house of Simon the Pharisee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:08">[01:53:08]</a>. Simon, a Pharisee in Galilee, invited Jesus to dinner, but did not properly welcome him (no water for feet, no kiss of greeting, no oil for head) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:38">[01:55:38]</a>. The woman, identified as a "sinner," entered the house, washed Jesus' feet with her tears, dried them with her hair, kissed them, and anointed them with expensive perfume <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:15">[01:53:15]</a>.

This woman's sin was not publicly known; Simon assumed Jesus, if truly a prophet, would know she was a sinner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:13">[01:00:13]</a>. Psychobiographical analysis suggests she may have been a victim of childhood sexual violence by family members, leading to feelings of guilt and shame <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:48">[01:01:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:57">[01:01:57]</a>. Jesus' declaration, "Your sins are forgiven," healed her psychological and emotional wounds, restoring her self-esteem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:07">[01:02:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:10">[01:02:10]</a>.

### Casting Out Seven Demons (Luke 8:1-3)
After this episode, the Gospel of Luke states that Mary Magdalene was among the women Jesus had cured of evil spirits and illnesses, "from whom seven demons came out" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:02">[01:04:02]</a>. This is interpreted as a "Stockholm syndrome" like experience, where the initial "demon" of trauma returned with six others, leading to a deeper state of self-loathing and a belief that she deserved the abuse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:45">[01:04:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:07">[01:05:07]</a>. Jesus performed another act of spiritual and psychological restoration, freeing her from these recurring "demons" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:02">[01:06:02]</a>.

### The Second Anointing in Bethany (John 12:1-8)
Mary Magdalene again anointed Jesus' feet with precious perfume, this time in Bethany, days before his crucifixion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:16">[01:16:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:23">[01:16:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:48">[01:16:48]</a>. This anointing, different from the first, was an act of worship and prophetic insight into Jesus' impending death and burial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:10">[01:21:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:13">[01:21:13]</a>. While the disciples were celebrating Jesus' entry as a king, Mary Magdalene discerned the gravity of the situation and anointed Him for burial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:31">[01:36:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:40">[01:36:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:20">[01:42:20]</a>.

### At the Cross and the Empty Tomb
Mary Magdalene was one of the few who remained at the foot of the cross until Jesus' death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:14">[01:32:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:59">[01:41:59]</a>. Initially, she did not believe in the resurrection, thinking Jesus' body had been stolen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:28">[01:48:28]</a>. She was then tasked to tell the disciples about His resurrection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:41">[01:51:41]</a>.

## Gnostic Views on Mary Magdalene

The Gnostics (from Greek "gnosis" meaning knowledge) were a group that sought to create a "more palatable" and mystical Christianity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:37">[01:37:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:40">[01:37:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:25">[01:38:25]</a>. They believed Jesus had secret teachings for an elite group <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:32">[01:38:32]</a>. In their view, the spiritual world was pure, while the material world was perverted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:37">[02:07:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:41">[02:07:41]</a>. They did not believe in the Incarnation (God becoming flesh), but rather that the "spirit of Christ" entered the man Jesus at his baptism and left him at the crucifixion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:58">[02:07:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:03">[02:08:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:39">[02:08:39]</a>.

In this context, the Gnostics praised the "sacred feminine," influenced by pagan religions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:13">[01:39:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:16">[01:39:16]</a>. They associated Mary Magdalene with Sophia (feminine wisdom), presenting her as a mystical companion of Jesus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:26">[01:46:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:38">[01:46:38]</a>. Some Gnostic movements saw Sophia as God the Father's wife who generated Jesus, while others placed her as Jesus' wife <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:50">[01:46:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:52">[01:46:52]</a>.

However, no Gnostic Gospel, including the Gospel of Philip or the Gospel of Mary Magdalene, speaks of a literal "royal wedding" or physical relationship between Jesus and Mary Magdalene <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:18">[01:47:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:21">[01:47:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:03:32">[02:03:32]</a>. Mentions of Jesus kissing her on the mouth, or loving her "more than others," are interpretations that do not imply an erotic relationship, but rather a closer, more attentive student-teacher dynamic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:37">[01:47:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:51">[01:47:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:04">[01:50:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:09">[01:50:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:11">[01:50:11]</a>.

Academic consensus holds that there is no credible evidence to support the claim that Mary Magdalene was Jesus's wife or lover <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:52">[02:00:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:03:22">[02:03:22]</a>.
