## Historical Layers of Jerusalem

Jerusalem's history is layered with occupations and transformations. It was originally called Salem around 2000-1800 BC, ruled by King Melchizedek, and met by Abraham <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:58:00">[07:58:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:56:00">[08:56:00]</a>. Later, it was occupied by the Jebusites and became known as the City of Jebus or Jerusalem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:10:00">[09:10:00]</a>. The oldest Hebrew version bearing the name Jerusalem is a papyrus from the 10th-8th century BC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:19:00">[09:19:00]</a>, while a cuneiform writing from the 14th century BC also mentions the city <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:38:00">[09:38:00]</a>.

Under King David in the 10th century BC, the Fortress of the Jebusites was conquered, and David bought land, later known as the threshing floor of Araunah, where Abraham is believed to have made a sacrifice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:32:00">[10:32:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:51:00">[10:51:00]</a>. This location is also identified with Moriah, a fundamental area for monotheistic tradition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:11:00">[22:11:00]</a>. David's son, Solomon, later built the Temple on this site <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:11:00">[11:11:00]</a>.

Jerusalem has faced numerous destructions and reconstructions:
*   **587/586 BC**: Destroyed by Nebuchadnezzar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:16:00">[11:16:00]</a>.
*   **538 BC**: Rebuilt under Persian rule during the time of Nehemiah <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:28:00">[11:28:00]</a>.
*   **Hellenistic Period**: Conquered again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:44:00">[11:44:00]</a>.
*   **64 BC**: Roman domination under Pompey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:01:00">[12:01:00]</a>.
*   **70 AD**: Destroyed again by the Romans, prohibiting Jews from living there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>.
*   **132-135 AD**: After a Jewish rebellion, Emperor Hadrian built a pagan temple to Jupiter and renamed the city Aelia Capitolina <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:39:00">[12:39:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:51:00">[12:51:00]</a>. He also renamed the region "Philistine" (Palestine) to provoke the Jews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:29:00">[13:29:00]</a>.
*   **Byzantine Period**: Jerusalem became Christianized under Constantine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:32:00">[14:32:00]</a>.
*   **690 AD**: Taken by Muslims, becoming the third holiest city in Islam <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:09:00">[14:09:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:12:00">[14:12:00]</a>. The Dome of the Rock, built in 690 AD, is believed to be the place where Muhammad ascended to heaven <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:10:00">[25:10:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:33:00">[25:33:00]</a>.
*   **Crusades (1099-1187)**: Christians attempted to regain control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:26:00">[14:26:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:14:00">[40:14:00]</a>.
*   **Ottoman Period (1517-1917)**: The Ottomans ruled Jerusalem for 400 years, the longest continuous rule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:44:00">[14:44:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:53:00">[14:53:00]</a>.
*   **1948**: Creation of the State of Israel, allowing Jews to return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:17:00">[15:17:00]</a>.

## Archaeology and Historical Texts

[[archaeology_and_paleontology | Archaeology]] plays a crucial role in validating [[historical_accuracy_and_interpretation_of_religious_texts | historical and biblical narratives]]. Discoveries continually prove the stories and locations mentioned in ancient texts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:02:00">[34:02:00]</a>. For instance, the "City of David" archaeological site in Jerusalem is linked to the era of Melchizedek and Abraham <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:07:00">[08:07:00]</a>.

### The Dead Sea Scrolls

One of the most significant [[historical_religious_artifacts_and_archaeology | archaeological finds]] of the 20th century is the Dead Sea Scrolls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.
*   **Discovery**: Accidentally found by a young boy named Mohamed in 1948 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:20">[03:12:20]</a>, the same year the State of Israel was founded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:24">[03:12:24]</a>.
*   **Content and Preservation**: These scrolls contain nearly all Old Testament books (except Esther and fragments of Ecclesiastes) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:16">[02:41:16]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:25">[02:42:25]</a>. Comparing the 270 BC Isaiah scroll with later texts, minimal differences demonstrate the Jews' meticulous preservation of the Bible over 1,200 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:58">[03:29:58]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:32:02">[03:32:02]</a>.
*   **Linguistic Significance**: The scrolls highlight the development of Hebrew writing. While Moses likely wrote early books in a primitive alphabetical script, later copyists transitioned to the "square" Babylonian alphabet used in the Dead Sea Scrolls, but the phonetic integrity remained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
*   **Impact on Biblical Study**: The Dead Sea Scrolls provide extraordinary insight into the 1st century Jewish world, helping to understand the background of both the Old and New Testaments and the life of Jesus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:23">[02:42:23]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:51">[02:42:51]</a>.

> "God made it so that it is there, Never stop being read if Moses had not written genesis in that language... God already knew that the people needed it, they couldn't put it here" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:48:53">[03:48:53]</a>

### Other Archaeological Insights

*   **Hezekiah's Wall**: Parts of King Hezekiah's wall from the 8th century BC can still be seen in Jerusalem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:43">[03:14:43]</a>.
*   **Pool of Siloam and Hezekiah's Tunnel**: The Pool of Siloam, where Jesus healed a blind man, and Hezekiah's Tunnel are also significant sites <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:07">[03:26:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:27:00">[03:27:00]</a>.
*   **Temple Stairs**: The remnants of the stairs leading to the Second Temple during Jesus' time are still visible, making it highly probable that Jesus walked them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:30">[03:36:30]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:37:37">[03:37:37]</a>.
*   **Biblical Prophecy Validation**: Archaeological findings often confirm [[religious_texts_and_interpretations | biblical prophecies]]. For example, the prophecy in Ezekiel about Tyre's destruction and its stones being thrown into the sea, becoming a bare rock for fishermen, is archaeologically verifiable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:15">[01:45:15]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:38">[01:45:38]</a>. Recent explorations confirm these ancient ruins are underwater, with fisherman's nets drying on top of the ancient capitals, just as prophesied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:56">[01:45:56]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:20">[01:46:20]</a>.

## Modern Preservation and Access

Israel is dedicated to preserving its [[historical_and_biblical_perspectives | historical and biblical heritage]]. The Israel Museum houses many of the Dead Sea Scrolls in the Shrine of the Book <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:24">[03:25:24]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:24">[02:29:24]</a>. Efforts are also underway to build a biblical [[archaeology_and_paleontology | archaeology museum]] in Brazil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:05:47">[03:05:47]</a>.

Despite its complex history and ongoing geopolitical conflicts, Israel ensures access and respect for various religious and historical sites <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:05">[01:06:05]</a>. Protestant, Muslim, and Jewish places are all respected within Israel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:55">[01:05:55]</a>. This openness allows for a unique learning experience, blending historical study with on-site exploration for individuals of all backgrounds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:11">[01:29:11]</a>.
