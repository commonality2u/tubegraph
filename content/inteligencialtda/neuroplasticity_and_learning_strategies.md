## Understanding Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is defined as the change of cells in the brain via experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:35">[01:35:35]</a>. Learning is fundamentally dependent on [[Neuroscience and brain function|neuroplasticity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:30">[01:43:30]</a>.

The process involves a [[Neuroscience and brain function|glutamate]] synapse, where the neurotransmitter [[Neuroscience and brain function|glutamate]] is released into the synaptic cleft, binding to AMPA receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:19">[01:44:19]</a>. This causes an influx of sodium, depolarizing the postsynaptic neuron's membrane and activating NMDA receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:36">[01:44:36]</a>. This chain reaction, including calcium influx, activates internal enzymes and proteins, which signal the neuron's DNA to produce new AMPA channels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:40">[01:45:40]</a>. These new channels make the neuron more sensitive, allowing for connections with less neurotransmitter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:06">[01:46:06]</a>.
