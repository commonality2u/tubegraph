## Habit Formation and the Brain
Forming a habit, particularly for exercise, takes an average of 66 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:56">[00:06:56]</a>. This duration is necessary for the brain to learn that an activity is something you *have* to do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:18">[00:07:18]</a>. This process involves two brain structures working together:
*   **Dorsal Striatum (orange jelly bean):** Transforms what brings well-being into routine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:39">[00:07:39]</a>.
*   **Nucleus Accumbens (purple jelly bean):** Realizes what brings well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>.

Habits can also form from repetition, even if they don't immediately bring well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:49">[00:07:49]</a>. The basic unit of memory related to reward is called an "engram" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>. To form a new habit, one must expend energy to "destroy" an existing reward memory and rebuild a new one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>.

For example, to replace a smoking habit, a patient might be asked to run on a treadmill or use a bike for 30 minutes, or engage in high-intensity interval training (HIIT) for 8 minutes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. This raises endorphins, providing an immediate reward feeling that helps the brain "forget" the cigarette <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>. The brain then learns to replace one pleasure with another <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:27">[00:09:27]</a>.

## The Craving Response
The amygdala (red jelly bean) is responsible for the "irritation of the sweet" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>. It perceives discomfort and signals the medial prefrontal cortex (green jelly bean) to seek immediate reward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. When the medial prefrontal cortex is cancelled out, individuals seek 100% reward, leading to behaviors like overeating or drug use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>.

A craving typically lasts around three minutes. Waiting this period can significantly decrease the urge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>.

## [[Sleep_and_Health_Impacts | Sleep and Health Impacts]]
The body has a metabolic clock, the circadian cycle, which dictates optimal times for waking, training, and eating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:39">[01:40:39]</a>. Hormones responsible for sleep quality are produced as it gets dark <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:20">[01:41:20]</a>. Disrupting this cycle, for example, by sleeping late, negatively impacts hormone release (e.g., testosterone peak is 7-9 AM) and overall daily function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:15">[01:37:15]</a>.

Sleep debt is cumulative; losing one hour of sleep per night means owing a full night's sleep per week <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:07">[01:38:07]</a>. [[sleep_and_health_impacts | Sleep restriction]] is a significant factor for developing insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:32">[01:38:32]</a>. Athletes, in particular, need sufficient sleep for body repair and maintenance due to high performance demands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:08">[01:46:08]</a>.

Polyphasic sleep, though practiced by some historical figures, is generally not recommended as a healthy lifestyle choice for most people, but rather a survival mechanism in extreme situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:37">[02:31:37]</a>.

## Training Principles
*   **Cadence:** Refers to the speed at which an exercise is executed, whether resisting gravity (eccentric phase) or fighting against it (concentric phase) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:55">[02:18:55]</a>.
*   **Intensity:** Can be increased through weight, interval between sets, cadence, exercise combinations, and enhancing exercises with more or less load within the same set <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:56">[02:17:56]</a>.
*   **Isometry:** Especially in shortening (maximum muscle contraction), improves contraction signaling and coordination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:19:47">[02:19:47]</a>.
*   **Partial Repetitions:** Allow for working with a larger load but require mastery of the load <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:51">[02:20:51]</a>. Shortening (end phase) is preferred over the initial (stretching) phase to decrease injury risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:11">[02:21:11]</a>.
*   **Machine vs. Free Weights:** Machines aid beginners by simplifying exercises and reducing the risk of incorrect execution, while free weights offer greater variety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:35">[02:01:35]</a>.
*   **Muscle Activation:** Understanding which muscles are being activated during exercise is crucial; without this knowledge, steroids will only lead to side effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:10">[02:25:10]</a>.
*   **Rest between sets:** Varies from 30 seconds to 2-3 minutes for heavier sets, longer for those without good conditioning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:32">[02:17:32]</a>.
