## Debunking the Prostitute Myth
A widespread but incorrect idea is that Mary Magdalene was a prostitute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:47">[01:57:47]</a>. This notion was created by Pope Gregory the Great in 591 AD during a homily, where he confused Mary Magdalene with an adulterous woman mentioned in Revelation Chapter 8 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>, who was also not a prostitute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:00">[01:37:00]</a>.
*   A prostitute is a sex worker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:00">[01:46:00]</a>.
*   An adulterous woman is a married woman who has a lover <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>. A single woman cannot be an adulteress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:00">[01:48:00]</a>.

Pope Gregory's error integrated these separate figures, leading to the long-held misconception that Mary Magdalene was a repentant sinner, often depicted with a "Mary Magdalene face" implying repentance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:00">[01:28:00]</a>. Despite this, she was not canonized as a saint in the Catholic Church until Pope Francis recognized her in July (her day) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>, designating her as the "apostle to the apostles" (*apostola apostolorum*) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:00">[01:59:00]</a>. She had already been canonized in the Orthodox Church <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>.

## Prominence and Women's Role in Christianity
Mary Magdalene achieved prominence in a universe dominated by men, with her importance endorsed by Jesus of Nazareth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>. This highlights the value God and Christ placed on women, consistently uplifting them from historical subjugation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:00">[00:34:00]</a>.

### [[gender_roles_in_biblical_times | Biblical Perspective on Women]]
The [[biblical_interpretation_of_mary_magdalene | Biblical interpretation of Mary Magdalene]] suggests she is a prototype of how God and the Bible truly see women <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:00">[00:39:00]</a>. Criticisms that the Bible is sexist, particularly regarding the creation story where Eve was created after Adam, are often based on anachronistic readings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:00">[00:46:00]</a>.
*   The Hebrew verbs for creation differentiate: God "baked" (Y to bake) Adam like a clay jar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:00">[01:07:00]</a>, but "formed" (banana) Eve, implying a more polished and finished creation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:00">[01:14:00]</a>.
*   Adam named Eve "she who carries life" (Eva/Havah in Hebrew) after the sentence of death, indicating her role in bringing forth the Messiah, demonstrating a profound honor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>.
*   This perspective counters the anachronistic application of modern "patriarchal oppression" concepts to a historical context where women had a significant place of importance in God's eyes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>.

## [[conspiracy_theories_about_mary_magdalene | Conspiracy Theories]] and [[gnostic_views_on_mary_magdalene | Gnostic Views]]
The delay in Mary Magdalene's Catholic canonization fueled [[conspiracy_theories_about_mary_magdalene | conspiracy theories]] that the church sought to suppress her due to her growing power and potential threat to the patriarchy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:00">[01:55:00]</a>. These theories claim she was Jesus's wife and that the church hid a lineage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:00">[02:08:00]</a>.

### Dan Brown's *The Da Vinci Code*
Dan Brown's *The Da Vinci Code* popularized the idea of a hidden lineage of Jesus with Mary Magdalene, with the story implying they had descendants in France (Merovingians) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:00">[02:11:00]</a>. However, this theory lacks historical proof and is criticized for its numerous factual errors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:00">[02:17:00]</a>. The book's popularity stemmed from its ability to arouse curiosity and controversy, using elements of adventure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:00">[02:46:00]</a>.

### [[gnostic_views_on_mary_magdalene | Gnostic Interpretations]]
The [[gnostic_views_on_mary_magdalene | Gnostics]], an early Christian current, sought to create a more palatable, mystical form of Christianity for the Greco-Roman world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:00">[03:14:00]</a>. They claimed Jesus had secret teachings revealed only to an elite few possessing "gnosis" (knowledge) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:00">[03:36:00]</a>.
*   Within this context, Mary Magdalene was praised as a figure embodying the "sacred feminine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:27:00">[03:27:00]</a>, often associated with Greek/Roman goddesses like Artemis due to pagan religions' emphasis on powerful female deities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>.
*   She was seen as a "mystical companion" to Jesus, a "materialization of feminine wisdom" or "Sophia," rather than a physical wife <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:36:00">[04:36:00]</a>.
*   Gnostic gospels, such as the *Gospel of Philip* and the *Gospel of Mary Magdalene*, do not speak of a literal marriage between Jesus and Mary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:15:00">[04:15:00]</a>. A phrase like "Jesus kissed her on the mouth" from the *Gospel of Philip* is often misinterpreted; kisses were a normal greeting in the Middle East, and it signified her closeness as a student <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:52:00">[04:52:00]</a>.

## Her "Seven Demons" and Potential Trauma
Mary Magdalene is described in the Bible as having "seven demons cast out of her" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:08:00">[04:08:00]</a>. Through psychobiography (a historical-psychological analysis of past figures), it's hypothesized that these "seven demons" might refer to the severe psychological trauma resulting from childhood sexual violence, likely within her own home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:48:00">[03:48:00]</a>.
*   The term "sinner" (hamartia in Greek) applied to the woman who anointed Jesus's feet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:00">[00:57:00]</a> did not necessarily imply prostitution, but rather someone who had difficulties with moral gravity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:00">[00:58:00]</a>.
*   This suggests her sin was not public knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>. In biblical times, any personal problem was believed to be a result of secret sin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:00">[01:07:00]</a>.
*   The interpretation suggests that after being "cleansed" by Jesus, she returned to her abusive home, leading to a relapse of trauma, symbolized by the "seven demons" returning, worse than before <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:25:00">[04:25:00]</a>. This parallels Stockholm Syndrome, where a victim may feel guilt or believe they deserve abuse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:52:00">[04:52:00]</a>. Jesus's forgiveness was crucial for her healing, as she felt she needed it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:07:00">[05:07:07]</a>.

## Anointing of Jesus and Self-Esteem Restoration
Mary Magdalene is prominently featured in two anointing events of Jesus, which highlight her journey of restored self-esteem and devotion.

### First Anointing (Galilee)
In Luke Chapter 7, a "sinful woman of the city" (interpreted as Mary Magdalene) anoints Jesus's feet with perfume and her hair at the house of Simon the Pharisee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:32:00">[05:32:00]</a>.
*   Simon, the host, did not extend traditional hospitality to Jesus (washing feet, anointing with oil) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:40:00">[05:40:00]</a>.
*   The woman's intimate presence in Simon's house and the lack of public knowledge of her "sin" (only Simon's silent judgment) support the idea of a private trauma, likely within that very household <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:54:00">[05:54:00]</a>.
*   Jesus uses a parable of two debtors to explain that the one forgiven a greater debt shows more love, implying her deep gratitude for His acceptance and forgiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:21:00">[06:21:00]</a>. This act shows her immense gratitude and willingness to break social conventions for her savior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:40:00">[06:40:00]</a>.

### Second Anointing (Bethany)
In John Chapter 12, days before Jesus's crucifixion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>, Mary (sister of Lazarus, believed by some to be Mary Magdalene) again anoints Jesus's feet, this time in Bethany <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:00">[01:23:00]</a>.
*   This anointing was a lavish act, using expensive perfume worth a year's wages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>.
*   Unlike the first anointing, this was not about sin, but an act of worship and recognition of Jesus's impending death and kingship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:00">[01:25:00]</a>.
*   Judas Iscariot complains about the waste, suggesting the money could have been given to the poor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:00">[01:25:00]</a>, but his true motive was greed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:00">[01:45:00]</a>.
*   Mary Magdalene's theological insight allowed her to understand the gravity of Jesus's upcoming passion and crucifixion, an understanding that even the disciples lacked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:00">[01:36:00]</a>.

## Loyalty and the Resurrection
Mary Magdalene remained loyal to Jesus through His [[events_surrounding_the_birth_and_crucifixion_of_jesus | events surrounding the birth and crucifixion of Jesus]].
*   She was one of the few who stayed at the foot of the cross until the end <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:00">[01:41:00]</a>, while most disciples fled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:00">[01:41:00]</a>.
*   She was the first to visit Jesus's tomb on Sunday morning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a> to complete the embalming of His body, a custom for dignified burial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. This act shows her unwavering devotion even after His death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>.
*   She is the first recorded witness to the resurrection of Jesus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>. Initially, she didn't recognize Him, thinking He was the gardener <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>. When Jesus spoke her name, "Mary," she recognized Him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:00">[01:50:00]</a>.
*   Jesus told her, "Do not hold me back" (or "Do not touch me" in some translations, which became the title of a famous Caravaggio painting) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>. This implied she was clinging to Him in ecstasy, and He needed to ascend to His Father <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>. He then sent her to tell the disciples about His resurrection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>.

After the resurrection, Mary Magdalene's ultimate fate is not explicitly detailed in the Bible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. *   Later medieval legends, popularized by Dan Brown, suggest she fled to Gaul (France) pregnant with Jesus's daughter, accompanied by Joseph of Arimathea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:00">[01:58:00]</a>. However, these are late legends without biblical or academic basis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:00">[01:58:00]</a>.

She inspired other women to serve, set up groups, and pray, establishing a form of leadership uncommon for women in the Judaism of that era <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:37:00">[03:37:00]</a>.
*   She gained financial independence and used her resources to support Jesus's ministry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:33:00">[03:33:00]</a>.
