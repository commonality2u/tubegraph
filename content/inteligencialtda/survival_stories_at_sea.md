## The Vastness and Dangers of the Sea
The ocean is described as being "very big, very big" and its immense size is often underestimated by most people, especially those who only experience the sea from a beach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:26">[00:08:26]</a>. The sea occupies 70% of the planet's surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

The "high seas" are technically considered to be beyond 200 miles from a country's coast, existing in "international waters" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. These waters are "no man's land" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>, belonging to no country and therefore lacking traditional jurisdiction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>. This unique status means that if a crime is committed on a boat in international waters, the country of the boat's flag is typically responsible for investigation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:56">[00:06:56]</a>.

The ocean's vastness contributes to many unexplained disappearances and makes rescue operations incredibly difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>. A human being floating in the sea, only showing their head, is "less than a point" in the immense expanse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>. Even a 5-meter boat can go unnoticed for months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:19">[00:17:19]</a>.

Once someone falls into the high seas, survival is extremely unlikely due to factors like the body's inability to withstand temperatures and physical effort, leading to hypothermia and death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:04">[00:18:04]</a>. The sea is described as "a perfect environment for perfect crime because there are no witnesses" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:09">[00:18:09]</a>.

### Respecting the Sea
The sea "demands respect, much respect" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:29">[01:32:29]</a>. ### José Salvador Alvarenga (Salvadoran Fisherman)
In 2014, a Salvadoran fisherman living on the coast of Mexico, José Salvador Alvarenga, went fishing in an open fiberglass canoe with an outboard motor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:41">[00:09:41]</a>. A storm turned the weather bad, the engine failed, and they were carried away by wind and current <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:05">[00:10:05]</a>. They had no water or food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. His companion died of starvation and dehydration after three or four months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:02">[00:11:02]</a>. Alvarenga, however, survived for 14 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>.

He primarily subsisted on turtles, which are relatively easy to catch as they come to the surface to breathe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:31">[01:12:31]</a>. He also occasionally caught flying fish that jumped into the boat at night <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:53">[01:12:53]</a>. He drank rainwater and turtle blood for hydration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:20">[01:22:20]</a>, but never seawater, as it dehydrates the body <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:30">[01:26:30]</a>. He developed techniques, including hiding in a Styrofoam box (also used for sun protection) to catch birds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:50">[01:51:50]</a>.

Carried by currents, he drifted from the coast of Mexico to the Marshall Islands, located on the other side of the Pacific <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:05">[01:14:05]</a>. Upon arrival, he was naked and encountered a couple who didn't speak his language <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:32">[01:14:32]</a>. He was found in "good condition" for someone who had spent so long at sea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:59">[01:14:59]</a>.

His story, detailed in his book "438 Days," was met with skepticism, with some questioning if he committed cannibalism to survive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:32">[01:15:32]</a>. He even underwent a lie detector test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:58">[01:15:58]</a>. Later, his deceased companion's family sued him, reigniting suspicions of cannibalism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:48">[01:16:48]</a>. He denies it, stating his friend died from nausea and inability to eat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:59">[01:21:59]</a>.

### Jean-Jacques Savin (The Man in the Barrel)
A French gentleman in his early 70s designed a barrel-like vessel, 3 meters long, equipped with solar panels and navigation equipment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:25">[02:00:25]</a>. With no propulsion, he relied on the ocean currents to cross the Atlantic from the Canary Islands to the Caribbean, a well-known route due to predictable currents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:40">[01:59:40]</a>. He spent over three months adrift <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:32">[02:01:32]</a>. He arrived safely and healthy, only needing to be towed at the end to avoid crashing on rocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:50">[02:01:50]</a>. Tragically, he disappeared two years later on a subsequent, simpler kayak trip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:03:36">[02:03:36]</a>.

### Chinese Man (WWII Survivor)
During World War II, a Chinese man survived over four months adrift on a wooden raft after his cargo ship was torpedoed off the coast of Africa by a German submarine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:43:19">[02:43:19]</a>. He had "nothing nothing nothing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:07">[02:44:07]</a>. He developed numerous survival techniques, including using a piece of canvas as a cover from the sun, a rainwater collector, and a bird trap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:33">[02:44:33]</a>. He improvised a hook from a staple and fished <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:45:05">[02:45:05]</a>. ## Tragedies and Mysteries at Sea
### [[maritime_disasters_and_exploration | Submersible Titan Tragedy]]
The recent tragedy involving the submersible Titan, which was on a tourist expedition to the [[historical_shipwrecks_and_maritime_legends | Titanic]] wreck, captivated global attention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:54">[00:39:54]</a>. The Titan was a submersible, not a submarine, meaning it wasn't autonomous and needed to be transported and rescued by a surface ship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:56">[00:33:56]</a>. The Titanic lies at a depth of almost 4,000 meters (4 km) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:59">[00:35:59]</a>, where pressure is 300 times greater than at the surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:12">[00:36:12]</a>.

It is highly likely that the submersible imploded during its descent on Sunday morning, shortly after losing contact with the surface vessel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:48">[00:42:48]</a>. This implosion would have been instantaneous and catastrophic, leaving no human remains due to the immense pressure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:28">[00:36:28]</a>. Debris found approximately 500 meters from the Titanic wreck suggests the implosion occurred before reaching the full depth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:50">[01:06:50]</a>.

The expedition itself was controversial. The submersible was controlled by a video game joystick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:59">[00:49:59]</a>, and there were concerns about its structural integrity and safety protocols, as it was designed for profit rather than purely scientific purposes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:29">[00:50:29]</a>. The lack of an internal way for passengers to open the hatch also meant they were reliant on external support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:54">[00:39:54]</a>.

Other notable facts about the Titan:
*   The dive cost $250,000 per person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:39">[00:38:39]</a>.
*   The entire operation was scheduled for 8 hours, with a 2-hour descent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:03">[00:39:03]</a>.
*   Inside, passengers sat on the floor in a narrow cylinder, with limited visibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:46">[00:40:46]</a>.

### Rogue Waves

Unlike typical swells, they are unpredictable "mountains of water" that can appear suddenly in calm seas and capsize large ships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:08:42">[03:08:42]</a>.
*   **München (1978):** A large cargo ship that sent a distress call in the North Atlantic and was believed to have been hit by a rogue wave around 20 meters high, deduced from how high the lifeboat was torn off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:40">[03:06:40]</a>.
*   **MV Derbyshire (1980):** This ship disappeared off the coast of Africa, with the most plausible explanation being that it was struck by a rogue wave and sank immediately, leaving no trace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:44">[03:12:44]</a>.

## Debunking Maritime Myths and Legends
### The Bermuda Triangle
The Bermuda Triangle, described as a "great fiction" and "pure pure fiction" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:15:02">[03:15:02]</a>, is an area in the Caribbean (formed by Bermuda Islands, Puerto Rico, and Florida) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:17:09">[03:17:09]</a>. ### Ghost Ships
Real "ghost ships" are vessels found adrift without a crew <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:56">[02:06:56]</a>. One famous example is the Bima, a ship found repeatedly in different parts of Alaska over the years, adrift without anyone, having been abandoned and pushed by ice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:37">[02:08:37]</a>. While some traditional [[historical_shipwrecks_and_maritime_legends | legends]] like the Caleuche (Chilean ghost ship) exist, these are often rooted in unexplained real-world disappearances that offer room for imagination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:37">[02:09:37]</a>.
