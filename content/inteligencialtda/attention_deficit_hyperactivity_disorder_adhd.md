Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is classified as a [[neuroscience_of_addiction_and_behavior | neurodevelopmental disorder]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:26">[01:20:26]</a>. It is characterized by a different brain development compared to individuals without the disorder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:37">[01:37:37]</a>. ADHD affects approximately 3-5% of the population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:13">[01:35:13]</a>.

The perceived increase in ADHD diagnoses might be due to our current multitasking society highlighting it more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:29">[01:34:29]</a>. A person whose life was more relaxed, like a supermarket owner in an earlier era, might not have had their inattention become a problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:41">[01:34:41]</a>. However, in today's context of software development, scheduling, social media, and numerous responsibilities, it becomes more evident <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:53">[01:34:53]</a>.

## Symptoms of ADHD

Common symptoms of ADHD include:
*   **Inattention:** Individuals with ADHD don't necessarily have *little* attention; rather, they have *a lot* of attention directed at *many* things simultaneously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:22">[01:22:22]</a>. Their attentional system struggles to block out parallel stimuli, making them easily distracted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:31">[01:22:31]</a>. This results in difficulty channeling and sustaining attention on a single task <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:50">[01:22:50]</a>.
*   **Procrastination:** This is a very common symptom, often linked to the reduced dopamine levels in the brain, which makes it hard to start tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:25">[01:35:25]</a>.
*   **Hyperactivity:** Not all ADHD patients are hyperactive, but for those who are, it stems from insufficient dopamine in the brain region responsible for behavioral control and inhibition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:54">[01:27:54]</a>. This makes it difficult for them to remain still, akin to a car with faulty brakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:06">[01:28:06]</a>.

## Neurobiological Basis of ADHD

A key region of the brain affected in ADHD patients is the [[neuroscience_of_addiction_and_behavior | prefrontal cortex]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:24">[01:21:24]</a>. This area is crucial for attention, behavioral control, and future planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:30">[01:21:30]</a>. Its primary function related to ADHD is to sustain motivated attention for prolonged periods on a given task <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:42">[01:21:42]</a>.

Neurons that produce dopamine originate in the ventral tegmental area and project to both the prefrontal cortex and the nucleus accumbens (the reward system) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:32">[01:24:32]</a>. In ADHD, there is less tonic dopamine in the prefrontal cortex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:30">[01:30:30]</a>. This reduction in dopamine compromises the ability to sustain attention because it reduces the physiological motivation needed to focus and ignore distractions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:06">[01:26:06]</a>. It's like having less "oil" lubricating the brain's gears that channel attention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:29">[01:26:29]</a>.

## Treatment and Management

### Medication
Medications for ADHD, such as Venvanse and Ritalin, are psychostimulants, often derivatives of amphetamines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:06">[01:32:06]</a>. These medicines increase dopamine levels in the prefrontal cortex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:02">[01:29:02]</a>. For an ADHD patient, taking these stimulants paradoxically leads to calmness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:21">[01:32:21]</a>. They enable the patient to focus, as if someone removed dark glasses, allowing them to see clearly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:24">[01:31:24]</a>. They also reduce hyperactivity by restoring the "brake" function of the prefrontal cortex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:41">[01:31:41]</a>.

[!WARNING|Misuse of Medication]
For individuals without ADHD, taking these psychostimulants can lead to anxiety and a feeling akin to a "pinscher" (a restless dog), as their brain already has sufficient dopamine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:17">[01:33:17]</a>. This can disrupt sleep and lead to increased anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:24">[01:33:24]</a>.

### Seeking Dopamine and Hyperfocus
The reduction in dopamine in the prefrontal cortex often leads ADHD patients to seek other sources of dopamine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:50">[01:35:50]</a>. This can manifest in behaviors such as smoking, alcohol use, drug use, or food problems (e.g., chocolate addiction) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:57">[01:35:57]</a>. They tend to be more impulsive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:07">[01:36:07]</a>.

Some individuals with ADHD may develop "hyperfocus," becoming extremely concentrated on a specific behavior that provides them with dopamine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:17">[01:36:17]</a>. Michael Phelps, the Olympic swimmer, famously stated that water calmed him, suggesting that swimming became his source of dopamine, leading to his hyperfocus and eventual success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:28">[01:36:28]</a>.

### Diagnosis and Professional Help
It is important to seek a professional for an ADHD diagnosis, rather than self-diagnosing based on symptoms from a podcast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:45">[01:36:45]</a>. Professional help can significantly improve a person's life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:53">[01:36:53]</a>.
