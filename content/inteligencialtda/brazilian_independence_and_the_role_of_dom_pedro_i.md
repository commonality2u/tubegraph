## Historical Foundations

Brazil's discovery is largely attributed to [[pedro_lvares_cabral_and_the_discovery_of_brazil | Pedro Alvares Cabral]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>. The narrative often emphasizes Brazil's accidental discovery and subsequent exploitation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:09">[00:20:09]</a>. However, it's crucial to understand this period through the eyes of the time, not modern values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:30">[00:22:30]</a>.

The Brazilian Historical and Geographical Institute (IHGB), founded in 1837, sought to define the nation's identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:42">[00:23:42]</a>. A contest held by the IHGB concluded that Brazil's history should be told from the "union between the three races" – Portuguese, Indigenous, and African <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:09">[00:25:09]</a>. The Battle of Guararapes, where Portuguese, Indigenous, and African leaders united against Dutch invaders, is often highlighted as a foundational moment for the Brazilian army <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:37">[00:25:37]</a>.

The arrival of the Portuguese wasn't solely for economic gain; religious motivations, deeply intertwined with the desire for wealth, played a significant role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:05">[00:29:05]</a>. Explorers, including Cabral, were part of the Order of Christ, an heir to the Templars, which held both religious and economic missions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:56">[00:28:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:53">[00:39:53]</a>. The initial contact was generally friendly, though subsequent conflicts arose from mutual motivations and the total contextual change brought by the Portuguese <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:56">[01:34:56]</a>.

Early colonial economy revolved around brazilwood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:25">[01:25:25]</a>, which later gave way to sugar, a highly valuable commodity in Europe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:47">[01:18:47]</a>. This sugar economy led to the enslavement of Indigenous peoples and, more extensively, Africans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:51">[01:21:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:00">[01:26:00]</a>. While Indigenous slavery was more questioned from a Christian and legal standpoint, African slavery remains a "biggest stain on our history" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:06">[01:26:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:15">[01:05:15]</a>. The discovery of gold in Minas Gerais later shifted economic focus, attracting migration and leading to the development of new trade routes and infrastructure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.

## Road to Independence

The move of the Portuguese royal court to Brazil in 1808, fleeing Napoleon Bonaparte's invasion of Portugal, marked a pivotal moment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:46">[02:00:46]</a>. This strategic decision, long planned, involved bringing the entire state apparatus to Brazil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:40">[02:02:40]</a>. Dom João VI's arrival effectively turned Brazil into the capital of the empire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:24">[02:02:24]</a>. The opening of the ports in 1808 allowed Brazil to trade directly with other nations, particularly England, which significantly boosted its economic independence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:55">[02:02:55]</a>.

Dom João VI was crowned king of the United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil, and Algarves in 1816, elevating Brazil from a colony to a kingdom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:38">[02:11:38]</a>. This act laid the foundation for the Brazilian monarchy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:17">[02:12:17]</a>.

However, Portugal's revolution in 1820, seeking a constitution and the king's return, created tension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:05">[02:13:05]</a>. Dom João VI, under pressure, returned to Portugal, leaving his son, Dom Pedro, as Prince Regent in Brazil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:52">[02:17:52]</a>.

## Dom Pedro I's Role in Independence

The Portuguese Cortes demanded Dom Pedro's return to Portugal, aiming to re-establish Brazil as a dismembered colony <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:40">[02:18:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:01">[02:33:01]</a>. However, Brazil's elites, led by figures like José Bonifácio, rallied around Dom Pedro, urging him to stay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:56">[02:24:56]</a>. This public support culminated in the "Dia do Fico" (Day of I Will Stay) on January 9, 1822, when Dom Pedro famously declared, "If it is for the good of all and general happiness of the Nation, I am ready: Tell the people that I will stay" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:34:04">[02:34:04]</a>.

Leopoldina, Dom Pedro's wife, played a crucial role, often seen as a proponent for Brazilian independence due to her popularity and belief that Brazil needed to be independent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:05">[02:25:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:02">[02:16:02]</a>. She sent a dramatic letter to Dom Pedro, urging him to declare independence, stating "the pigeon is ripe" for harvest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:35">[02:31:35]</a>.

José Bonifácio, a key advisor and minister, drafted a constitution that aimed to maintain Brazil's unity and its status as a kingdom, even proposing a "double capitality" with Portugal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:57">[02:22:57]</a>. He also penned the "Paulista Manifesto" for Dom Pedro, a powerful letter warning of a "river of blood" if Brazil were to be dismembered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:52">[02:25:52]</a>.

In August 1822, two manifestos, essentially declarations of independence, were issued, signaling Brazil's clear path towards autonomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:28:02">[02:28:02]</a>. On September 7, 1822, upon receiving news of Portugal's intent to send troops to force his return, Dom Pedro, on the banks of the Ipiranga River, declared "Independence or Death" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:24">[02:33:24]</a>. This act, though symbolic, was followed by a "terrible war" for independence against Portugal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:50">[02:33:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:38">[02:37:38]</a>.

Brazil's independence was recognized by the United States quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:40:04">[02:40:04]</a>. England, while having strong commercial ties with Brazil, only recognized independence after Portugal did, in 1825 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:18">[02:41:18]</a>. This recognition came at a price, as Brazil had to pay an indemnity to Portugal and assume Portugal's debt to England <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:38:23">[02:38:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:35">[02:41:35]</a>.

Dom Pedro I's reign faced challenges, including economic struggles and regional revolts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:15">[02:39:15]</a>. In 1831, he abdicated in favor of his young son, Pedro II, to return to Portugal and fight for his daughter's right to the throne <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:29">[02:46:29]</a>. This ensured the continuation of his dynasty in both kingdoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:09">[02:47:09]</a>.
