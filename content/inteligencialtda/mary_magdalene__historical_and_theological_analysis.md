## Debunking Myths: Not a Prostitute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:04">[01:55:04]</a>

A common misconception is that Mary Magdalene was a prostitute. This idea was primarily created by Pope Gregory the Great in 591 AD through a homily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:52">[01:57:52]</a>. He mistakenly conflated Mary Magdalene with an adulterous woman mentioned in Revelation Chapter 8, who was also not a prostitute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:44">[01:37:44]</a>. An adulterous woman is a married woman who has a lover, while a prostitute is a sex worker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:17">[01:46:17]</a>. Neither the adulterous woman nor Mary Magdalene were prostitutes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:47">[01:48:47]</a>. The notion of her being a prostitute is so ingrained that phrases like "that face of Mary Magdalene" are used to describe someone truly repentant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:54">[01:52:54]</a>.

### Delayed Canonization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:17">[01:59:17]</a>

Mary Magdalene was canonized in the Orthodox Church but not in the Catholic Church until Pope Francis recognized her in July (her saint day) around 2000 years after her time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:17">[01:59:17]</a>. She was called "apostle of the Apostles" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:23">[02:00:23]</a>.

## The Da Vinci Code and Conspiracy Theories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:50">[02:06:50]</a>

Pope Gregory's creation of the prostitute figure for Mary Magdalene is thought by some to be a way to curb her growing influence in the church, threatening the patriarchy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:06">[02:27:06]</a>. This contributed to later conspiracy theories, including the idea that she was Jesus's wife <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:53:51">[02:53:51]</a>.

Dan Brown's novel *The Da Vinci Code* popularized the theory of Jesus having a lineage with Mary Magdalene, hidden in France <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:17">[02:11:17]</a>. However, this theory lacks proof <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:42">[02:17:42]</a>. The book contains numerous historical errors, such as incorrect dates for Paris's foundation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:04">[02:21:04]</a>. It's suggested the book's publisher, a major client of the Catholic Church, may have released it as a "smoke screen" to divert attention from exploding pedophilia scandals within the church <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:58:30">[02:58:30]</a>.

## Mary Magdalene in the Gospels: A Sinner, Not a Prostitute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:53:00">[03:53:00]</a>

The Gospel of Luke describes a "woman of the city who was a sinner" anointing Jesus's feet at a Pharisee's house <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:26:00">[05:26:00]</a>. This woman is often identified with Mary Magdalene. The Greek word for "sinner" (hamartei) does not imply prostitution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:00">[05:53:00]</a>. In that era, "sinner" could refer to someone facing public difficulty or punishment for a secret sin, often implying a sexual nature, but not necessarily prostitution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>.

The Pharisee, Simon, who hosted Jesus, thought to himself that if Jesus were a prophet, he would know the woman touching him was a sinner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:00">[05:53:00]</a>. This implies her "sin" was not public knowledge; otherwise, Simon wouldn't need a prophet to know <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.

Jesus's response to the woman, "daughter, your sins are forgiven," suggests she felt a need for forgiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:00">[01:06:00]</a>. Given the high percentage of sexual violence cases occurring within families that result in victims feeling guilty, this narrative aligns with the idea of her being a victim rather than a perpetrator of public sin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:00">[01:01:00]</a>.

### Seven Demons and Restoration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>

Luke Chapter 8 mentions Mary Magdalene, "from whom seven demons came out" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:08">[01:04:08]</a>. This doesn't necessarily imply possession in a literal sense but rather severe psychological trauma or moral degradation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>. It could symbolize her return to a distressing situation after initial healing, leading to a worse state, which Jesus then fully restored <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>. This highlights the emotional and psychological healing aspects of [[historical_jesus_and_early_christianity | Christian conversion]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:00">[01:26:00]</a>.

### Anointing Jesus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>

Mary Magdalene anointed Jesus's feet twice. The first time, early in his ministry, she approached with humility and tears, expressing gratitude <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:00">[01:17:00]</a>. The second time, days before his death in Bethany, she anointed him as an act of prophetic recognition of his kingship and impending burial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:00">[01:17:00]</a>. This second anointing showed her understanding of Jesus's mission, unlike many disciples who were still expecting a political king <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:00">[01:46:00]</a>.

### Witness to the Crucifixion and Resurrection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:00">[01:40:00]</a>

Mary Magdalene was one of the few who remained at the foot of the cross until the end <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:00">[01:41:00]</a>. ## Mary Magdalene's Prominence and [[Role of women in church | Leadership]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:00">[01:32:00]</a>

Mary Magdalene achieved prominence in a patriarchal society, a prominence endorsed by Jesus of Nazareth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>. ## Gnostic Views of Mary Magdalene <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:00">[03:12:00]</a>

The Gnostics, a group that emerged after the early Christian church, sought to create a more palatable, mystical form of Christianity for the Greco-Roman world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:15:00">[03:15:00]</a>. They believed Jesus possessed secret teachings accessible only to an elite few (gnosis, meaning "knowledge") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:37:00">[03:37:00]</a>.

In this context, Gnostics also emphasized the "sacred feminine" because pagan religions often praised goddesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:16:00">[03:16:00]</a>. They associated Mary Magdalene with this theme, seeing her as a mystical companion of Jesus, not in a physical sense but as the "feminine wisdom of Sophia" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:00">[03:46:00]</a>. Some Gnostic movements considered Sophia as the wife of God the Father, who generated Jesus, while others saw her as Jesus's wife <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:50:00">[03:50:00]</a>.

Gnostic gospels, such as the Gospel of Philip and the Gospel of Mary Magdalene, never explicitly stated that Jesus had a *royal* (physical) wedding with Mary Magdalene <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:00:00">[04:00:00]</a>. When these texts mention Jesus kissing her, it's not interpreted as erotic but rather as a gesture of her being a favored, attentive student, similar to how a teacher might show affection to a dedicated pupil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:03:00">[04:03:00]</a>.
