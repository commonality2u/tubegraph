## Why Recruiters Ask About Salary Expectations

Recruiters typically ask about salary expectations towards the end of an interview, especially for candidates they are interested in moving forward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:09">[00:01:09]</a>. The primary reason is to determine if your expectations align with the company's established salary band for the role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>. This information is included in your initial submission to the hiring manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a>.

It's generally not a trick question; recruiters genuinely want to know your expectations to ensure that if an offer is extended, you will accept and be excited about the compensation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. Accepting a salary below your expectations can lead to dissatisfaction and prompt you to seek other opportunities, which employers want to avoid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>. Therefore, the salary question is important for both parties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. While some low-quality employers may try to [[dealing_with_a_lowball_salary_offer | lowball an offer]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:54">[00:01:54]</a>, reputable employers aim for mutual satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>. If your realistic salary expectations deter an employer, it might indicate they are not the right fit for you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.

## Scenarios When Discussing Salary Expectations

### Your Salary Expectation is Higher Than the Company's Range

If your expected salary is too high for the role, you might be moved to the "no" pile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a>.
*   **Small Gap (e.g., $10,000 apart):** Some negotiation might occur even before further interviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>. A recruiter might propose hypothetical scenarios to gauge your flexibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:36">[00:02:36]</a>.
*   **Large Gap (e.g., $25,000 apart):** Realistically, an offer won't make sense, and the recruiter might suggest keeping you in mind for a more "enriched" future role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>.
*   **Superstar Candidate:** For exceptional candidates, a recruiter might still forward your resume with accurate salary expectations to the hiring manager, suggesting the role could be "enriched" (e.g., made a Level 2 or 3 position) to open up wider salary ranges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>. However, this is an exception, not the norm <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>.

It's crucial to be honest with your expectations; attempting a "bait and switch" tactic will not work well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.

### Your Salary Expectation is Lower Than the Company's Range

This is a favorable situation for both parties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:03">[00:04:03]</a>. Major corporations use salary bands (minimum, midpoint, maximum) based on local market wage surveys and internal equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>. Internal equity ensures similar roles within the organization are compensated similarly to prevent new hires from earning significantly more than long-term employees in the same role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.

If your compensation expectation is below the lowest point of the company's range, you will automatically be raised to that minimum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>. This often happens when candidates come from smaller or less reputable companies that don't pay market value, or when they are stepping into a higher-level role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:59">[00:04:59]</a>.

### Refusing to Answer the Question

If you are asked about your salary expectations and provide a vague or evasive answer, you will almost always be rejected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>. Recruiters will not put you through the interview process without knowing if a deal can be made, and it may lead them to believe you are hiding something <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>. Being ambiguous and hoping to negotiate at the offer stage rarely works out well and can annoy the hiring manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>. If a recruiter shows frustration, it's best to provide an answer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>.

## Preparing to Answer the Salary Question

To effectively answer the question, you need to know three key things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>:

1.  **Average Salary Range in the Open Market:** Research this type of role in your local area using resources like Google, Glassdoor, or Salary.com <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>. Ensure you compare jobs in your specific geographic region, as salaries can vary significantly (e.g., Alabama versus the Bay Area) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:53">[00:06:53]</a>.
2.  **What the Company is Expecting to Pay:** While harder to pinpoint precisely, your research should give you an idea.
3.  **Your Bottom Line Salary Expectation:** This is the absolute minimum salary you can accept, below which you would walk away from the opportunity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>. You don't necessarily share this number with the recruiter, but it's vital for your internal decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

## Strategies for Answering the Salary Expectation Question

Here are [[effective_salary_negotiation_strategies | effective salary negotiation strategies]] for [[discussing_salary_expectations_in_interviews | discussing salary expectations in interviews]]:

### 1. Directly State Your Expectation

If you know your worth and are reasonably confident that your expectation aligns with the company's range, you can state your actual number <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>. Ensure the number is reasonable and something you can live with throughout the process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>.

### 2. Frame Your Answer with Market Research

If you are concerned the company might make a [[dealing_with_a_lowball_salary_offer | lowball offer]] or that your expectations are significantly higher than theirs, frame your answer based on your market research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>:
*   "From the research that I've done in the local market, similar types of roles are paying between [lower bound] to [upper bound]. Does that align with your target?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>
This approach puts the onus on the company to disclose their range and helps you determine if their compensation will be competitive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.

### 3. Turn the Question Back to the Recruiter

If you are willing to consider a lower salary for an opportunity you are genuinely interested in, you can try to shift the question back to the recruiter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>:
*   "While I've been compensated very fairly in the past, an offer is more than just the cash compensation. I understand that companies have salary bands that they need to keep within and I respect that. Can you give me a ballpark of where the typical candidate that you're talking to is coming in, and I can tell you if that fits?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>
A recruiter might disclose the typical compensation range they are considering for other candidates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>. However, be prepared for them to push back, so still have an answer ready <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>.

Remember, the goal is to negotiate a salary that makes you excited to join the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:39">[00:09:39]</a>.
