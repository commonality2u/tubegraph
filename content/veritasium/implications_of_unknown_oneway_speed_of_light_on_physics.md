From: [[veritasium]] <br/> 

The speed of light in a vacuum is conventionally defined as exactly 299,792,458 meters per second, a value so precise that since 1983, it has been used to define the length of a meter itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>. However, this widely accepted value refers only to the *two-way* or *round-trip* speed of light, not the *one-way* speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a>. No one has ever definitively measured the one-way speed of light, leading to profound [[challenges_in_measuring_the_speed_of_light|challenges in measuring the speed of light]] and significant philosophical implications for physics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:46">[00:00:46]</a>.

## The Measurement Challenge

Measuring the speed of an object, like a baseball, typically requires knowing the distance between two points and the time it takes to travel that distance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>. However, this simple method breaks down when attempting to measure the one-way speed of light:

*   **Single Clock Problem:** If a single clock is used at the starting point, there's no way to know the exact instant light reaches a distant end <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>.
*   **Two Clock Synchronization Problem:** Using two clocks, one at the start and one at the end, introduces the problem of synchronization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>.
    *   Connecting them via a wire sends a pulse at the speed of light, meaning the delay caused by synchronization is the very value one is trying to measure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>.
    *   Synchronizing clocks together and then moving one to the end point also fails due to relativistic time dilation: moving clocks tick slower relative to stationary observers, so they would no longer be in sync upon arrival <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>. This problem is further complicated if the speed of light itself varies with direction, as time dilation would also depend on direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>.

The only reliable method found so far is to use a single clock at the start and reflect the light back from a mirror at the end, timing the full round trip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>. This approach inherently measures the two-way speed of light.

### Historical Context: Fizeau's Experiment

The first experimental measurement of the speed of light, conducted by Hippolyte Fizeau in 1849, utilized this round-trip method <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>. He shone a beam of light through a rapidly spinning gear's teeth to a mirror 8 kilometers away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:39">[00:03:39]</a>. By adjusting the gear's speed, he observed when the reflected light passed through the *next* gap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. Fizeau measured the speed of light at 313,000 kilometers per second, within 5% of the currently accepted value, but crucially, this was the *round-trip* speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>.

## The Einstein Synchronization Convention

[[einsteins_general_theory_of_relativity | Einstein's 1905 paper]], "On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies," addressed the problem of synchronizing clocks at different locations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>. He stipulated that, by definition, the time light takes to travel from point A to point B must equal the time it takes to travel from B to A <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. This is known as the [[understanding_the_twoway_vs_oneway_speed_of_light | Einstein synchronization convention]], a stipulation rather than an empirically verified fact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:18">[00:07:18]</a>.

### The Unknowable One-Way Speed

Because of this definitional choice, it is possible that the one-way speed of light is not the same in all directions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>. For example, light could travel at half of 'c' in one direction and be instantaneous on the return journey, while the round-trip still averages out to 'c' <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>. Physicists have developed internally consistent theories where this variation is possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>.

Every attempt to measure the one-way speed directly invariably measures the two-way speed:
*   **High-speed cameras:** While appearing to capture light's one-way movement, the observation itself depends on light bouncing back to the camera, making it a two-way measurement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>.
*   **Fiber optic cables:** Light in a coiled fiber undergoes numerous internal reflections, effectively measuring multiple round trips, not a single one-way passage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:31">[00:09:31]</a>.
*   **Central synchronizing device:** Sending simultaneous pulses from a central point to two clocks. If the one-way speed differs, the clocks will be out of sync by precisely the amount that makes the subsequent one-way measurement *appear* to be 'c' <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>.
*   **GPS:** The entire GPS system relies on the assumption that the speed of light is the same in all directions for clock synchronization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:20">[00:10:20]</a>. If this assumption were false, the clocks would still *appear* to measure 'c' for the one-way speed, regardless of its true value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:35">[00:10:35]</a>.

This forms a fundamental conundrum: synchronized clocks are needed to measure the one-way speed of light, but the one-way speed of light must be known to synchronize clocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:25">[00:11:25]</a>.

## Implications for Simultaneity

The most significant implication of the unknown one-way speed of light relates to the concept of simultaneity.
Consider the scenario of communicating with an astronaut, Mark, on Mars:
*   **Conventional View (Einstein Convention):** A signal sent at noon from Earth arrives on Mars in 10 minutes (at 12:10 PM Earth time), and a reply also takes 10 minutes to return (received on Earth at 12:20 PM Earth time). Mark, assuming the 10-minute travel time, sets his clock to 12:10 PM upon receiving the signal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:56">[00:11:56]</a>.
*   **Alternative View (Variable One-Way Speed):** If light travels at 'c/2' from Earth to Mars (taking 20 minutes) and instantaneously from Mars back to Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:32">[00:12:32]</a>:
    *   A signal sent from Earth at noon still takes 20 minutes to reach Mars. But Mark, unaware of the variable speed, still assumes 10 minutes and sets his clock to 12:10 PM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:45">[00:12:45]</a>.
    *   At this "12:10 PM Mars time," Earth's time is actually 12:20 PM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:55">[00:12:55]</a>.
    *   Mark's reply at "12:10 PM Mars time" is instantaneously received on Earth at 12:20 PM Earth time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:02">[00:13:02]</a>.
*   **Result:** Despite identical experiences of signal delays and successful "synchronization" of event timing from a round-trip perspective, the clocks on Earth and Mars are actually 10 minutes out of sync <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:15">[00:13:15]</a>. They have no way to recognize or correct this error <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:24">[00:13:24]</a>.

This demonstrates that there is inherent flexibility in defining simultaneity between distant locations and the one-way speed of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:50">[00:13:50]</a>. While Einstein chose a convention where the one-way speed is constant, any other convention is experimentally just as valid, provided the round-trip speed remains 'c' <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:01">[00:14:01]</a>.

This could lead to extraordinary perceptions: if light from Earth to Mars is slow and instantaneous on return, Earth would see Mars in real-time, while Mars would see Earth as it was 20 minutes ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:24">[00:14:24]</a>. Extending this, Earth could potentially see distant stars hundreds of light-years away exactly as they are "right now," not as they were centuries ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:39">[00:14:39]</a>.

## Ongoing Debate and Future Possibilities

The fact that the one-way speed of light is an unmeasured convention has been debated since 1905 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:48">[00:15:48]</a>. Some physicists invoke Occam's Razor, arguing that it's simpler to assume light travels at the same speed in all directions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:51">[00:15:51]</a>. Most working physicists simply accept the convention and proceed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:58">[00:15:58]</a>.

However, the unknowable nature of the one-way speed of light highlights that it is a convention, not an empirically proven fact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:04">[00:16:04]</a>. This leads to fundamental questions about the nature of the universe:
*   Does the one-way speed even have a well-defined value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:22">[00:16:22]</a>?
*   What does this mean for the concept of simultaneity, especially for events separated by distance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:27">[00:16:27]</a>?
*   Could this "odd quirk" be a crucial clue for the next major paradigmatic leap in physics, potentially connecting [[einsteins_general_theory_of_relativity | General Theory of Relativity and black holes]], quantum mechanics, space, and time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:45">[00:16:45]</a>? The inability to measure the one-way speed of light might be an obvious pointer to a deeper understanding of the universe that is currently hidden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:50">[00:16:50]</a>.
