## The Long Circuit Thought Experiment

Imagine a circuit composed of a battery, a switch, a light bulb, and two wires, each 300,000 kilometers long (the distance light travels in one second, roughly halfway to the moon and back) connected to a light bulb one meter away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:09">[00:00:09]</a>.

> After closing the switch, how long would it take for the bulb to light up? Is it half a second, one second, two seconds, 1/c seconds, or none of the above? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:24">[00:00:24]</a>
>
> *Assumptions*: Wires have no resistance, and the light bulb turns on immediately when current passes through it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:37">[00:00:37]</a>.

This question relates to how electrical energy travels from a power plant to your home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>.

## The Misconception About Electrical Energy Transmission

A common, yet incorrect, understanding of electricity involves electrons flowing through wires from a power source to a device <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.

In [[Alternating current AC dynamics and energy flow | AC systems]], such as the electrical grid, electrons in power lines merely wiggle back and forth, never actually moving from the power plant to your home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>. This immediately brings up the question: if charges don't travel to your home, how does electrical energy reach you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>?

This misconception is often taught with analogies, such as electrons acting like a chain within a flexible plastic tube, where a power station pushes and pulls electrons (e.g., 60 times a second for AC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. According to this model, electrons encounter resistance in a device (like a toaster), dissipating their energy as heat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>.

However, this model is flawed for several reasons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>:
*   There are physical gaps and breaks in the line (e.g., transformers) where electrons cannot continuously flow from a power station to a house <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>.
*   If electrons carry energy, why don't they carry energy back to the power station when they flow in the reverse direction in AC circuits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>? [[The misconception about electricity and how it flows | The misconception about electricity and how it flows]] states that electrons themselves have potential energy and dissipate it in the device <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>. All of this is false <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>.

## The True Nature of Electrical Energy Transmission

The correct understanding of energy transmission in electrical circuits stems from the work of James Clerk Maxwell and John Henry Poynting.

### Maxwell's Equations and Electromagnetic Waves

In the 1860s and 70s, James Clerk Maxwell's work revealed that light is composed of oscillating electric and magnetic fields, which are perpendicular to each other and in phase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>. His equations describe the behavior of these fields and waves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.

### Poynting Vector and Energy Flux

In 1883, John Henry Poynting, a student of Maxwell, considered the conservation of energy and realized that if energy is locally conserved, its path of flow should be traceable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. For example, the energy from the sun travels as stored energy in the electric and magnetic fields of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.

Poynting developed an equation, known as the [[Poynting vector and energy flux in electrical circuits | Poynting vector]] (symbol S), to describe energy flux – the amount of electromagnetic energy passing through an area per second <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>.
*   The formula for the [[Poynting vector and energy flux in electrical circuits | Poynting vector]] is S = (1/μ₀) * E × B, where μ₀ is the permeability of free space, E is the electric field, and B is the magnetic field <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a>.
*   The cross product (E × B) determines the direction of energy flow: point fingers in the direction of the electric field (E), curl them towards the magnetic field (B), and your thumb points in the direction of the energy flux (S) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.
*   For light, this shows that energy flows perpendicular to both the electric and magnetic fields, in the same direction as the light travels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.

Crucially, Poynting's equation applies whenever electric and magnetic fields coincide <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>. Anytime these fields are present together, there is a flow of energy that can be calculated using the [[Poynting vector and energy flux in electrical circuits | Poynting vector]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>. This highlights the [[Role of electric and magnetic fields in energy transfer | role of electric and magnetic fields in energy transfer]].

### Energy Flow in a Simple Circuit (DC Example)

Consider a simple circuit with a battery and a light bulb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>.
1.  **Electric Field Propagation**: When the battery is connected, its electric field extends through the circuit at the speed of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.
2.  **[[Electric fields and surface charges in circuits | Surface Charges]]**: This electric field pushes electrons, causing them to accumulate on some conductor surfaces (negatively charged) and deplete elsewhere (positively charged) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>.
3.  **Current and Fields**: These [[Electric fields and surface charges in circuits | surface charges]] create a small electric field *inside* the wires, causing electrons to drift very slowly (around a tenth of a millimeter per second) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:34">[00:06:34]</a>. The charge on the surfaces also creates an electric field *outside* the wires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>, and the current inside the wires creates a magnetic field *outside* the wires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
4.  **Energy Flow via Fields**: With electric and magnetic fields now present in the space around the circuit, energy flows according to Poynting's theory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.
    *   Around the battery, energy flows radially outwards into the fields <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>.
    *   Along the wires, energy flows to the right (towards the bulb) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:49">[00:07:49]</a>.
    *   At the light bulb's filament, the Poynting vector is directed *inward* toward the bulb, indicating the bulb is receiving energy from the surrounding fields <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.

Energy takes many paths from the battery to the bulb, but in all cases, it is transmitted by the electric and magnetic fields *outside* the wires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>. The electrons themselves barely move and do not carry the energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>. Even though electrons may move in two directions (away from and towards the battery), the [[Poynting vector and energy flux in electrical circuits | Poynting vector]] shows that the energy flux flows in only one direction: from the battery to the bulb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:45">[00:08:45]</a>. This is a core aspect of [[Role of electric and magnetic fields in energy transfer | role of electric and magnetic fields in energy transfer]].

### AC Circuit Dynamics and Energy Flow

The same analysis applies to [[Alternating current AC dynamics and energy flow | alternating current (AC)]] sources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>.
*   Although the direction of current reverses every half cycle, both the electric and magnetic fields flip simultaneously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>.
*   This means the [[Poynting vector and energy flux in electrical circuits | Poynting vector]] continues to point in the same direction, from the source to the bulb, at any given instant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
*   Therefore, the analysis for DC circuits is directly applicable to AC, explaining how energy flows from power plants to homes via power lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:29">[00:09:29]</a>.
*   Electrons inside the wires merely oscillate, while oscillating electric and magnetic fields *outside* the wires travel from the power station to your home, delivering power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.

### Historical Validation

This understanding is not just academic; it was proven through practical experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:02">[00:10:02]</a>. When the first Transatlantic telegraph cable was laid in 1858, it worked poorly due to severe signal distortions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>. Signals sent over long undersea distances became distorted and lengthened, making it difficult to differentiate Morse code dots from dashes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:32">[00:10:32]</a>.

A debate ensued among scientists:
*   William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) believed signals moved like water through a rubber tube <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>.
*   Others, like Oliver Heaviside and George Francis FitzGerald, argued that the fields *around* the wires carried the energy and information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:56">[00:10:56]</a>.

Ultimately, Heaviside and FitzGerald's view proved correct <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:05">[00:11:05]</a>. The problem with the Transatlantic cable was its design: the copper conductor was coated in an insulator and then encased in an iron sheath <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a>. Although intended for strength, the iron, being a good conductor, interfered with the propagation of electromagnetic fields by increasing the line's capacitance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>. This highlights the [[historical_misunderstanding_of_electrical_energy_transmission | historical misunderstanding of electrical energy transmission]].

This is why modern power lines are suspended high up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:27">[00:11:27]</a>, maintaining a large insulating gap of air between the wires and the ground (even damp earth acts as a conductor) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>.

## Resolution to the Long Circuit Question

Based on the understanding that energy is transmitted by fields around the wires, the answer to the giant circuit light bulb question is:

> The light bulb will turn on almost instantaneously, in roughly 1/C seconds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:45">[00:11:45]</a>. The correct answer is D <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:54">[00:11:54]</a>.

The electric field does not need to travel all the way down the 300,000 km wire. Instead, the electric and magnetic fields propagate outwards through space from the battery to the light bulb, which is only one meter away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:07">[00:12:07]</a>. This takes only a few nanoseconds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>, which is the limiting factor for the bulb turning on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>. While the full voltage might not be delivered immediately due to impedance, the initial illumination is almost instantaneous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:29">[00:12:29]</a>.

This phenomenon is one of the things we use daily but rarely think about or understand correctly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:10">[00:13:10]</a>. The traveling electromagnetic waves around power lines are what truly deliver power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:20">[00:13:20]</a>.
