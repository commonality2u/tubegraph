## What is a Perfect Number?
A [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] is a positive integer that is equal to the sum of its proper divisors (divisors excluding the number itself) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. For example, the number 6 has proper divisors 1, 2, and 3. When added together (1 + 2 + 3), they sum to 6, making 6 a [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. The number 10, with proper divisors 1, 2, and 5, sums to 8, and thus is not a [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>.

Between 1 and 100, only 6 and 28 are [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>. Up to 10,000, the next two are 496 and 8,128 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>. These four numbers were the only [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] known to the ancient Greeks and remained the only known ones for over a thousand years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

## Euclid's Formula for Even Perfect Numbers
Around 300 BC, the Greek mathematician [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Euclid]] discovered a pattern for generating [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:05">[00:04:05]</a>. His formula states that if the sum of consecutive powers of 2 (starting from 2 to the power of 0), which is 2<sup>P</sup> - 1, results in a [[mathematical_uniqueness_of_prime_numbers | prime number]] (P being an exponent), then multiplying this [[mathematical_uniqueness_of_prime_numbers | prime number]] by the last power of 2 in the sum (2<sup>P-1</sup>) yields a [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:32">[00:06:32]</a>.

This formula, 2<sup>P-1</sup> * (2<sup>P</sup> - 1), always produces an even number because 2<sup>P-1</sup> is even <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>. However, [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Euclid]] did not prove that this was the *only* way to generate [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>.

## The Birth of Mersenne Primes
The term "Mersenne Prime" originates from the French polymath [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Marin Mersenne]], who extensively studied numbers of the form 2<sup>P</sup> - 1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. In 1644, [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Mersenne]] published a list of 11 values for P for which he claimed 2<sup>P</sup> - 1 would result in a [[mathematical_uniqueness_of_prime_numbers | prime number]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a>. Numbers of this form that are prime are now known as Mersenne Primes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.

Of his list, the first seven exponents of P did indeed result in Mersenne Primes, corresponding to the first seven [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Mersenne]] famously admitted that checking the primality of numbers with 15 to 20 digits was beyond the capabilities of his time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>.

## Euler's Breakthrough: The Euclid-Euler Theorem
Centuries later, the Prussian mathematician [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Leonhard Euler]] picked up the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:45">[00:10:45]</a>. In 1732, [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Euler]] discovered the eighth [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] by verifying that 2<sup>31</sup> - 1 is prime, as [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Mersenne]] had predicted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:56">[00:10:56]</a>.

[[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Euler]]'s most significant contribution was proving that *every* even [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] must be of [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | Euclid]]'s form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:01">[00:13:01]</a>. This monumental discovery, known as the Euclid-Euler theorem, resolved a 1600-year-old question and proved Nicomachus's fourth conjecture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:09">[00:13:09]</a>. This theorem solidified the direct and exclusive link between Mersenne primes and even [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]]: each Mersenne prime corresponds to a unique even [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]], and every even [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect number]] is derived from a Mersenne prime.

## The Search for Mersenne Primes
The primary difficulty in finding new [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] was checking whether large Mersenne numbers (2<sup>P</sup> - 1) were actually [[mathematical_uniqueness_of_prime_numbers | prime]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:01">[00:18:01]</a>.

*   **Manual Calculation:** In 1876, Edouard Lucas proved that 2<sup>67</sup> - 1 was not [[mathematical_uniqueness_of_prime_numbers | prime]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:48">[00:16:48]</a>. Later, in 1903, Frank Nelson Cole famously demonstrated the factors of 2<sup>67</sup> - 1 to the American Mathematical Society, a calculation that took him three years of work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:57">[00:16:57]</a>.

*   **Computers Revolutionize the Search:** The advent of computers dramatically accelerated the discovery of Mersenne primes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:25">[00:18:25]</a>. In 1952, [[History and contributions of mathematicians to perfect numbers | American mathematician]] Raphael Robinson wrote a computer program and ran it on the SWAC (Standards Western Automatic Computer), finding five new Mersenne primes within 10 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:06">[00:18:06]</a>.

*   **GIMPS Project:** As Mersenne primes became astronomically large, finding new ones grew more difficult, even for supercomputers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:53">[00:18:53]</a>. In 1996, computer scientist George Woltman launched the Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search (GIMPS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:04">[00:19:04]</a>. GIMPS distributes the computational work across many volunteer computers worldwide, allowing anyone to contribute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:09">[00:19:09]</a>. The project has discovered 17 new Mersenne primes, 15 of which were the largest known primes at the time of their discovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:17">[00:19:17]</a>.

## Current Status and Conjectures
As of the video's context, 51 Mersenne Primes had been found <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:31">[00:21:31]</a>. The 50th Mersenne Prime, 2<sup>77,232,917</sup> - 1, discovered in 2017 by John Pace using GIMPS, was over 23 million digits long <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:45">[00:19:45]</a>. This number was so immense that a Japanese publishing house released it as a 719-page book <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:06">[00:20:06]</a>. The 51st Mersenne Prime, 2<sup>82,589,933</sup> - 1, discovered a year later, has over 24 million digits and is still the largest known prime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:31">[00:20:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:11">[00:21:11]</a>. Due to their rapid growth, the largest Mersenne Prime is almost always the largest known prime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:15">[00:21:15]</a>.

The Lenstra, Pomerance, and Wagstaff conjecture predicts that there are infinitely many Mersenne primes, which would imply infinitely many even [[Perfect numbers and their properties | perfect numbers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:48">[00:21:48]</a>. While this is a conjecture and not a proof, the ongoing search continues to validate its predictions.
