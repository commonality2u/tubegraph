From: [[veritasium]] <br/> 

The debate surrounding the "Blackbird" car, a vehicle capable of traveling downwind faster than the wind itself without an external energy source, led to a public bet of $10,000 between the video's host and UCLA Physics Professor Alex Kusenko <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>. The core claim was that the car could maintain speeds faster than the wind pushing it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>. The wager and discourse were made public, witnessed by notable figures like Neil deGrasse Tyson, Bill Nye, and Sean Carroll <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

## Professor Kusenko's Counterarguments and Challenges

Professor Kusenko initially argued that the previous video's explanation was incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. His main points challenging the car's sustained speed faster than the wind included:

*   **Gusty Wind Hypothesis** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:46">[00:01:46]</a>: Kusenko suggested the car's high speed was due to an initial strong gust pushing it, after which the wind speed dropped, causing the car to momentarily go faster than the wind but decelerating due to inertia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:03">[00:02:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>. He posited that acceleration would be negative whenever the velocity exceeded the wind speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>. He also suggested that record speeds of 2.8 times wind speed might be achieved by repeatedly sampling gusty wind conditions until a favorable strong gust allows for such a record <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.
*   **Wind Gradient Effect** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>: Wind travels slower closer to the ground and faster higher up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>. As the wind measurement was at a meter or a meter and a half, while the propeller reached three meters, Kusenko estimated the wind speed at the propeller might be 10-15% higher <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>. This could make the car *appear* to move faster than the measured wind, while still being slower than the actual wind interacting with the propeller <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>. He considered this a small effect, but one that could contribute to the observed phenomena in combination with gustiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>.
*   **Treadmill Test Flaws** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>: [[realworld_experiments_and_simulations_demonstrating_the_effect | Treadmill tests]], designed to simulate a perfectly steady tailwind in still air by moving the ground backward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>, were deemed flawed. Kusenko suggested that fluctuations in treadmill speed or unconscious human steering could introduce a bias towards the desired result <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>. This highlights potential issues related to [[cognitive_bias and hypothesis testing | cognitive bias and hypothesis testing]] in experimental design.
*   **Theoretical Problems (Divide by Zero)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>: Kusenko raised concerns about theoretical analyses, such as one by MIT Aero Professor Mark Drela. He noted that the equation for net force included the difference between car and wind speeds in the denominator, which seemed to imply infinite force when traveling exactly at wind speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>. His own analysis found no such divide-by-zero problems, concluding there was no way for the car to accelerate at or above wind speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>.

## Presenting Further Evidence and Explanations

In response to these challenges, more definitive evidence was presented to support the Blackbird's capability:

### Addressing Wind Gradient and Gusts

*   **Propeller-Height Measurements** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>: Previous [[realworld_experiments_and_simulations_demonstrating_the_effect | experiments]] had already addressed the wind gradient concern by mounting tell-tales on fishing poles to the sides and above the propeller <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>. All tell-tales eventually flipped backward, indicating that every part of the vehicle was moving faster than the wind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>.
*   **Sustained Acceleration** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>: Analysis of video footage using back wheel rotation showed that even after the tell-tale flipped backward, the car continued to accelerate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. The tell-tale consistently pointed straight back for over 30 seconds before the car had to brake, refuting the idea of transient gust effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:00">[00:09:00]</a>.
*   **Record Speed Data** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>: When Blackbird achieved its record speed of 27.7 mph in a 10 mph tailwind, it was still accelerating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>. This was confirmed by multiple GPS units in the car and wind speeds measured at the propeller's height at various locations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
*   **Physics Olympiad Confirmation** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>: In 2013, the U.S. Physics Olympiad Semifinal Exam included questions about Blackbird, with the solution stating that both downwind and upwind modes of operation (faster than the wind) are possible, and "with sufficiently low energy loss, any speed is possible" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:42">[00:09:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:09:50]</a>.

### Explaining the Mechanism

The explanation delves into how the Blackbird works, refuting the notion of it being a simple windmill:

*   **Propeller Function** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a>: The propeller functions as a fan, turning in the *opposite* direction of the tailwind and pushing air backward, generating forward thrust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:26">[00:10:26]</a>. This fan is powered by the wheels, connected by a bike chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:35">[00:10:35]</a>.
*   **Energy Transfer** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>: In the car's frame of reference, power is input at the wheels from the ground (Force of ground on wheels × Car velocity) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:12">[00:11:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>. This power is output at the propeller, which pushes air backward (Force of prop on air × (Car speed - Wind speed)) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:30">[00:11:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:35">[00:11:35]</a>. Because the wheels move much faster over the ground than the propeller moves through the air, the thrust force from the propeller can be greater than the backward force on the wheels, enabling acceleration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a>.
*   **Addressing the Divide-by-Zero Problem** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>: The theoretical "divide by zero" problem (where force seems infinite at wind speed) is resolved by:
    *   **Lever Analogy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:48">[00:12:48]</a>: Like a lever, if one arm approaches zero, infinite weight can be lifted with minimal force, but with zero displacement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:56">[00:12:56]</a>.
    *   **Propeller Efficiency** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:08">[00:13:08]</a>: In practice, a propeller efficiency term resolves the issue, becoming well-defined even at zero air speed, removing the mathematical singularity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:10">[00:13:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>.

### [[realworld_experiments_and_simulations_demonstrating_the_effect | Realworld Experiments and Simulations Demonstrating the Effect]]

To provide convincing experimental evidence, the video shows several [[realworld_experiments_and_simulations_demonstrating_the_effect | demonstrations]]:

*   **Roller Cart Analogy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:32">[00:13:32]</a>: A cart with a large wheel rolling on two smaller spools demonstrates the principle with two relative media. When a board is pushed, the cart moves faster than the board, with its large wheel rotating in the opposite direction, mirroring the Blackbird's propeller function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>.
*   **Replication of Treadmill Experiments** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>: Collaborator Xyla Foxlin undertook multiple [[realworld_experiments_and_simulations_demonstrating_the_effect | attempts to replicate]] the treadmill experiments with model downwind carts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>. While initial versions failed, her fourth version worked "spectacularly," proving the concept and designed for easy replication using 3D printing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:01">[00:15:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:04">[00:15:04]</a>.

Professor Kusenko conceded the bet and transferred the $10,000, acknowledging the evidence presented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:16">[00:15:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:20">[00:15:20]</a>.
