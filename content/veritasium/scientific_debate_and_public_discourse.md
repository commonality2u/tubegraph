From: [[veritasium]] <br/> 

Scientific debate and public discourse, particularly concerning counterintuitive physical phenomena, can lead to significant learning opportunities. A notable example is the $10,000 public bet between Veritasium creator Derek Muller and UCLA Physics Professor Alex Kusenko regarding the "Blackbird" vehicle, a car designed to travel faster than the wind it's being pushed by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>. This wager, openly discussed and witnessed by figures like Neil deGrasse Tyson, Bill Nye, and Sean Carroll <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>, exemplified how disagreements can be resolved through public presentation of evidence and analysis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>.

## Initial Skepticism and Counterarguments
Professor Kusenko challenged the initial video's explanation, suggesting several reasons why the car's performance might be misinterpreted:

### Effect of Gusty Winds and Inertia
Kusenko argued that the vehicle's apparent speed advantage might be due to gusty wind conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:46">[00:01:46]</a>. He posited that a strong gust could initially push the car to a high speed, and then, as the wind speed drops, the car would continue moving by inertia, momentarily appearing faster than the dying wind, but ultimately decelerating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>. He theorized that whenever the car's velocity exceeded the wind speed, its acceleration would be negative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>. He also suggested that record speeds, like 2.8 times wind speed, could be achieved by sampling gusty wind over many attempts until a strong gust followed by dying wind allowed for a high clocking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

### Wind Gradient
Another point raised was the effect of wind gradient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>. Wind travels slower near the ground and faster at higher altitudes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>. As the wind speed in the original video was measured at about 1 to 1.5 meters high, while the propeller extends to three meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>, Kusenko estimated the propeller's wind speed could be 10-15% higher <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>. This could make the car appear to be moving faster than the wind at the lower measurement point, while still moving slower than the wind at the propeller's height <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>.

### Doubts on Experimental Evidence: Treadmill Tests
Kusenko questioned the treadmill tests, which are conducted in still air by moving the ground backwards to simulate a steady tailwind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>. He suggested that fluctuations in treadmill speed or unconscious steering bias by the operator could introduce a bias towards the desired result <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>. While acknowledging that any such bias would not be intentional, he noted that expecting a forward drift could unconsciously influence the operation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.

### Theoretical Analysis: The "Divide by Zero" Problem
In the absence of what he considered convincing [[experimental_evidence_and_replication | experimental evidence]], Kusenko critiqued existing theoretical analyses, specifically one by MIT Aero Professor Mark Drela <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. His main concern was an equation for net force that included the difference between the car's speed and the wind's speed in the denominator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. This implied that when the car traveled exactly at wind speed, an infinite force would be generated, which he deemed problematic and dangerous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>. Kusenko's own analysis found no such "divide by zero" issues and concluded that the car could not accelerate at or above wind speed, meaning its acceleration would always be negative under those conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:06:13]</a>.

## Presenting Definitive Evidence and Explanation

### Addressing Gusts and Gradients
To counter the gust and wind gradient arguments, additional evidence was presented:
*   Tell-tales mounted on fishing poles at various heights, including above the propeller, all eventually flipped backwards, indicating every part of the vehicle was going faster than the wind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>.
*   Analysis of video footage using the back wheel's rotation showed the car continued to accelerate even after the tell-tales flipped backwards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>.
*   The tell-tale consistently pointed straight back for over 30 seconds, demonstrating sustained speed relative to the wind, not momentary jumps due to gusts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>.
*   When Blackbird set its record speed (27.7 mph in 10 mph tailwind), it was still accelerating, confirmed by multiple GPS units and wind speed measurements at the propeller's height <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>.

### Endorsement from US Physics Olympiad
The fact that the 2013 U.S. Physics Olympiad Semifinal Exam included questions about Blackbird, with solutions stating that both downwind and upwind modes of travel (faster than the wind) are possible, and that "with sufficiently low energy loss, any speed is possible," served as an academic endorsement of the concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>.

### Explaining the Mechanism
A clearer explanation of the Blackbird's operation was provided to dismantle misconceptions:
*   **Propeller Function:** The propeller does not work like a windmill, turning with the tailwind. Instead, it turns in the opposite direction, acting like a fan pushing air backwards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.
*   **Power Source:** This fan is powered by the wheels, connected via a bike chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:35">[00:10:35]</a>.
*   **Thrust Generation:** At wind speed, the car continues to accelerate because the wheels turn the fan, which blows air back, generating forward thrust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>.
*   **Force Balance:** While there's a backward force on the wheels to drive the fan, the thrust from the propeller can be larger. This is because the wheels move much faster over the ground than the propeller moves through the air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.
*   **Lever Analogy:** The car functions like a lever or pulley system. By applying a small force to the wheels over a larger distance (due to the car's speed relative to the ground), the propeller can apply a larger force over a smaller distance (due to its speed relative to the air) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:12">[00:12:12]</a>. This is analogous to riding a bike uphill: fast, small force pedal movements result in slower, larger force wheel movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>.

### Resolving the "Divide by Zero" Problem
The perceived "divide by zero" problem in the theoretical analysis was addressed:
*   Theoretically, for a lever with one arm of zero, an infinite weight could be lifted with any force, but its displacement would be zero <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:53">[00:12:53]</a>.
*   Practically, the issue is eliminated by a propeller efficiency term that is well-defined even when the propeller is not moving through the air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:10">[00:13:10]</a>.

### Analogous Physical Model and Replication
A simple cart model with a big wheel rolling on two smaller spools was demonstrated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a>. When the board it rests on is pushed, the cart moves faster than the board's velocity, and the big wheel rotates in the opposite direction of the board's push, directly mirroring Blackbird's propeller function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>. This provided a tangible, easily replicable analogy.

Further, a fourth version of a model downwind cart, designed for 3D printing and simple materials, successfully demonstrated the principle, making [[experimental_evidence_and_replication | experimental evidence and replication]] accessible to anyone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:01">[00:15:01]</a>.

## Resolution and Lessons Learned

Professor Kusenko conceded the bet, transferring $10,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:16">[00:15:16]</a>. This outcome underscored the importance of changing one's mind in light of new evidence, especially in a public debate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:24">[00:15:24]</a>. The money was subsequently invested in science communication through a video competition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:34">[00:15:34]</a>.

The experience highlighted several valuable lessons for science communication:
*   **Disagreements as Opportunities:** Disagreements in science are not problems but opportunities for learning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:52">[00:15:52]</a>.
*   **Depth and Clarity:** The need to go into more depth in explanations, provide overwhelmingly convincing evidence, and include equations for those desiring greater detail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:05">[00:16:05]</a>.
*   **[[discovering_truth_through_disproof | Discovering Truth Through Disproof]]:** The process reaffirmed that the core point of scientific inquiry is to arrive at the truth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>.

The debate also showcased the role of various individuals, from renowned scientists to engineers and fellow content creators, in contributing to scientific understanding and discourse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:13">[00:16:13]</a>. The goal was to definitively put the issue to rest and help people understand this area of physics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:35">[00:16:35]</a>.
