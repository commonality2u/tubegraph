From: [[veritasium]] <br/> 
Compliant mechanisms are devices that achieve motion through the bending and flexing of their components, rather than relying on traditional rigid-body joints like hinges and bearings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:06">[00:00:06]</a>. This approach leverages the inherent flexibility of materials to perform mechanical functions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>. Professor Larry Howell, who authored a highly cited book on the subject, highlights how this seemingly "bad" characteristic of flexibility can be used to advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>.

[[compliant_mechanisms_and_their_advantages | Compliant mechanisms]] offer several advantages over traditional mechanisms, including reduced part count, lower cost, and precise motion without backlash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>.

## Examples in Everyday Objects and Common Applications

### Compliant Gripper
An early example of a compliant mechanism is a simple gripper designed to hold objects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:33">[00:01:33]</a>. This gripper is a single piece of plastic that can generate significant force, capable of breaking chalk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. It can amplify force by approximately 30-to-1, meaning one pound of input force can produce 30 pounds of output force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>. Unlike traditional vice grips, which have many separate components like hinges and bearings, this compliant gripper achieves a similar function with a single, flexible piece <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. Its simple design makes it extremely inexpensive to produce, potentially costing mere cents if injection molded or extruded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

### Plastic Switches
Many modern switches utilize compliant mechanisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>. These switches are often made from a single piece of plastic, replacing the multiple springs, hinges, and rigid parts found in conventional switch designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>. This integration into a single component simplifies manufacturing and reduces costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>. Such compliant switches have demonstrated impressive durability, withstanding over a million cycles in fatigue testing without failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.

### Tactile Clicking Device
A popular demonstration of compliant mechanism principles is a small, satisfying clicking device <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>. This device, inspired by microscopic compliant mechanisms built on silicon chips, offers a pleasing tactile and auditory "snap" when operated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>. The ability to create such mechanisms from brittle materials like silicon or less-than-ideal materials like PLA demonstrates the versatility of compliant design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27">[00:06:27]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>.

### Centrifugal Clutch
Another application is the centrifugal clutch, which uses compliant principles for engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>. As the device spins at a certain speed, its flexible outer part expands outwards due to centrifugal force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:09">[00:09:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:39">[00:09:39]</a>. This expansion allows it to engage with an outer drum, transferring power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>. This mechanism is similar to those found in chainsaws, where the chain only engages once the engine reaches a sufficient RPM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>. While often demonstrated in plastic, these clutches can be manufactured from materials like steel for real-world applications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:42">[00:09:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:46">[00:09:46]</a>.

## Specialized Applications

### [[applications_of_compliant_mechanisms_in_nuclear_weapons_safety | Nuclear Weapons Safing and Arming Device]]
One critical application of compliant mechanisms is in the safing and arming of nuclear weapons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:53">[00:00:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>. These devices are designed to prevent accidental detonation, ensuring that random vibrations (e.g., from an earthquake) do not inadvertently disarm the weapon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>.

A key requirement for this device was extreme miniaturization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>. Using compliant mechanisms, engineers created a device from hardened stainless steel with components as fine as a human hair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43">[00:10:43]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>. The device features a rotor wheel with a hole that rotates when specific inputs are given <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>. An arming laser beam must align with this hole to initiate the arming sequence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:08">[00:11:08]</a>. The design ensures predictable and reliable performance, even if the device remains unused for decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>.

### [[compliant_mechanisms_in_space_technology | Space Technology]]
Compliant mechanisms are well-suited for space applications due to their lightweight and simplified designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:32">[00:07:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>.

*   **Solar Panel Hinges**: NASA has explored using compliant hinges, often 3D-printed from titanium, to replace traditional bearings for deploying solar panels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:46">[00:07:46]</a>. A single piece of titanium can achieve a 180-degree deflection, demonstrating remarkable flexibility in a solid material <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>.
*   **Thruster Director**: A complex compliant mechanism designed for NASA allows a single thruster to be directed in any direction using two motor inputs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>. The entire motion relies on bending, and the design ensures no pinch points for critical fuel or electrical lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:54">[00:08:54]</a>. This innovation allows one thruster to perform the function of two, further optimizing space systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.
