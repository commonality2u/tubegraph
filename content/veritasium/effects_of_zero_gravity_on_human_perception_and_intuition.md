## The Zero-G Flight Experience

Weightlessness is achieved when a plane, and everything inside it, accelerates towards the Earth at the same rate as a freely falling object in a vacuum, which is 9.8 meters per second squared <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>. This principle means there's no contact force between an object and its container, making the object feel weightless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>.

To create zero-G, the plane doesn't just dive; it begins by climbing at a steadily increasing angle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:16">[00:01:16]</a>. During this climb, known as hyper-G, passengers are pressed into the floor with a force 1.8 times their body weight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. This upward acceleration makes standing very difficult and causes blood to drain from the head into the feet, leading to dizziness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. To mitigate motion sickness, often why these flights are called the "vomit comet," passengers are advised to lie on their back and look straight ahead, as the vestibular system becomes hypersensitive to movement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

Once the plane is climbing at approximately 50 degrees, the engines are reduced, and the aircraft enters a parabolic trajectory, initiating [[experiencing_weightlessness_for_the_first_time | weightlessness]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>. Passengers immediately report feeling themselves getting lighter, describing it as a "strange feeling" and "so weird" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>. Even as the plane and passengers continue to move upward, they are accelerating downward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>. This period of [[the_physics_behind_creating_zero_gravity_environments | weightlessness]] lasts about 22 seconds before the pilots pull out of the dive, subjecting occupants to hyper-G again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>. In addition to zero-G, flights can also simulate other gravitational environments, such as Martian and Lunar gravity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>.

## Challenging Intuition with Experiments

The experience of [[experiencing_weightlessness_for_the_first_time | weightlessness]] profoundly challenges intuition built from a lifetime in gravity, necessitating [[conducting_experiments_in_zero_gravity | research in zero-G]] to understand phenomena that behave differently without the constant pull of gravity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

### Flame Behavior

Familiar flames on Earth have a distinctive shape because of gravity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. Hot combustion products are less dense and more energetic, creating a buoyant force that causes them to rise, pulling in oxygen to sustain the reaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.

In hyper-G, this effect is magnified; the increased gravitational pull amplifies the difference in weight between hot and cooler air, causing the hot air to rise faster and resulting in a longer flame <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.

However, in [[effects_of_zero_gravity_on_fire_and_flames | zero-G]], the absence of weight means there is significantly less buoyant force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>. Although hot air is still less dense, it has no weight, so it doesn't rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>. As a result, combustion is less efficient, producing more smoke <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>. Without a gas flow, the flame would form a sphere, as seen on the International Space Station, where maintaining combustion is difficult due to combustion products obstructing oxygen access to the fuel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:24">[00:05:24]</a>. Observing a flame's behavior can therefore serve as a good indicator of gravitational acceleration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.

### Slinky Dynamics

Another experiment involved a slinky. On Earth, a slinky dangles under its own weight. In [[the_physics_behind_creating_zero_gravity_environments | zero-G]], it cannot dangle, so it was swung around to achieve uniform circular motion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>. Each coil stretches to provide the tension needed to keep the slinky's length accelerating in uniform circular motion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.

When the slinky was released, it remained stretched out and continued rotating at roughly the same frequency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. This counter-intuitive result occurs because its stretched state is due to its rotation, not its weight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>. As long as the tension in the slinky provides the centripetal force for its rotation, it will maintain its extended form and continue spinning without contracting, challenging everyday gravitational intuition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>.

These experiences highlight how [[experiencing_weightlessness_for_the_first_time | weightlessness]] can reshape understanding and challenge deeply ingrained intuitions forged by living in a gravitational environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:04">[00:07:04]</a>.
