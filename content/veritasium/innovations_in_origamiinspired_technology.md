## The Evolution of Origami Design

Origami, literally meaning "folding paper," has roots dating back at least 400 years in Japan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. Historically, the number of designs was limited, perhaps only 100 to 200 patterns in total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. A significant shift occurred in the 20th century, largely due to Japanese origami masters, particularly Akira Yoshizawa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. Yoshizawa created thousands of new designs and published numerous books, sparking a worldwide renaissance in origami creativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. Today, tens of thousands of designs have been documented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:37">[00:00:37]</a>.

## Core Principles for Engineering Applications

The fundamental benefit of origami for engineering is its method of transforming a flat sheet into complex shapes with minimal processing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>. This includes:

*   **Size Reduction**: Origami allows for a huge amount of size reduction, such as transforming a one-meter square sheet into a small, complex object like a cactus with many spines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>.
*   **Increased Rigidity**: The simple act of folding a material can inherently make it more rigid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>.
*   **Specific Motions**: Origami designs can create "bi-stable" mechanisms that hold stable in two positions, or combine mechanisms for "magical" effects like color change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>.
*   **Scalability**: Origami principles are highly scalable, enabling both large-scale deployable structures and the miniaturization of devices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>.
*   **Folding Thick Materials**: Innovations in scoring and adding hinges allow thick, rigid materials like polypropylene to be folded, accommodating their thickness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:11">[00:05:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.

*   **Nano Injectors**: Microscopic origami designs can be used for [[applications_of_origami_in_medical_devices_and_space_technology | medical applications]] like nano injectors for gene therapy, capable of delivering DNA to cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>. ## Mathematical Principles in Origami Design

[[mathematical_principles_in_origami_design | The design of origami for engineering applications relies heavily on mathematics]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>. Understanding the curvature and coupling of lines and bends requires mathematical methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>.

*   **Crease Patterns**: Mathematical approaches allow for the representation of an origami design as a "crease pattern," which is a plan for how to fold a specific object <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a>.
*   **Circle Packing Method**: For designs that can be represented as a stick figure (e.g., a scorpion with claws, legs, and a tail), the circle packing method is used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a>. This abstract concept involves:
    1.  Representing each feature (claw, leg, tail) as a circular region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:28">[00:12:28]</a>.
    2.  Arranging these circles on a square sheet of paper, similar to packing balls into a box <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>.
    3.  The arrangement of these circles forms the "skeleton" of the crease pattern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>.
    4.  Geometric rules are then applied to construct the full crease pattern, such as drawing lines between circle centers and adding "ridge folds" where lines meet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:22">[00:13:22]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:32">[00:13:32]</a>.
    5.  This systematic process ensures the folded pattern will produce the exact desired shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:15">[00:14:15]</a>.

*   **Origamizer Algorithm**: For more complex, surface-like shapes (e.g., a sphere or an elephant's body), a Japanese mathematician named Tomohiro Tachi developed an algorithm called "Origamizer" around 10 years ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>. This algorithm takes a triangulated surface as a mathematical description and outputs the folding pattern to create that surface from a sheet of material <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:16">[00:15:16]</a>.

In essence, [[origami_in_modern_engineering | origami-inspired technology]] leverages centuries of folding experimentation, combined with rigorous mathematical modeling and experimentation, to develop innovative practical solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:11">[00:16:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:20">[00:16:20]</a>.
