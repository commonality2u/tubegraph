## Historical Context

The practice of [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]], literally "folding paper," originated in Japan at least 400 years ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. Initially, the number of designs was limited to perhaps 100 to 200 patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. A significant transformation occurred in the 20th century, leading to tens of thousands of documented designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>. Akira Yoshizawa, a prominent Japanese [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] master, played a crucial role in this renaissance by creating thousands of new designs and inspiring a worldwide surge in [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] creativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>.

One example of complex [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] design is a cactus and pot made from a single, uncut, one-meter square sheet of paper, which took about seven years to design and fold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:33">[00:01:33]</a>.

## Why is Origami Useful for Engineering?

Origami's utility in engineering stems from several key principles:
*   **Transformation of Flat Sheets** It offers a method of transforming a flat sheet into another complex shape with minimal processing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.
*   **Size Reduction** [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | Origami]] provides a way to significantly reduce the dimensions of a product for storage or transport while allowing for easy deployment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>.
*   **Increased Rigidity** The simple act of folding a material can inherently make it more rigid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>. For rigid materials, techniques like cutting, scoring, and adding hinges can facilitate folding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>.
*   **Specific Motions and Flexibility** [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | Origami]] can leverage the flexibility of materials to create precise and functional motions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>.
*   **Scalability and Miniaturization** The principles of [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] are scalable, enabling the creation of both large-scale structures and microscopic devices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>.

## Mathematical Principles in Origami Design

[[mathematical_principles_in_origami_design | Mathematical principles]] are fundamental to [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] design, especially for complex and functional shapes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>. The curvature of lines, bending, and coupling of folds all rely on mathematical methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>.

A key concept is the **crease pattern**, a plan for how to fold a design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:16">[00:12:16]</a>.
*   **Circle Packing Method:** This method is used for designs that can be represented as a "stick figure," such as a scorpion with its body, tail, legs, and claws <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a>. Each feature is represented by an abstract circular region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:30">[00:12:30]</a>. These circles are "packed" onto a square sheet, similar to packing balls into a box <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>. The arrangement of these circles forms the skeleton of the crease pattern, from which all other folds can be geometrically constructed by following specific rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>. This systematic process revolutionized [[origami_for_structural_and_mechanical_improvements | origami]] design by ensuring the fold pattern yields the exact desired shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:15">[00:14:15]</a>.
*   **Origamizer Algorithm:** For designs that are more like surfaces (e.g., a sphere or a blobby animal), the stick figure algorithm is not sufficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:48">[00:14:48]</a>. Approximately 10 years ago, Japanese mathematician Tomohiro Tachi developed the "Origamizer" algorithm, which can generate a folding pattern for any given triangulated surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>. This means a sheet of any material can be designed to take on virtually any three-dimensional shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:31">[00:15:31]</a>.
