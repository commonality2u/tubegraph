## What is Potash?
The answer to [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash's]] importance begins with a 1,500-year-old recipe involving a pot and a hardwood fire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>. To make it, hardwood is burned for its ash, which is then combined with water in a pot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>. A water-soluble chemical compound dissolves from the ash, forming a slippery solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>. After straining out solids and evaporating the water under the sun, a crystalline substance remains, which is called [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>. The name itself comes from its origin: "pot ash" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>.

In 1807, British scientist Humphry Davy discovered a new element by applying electrodes to damp [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:06">[00:03:06]</a>. This process led to the formation of tiny metal globules that spontaneously caught fire, leading him to name the element [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] after [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>. While many associate [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] with foods like bananas or Gatorade, those are not pure elemental [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>. Pure elemental [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] is a highly reactive metal, easily squishable, and must be stored submerged under oil to prevent reaction with atmospheric water <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>. It reacts violently with water, forming [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] hydroxide and hydrogen gas, releasing significant heat that causes spontaneous combustion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. The term [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]] has evolved to be a catchall for various [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]]-containing compounds, not just the original [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potassium]] carbonate derived from ash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

## Early American Industries and Uses

### Soap and Glass Production
For centuries, [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]] was crucial in manufacturing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>. [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | Potash]] was also essential for glass production; adding it to sand (silicon dioxide) lowered the melting point, making glass less brittle and easier to work with in early furnaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.

This [[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | potash]]-derived saltpeter was vital for infusing gunpowder used in the muskets and cannons during battles across Europe and the American Revolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>.

[[the_history_and_discovery_of_potash_and_potassium | Potash]] was by far the main chemical product of the early American colonies, serving as a substantial source of revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:48">[00:07:48]</a>.
