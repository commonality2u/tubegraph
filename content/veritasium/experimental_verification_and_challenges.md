From: [[veritasium]] <br/> 

The Blackbird vehicle, a car designed to travel faster than the wind directly downwind, became the subject of a $10,000 wager between the video's host and UCLA Physics Professor Alex Kusenko <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. The counterintuitive claim is that this car, which has no motor, batteries, or energy source besides the wind itself, can maintain speeds faster than the wind pushing it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>. The debate highlighted several [[experimental_demonstrations_in_physics | experimental demonstrations]] and [[theoretical_analyses_and_misconceptions | theoretical analyses and misconceptions]] surrounding the vehicle's operation.

## Initial Challenge and Criticisms

Professor Kusenko believed the explanation for the Blackbird's operation was incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. The wager and associated discourse were made public, with Neil deGrasse Tyson, Bill Nye, and Sean Carroll serving as witnesses to the bet signing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.

Professor Kusenko's main arguments against the Blackbird's claim were:

*   **Gusty Wind and Inertia** He argued that the vehicle operates in a gusty wind, where an initial strong gust pushes the car to a high speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:46">[00:01:46]</a>. When the wind speed subsequently drops, the car moves by inertia, momentarily appearing to go faster than the wind but decelerating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. His conclusion was that whenever the car's velocity is faster than the wind, its acceleration must be negative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>.
*   **Wind Gradient Effect** The wind in the video was measured at a height of about 1 to 1.5 meters, while the propeller extends up to 3 meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>. Due to interactions with the ground, wind travels slower closer to the ground and faster higher up (wind gradient) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>. Kusenko estimated the wind speed at the propeller might be 10-15% higher than at the lower tell-tale, suggesting the car might appear to move faster than the measured wind but actually be slower than the wind at the propeller height <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>. This effect, combined with gusty conditions, could make the observed phenomenon more frequent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. He also suggested that a record speed of 2.8 times wind speed could be achieved by sampling gusty wind over many [[attempts_to_prove_or_disprove_the_collatz_conjecture | attempts]], catching a strong gust followed by calmer air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.
*   **Challenges to Treadmill Tests** Kusenko raised concerns about the treadmill tests, which are conducted in still air and simulate a steady tailwind by moving the ground backwards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>. He argued that fluctuations in treadmill speed and unconscious human steering bias towards the desired result could invalidate the findings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>. This points to [[challenges_in_standardized_testing | challenges in standardized testing]] of experimental setups where human interaction is present.
*   **Theoretical "Divide by Zero" Problem** In the absence of convincing experimental evidence, Kusenko referred to [[theoretical_analyses_and_misconceptions | theoretical analyses]], like one by MIT Professor Mark Drela <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. His main concern was an equation for net force that includes the difference between car speed and wind speed in the denominator. He argued that if the car travels exactly at wind speed, this would imply infinite force, a physically impossible scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. His own analysis found no such divide-by-zero problems, but concluded there's no way for the car to accelerate at or above wind speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.

## Experimental Verification and Rebuttals

The video's host addressed Professor Kusenko's points with further evidence and explanations:

*   **Addressing Wind Gradient and Gusts**:
    *   **Wind Gradient Measurement**: Tell-tales were mounted on fishing poles at various heights (sides and above the propeller) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>. All tell-tales eventually flipped backwards, showing that every part of the vehicle, including the highest points, was going faster than the wind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:23">[00:08:23]</a>.
    *   **Sustained Acceleration**: Using video footage and the rotation of the back wheel, it was shown that the car kept accelerating even after the tell-tale flipped backwards, disproving the gust/inertia theory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>. The tell-tale also consistently pointed straight back for over 30 seconds, not jumping around as expected with gusts or gradients <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:00">[00:09:00]</a>.
    *   **Record Speed Evidence**: When Blackbird achieved its record speed of 27.7 mph in a 10 mph tailwind, it was still accelerating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>. This was confirmed by multiple GPS units in the car and wind speed measurements at the propeller's height in multiple locations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.

*   **Replication of Treadmill Experiments**:
    *   To counter skepticism about the treadmill tests, the host asked engineer Xyla Foxlin to build a model downwind cart <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>. After initial failures, her fourth version worked spectacularly on a treadmill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:55">[00:14:55]</a>. This [[experimental_demonstrations_in_physics | experimental demonstration]] provided replicable evidence for the car's principle.

*   **Historical and Academic Validation**:
    *   The concept was established as early as 1969 when Andrew Bauer built the first successful downwind cart to settle a wager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>.
    *   The U.S. Physics Olympiad Semifinal Exam in 2013 included questions about Blackbird, with the solution confirming that both downwind and upwind modes are possible and that, with sufficiently low energy loss, any speed is theoretically possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>.

## How the Blackbird Works: Overcoming Theoretical Challenges

To further clarify, a deeper explanation of the Blackbird's mechanism was provided:

*   **Propeller Function**: The propeller does not work like a windmill. Instead, it turns in the opposite direction to the tailwind, functioning as a fan that pushes air backwards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>. This fan is powered by the wheels, connected via a bike chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:35">[00:10:35]</a>.
*   **Energy Transfer and Thrust**: At wind speed, the car accelerates because the wheels turn the fan, generating forward thrust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>. While there is a backwards force on the wheels to drive the fan, the key is that the wheels move much faster over the ground than the propeller moves through the air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.
*   **Power Equation Analysis**: In the car's frame of reference, using the equation `power = force x velocity`:
    *   Power input at the wheels = (force of ground on wheels) x (velocity of car) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.
    *   Power output at the propeller = (force of prop on air) x (speed of car - speed of wind) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:30">[00:11:30]</a>.
    *   Assuming no losses, power in equals power out. Because the propeller is moving slower *relative to the air* (due to the tailwind), the force at the propeller can be greater than the force at the wheels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>. This allows the thrust force from the propeller to be greater than the backwards force on the wheels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:05">[00:12:05]</a>.
*   **Lever Analogy**: The car functions like a lever or pulley, applying a small force to the wheels over a larger distance to apply a larger force with the propeller over a smaller distance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:12">[00:12:12]</a>.
*   **Addressing the "Divide by Zero"**: The apparent "divide by zero" problem when car speed equals wind speed (implying infinite force) is addressed in two ways:
    1.  **Theoretical Expectation**: Theoretically, with a lever where one arm is zero, infinite weight can be lifted, but with zero displacement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:53">[00:12:53]</a>.
    2.  **Propeller Efficiency Term**: In practice, a propeller efficiency term resolves this, which is ill-defined when the propeller isn't moving through the air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:10">[00:13:10]</a>. A more comprehensive formula for propeller efficiency, though algebraically complex, is well-defined in the zero-speed limit and eliminates the divide-by-zero issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>.

## Simple Mechanical Analogy

To further illustrate the concept without complex aerodynamics, a simple cart with a large wheel rolling on two smaller spools was demonstrated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a>. When a board is pushed to the right, the cart moves down the board faster than the board itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:55">[00:13:55]</a>. The big wheel rotates in the opposite direction of the board's push, analogous to the Blackbird's propeller pushing back against the air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:07">[00:14:07]</a>.

Professor Kusenko conceded the bet and transferred $10,000, acknowledging the evidence presented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:16">[00:15:16]</a>.
