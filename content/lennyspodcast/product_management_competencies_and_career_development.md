## Product Management Competencies Framework
Ravi Mehta developed a [[Product management career growth framework | framework]] at TripAdvisor to clearly define [[craft_of_product_management_and_its_components | product management]] and help individuals identify and develop the skills needed to be effective product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:06">[00:56:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:10">[00:56:10]</a>. This framework consists of 12 competencies organized into four key areas, applicable to all levels from Associate Product Manager (APM) to Chief Product Officer (CPO) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:32">[00:56:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:40">[00:56:40]</a>. The specifics of how each competency is applied may change with seniority, but the underlying structure remains consistent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:47">[00:56:47]</a>.

The four areas and their sub-competencies are:

### 1. Product Execution
This forms the foundation of successful product management, focusing on the ability to build products effectively with teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:34">[00:57:34]</a>.
*   **Functional Specification:** The ability to work with a team to define a Product Requirements Document (PRD) or functional specification for what needs to be built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:00">[00:57:00]</a>.
*   **Product Delivery:** The ability to collaborate with engineering, design, and other teams to translate specifications into a working product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:12">[00:57:12]</a>.
*   **Product Quality:** Ensuring that what is built is of high quality from technical, design, usability, and business perspectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:21">[00:57:21]</a>.

### 2. Customer Insight
Beyond building, understanding customers is crucial for determining *what* to build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:06">[00:58:06]</a>.
*   **Fluency with Data:** Utilizing available data to inform decisions about customer needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:13">[00:58:13]</a>.
*   **Voice of the Customer:** Engaging in conversations with customers to advocate for their needs throughout the company and within the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>.
*   **User Experience Design:** A fundamental part of being a good product manager, involving detailed thinking about the user experience, not just defining functionality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:32">[00:58:32]</a>. This extends beyond User Interface (UI) design to include API specifications or ML model training systems for effective user experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:51">[00:58:51]</a>.

### 3. Product Strategy
This area covers how product efforts align with and drive business value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:16">[00:59:16]</a>.
*   **Owning Business Outcomes:** Shifting focus from merely shipping features to driving valuable business outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:22">[00:59:22]</a>.
*   **Product Vision and Roadmapping:** The ability to integrate individual work pieces into a coherent product vision and roadmap that supports the overall product and company strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:42">[00:59:42]</a>.
*   **Strategic Impact:** Ensuring that the sequence of business outcomes moves the strategy forward and delivers impactful results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:56">[00:59:56]</a>.

### 4. Leadership
Involves influencing people and guiding teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:21">[01:00:21]</a>.
*   **Stakeholder Inclusion:** Collaborating with various individuals across the organization to gain support for product work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:25">[01:00:25]</a>.
*   **Team Leadership:** For those with direct reports, helping team members become highly effective product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:34">[01:00:34]</a>.
*   **Managing Up:** Gaining the support of leadership within the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:46">[01:00:46]</a>.

## Applying the Framework for Career Development
To grow using this framework, individuals can:
1.  **Self-evaluate:** Rate themselves on each of the 12 competencies (e.g., needs focus, on track, outperforming) to quickly understand their current standing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:59">[01:02:59]</a>.
2.  **Seek Manager Feedback:** Ask their manager to perform the same evaluation, then discuss areas of agreement and difference for a deeper conversation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:11">[01:03:11]</a>.

This process facilitates "exponential feedback," which provides compounding returns on growth by focusing on underlying behaviors rather than just surface-level symptoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:28">[01:03:28]</a>. By categorizing areas for growth, individuals can address root causes and continuously improve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:11">[01:04:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:22">[01:04:22]</a>.

## Leadership in Product Management: Scalable vs. Selective Micromanagement
When transitioning into leadership roles, product managers often encounter two common failure modes:
1.  **Micromanagement:** Over-controlling direct reports, which erodes trust and limits the leader's bandwidth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:20">[01:06:20]</a>.
2.  **Completely Hands-Off:** Granting too much autonomy without providing necessary context or guardrails, potentially leading the team off track <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:52">[01:06:52]</a>.

Ravi Mehta suggests a matrix for effective leadership:
*   **Ideal Goal: Scalable Leadership** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:12">[01:08:12]</a>
    *   The leader is confident in the team's direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:15">[01:08:15]</a>.
    *   The team has the autonomy to move in that direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:18">[01:08:18]</a>.
*   **Effective Alternative: Selective Micromanagement** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:24">[01:08:24]</a>
    *   Used when the leader is not confident in the team's direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:27">[01:08:27]</a>.
    *   Involves tactical, temporary intervention to guide the team onto the right path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:38">[01:08:38]</a>.
    *   The aim is to help the team understand the correct direction, allowing the leader to eventually pull back and return to scalable leadership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:42">[01:08:42]</a>.

This approach emphasizes increasing a leader's "dynamic range" â€“ the ability to both focus on high-level strategy and zoom into specific tactical issues when necessary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:54">[01:07:54]</a>. The ultimate goal is to empower teams with frameworks that enable them to make decisions aligned with strategic direction, ideally leading to solutions even better than the leader could have devised <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:21">[01:10:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:48">[01:10:48]</a>.

Leaders should also consider their alignment and confidence levels with their managers:
*   High alignment and high confidence result in full support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:40">[01:11:40]</a>.
*   If alignment is low but confidence is high, the manager may grant permission to pursue a different direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:45">[01:11:45]</a>.
*   Low alignment and low confidence is a recipe for failure, requiring a change in approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:08">[01:12:08]</a>.
