From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

The product management field is seeing a growing distinction between two primary team structures: **feature teams** and **empowered product teams**. This distinction is critical for understanding the effectiveness, value, and future viability of product roles within organizations. Many organizations, especially outside of Silicon Valley, have struggled with efficiency, innovation, and value delivery due to misconceptions about what product management entails and how teams should operate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:50">[01:13:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:13">[01:16:13]</a>.

## Feature Teams (or Feature Factories)

Feature teams, often described as "feature factories," are characterized by their focus on delivering pre-defined outputs rather than solving problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:07">[02:07:07]</a>.

*   **Roadmap Focus**: Feature teams are typically given a roadmap of specific features or projects, often originating from executives, large customers, or other internal stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:14">[02:11:14]</a>. These assignments often come with strict dates and timeframes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:30:52">[02:30:52]</a>.
*   **Role of Product Managers**: In this model, individuals with "product manager" titles often function more as project managers or "product owners" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:31">[01:31:31]</a>. Their primary tasks involve designing, building, testing, and deploying the assigned features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:25">[02:12:25]</a>.
    *   Many are "dramatically overpaid for the value they provide" because their role is essentially project management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:18">[01:18:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:02">[02:02:02]</a>.
    *   Engineers and designers often perceive these "product managers" as not contributing real value, sometimes even preferring to manage tasks themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:47">[01:50:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:02">[01:59:02]</a>.
    *   Product managers on feature teams often describe their role as "facilitating," "communication," or "herding cats," rather than driving strategic outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:30:33">[02:30:33]</a>. The "why" behind the work usually comes from a broader product strategy, not the individual PM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:01">[02:42:01]</a>.
*   **Outcomes vs. Output**: A significant challenge for feature teams is their focus on delivering "output" (features) rather than achieving "outcomes" (solving customer or business problems) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:02">[00:24:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:34">[02:12:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:40:02">[02:40:02]</a>. This often results in a "shocking" amount of waste and little to show for the investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:02">[01:34:02]</a>.
*   **Vulnerability**: Product managers in feature teams are considered among the most vulnerable roles, especially in times of economic tightening or with the rise of [[impact_of_ai_on_product_roles | generative AI]], which can automate administrative tasks like backlog management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:02">[02:22:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:02">[02:59:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:01:02">[05:01:02]</a>.
*   **Industry Impact**: This model often leads to poor quality software, especially prevalent in B2B sectors where companies might be sales-driven and led by non-product CEOs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:53:02">[02:53:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.

## Empowered Product Teams

In contrast, empowered product teams are focused on solving real problems for customers and the business, driving measurable outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.

*   **Problem-Solving Focus**: Instead of a feature roadmap, empowered teams are given specific customer or business problems to solve, typically one or two per quarter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:46">[02:11:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.
*   **Outcome Measurement**: Success is measured by whether the problem is solved and value is delivered, not simply by shipping a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:02">[02:57:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:02">[02:18:02]</a>. The focus shifts from "time to market" to "time to money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:02">[02:33:02]</a>.
*   **Role of Product Managers**: On an empowered team, the product manager is a "creator," not just a "facilitator" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:02">[02:50:02]</a>. They are responsible for the **value** (whether customers will buy/use it) and **viability** (whether it works for the business) of the solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:02">[02:17:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:02">[02:49:02]</a>.
    *   This requires specific skills:
        *   **Expertise in Users and Customers**: Deep understanding of customer needs and behaviors, often gained through direct engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:01">[02:26:01]</a>.
        *   **Data Analysis**: Proficiency in understanding how products are used and purchased, and how usage changes over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:02">[02:27:02]</a>.
        *   **Business Acumen**: Representing and integrating compliance, sales, marketing, financial, monetization, and legal considerations into product decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:02">[02:37:02]</a>. This prevents "design by committee" by having a dedicated person bring this knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:02">[02:10:02]</a>.
        *   **Market Understanding**: Deep knowledge of the broader market context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:02">[02:18:02]</a>.
*   **Collaboration**: The product manager works side-by-side with designers (responsible for usability) and engineers (responsible for feasibility) to create solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:02">[02:48:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:02">[02:10:02]</a>. This is often referred to as the product triad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:02">[01:43:02]</a>.
*   **[[empowering_product_teams_and_the_concept_of_context_over_control | Empowerment]]**: True empowerment means leaders define the strategic "bets" (problems to solve), and teams have the latitude to figure out the best solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:46:02">[04:46:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:02">[04:57:02]</a>. This is distinct from a "top-down" feature factory approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:53:02">[04:53:02]</a>.
*   **Key Principles of the Product Operating Model**:
    *   Innovation over predictability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:48">[01:08:48]</a>.
    *   Learning over fear of failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:56">[01:08:56]</a>.
    *   Principles over rigid processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:58">[01:08:58]</a>.
    *   Teams are empowered with problems to solve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:05">[01:09:05]</a>.
    *   Real sense of ownership within teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:14">[01:09:14]</a>.
    *   Addressing product risks and embracing quick experimentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:23">[01:09:23]</a>.
    *   Testing ideas responsibly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:29">[01:09:29]</a>.
    *   Small, frequent, uncoupled releases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:35">[01:09:35]</a>.
    *   Continuous Integration/Continuous Deployment (CI/CD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:40">[01:09:40]</a>.
    *   Instrumentation and monitoring of everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:42">[01:09:42]</a>.

*   **Vulnerability**: Product managers in feature teams are considered among the most vulnerable roles, especially in times of economic tightening or with the rise of [[impact_of_ai_on_product_roles | generative AI]], which can automate administrative tasks like backlog management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:02">[02:22:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:02">[02:59:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:01:02">[05:01:02]</a>.
*   **Industry Impact**: This model often leads to poor quality software, particularly prevalent in B2B sectors where companies might be sales-driven and led by non-product CEOs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:53:02">[02:53:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.

## Empowered Product Teams

In contrast, [[empowering_product_teams_and_the_concept_of_context_over_control | empowered product teams]] are focused on solving real problems for customers and the business, driving measurable outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.

*   **Problem-Solving Focus**: Instead of a feature roadmap, empowered teams are given specific customer or business problems to solve, typically one or two per quarter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:46">[02:11:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:02">[02:54:02]</a>.
*   **Outcome Measurement**: Success is measured by whether the problem is solved and value is delivered, not simply by shipping a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:02">[02:57:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:02">[02:18:02]</a>. The focus shifts from "time to market" to "time to money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:02">[02:33:02]</a>.
*   **Role of Product Managers**: On an empowered team, the product manager is a "creator," not just a "facilitator" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:02">[02:50:02]</a>. They are responsible for the **value** (whether customers will buy/use it) and **viability** (whether it works for the business) of the solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:02">[02:17:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:02">[02:49:02]</a>.
    *   This requires specific skills:
        *   **Expertise in Users and Customers**: Deep understanding of customer needs and behaviors, often gained through direct engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:01">[02:26:01]</a>.
        *   **Data Analysis**: Proficiency in understanding how products are used and purchased, and how usage changes over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:02">[02:27:02]</a>.
        *   **Business Acumen**: Representing and integrating compliance, sales, marketing, financial, monetization, and legal considerations into product decisions. This prevents "design by committee" by having a dedicated person bring this knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:02">[02:37:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:02">[02:10:02]</a>.
        *   **Market Understanding**: Deep knowledge of the broader market context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:02">[02:18:02]</a>.
*   **Collaboration**: The product manager works side-by-side with designers (responsible for usability) and engineers (responsible for feasibility) to create solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:02">[02:48:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:02">[02:10:02]</a>. This is often referred to as the product triad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:02">[01:43:02]</a>.
*   **[[Empowering product teams and the concept of context over control | Empowerment]]**: True [[Empowering product teams and the concept of context over control | empowerment]] means leaders define the strategic "bets" (problems to solve), and teams have the latitude to figure out the best solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:46:02">[04:46:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:02">[04:57:02]</a>. This is distinct from a "top-down" feature factory approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:53:02">[04:53:02]</a>.
*   **Key Principles of the Product Operating Model**:
    *   Innovation is more important than predictability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:48">[01:08:48]</a>.
    *   Learning is more important than failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:56">[01:08:56]</a>.
    *   Principles are more important than process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:58">[01:08:58]</a>.
    *   Teams are empowered with problems to solve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:05">[01:09:05]</a>.
    *   There is a real sense of ownership within teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:14">[01:09:14]</a>.
    *   Emphasis on addressing product risks and embracing quick experimentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:23">[01:09:23]</a>.
    *   Responsible testing of ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:29">[01:09:29]</a>.
    *   Small, frequent, uncoupled releases are preferred <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:35">[01:09:35]</a>.
    *   Continuous Integration/Continuous Deployment (CI/CD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:40">[01:09:40]</a>.
    *   Instrumentation and monitoring of everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:42">[01:09:42]</a>.
*   **Product Leaders**: Product leaders are crucial for setting product strategy and making strategic bets, ensuring that teams' efforts align with overarching business goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:46:02">[04:46:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:53:02">[04:53:02]</a>. This avoids anarchy and ensures coherence across multiple teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:02">[04:50:02]</a>.

## Conclusion

The fundamental difference lies in the shift from a delivery-focused "output" mindset to an "outcome"-driven approach where teams are empowered to solve problems and deliver real value. While feature teams may seem efficient in delivering discrete tasks, they often lack the agility, innovation, and accountability for true [[impact_in_product_management | impact]] that [[Empowering product teams and the concept of context over control | empowered product teams]] achieve. Companies aiming for sustainable success and genuine customer satisfaction are increasingly realizing the necessity of transforming towards the product operating model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:02">[01:24:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:02">[01:31:02]</a>. This requires significant organizational [[Key strategies and practices for effective product management and team building | transformation]] and a commitment to investing in the right skills and mindset for product managers and leaders alike <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:02">[00:57:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:02">[02:50:02]</a>.
