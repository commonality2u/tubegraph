Bengali Caba, Director of Product Management at YouTube, and former early growth PM at Facebook, Head of Growth at Instagram, and VP of Product at Instacart, shares several frameworks and strategies crucial for driving product growth and improving [[improving_product_management_skills | product management skills]]. ## Impact-Driven Approach to Product Growth

At the core of Bengali Caba's philosophy is the drive for impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>. He emphasizes that impact is the most important variable to optimize for, as compensation and career leveling are derivatives of the impact one drives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:00">[00:09:00]</a>. For a [[skills_for_modern_product_managers | product manager]], impact can mean:
*   Driving extreme clarity about product problems, opportunities, and prioritization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:54">[00:09:54]</a>.
*   Delivering on that clarity by showing quick progress, consistent delivery, and a high win rate for shipped features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:34">[00:10:34]</a>.

### The "Understand, Identify, Execute" Framework

Bengali Caba highlights the "Understand, Identify, Execute" framework, which he calls the "Facebook way of thinking" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:37">[00:27:37]</a>. This framework counters the "Identify, Justify, Execute" anti-pattern, where teams build something they *think* is a good idea, justify it with data, and then realize it doesn't succeed because key components or user needs were not truly understood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:45">[00:26:45]</a>.

> [!INFO] Understand Work
> "Understand work" is an intentional allowance in the execution plan to de-risk a project and gain crucial knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:56">[00:27:56]</a>. It should be explicitly planned and can involve various team members:
> *   **Product Managers:** Writing a strategy or dogfooding the product in a specific user state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:10">[00:28:10]</a>.
> *   **Data Scientists:** Pulling funnels, analyzing different user types, or understanding proxy metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:51">[00:28:51]</a>.
> *   **Engineers:** Instrumenting logging, refactoring code for scalability, or ensuring data visibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:01">[00:29:01]</a>.
> *   **Cross-functional teams:** Including go-to-market and marketing partners in planning sessions to ensure inclusive understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:41">[00:37:41]</a>.

Understand work helps clarify root causes, jobs-to-be-done, and use cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:16">[00:29:16]</a>. It creates a "velocity multiplier" over time by leading to more informed execution and a higher win rate for shipped features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:32">[00:30:32]</a>. For new teams or problem spaces, the balance of work should initially lean higher towards understand work (e.g., 40-60%), shifting to more execution as understanding grows (e.g., 80-85% execution) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:51">[00:31:51]</a>.

Examples of "Understand Work" in action:
*   At Instagram, initial "understand work" involved instrumenting the signup funnel to identify drop-off points, which then informed targeted tests to improve the experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:51">[00:39:51]</a>.
*   At YouTube, a team working on paid virtual goods in live experiences first had to map out the entire live ecosystem funnel to understand where gaps and opportunities existed before shipping iterations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:05">[00:33:05]</a>.
*   At Facebook, understanding why friend recommendations were poor in India led to on-the-ground research revealing cultural differences in how people identified connections and used profile information, which was vastly different from Western paradigms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:50">[01:26:50]</a>. This unexpected data led to further "understand work" on common names and how people navigated identity on the platform <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:40">[01:28:40]</a>.

### "Managing Complex Change" Framework for Team Effectiveness

To turn around cultures and improve team speed, Bengali Caba uses a framework with five components:
*   **Vision**
*   **Skills**
*   **Incentives**
*   **Resources**
*   **Action Plan** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:06">[00:42:06]</a>

Missing any one component leads to specific negative outcomes:
*   Missing **Vision** leads to confusion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:45">[00:42:45]</a>.
*   Missing **Skills** leads to anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:53">[00:44:53]</a>.
*   Missing **Incentives** leads to resistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:50">[00:42:50]</a>.
*   Missing **Resources** leads to frustration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:09">[00:42:09]</a>.
*   Missing **Action Plan** leads to false starts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>.

As a leader, this framework helps diagnose where teams are struggling by observing their symptoms and then pulling the right levers. Action plans are often easier to institute (e.g., PRD frameworks, meeting structures), while changing vision and skills takes more time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:22">[00:43:22]</a>. Bengali Caba brings a "deck of different skills and frameworks" to help uplevel teams quickly and establish a shared language <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:49">[00:43:49]</a>.

### The "Adjacent User Theory" for Growth

This theory, developed during his time at Instagram, focuses on understanding the "next user" who could be using the product but isn't, or isn't having a great experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:01">[01:06:01]</a>. For hypergrowth products, user personas change rapidly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:15">[01:04:15]</a>.

> [!TIP] Adjacent User Theory
> To apply this theory, teams should:
> 1.  Understand who their current users are and their motivations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:47">[01:05:47]</a>.
> 2.  Identify who the "next user" is and why the product isn't working for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:01">[01:06:01]</a>.
> 3.  **Dogfood the product as the adjacent user** to understand their experience and what's broken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:04">[01:06:04]</a>. This often means using the product from a new account, without prior history or existing connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:53">[00:56:53]</a>.
> 4.  Talk to, observe, and visit these users to understand their needs and how to enable the right jobs for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:47">[01:06:47]</a>.

Examples:
*   At Instacart, the initial user might have been an office admin, but the adjacent user became a parent of three or four kids who needed to depend on the service <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:10">[01:06:10]</a>.
*   Recognizing that Instagram's core users were changing (e.g., from early adopters to people in India or the Philippines who were less tech-savvy or used different phones) was critical to addressing declining conversion rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:34">[01:05:34]</a>.

### Building Compounding Growth Loops (Flywheels)

Bengali Caba emphasizes the importance of understanding the value proposition at all points of the experience, especially in multi-sided marketplaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:48">[00:55:48]</a>. This involves focusing on the "jobs to be done" for users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:03">[00:59:03]</a>.

Growth is not just about one way of acquiring people; it's about building multiple compounding growth loops <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:05">[01:10:05]</a>.
*   **Instagram's Compounding Growth Engines:**
    *   **Invitations:** People inviting others and ensuring these invitations led to valuable connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:37">[01:10:37]</a>.
    *   **Celebrity Partnerships:** A savvy and astute strategy where a dedicated partnerships team taught celebrities how to use Instagram to tell their stories and build their brands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:52">[01:10:52]</a>. These celebrities set norms for platform usage and generated media coverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:17">[01:11:17]</a>.
    *   **SEO:** News articles linking to celebrity Instagram accounts and posts, combined with the launch of a web presence and embeds, created a powerful SEO engine that magnified top-of-funnel growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:32">[01:13:32]</a>.
    *   **Paid Media:** Using signals from the other loops to optimize paid acquisition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:21">[01:12:21]</a>.

Each of these engines compounded the others, supercharging the user acquisition process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:30">[01:12:30]</a>.

### Focusing on Onboarding, Activation, and Retention

The biggest growth opportunities often lie in the onboarding to habit-building experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:02">[01:09:02]</a>. It's critical for teams to understand the product's "aha moment" and how to help users build habits and retain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:17">[01:09:17]</a>.

> [!WARNING] Leaky Bucket Syndrome
> Many teams focus solely on top-of-funnel growth (acquisition), but if users aren't finding value and retaining, the product becomes a "leaky bucket," negating acquisition efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:53">[01:09:53]</a>.

A significant growth win at Instagram was solving "account access churn" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:55">[01:20:55]</a>. Hundreds of thousands of users were logging out and couldn't get back in, often due to forgetting login details or sharing phones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:24">[01:20:24]</a>. The solution involved:
*   An "Omni box" login experience that accepted email, phone, or handle in one field <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:01">[01:22:01]</a>.
*   Automatically sending a text message with a login link if initial attempts failed on a trusted device <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:11">[01:22:11]</a>.
*   Prompting users to save credentials or set a passcode upon logging out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:26">[01:22:26]</a>.

This effort not only added millions of monthly active users but also unexpectedly drove more content creation, as users were accessing second and third accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:03">[01:23:03]</a>. This led to the development of a dedicated "multiple accounts" team to facilitate seamless navigation between accounts, demonstrating the iterative power of "understand work" leading to new product opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:34">[01:23:34]</a>.
