From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

When applying the [[understanding_jobs_to_be_done_framework | Jobs to be Done]] (JTBD) framework, certain misconceptions and common [[challenges_and_pitfalls_in_product_management | pitfalls]] can hinder its effectiveness. Understanding these can help practitioners leverage the framework more successfully.

## Core Misconceptions

### Pain and Gain vs. Context and Outcome
One of the biggest misconceptions about JTBD is the notion that it's solely about "pain and gain" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:02">[00:00:02]</a>. The reality is that the framework emphasizes understanding the *context* in which a customer finds themselves and the desired *outcome* they seek <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>. The context makes seemingly irrational behavior rational <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:13">[00:00:13]</a>. Without the full story of the context, actions might appear unbelievable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>. This means framing interactions as a "vector of progress" or "intention" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:36">[00:10:36]</a>.

### Supply-Side vs. Demand-Side Competition
Companies often view their competitive set from the "supply side" – comparing their product to similar products based on technology or business model (e.g., Snickers vs. Milky Way) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:11">[00:07:11]</a>. However, JTBD helps to see the true competitive set from the "demand side" – understanding what diverse solutions customers "hire" to make progress in their lives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:11">[00:07:11]</a>. For example, a Snickers bar might compete with a protein drink, Red Bull, or coffee if the job is to quickly regain energy after missing a meal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.

### Product-First Thinking
A common pitfall is to start with the product and then try to find demand, operating under the assumption of "build it and they will come" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. Instead, demand is caused by a "struggling moment" a customer experiences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:56">[00:07:56]</a>. Focusing on these struggling moments, rather than just building more features, is the seed for real innovation and new products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:45">[00:09:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:14">[00:13:14]</a>.

### The "Build the Best Product" Fallacy
It's often believed that building the "best" product with the most features will lead to better sales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>. However, a "kickass half is better than a half-ass whole" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:54">[00:10:54]</a>. Success lies in meeting customers where they are, not trying to overwhelm or convince them, as they convince themselves to make progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>.

### Ignoring the Four Forces of Progress
People don't randomly switch products or behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:49">[00:11:49]</a>. The JTBD framework highlights four forces at play when a customer considers a change:
1.  **Push** from the old product/situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:02">[00:12:02]</a>.
2.  **Pull** towards a new outcome or state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a>.
3.  **Anxiety** of the new solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>.
4.  **Habit** of the present (inertia) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:53">[00:12:53]</a>.

For a customer to adopt a new product, the push and pull forces must be greater than the anxiety and habit forces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a>. Simply adding features (increasing pull) can also increase anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:19">[00:13:19]</a>. Reducing friction (anxiety and habit) can be as effective as improving the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a>.

### Sales vs. Enabling Purchase
Instead of trying to "sell" products, companies should focus on "enabling people to buy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:20">[00:14:20]</a>. This means designing the sales process around how customers want to buy, not how the company wants to sell <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:38">[00:14:38]</a>. This involves understanding the different phases of a customer's buying process, such as first thought, passive looking, active looking, deciding, first use, and ongoing use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:00">[00:16:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a>.

### Trusting Stated Preferences Over Observed Behavior
Customers often state what they *think* they want (e.g., granite countertops, energy-efficient homes), but their actual purchasing decisions involve different trade-offs (e.g., finished basements) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:25">[00:17:25]</a>. JTBD research is not based on traditional market research that asks people what they want, but rather on "criminal and intelligence interrogation" techniques, prompting individuals to recount the story of *how* they made a decision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:53">[00:17:53]</a>. It's crucial to study what people *did* rather than what they *say they will do* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:44">[00:36:44]</a>.

### "Bitching Ain't Switching"
Customer complaints ("bitching") do not automatically mean they are ready to switch ("switching") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:04">[00:37:04]</a>. Many users will complain about missing features (e.g., Gantt charts in Basecamp) but will not actually leave a product because of it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:13">[00:37:13]</a>. Following the demands of "best users" can lead to feature bloat, complicating a product and alienating the core user base who value simplicity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:25">[00:37:25]</a>.

### Hypothesizing Jobs in a Conference Room
One of the most dangerous things to do is to hypothesize what the jobs are by sitting in a conference room without talking to real people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:27">[00:45:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:54">[00:54:54]</a>. This approach is prone to being "100% wrong" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:35">[00:45:35]</a>. Real jobs research is about *hypothesis building*, not hypothesis testing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:46">[00:41:46]</a>.

### Products Don't Have Jobs, People Do
A fundamental distinction is that "only people have jobs; products don't have jobs" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:48">[00:49:48]</a>. This perspective highlights the importance of understanding the irrational human elements and specific contexts that drive a person's decision to "hire" a product or service <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:55">[00:49:55]</a>.

### When JTBD May Not Be the Right Framework
JTBD may be less applicable in situations where there is:
*   **No real customer choice**: For example, choosing health insurance often lacks true choice, as it's typically provided by an employer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:12">[00:51:12]</a>.
*   **Deep-seated, unconscious habits**: For products that are consumed out of habit (like chewing gum), people often can't recall the specific moment of purchase or the reasoning behind it, making it difficult to extract job insights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:51">[00:52:51]</a>. In such cases, observing *usage* patterns (ethnography) might be more insightful than asking about purchase decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>.

## Practical Challenges in Application

### Interviewing Effectively
Getting accurate insights requires specific interview techniques:
*   **Avoid discussion guides**: These can prevent interviewers from following truly meaningful information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:28">[00:30:28]</a>.
*   **Focus on recent purchases/changes**: Interview people who have recently "hired" a product or "fired" an old one (e.g., churned customers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:29">[00:19:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:13">[00:35:13]</a>.
*   **Ask "why" indirectly**: Instead of "why, why, why," use phrases like "tell me more about that" or "give me an example" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:55">[00:30:55]</a>.
*   **Mirroring and playing back incorrectly**: Techniques like mirroring or deliberately playing back information incorrectly can prompt the interviewee to elaborate and provide more detail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:51">[00:27:51]</a>.
*   **Push beyond superficial answers**: Move past the "palladium layer" of general, polite answers to the "fantasy/nightmare layer" where people exaggerate, and then to the deeper layer of "what actually happened" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:20">[00:38:20]</a>.
*   **Small number of interviews**: For qualitative research, around 7-8 interviews can reveal repeating patterns of pushes, pulls, anxieties, and habits; 10-12 are usually sufficient, rather than conducting 50 or more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:10">[00:29:10]</a>.

### Dealing with Contradictions
When a customer's story seems irrational or contradictory (e.g., saying they don't want a dining room table but refusing to move without a place for it), it's a sign that the context is crucial and reveals deeper, unstated motivations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:13">[00:00:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:59">[00:54:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:49">[01:04:49]</a>. These contradictions often expose the true "irrational piece" that drives behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:31">[00:46:31]</a>.

By understanding these [[challenges_and_misconceptions_in_strategic_thinking | challenges and misconceptions]], organizations can more effectively apply the [[understanding_jobs_to_be_done_framework | Jobs to be Done]] framework to uncover hidden insights and create successful products and services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>.
