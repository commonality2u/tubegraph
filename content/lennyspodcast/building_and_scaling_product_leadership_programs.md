## Origin of Product Leadership Programs
The framework for [[key_strategies_and_practices_for_effective_product_management_and_team_building | product management competencies]] was developed at TripAdvisor when the company needed to rapidly grow its product team after going public <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:04">[00:55:04]</a>. Hiring experienced Product Managers (PMs) was slow, taking three to six months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:25">[00:55:25]</a>. To address this, a "product rotational program" was initiated to hire individuals directly from business school and undergrad into their first product roles, regardless of prior experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:36">[00:55:36]</a>. This two-year program included four six-month rotations across different team types (e.g., zero to one, growth, infrastructure) to provide diverse experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:51">[00:55:51]</a>. The core need was to clearly define what product management entails and how to help individuals develop the necessary skills to become senior and effective product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:10">[00:56:10]</a>.

## Key Competencies for Product Managers
The framework consists of 12 competencies across four main areas, applicable to all levels from Associate Product Manager (APM) to Chief Product Officer (CPO) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:34">[00:56:34]</a>:

1.  **Product Execution** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:51">[00:56:51]</a>
    *   **Functional Specification**: Ability to define product requirements (e.g., PRD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:02">[00:57:02]</a>.
    *   **Product Delivery**: Ability to work with engineering, design, and other teams to turn specifications into working products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:14">[00:57:14]</a>.
    *   **Product Quality**: Ensuring the built product is high-quality from technical, design, usability, and business perspectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:24">[00:57:24]</a>.
    *   *Application*: An APM focuses on individual contribution, while a CPO designs systems to ensure effective execution and high quality across teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:44">[00:57:44]</a>.

2.  **Customer Insight** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:04">[00:58:04]</a>
    *   **Fluency with Data**: Using data to make decisions about customer needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:13">[00:58:13]</a>.
    *   **Voice of the Customer**: Engaging in conversations with customers to advocate for their needs throughout the company and within the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>.
    *   **User Experience Design**: Understanding and defining user experience in detail, whether through wireframes for pixels or API specs for APIs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:32">[00:58:32]</a>.

3.  **Product Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:16">[00:59:16]</a>
    *   **Owning Business Outcomes**: Shifting focus from shipping features to driving [[product_operations_and_scaling_product_management | business outcomes]] and understanding how the product creates value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:22">[00:59:22]</a>.
    *   **Product Vision and Roadmapping**: Consolidating individual work into a coherent vision and roadmap that aligns with product and company strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:42">[00:59:42]</a>.
    *   **Strategic Impact**: Ensuring that a sequence of business outcomes effectively moves the overall strategy forward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:56">[00:59:56]</a>.

4.  **Leadership** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:21">[01:00:21]</a>
    *   **Stakeholder Inclusion**: Collaborating with diverse stakeholders to build consensus around product work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:25">[01:00:25]</a>.
    *   [[hiring_and_developing_growth_leaders | Team Leadership]]: For those with direct reports, helping them become effective product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:34">[01:00:34]</a>.
    *   **Managing Up**: Gaining the support of leadership within the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:47">[01:00:47]</a>.

### Exponential Feedback for [[Career Growth Strategies for Product Managers | PM Growth]]
To foster growth, managers should provide "exponential feedback" that yields compounding returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:22">[01:03:22]</a>. This means focusing on underlying behaviors and root causes rather than just surface-level symptoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:47">[01:03:47]</a>. A simple exercise is for both PM and manager to rate the PM's performance across the 12 competencies, using the differences and agreements as a basis for deep conversation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:00">[01:03:00]</a>.

## Effective Product Leadership Approaches
Product leaders often struggle with how to empower their teams while ensuring strategic alignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:07">[01:06:07]</a>.

### Scalable Leadership vs. Selective Micromanagement
> [!NOTE] Leadership Matrix
> Ravi Mehta proposes a matrix for product leadership, with the ideal goal being **scalable leadership** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:12">[01:08:12]</a>.
>
> *   **Scalable Leadership**: The leader is confident in the team's direction, and the team has the autonomy to execute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:15">[01:08:15]</a>.
> *   **Selective Micromanagement**: If the leader is not confident in the team's direction, the correct approach is temporary, tactical micromanagement to guide them towards the right direction, with the goal of then pulling back and restoring autonomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:38">[01:08:38]</a>.
>
> **Failure Modes**:
> *   **Micro-mismanagement**: The leader lacks confidence, the team lacks autonomy, and there's no clear end in sight, leading to frustration for both parties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:05">[01:09:05]</a>.
> *   **Completely Hands-Off**: The leader gives autonomy without sufficient context or guardrails, potentially leading the team astray <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:54">[01:06:54]</a>.
>
> Great product leaders, like Steve Jobs or Elon Musk, increase their "dynamic range" â€“ they can zoom in on details when needed but primarily focus on strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:54">[01:07:54]</a>. The objective is to replace active guidance with providing a framework that the team understands, enabling them to make aligned decisions autonomously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:21">[01:10:21]</a>.

## Coaching in Product Leadership
Ravi Mehta's current company, Outpace, focuses on making expert-driven coaching accessible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>. Outpace combines content (audio lessons, interactive exercises) with coaching, using AI to assist coaches in providing structured, efficient feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:20">[01:13:20]</a>. This AI augmentation allows coaches to tailor suggestions based on an individual's style, whether action-oriented, sympathetic, inquisitive, or informative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:12">[01:14:12]</a>. The role of AI is seen as amplifying human capabilities rather than replacing them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:42">[01:14:42]</a>.

For individuals seeking a coach, Ravi recommends asking, "Tell me about the client that you're most proud of helping?" This reveals the coach's values, engagement style, and areas of expertise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:35">[01:19:35]</a>.
