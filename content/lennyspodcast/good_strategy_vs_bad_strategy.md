## The Kernel of Good Strategy

A good strategy, often referred to as "the kernel," comprises three essential elements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>:

1.  **Diagnosis**
    The first step is to understand the situation, identifying the nature of the reality being dealt with <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:24">[00:09:24]</a>. A diagnosis involves deciding what to pay attention to and forming hypotheses about how things connect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>. It's not just about understanding the world, but specifically understanding *the challenge* that makes the situation difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:31">[00:56:31]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:39">[00:56:39]</a>.

2.  **Guiding Policy**
    This element outlines how the organization will deal with the diagnosed situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:02">[00:12:02]</a>. It defines what the organization *will* do and, crucially, what it *will not* do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:07">[00:12:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:09">[00:12:09]</a>. A guiding policy is focused, unlike a "lottery list" of 17 priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>.

3.  **Coherent Actions**
    This is the most crucial part of a strategy, as it involves taking concrete steps that are aligned with the diagnosis and guiding policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:13">[00:12:13]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. Actions must directly address the problem and should not be self-contradictory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:36">[00:12:36]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:47">[00:12:47]</a>. Without coherent actions, a strategy remains a high-level concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:22">[00:12:22]</a>.

### Example of a Good Strategy
For Microsoft adapting to AI, a good strategy would involve:
*   **Diagnosis:** Recognizing the challenge of adapting to AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:24">[00:15:24]</a>.
*   **Guiding Policy:** Deciding to invest in a major AI leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:32">[00:15:32]</a>.
*   **Coherent Actions:** Incorporating AI into its search engine and other products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:42">[00:15:42]</a>.

## Signs of Bad Strategy

Many organizations fail to develop good strategies, instead creating "bad strategy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:40">[00:16:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:49">[00:16:49]</a>. Common signs include:

*   **Abstract Goals Masquerading as Strategy:** A set of profit or performance goals is an ambition, not a strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:37">[00:17:37]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:51">[00:17:51]</a>. Stating "we want to grow faster" is a statement of value, not a strategy, as it doesn't address *what's holding you back* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:50">[00:19:50]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:13">[00:20:13]</a>.
*   **Fluff and Word Salad:** Using fancy, abstract words to describe a situation, often leading to a lack of clarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:43">[00:20:43]</a>.
*   **Incoherent Actions:** Proposing actions that contradict each other, such as increasing growth and profit magically without addressing the inherent trade-offs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:59">[00:20:59]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>.
*   **Missing Diagnosis:** Failing to articulate the core problem or challenge, often leading to strategies that don't address the root cause <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:57">[00:23:57]</a>. For example, stating a strategy is "agencies should work together more effectively" without diagnosing *why coordination is hard* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:35">[00:18:35]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:22">[00:19:22]</a>.
*   **Lottery List of Priorities:** Presenting a long list of desired outcomes, where everything is a "priority," dilutes focus and makes genuine progress impossible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>.

## The Role of [[the_concept_of_power_in_competitive_strategy | Power]] and Focus

A good strategy leverages a source of [[the_concept_of_power_in_competitive_strategy | power]] by exploiting an asymmetry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:45">[00:28:45]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:17">[00:29:17]</a>. This means identifying something different between competitors that can be leveraged, such as being first, having a strong reputation, or possessing unique relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:22">[00:29:22]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:41">[00:29:41]</a>.

> "Focus is the fundamental source of power." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:09">[00:25:09]</a>

Diffusion of effort, often seen in large organizations like governments, weakens strategic impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:37">[00:27:37]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>.

[[the_concept_of_power_in_competitive_strategy | Power]] can stem from:
*   Asymmetry (e.g., speed, unique knowledge, resources) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:52">[00:32:52]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:22">[00:42:22]</a>.
*   Network effects (e.g., user base size, like Amazon or social media) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:16">[00:35:16]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:35">[00:36:35]</a>.
*   Specialization, allowing smaller entities to compete with market leaders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:18">[00:39:18]</a>.

## Challenges to Good Strategy: Organizational Dynamics

One of the biggest hindrances to winning strategies is organizational dynamics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:57">[01:40:57]</a>.
[[addressing_execution_problems_by_looking_at_strategy_and_culture | Organizational politics]] and diverse interests within a company often lead to:
*   **Lack of Decision-Making:** Executives may know the issues but disagree on their importance, or their personal interests prevent unified action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:17">[01:15:17]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:38">[01:15:38]</a>.
*   **Fear of Action:** Implementing a strategy involves changing what people do and altering power relationships, which can be challenging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:55">[01:15:55]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:05">[01:16:05]</a>.
*   **Displacement by Leadership Theory:** Modern leadership theory often focuses on self-perfection and vision, displacing the need for direct instruction and hard choices in strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:30">[01:17:30]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:01">[01:18:01]</a>.

To overcome this, a clear hierarchy of [[the_concept_of_power_in_competitive_strategy | power]] is typically needed, as someone ultimately has to make choices and resolve disagreements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:04">[01:11:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:24">[01:16:24]</a>.

## Developing a Strategy: The Action Agenda

Instead of calling it a "strategy," it's more effective to call it an "action agenda" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:09">[00:58:09]</a>. This highlights the focus on concrete, coherent actions designed to deal with a specific problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>.

To craft an action agenda:
1.  **State the Problem:** Clearly articulate "what makes it hard" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:05">[00:58:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:46">[00:56:46]</a>. This involves filtering ambitions to identify which ones can realistically be made progress on and what barriers exist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:55">[01:00:55]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:01">[01:01:01]</a>.
2.  **Focus on the Crux:** Identify the single most challenging point of a problem, similar to the "crux" in mountain climbing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:01">[01:03:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:32">[01:03:32]</a>.
    *   The chosen challenge must be **important** (close to ambition) and **achievable** (addressable) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:03">[01:02:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:08">[01:02:08]</a>.
    *   This focus helps achieve "insight," which is crucial for seeing a way to solve the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:52">[01:08:52]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:21">[01:09:21]</a>. Insight comes from deep immersion in the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:28">[01:09:28]</a>.
3.  **Define Action Steps:** Detail specific tasks that will be undertaken, not just goals to be achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:36">[01:02:36]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:37">[01:02:37]</a>. These steps can span from six months to a few years depending on the company size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:31">[01:02:31]</a>.

There is no "science of strategy" like physics or engineering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:54">[01:47:54]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:57">[01:47:57]</a>. Strategic thinking is heavily based on analogy to previous human experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:10">[01:48:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:15">[01:48:15]</a>. Reading history, especially business history, biographies, and world history, provides valuable context and helps in understanding complex situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:29">[01:47:29]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:35">[01:47:35]</a>. ## Strategy for Startups

For startups, especially those pre-product market fit, strategy involves making calculated bets amidst high uncertainty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:55">[02:10:55]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:05">[02:11:05]</a>. The core task is to find an important and achievable problem to solve for a specific target market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:17">[02:21:17]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:19">[02:22:19]</a>. Successful startups often "switch" their approach, adapting rapidly as market realities are revealed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:35">[02:22:35]</a>. An effective action agenda for a startup emphasizes quick adaptation to changing conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:57">[02:59:57]</a>.

Thinking about "value denied" – things people *should* be able to buy but *can't* – can spark new business ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:40">[02:26:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:49">[02:26:49]</a>. This approach involves asking how something "ought to be" and identifying opportunities where innovation is possible despite materials limitations or regulations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:48">[02:27:48]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>.
