From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Gustav Alstromer, a group partner at Y Combinator, spent over four years at Airbnb, where he started the original Airbnb growth team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>. His experience there profoundly shaped his understanding of company building, culture, and growth.

## The Special Culture of Airbnb

Gustav often reflects on his time at Airbnb as the "highlight of my career" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>, noting that many former employees struggle to find a workplace as special <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>. He describes Airbnb as not feeling like a normal job, but rather "like a group of friends trying to just do something together" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37">[00:05:37]</a>, or an "assembly of amazing people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.

Key factors contributing to this unique environment included:
*   **Family-like Bond**: Gustav feels a "very special bond" with Airbnb alumni, more akin to "close social connections" than co-workers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>.
*   **Exceptional Hiring**: Airbnb "brought in a special type of people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. This was considered the main piece that impacted the company's outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>. The early hires set a high standard for subsequent recruitment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.
    *   **Excitement to Build**: They hired individuals who were "really excited to be there" and build Airbnb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.
    *   **Culture Interviews**: The company conducted "culture interviews" to ensure new hires mapped to their core values and were genuinely excited to work on Airbnb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:38">[00:08:38]</a>.
    *   **Diverse Backgrounds**: Many early Product Managers (PMs) at Airbnb were former founders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:29">[00:06:29]</a>. This diversity in backgrounds was believed to "made a big difference for how you make decisions and how you get started on things" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>.
*   **Strong Business Model**: Airbnb had "an incredible business model an incredible business from early on and it was hard to to fail" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:54">[00:09:54]</a>. This allowed the company to take risks that many other startups could not <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a>.

## Lessons for Startups

Gustav highlights several key takeaways from the Airbnb experience that apply to early-stage companies:

### The Primacy of the Customer
The most common mistake startups make, and the primary reason they fail, is that "they don't talk to users which means they don't find proc Market fit and if they don't find product Market fit like nothing else really matters" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:57">[00:25:57]</a>. The core message is to "make things people want" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:17">[00:26:17]</a>.

*   **Overcoming Fear of Rejection**: Founders, especially technical ones, are often afraid to talk to customers because they fear rejection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:52">[00:23:52]</a>. Gustav emphasizes that "the worst thing that can happen to Startup is not that people hate what you're doing is that they're completely indifferent to what you're doing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:08">[00:30:08]</a>. Customers often simply forget about or are indifferent to new services they sign up for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>.
*   **Targeting Early Adopters**: 90% of customers are not early adopters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:34">[00:24:34]</a>. Founders need to reach out to many people to find the 10% who are willing to take risks and try new things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:47">[00:24:47]</a>.
*   **Understanding Pain Points**: The best way to understand the intensity of a customer's problem is not to ask them directly, but to "watch them or to watch them solve the thing that they do" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:04">[00:33:04]</a>. People often don't realize how difficult or inefficient their current processes are <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:25">[00:33:25]</a>.
*   **[[role_of_inflections_and_insights_in_startups | Doing Things That Don't Scale]]**: The classic Airbnb story of Paul Graham telling the founders to go to New York and talk to their customers highlights the importance of unscalable, direct engagement with users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:23">[00:27:23]</a>. This intensive customer interaction is crucial for building a product that is truly valuable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:48">[00:27:48]</a>.

### The Role of Technical Co-Founders
YC doesn't accept many teams that "don't know how to build or get their first prototype built themselves" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:41">[00:25:41]</a>.

*   **Valuing Engineering**: Founders need to "learn how to Value the engineering piece" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:23">[00:35:23]</a>. The engineering part is often the hardest part <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:18">[00:35:18]</a>.
*   **Iterative Development**: So many decisions and iterations in building a successful technology company are technical and rely on engineering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:35">[00:36:35]</a>. It's not a one-off process of writing a spec and being done <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:22">[00:38:22]</a>.
*   **Learning to Code**: Many successful founders learn to code themselves to build a prototype, even if they aren't the best engineers long-term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:57">[00:36:57]</a>.

### Execution Over Strategy
For small startups, "strategy does not matter" because they can only focus on one thing at a time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:34">[00:53:34]</a>. Good founders are "execution oriented" and consistently hit one priority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:10">[00:54:10]</a>. Thinking about corporate strategy assumes product-market fit, which early startups typically lack <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:27">[00:54:27]</a>.

### Product vs. Distribution
While "caring about a product" means caring about the customer's needs and usage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:38">[00:56:38]</a>, scalable distribution becomes crucial once a product is working <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:19">[00:57:19]</a>. You can't start a startup with a "growth team mindset" because it focuses on scalable things; initially, you must "do things that don't scale" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:51">[00:57:51]</a>.
