## The Interplay of Beauty, Functionality, and Trust
The use of the word "beauty" in digitized books has decreased dramatically, aligning with the perception that "functionality is King" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a>. However, [[importance_of_design_and_functionality_in_product_development | beauty enhances functionality]] by making things easier to use, more approachable, and more compelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>. Beyond practical improvements, beautiful design also increases trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>. When users see painstaking detail in a product, it assures them that the creators care about hidden details as well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>.

### Levels of Quality
Quality exists on a spectrum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:55">[00:11:55]</a>:
1.  **Baseline Quality**: Does the product work and provide value? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>
2.  **Well-Rounded Quality**: Is it error-free and does it function well in all aspects? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>
3.  **Exceeding Expectations**: Does it surprise and delight users by doing something they weren't even seeking? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>

The level of quality should be based on user expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. Top companies view quality as non-negotiable and a long-term necessity, recognizing that it directly leads to great usability and desirability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>.

## Addressing Challenges in Design Prioritization
Product managers and founders often intellectually understand design's value but may not prioritize it daily against new features due to unclear ROI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>. This often comes down to being "seduced by the chase of another feature" rather than perfecting existing ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:47">[00:12:47]</a>. Prioritizing features with measurable business goals over those with less clear immediate impact is an enchantment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>.

However, "quality is growth" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:47">[00:15:47]</a>. Making a product easier to use and more understandable drives adoption, increased usage, and positive word-of-mouth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:52">[00:15:52]</a>.

*   **Stripe's Growth Team Example**: Stripe's growth team focuses on [[creating_productmarket_fit_and_operationalizing_product_quality | building better experiences]] because it directly ties to business metrics. *   **Checkout Experience Impact**: Research on top e-commerce sites showed 99% had checkout flow errors hindering conversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:50">[00:16:50]</a>. By maniacally focusing on improving the checkout experience, Stripe saw a 10.5% increase in businesses' revenue when transitioning from an older to a newer checkout form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:33">[00:17:33]</a>. Small details can have a material impact on revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:49">[00:17:49]</a>.

### Key Learnings for [[creating_productmarket_fit_and_operationalizing_product_quality | Operationalizing Quality]]
From discussions with various design, product, and engineering leaders, Katie Dill identified several key themes:
1.  **Quality is a Group Effort**: It's not achievable by a single talented individual or one organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:29">[00:29:29]</a>. Internal standards and communication directly reflect in external product quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:52">[00:29:52]</a>.
2.  **Vision and Alignment**: Even with the best people, a shared vision is needed to ensure individual great work fits into a cohesive whole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:22">[00:30:22]</a>. Analogous to building a house, plans, a general contractor (GC), and an architect are necessary to ensure all pieces fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:50">[00:30:50]</a>.
3.  **Editing and Courage**: An "editor" (GC, architect, or leader) helps unify the product by narrowing, reducing, and removing elements that don't fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:21">[00:31:21]</a>. Leaders need the courage to say "no, this isn't good enough" even after significant effort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:55">[00:31:55]</a>.
4.  **User Perspective**: To build quality, one must understand it from the user's perspective, especially through their complete journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:37">[00:32:37]</a>. Users rarely interact with a product in isolation; their experience spans learning, usage, and adaptation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:51">[00:32:51]</a>.

### The "Walk the Store" Process
To combat quality regression over time (where new features make old ones look worse) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:46">[00:33:46]</a> and organizational silos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:16">[00:34:16]</a>, Stripe implemented the "Walk the Store" program <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:21">[00:34:21]</a>:
*   **Essential User Journeys**: They identified 15 critical user journeys that deeply matter to users and must be of the highest quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:56">[00:34:56]</a>.
*   **Leader Accountability**: Engineering, product, and design leaders are assigned ownership of these journeys <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:20">[00:35:20]</a>.
*   **Regular Review**: Leaders regularly "walk the store," reviewing these journeys as if they were users, starting from an internet search through various product touchpoints (website, docs, dashboard) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:31">[00:35:31]</a>.
*   **Friction Logging**: They use [[importance_of_execution_and_output_in_product_management | friction logs]] to document experiences, noting what works and what doesn't, filing bugs, and reaching out to relevant teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:39">[00:35:39]</a>. A template is provided with screenshots and tags for different levels of severity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:47">[00:44:47]</a>.
*   **Scoring and Calibration**: Journeys are scored based on a rubric that considers usability, utility, desirability, and "surprisingly great" moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:12">[00:45:12]</a>. Scores are qualitative (color-based) rather than purely quantitative to encourage judgment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:43">[00:45:43]</a>. Quarterly "Product Quality Reviews" (PQR) are held where leaders present their findings and scores. These meetings, attended by leaders like Katie Dill and David Singleton, serve as calibration sessions to ensure consistent quality standards across the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:33">[00:36:33]</a>.
*   **Impact**: This process increases awareness and accountability, helps identify larger opportunities for product improvement, and leads to process changes to ensure better products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:40">[00:37:40]</a>. It has converted initial skeptics from different functions into advocates for quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:44">[00:38:44]</a>.

## Team Building and Leadership
When [[key_strategies_and_practices_for_effective_product_management_and_team_building | building, leading, managing, and scaling large teams]], Katie Dill relies on several principles:

### Performance = Potential - Interference
This formula suggests that performance is maximized by increasing team potential (hiring well, developing talent) and decreasing interferences (obstacles, inefficient processes) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:05">[00:55:05]</a>. As a company grows, processes must constantly adapt to avoid becoming faulty and holding back talented individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:21">[00:56:21]</a>.

### Improving Awareness
As companies grow, people lose touch with what's happening across the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:14">[00:57:14]</a>. A simple solution is to have designers share screenshots or prototypes of their work in a shared Google Slide deck every couple of weeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:47">[00:57:47]</a>. This low-maintenance method allows designers to see related work, discover patterns, and fosters alignment across product managers, engineering leaders, and company leaders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:00">[00:58:00]</a>. It helps avoid redundant work and ensures pieces fit together, ultimately making the work better and saving time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:16">[01:01:16]</a>.

### Reducing Interference through Team Structure
At Lyft, Katie observed interference stemming from designers sitting physically separate from engineers and product managers, leading to wasted work and misalignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:19">[01:02:19]</a>. While the design team had a safe space for creative discovery, it caused slower iteration cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:45">[01:02:45]</a>.

To reduce this interference, Katie evolved the team's working style by [[building_trust_and_engagement_within_design_teams | breaking down physical and organizational walls]] to bring design, engineering, and product teams together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:07">[01:04:07]</a>. Designers were embedded within multi-disciplinary teams (e.g., driver, rider, safety teams) while retaining dedicated creative spaces for critique and working sessions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:47">[01:04:47]</a>. This led to faster iteration and better clarity, combining the "best of both worlds" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:06">[01:05:06]</a>.

### Fighting the Fear of Bold Ideas
Companies often fear bold ideas and large changes because they are hard to measure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:51">[01:07:51]</a>. Incremental approaches feel safer, measurable, and fit into quarterly performance cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:19">[01:08:19]</a>. This tendency is dangerous as it prevents making the "whole thing" better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:54">[01:08:54]</a>.

The approach should be to "reach for the stars and land on the Moon" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:02">[01:09:02]</a>. Vision work, which sketches out the ideal "11-star experience" across the entire user journey, is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:24">[01:09:24]</a>. This North Star guides incremental steps, allowing for measured risk-taking and consistent progress toward a comprehensive vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:37">[01:10:37]</a>.

### Hiring Designers
When hiring a designer, focus on:
*   **Taste and Character**: It's easier to teach tools and processes than [[developing_taste_and_quality_in_product_design | taste and character]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:24">[01:11:24]</a>. Look for a natural inclination for great things and strong judgment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:41">[01:11:41]</a>.
*   **Humility**: Humility is vital for teamwork, respect, empathy, and understanding users. Humble designers are more likely to pay attention to user feedback and strive for continuous improvement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:07">[01:12:07]</a>.
*   **Hustle/Hutzpah**: Design involves the scary act of creation and the courage to say "this is not good enough." Look for someone with the courage to fight for greatness and the drive to execute rapidly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:47">[01:12:47]</a>.

For younger companies, a "doer" is needed, but it's important to also have a lens for building a user-focused organization from the start <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:43">[01:13:43]</a>. This might involve a senior design leader as an advisor alongside a full-time executor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:04">[01:14:04]</a>. To assess taste, consider the context of the product; a power tool for professionals might be dense, while a consumer product might be light and sparse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:58">[01:14:58]</a>.
