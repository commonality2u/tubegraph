From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Nikhail Singall, a product leader with experience at companies like Facebook, Credit Karma, Google Hangouts, and Google Photos, offers insights on navigating and building a successful and fulfilling career in product management and beyond. His advice emphasizes long-term thinking, continuous learning, and self-awareness.

## The Long View: Beyond the Next Promotion

Singall highlights a common pitfall: focusing too narrowly on immediate goals like the next promotion or escaping a bad boss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>. He compares this to a "dog track" analogy: dogs chase a fake rabbit, and if they ever catch it, they stop running because they've achieved their only goal and don't know what's next <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:09">[00:00:09]</a>. This phenomenon applies to career paths, where achieving a desired title or financial milestone can lead to a feeling of being lost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:09">[01:04:09]</a>.

> [!NOTE] [[the_pitfalls_of_shortterm_career_focus | The Pitfalls of Short-Term Career Focus]]
> Many professionals focus on "one day I will be X" (e.g., a vice president, have more money, build a company) without considering "what happens next?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. This can lead to sadness or creating conflict when peace is needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.

Instead, Singall advocates for a **long-term perspective**, suggesting individuals "work backwards from your end state" and think of their career as a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. The focus should be on the job *after* the next one, or even the boss's boss's job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. This approach ensures you're always motivated and have something important to pursue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>.

One common short-term trap is conflating career progression with promotion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>. While promotions signify forward movement within a company's system, a career is a much longer arc <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>.

## Building a Diverse Skillset

Chasing "fancy logos" on a resume can feel shallow for builders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:57">[01:11:57]</a>. Singall emphasizes the value of a **diverse set of experiences** for product professionals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:59">[01:11:59]</a>. Learning about different stages of a product's life cycle – pre-product market fit, scaling "smoke into fire," and turning "fire into something great" – makes one a better builder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:02">[01:12:02]</a>.

> [!TIP] [[Career development and growth strategies | Diversify Your Experience]]
> Explore different types of problems, such as inside-the-building vs. outside-the-building, or consumer vs. business-to-business challenges. A diverse career generally leads to greater satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:25">[01:12:25]</a>.

For executives, having experience at "magma" or "FAANG" companies (large, influential tech companies) can be valuable as it signifies expertise in building things at scale and managing millions or billions of users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:42">[01:13:42]</a>. However, such experiences can be substituted with other later-stage companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:05">[01:14:05]</a>.

## Navigating Career Stages

### Early Career: Finding Your Niche and Story

For those in their early careers, Singall offers two key pieces of advice:
1.  **Become World-Class in an Ambiguity**: Identify one area of product ambiguity to master as early as possible. This could be crafting, market ambiguity, organizational ambiguity, domain expertise (e.g., ML, hardware), or managing teams/growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:07">[00:23:07]</a>.
2.  **Develop a Compelling Story**: Be able to articulate what you learned and what you did in previous roles. Think about the "press release" of your current role before you start, focusing on the problem solved, skills built, headwinds faced, and how you overcame them (using "I" not "we") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:28">[00:24:28]</a>.

### Mid-Career: Promotions and Management

When promotions aren't happening, it's crucial to understand why, as simply asking your manager may not reveal the full truth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:01">[00:26:01]</a>. Singall identifies four common reasons for not getting promoted:
1.  **Lack of Advocacy**: No one sees your "magic" or is willing to champion your promotion. This might require changing projects, managers, or even companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>.
2.  **No Role Exists**: The next level or specific job simply isn't available due to company structure, hiring freezes, or "ex-growth" status <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>.
3.  **Impatience**: High performers often set aggressive goals for promotion and get frustrated when leadership roles take longer to absorb or impact is lagging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>.
4.  **Undeveloped Areas**: There's a genuine development area that the individual may not recognize, acknowledge, or be willing to change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:57">[00:28:57]</a>. This is often the hardest to address, requiring significant self-awareness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:41">[00:29:41]</a>.

#### The State of Management
Singall believes the tech industry has historically failed to adequately train managers, often promoting good individual contributors (ICs) into management roles without proper preparation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:28">[00:35:28]</a>. He compares this to assuming someone who can build a good car can also build a factory that makes cars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:09">[00:36:09]</a>.

> [!WARNING] Common Pitfalls for New Managers
> 1.  **Failing to Share the Steering Wheel**: Management is not about dividing and conquering, nor is it like teaching someone to ride a bike where you eventually let go. It's more like a "sidecar on a motorcycle," where the manager provides counsel but the report is driving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:19">[00:37:19]</a>.
> 2.  **Managing Without Invitation**: Managers often assume their organizational power grants them the right to manage. Instead, you must be invited in by your reports, earning the right to be their manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:21">[00:38:21]</a>. Focus on understanding what you can help them with and being available when they need assistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:57">[00:38:57]</a>.

#### The Rise of the Senior IC Path
The current economic climate, with reduced hyper-growth and layoffs, has led to a growth in the Individual Contributor (IC) track <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:20">[00:40:20]</a>. Singall views this as "one of the best things to happen to our industry" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:50">[00:40:50]</a>. It allows product people, who often like to build, to continue focusing on creation rather than solely on managing others or navigating organizational politics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:52">[00:43:52]</a>. While the IC track is still solidifying, it is becoming a strong and promising career path for builders, mirroring established IC paths in engineering and design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:26">[00:42:26]</a>.

### Later Career: The "Shadows of Superpowers" and Finding a New North Star

As leaders advance, their past strengths (superpowers) can become their biggest development areas (shadows) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:31">[00:53:31]</a>. For example, a "great collaborator" might struggle when their collaboration relies on others agreeing with their opinion, leading to resistance from senior, diverse stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:21">[00:54:21]</a>. Other examples include:
*   Great collaborators being reticent to present their own opinions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:21">[00:55:21]</a>.
*   Amazing growth experts struggling with innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:34">[00:55:34]</a>.
*   World-class storytellers struggling with details <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:38">[00:55:38]</a>.
*   Decisive opinion leaders struggling to introduce change or adapt to collective input <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:45">[00:55:45]</a>.

These challenges often go unaddressed because leaders are moving fast, believe in their "superpower," and others are hesitant to provide honest feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:10">[00:55:10]</a>. To overcome this, individuals must learn to listen to contradictory feedback and examine "discarded" feedback that might be an artifact of their strength <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:30">[00:57:30]</a>.

#### The "Act 3" of Career and Mental Health
With careers potentially lasting 60 years or more, the "Act 3" of a career (post-achievement) is longer than ever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:48">[01:03:48]</a>. Singall observes that many successful individuals become "lost" after achieving their primary career goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:09">[01:04:09]</a>. This can lead to mental health challenges, continued working without purpose, or engaging in destructive behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:20">[01:06:20]</a>.

> [!TIP] [[Frameworks for life and career planning | Redefine Your North Star]]
> It's never too early to start thinking about what comes next after achieving initial career success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:06">[01:06:06]</a>. This involves defining a new "North Star" for the long arc of your career <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:06">[01:06:06]</a>.

While some might pursue scaled economics (e.g., investing, private equity), Singall highlights the **arc of giving** as a powerful and fulfilling path for Act 3 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:09">[01:09:09]</a>. This could involve content creation, volunteering, or founding a mission-based company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:22">[01:10:22]</a>. The liberation provided by early career success and equity can enable this shift <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:05">[01:11:05]</a>. Singall himself plans to devote his Act 3 to coaching and giving to others, specifically by lifting up those who can benefit from timely advice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:32">[01:11:32]</a>.

## Strategies for Continuous Growth

### Embrace Feedback
To overcome stalled careers or unrecognized development areas, seek out **real feedback** rather than just formal feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:41">[00:30:41]</a>. Create an environment of safety where people feel comfortable providing honest input without fear of retaliation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:53">[00:31:53]</a>. When receiving feedback, practice active listening, repeat it back to the giver to show understanding, and express appreciation, even if it's difficult to hear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:16">[00:32:16]</a>. As a manager, publicly acknowledging and recognizing those who provide valuable feedback can encourage others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:35">[00:34:35]</a>.

### Leverage Community for Learning
Formal learning and development programs in many organizations are weak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:11">[00:46:11]</a>. Singall believes **community** is the most powerful answer for learning and growth, whether within or outside a company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:16">[00:46:16]</a>. Communities create safety, allow for authentic conversations, foster a sense of shared experience, and enable the sharing of best practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:27">[00:46:27]</a>.

> [!NOTE] The Skip CPO Community
> Singall co-founded a community called "The Skip CPO Community" for Chief Product Officers who often face lonely challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:26">[00:48:26]</a>. This community fosters trust and allows members to share dilemmas and solutions in a safe environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:14">[00:49:14]</a>.

### Optimize Your "Meeting Operating System"
At scaled organizations, the meeting operating system is as crucial as the products being built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:46">[01:19:46]</a>. Treat meetings like a product, evolving them every quarter based on feedback to improve delegation, conversations, and decision-making acceleration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:13">[01:20:13]</a>. This helps people plan effectively and makes meeting time, which is the "most expensive time in a company," more productive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:47">[01:20:47]</a>. For new leaders, optimizing process (like meetings) is an effective way to make an impact when product context is low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:05">[01:21:05]</a>.

## Conclusion

Building a long and meaningful career requires looking beyond immediate goals, embracing diverse experiences, continuously seeking and acting on feedback, leveraging communities for growth, and proactively defining a "North Star" that provides purpose throughout all career stages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:50">[01:05:50]</a>.
