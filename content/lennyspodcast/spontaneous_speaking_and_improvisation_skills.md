## Key Techniques for Spontaneous Speaking

### Embrace Imperfection: Dare to Be Dull

A technique originating from the world of [[improving_public_speaking_through_interactive_exercises | improvisation]] is "Dare to be Dull" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:11">[00:11:11]</a>. Unlike common perceptions of [[improving_public_speaking_through_interactive_exercises | improv]] as stand-up comedy, it focuses on being present, collaborative, and open <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:20">[00:11:20]</a>. This approach helps individuals become present-oriented, akin to "meditation in action," and teaches valuable [[improving_communication_skills | communication skills]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:32">[00:11:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>.

By daring to be dull, one strives for connection over perfection, which means simply focusing on completing the task, answering the question, giving feedback, or engaging in small talk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:01">[00:12:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:36">[00:12:36]</a>. This reduces self-evaluation, freeing up cognitive resources that can be used to succeed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>. Paradoxically, by removing the pressure to be perfect, speakers often perform better and more insightfully <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:15">[00:13:15]</a>.

### Utilize [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | Communication Structures]]

For spontaneous speaking, [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:03">[00:36:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:29">[00:36:29]</a>. Without it, individuals tend to "spew out" information, creating disorienting journeys for the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:41">[00:36:41]</a>. The human brain processes information better through stories and logical connections than through lists <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:13">[00:37:13]</a>. A [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] provides a beginning, middle, and end, offering a recipe to organize thoughts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:23">[00:37:23]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:58">[00:37:58]</a>.

Key structures include:

*   **Problem-Solution-Benefit:** Common in advertising, it presents a problem, a solution, and its benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:33">[00:37:33]</a>.
*   **PREP (Point, Reason, Example, Point):** Useful for making a specific argument <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:38">[00:38:38]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:09">[00:40:09]</a>.
*   **What, So What, Now What:** Excellent for updates, presentations, or feedback sessions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:02">[00:39:02]</a>.
    *   **What:** The product, service, update, or feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:08">[00:39:08]</a>.
    *   **So What:** Why it's important and relevant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:17">[00:39:17]</a>.
    *   **Now What:** What comes next <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:24">[00:39:24]</a>.

To internalize these [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structures]], use them regularly by applying them to podcasts, articles, or daily conversations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:46">[00:40:46]</a>. This consistent practice, combined with reflection and feedback, is the only way to genuinely improve spontaneous communication skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:40">[00:41:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:20">[01:00:20]</a>.

### Practical Applications

#### Small Talk

Small talk, often dreaded, serves to build and foster relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:02">[00:46:02]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:16">[00:46:16]</a>. The best advice is to "be interested, not interesting" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:44">[00:46:44]</a>. Rather than trying to impress, focus on setting up the other person to share <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:55">[00:46:55]</a>.

Key principles for small talk:

*   **Balance of Disclosure:** Maintain comparable levels of disclosure with the other person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:12">[00:47:12]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:33">[00:47:33]</a>. Avoid disclosing too much too quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:57">[00:47:57]</a>.
*   **Supporting vs. Shifting Responses:** Aim for more supporting responses (asking follow-up questions) than shifting responses (redirecting to yourself) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:26">[00:48:26]</a>. A balance is needed, as constant support without personal sharing can seem disingenuous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:01">[00:49:01]</a>.

#### Giving Feedback

Feedback is an opportunity to problem-solve and requires collaboration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:53">[00:52:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:05">[00:53:05]</a>. It's about inviting the other person to work together on an issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:15">[00:53:15]</a>.

A useful [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] for feedback is **Four Eyes (IIII)**:

*   **Information:** Set the context and specify the behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:04">[00:55:04]</a>.
*   **Impact:** Explain the impact of the behavior on you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:10">[00:55:10]</a>.
*   **Invitation:** Invite collaboration to address the issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:15">[00:55:15]</a>.
*   **Implications/Consequences:** Discuss the potential outcomes of addressing or not addressing the issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:17">[00:55:17]</a>.

#### Giving Toasts and Tributes

Toasts are a common form of public speaking, occurring in various settings beyond celebrations, such as work tributes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:04">[00:57:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:54">[00:56:54]</a>.

A helpful [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] for toasts is **WHAT (W.H.A.T.)**:

*   **W (Why are we here):** State the purpose of the gathering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:23">[00:57:23]</a>.
*   **H (How are you connected):** Explain your relationship to the event or person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:42">[00:57:42]</a>.
*   **A (Anecdote):** Share a relevant, concise, and accessible story or example that provides insight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:03">[00:58:03]</a>. This is where the core content of the tribute is conveyed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:58">[00:58:58]</a>.
*   **T (Thank you/Gratitude):** Express appreciation and signal closure, often with a "cheers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:18">[00:58:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:21">[00:58:21]</a>.

General advice for toasts includes being brief, as no one complains about a toast being too short <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:32">[00:59:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:37">[00:59:37]</a>. Incorporating genuine emotion through anecdotes, rather than simply stating feelings, also connects powerfully with the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:47">[01:00:47]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:51">[01:01:51]</a>.

#### Q&A Sessions

Viewing Q&A as an opportunity to extend, expand, connect, and learn, even in challenging situations, is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:06">[01:03:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:10">[01:03:10]</a>. Avoid phrases like "good question" or "does that make sense," as they can undermine perceived confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:39">[01:03:39]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:43">[01:03:43]</a>. Instead, directly ask "Did I answer your question?" or "Can I tell you more?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:02">[01:04:02]</a>.

A useful [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] for answering questions is **ADD (Answer, Detailed example, Describe relevance)**:

*   **Answer:** Provide a clean and concise answer to the question <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:08">[01:07:08]</a>.
*   **Detailed Example:** Offer an example to reinforce the answer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:09">[01:07:09]</a>.
*   **Describe Relevance:** Explain the significance or relevance of the answer, connecting the dots for the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:12">[01:07:12]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:15">[01:06:15]</a>.

#### Apologizing

When apologizing, it's crucial to acknowledge the specific transgression rather than simply stating "I'm sorry you feel bad," which avoids responsibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:38">[01:07:38]</a>.

A recommended [[effective_communication_structures_for_speaking | structure]] for apologies is **AAA (Acknowledge, Appreciate, Amend)**:

*   **Acknowledge:** Clearly state the action or behavior that is being apologized for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:18">[01:08:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:09">[01:09:09]</a>.
*   **Appreciate:** Express understanding or appreciation for the difficulty or impact caused <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:21">[01:08:21]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:13">[01:09:13]</a>.
*   **Amend:** State specific actions that will be taken to remedy the situation or prevent recurrence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:21">[01:08:21]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:18">[01:09:18]</a>.
