## Fostering a Culture of Action and Momentum

At Stripe, a core philosophy in onboarding was about empowering individuals to act and make decisions. If you're unsure who the decision-maker is, it's likely you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>. Acting with initiative is preferred over inaction, which can "slow the whole company down" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:12">[00:00:12]</a>. The goal is to follow a process, get things done, and make decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>. If uncertainty persists about the decision-maker, simply ask <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:20">[00:00:20]</a>. The ultimate objective is to be a "force for positive momentum," which can be a significant career maker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>.

## Personal Operating Principles: The Foundation for Leadership

Effective [[managing_up_and_leadership_communication | management]] and [[decision_making_and_leadership_in_highgrowth_environments | leadership]] begin with the individual, not just the team or company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:34">[00:27:34]</a>. Johnson outlines four personal operating principles that are foundational to effective company building:

### 1. Build Self-Awareness to Build Mutual Awareness
Self-awareness is the most fundamental aspect for success in company building or management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:01">[00:28:01]</a>. Leaders should actively seek feedback to understand their motivators, strengths, blind spots, and tendencies, and expose these to others to become more effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:18">[00:28:18]</a>.

*   **Crystallizing Personal Values**: An exercise involves selecting personal values from a list (e.g., family, ambition, impact, competition, education, integrity) and narrowing them down to the top three, understanding the formative stories behind each <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:20">[00:29:20]</a>.
*   **Understanding Work Style Tendencies**: Utilize various work style assessments (Myers-Briggs, DISC, Enneagram) to understand tendencies such as introversion/extroversion or task/people orientation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:03">[00:32:03]</a>. This self-understanding helps in defining how one operates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:11">[00:33:11]</a>.

### 2. Say the Thing You Think You Cannot Say
Often, a significant strength is something instinctively done, like "breathing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:50">[00:33:50]</a>. Johnson notes her own ability to communicate directly but non-threateningly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:07">[00:34:07]</a>.

*   **Tactical Advice for Difficult Conversations**:
    *   **Ask a Question**: Questions are less threatening and can open up dialogue (e.g., "Is there something we're not talking about?") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:34">[00:35:34]</a>.
    *   **Own the Observation**: Frame feedback as your own observation or perception, not a judgment (e.g., "I feel like...", "My experience of you was...") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:47">[00:35:47]</a>.
    *   **Be an Explorer, Not a Lecturer**: Managers should enable people to be their best through coaching, not just telling them what to do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:31">[00:37:31]</a>. Use "hypothesis-based [[coaching_strategies_for_executive_success | coaching]]" â€“ form a well-informed intuition based on data and explore it with the person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:19">[00:38:19]</a>. This collaborative approach fosters trust and allows mutual discovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:49">[00:40:49]</a>.

### 3. Distinguish Between Management and Leadership
Leaders, like Reid Hoffman, may acknowledge their strengths lie more in [[decision_making_and_leadership_in_highgrowth_environments | leadership]] (vision, open feedback) than day-to-day management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:13">[00:41:13]</a>. The key is to create an environment of open feedback and trust where team members are comfortable sharing uncomfortable truths <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:56">[00:41:56]</a>.

### 4. Come Back to the Operating System
In high-growth, chaotic environments, an operating system provides stability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:17">[00:44:17]</a>. This involves consistent rituals and common practices (e.g., quarterly goals) that provide a predictable cadence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:54">[00:44:54]</a>. It allows leaders to "scale themselves" by applying a common approach across different functions and projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:06">[00:47:06]</a>.

## Building the "Company House": Operational Structure

Johnson uses a house metaphor to describe the essential components for [[building_and_managing_growth_teams | building and managing growth teams]] that scale:

### 1. Founding Documents (Foundation)
These are critical for aligning a growing team:
*   **Mission**: A concise statement of what the company seeks to accomplish (e.g., Stripe's mission to "increase the GDP of the internet") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>.
*   **Long-Term Goals**: More detailed objectives explaining *why* the company exists, beyond the short-term (e.g., "advance the state of the art in developer tools" for Stripe) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:52">[00:53:52]</a>. These guide daily choices and ensure everyone understands investment priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:28">[00:55:28]</a>.
*   **Operating Principles/Values**: Guidelines for how the company works, which can also be shared with candidates to ensure mutual fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:46">[00:55:46]</a>.

A sign to invest more in these documents is when new hires frequently ask "what's important" or "why do we do it this way" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:24">[00:56:24]</a>.

### 2. Operating System (Supporting Beams and Mechanicals)
This defines how work gets done and progress is measured:
*   **Goals/OKRs**: A structure for setting objectives and key results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:52">[00:58:52]</a>.
*   **Quarterly Business Reviews (QBRs)**: Regular reviews of business parts, using templates and standard forms to report progress against strategy and goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:07">[00:59:07]</a>.
*   **Metrics and Dashboards**: Essential for measuring progress, including input and output metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:25">[00:59:25]</a>. Ideally, these are live dashboards to avoid time-consuming special presentations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:30">[01:03:30]</a>.
*   **Planning Processes**: Crucial for annual or multi-year strategies, despite being often disliked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:01">[01:00:01]</a>.

### 3. Operating Cadence (Rhythm of Work)
This refers to the frequency and rhythm of activities:
*   **Calendar-based or Event-driven**: Cadences can be dictated by the calendar (e.g., quarterly) or by significant events (e.g., customer events like Stripe Sessions, which force internal planning and product demos) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:22">[00:48:22]</a>.
*   **Flexibility**: While mature companies use fixed cadences, younger companies can experiment with shorter timeframes (e.g., six-month or six-week reviews instead of quarterly) to avoid artificial restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:55">[01:00:55]</a>.
*   **Signs of an Off Cadence**:
    *   Too fast if not enough progress is made between reviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:18">[01:02:18]</a>.
    *   Too slow if content seems stale or metrics reviews become de facto strategy reviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:23">[01:02:23]</a>.
    *   Creating work for people by having new content for reviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:07">[01:03:07]</a>.

It's better to do "very few things consistently" rather than constantly experimenting with new processes, which creates chaos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:52">[00:49:52]</a>. There is no "perfect" system; commit to the best available one and work around its flaws <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:26">[00:50:26]</a>.

## Effective Communication Practices

*   **Repeater in Chief**: Leaders must consistently repeat core messages using different channels (email, videos, meetings) because not everyone consumes information in the same way <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:27">[01:12:27]</a>.
*   **Off-sites for Cohesion**: Yanking people out of their day-to-day routine for off-sites creates space for different thinking and imprints shared memories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:57">[01:12:57]</a>. These group experiences cement beliefs and plans, fostering a sense of co-creation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:21">[01:13:21]</a>.
*   **Internal Information Hubs**: Invest in internal communication tools like intranet sites, newsletters, and quick video updates from founders to keep everyone informed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:12">[01:14:12]</a>. A clear strategy and dedicated people ensure consistency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:48">[01:14:48]</a>.
*   **Running Effective Meetings**:
    *   **Explicit Objectives**: Clearly state *why* the meeting is happening and *what* its objective is (e.g., decision-making, information sharing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:32">[01:16:32]</a>.
    *   **Clarify Decision-Making**: Explicitly define who is making the decision and by what criteria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:57">[01:16:57]</a>.

## Decision Making and Alignment

For product managers who often have high accountability but limited authority, effective [[decision_making_and_leadership_in_highgrowth_environments | decision making]] requires making the process explicit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:01">[01:18:01]</a>. Utilize decision models (like Gokul Rajaram's SPADE framework or RACI) and communicate them clearly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:11">[01:18:11]</a>. It's crucial to evaluate the type of decision (e.g., Bezos's Type 1 vs. Type 2 for high impact/irreversible decisions) before applying a process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:56">[01:18:56]</a>. Don't get "stuck" in indecision; aim for progress, impact, and momentum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:15">[01:19:15]</a>.

Despite external perceptions of smooth operation, most growing companies experience internal chaos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:08">[01:11:08]</a>. The goal is not to eliminate chaos but to create a "well-run thing" through consistent adherence to operating practices, which provides a sense of stability both internally and externally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:26">[01:52:26]</a>.
