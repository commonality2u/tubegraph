From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Effective strategy, according to Richard Rumelt, is not a mysterious concept but rather a "designed for overcoming a high-stakes challenge" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. It's a blend of policy and action geared towards addressing a specific issue, whether an opportunity or a threat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>. Rumelt, author of *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* and *The Crux*, is a renowned expert who has advised companies like Microsoft and Apple, and government organizations like the US Army Special Operations Command <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>.

## The Core Components of a Good Strategy

Rumelt outlines three essential elements, often referred to as "the kernel," that define a good strategy:

1.  **Diagnosis:** A clear understanding of the situation and the critical [[challenges_in_business_strategy | challenges]] at hand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>. This involves deciding what to pay attention to and forming hypotheses about how things connect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>. It's crucial to identify the "crux" – the single hardest, most important, and addressable problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:04">[01:03:04]</a>.
2.  **Guiding Policy:** A chosen approach to deal with the identified [[challenges_in_business_strategy | challenge]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>. This is about defining what the organization *will* do and, importantly, what it *will not* do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:07">[01:02:07]</a>. It contrasts sharply with a long list of priorities, which are often just a "lottery list" of wishes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>.
3.  **Coherent Actions:** A set of specific, aligned actions to implement the guiding policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. These actions must directly address the diagnosis and guiding policy, and they should not be self-contradictory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>. A key characteristic is focus: concentrating efforts on a few key things rather than diffusing them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:03">[00:25:03]</a>.

Rumelt suggests thinking of [[developing_and_implementing_product_strategy | strategy]] as an "action agenda" rather than a lofty, abstract concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

> "Don't call it a [[strategy_and_its_four_critical_choices | strategy]] call it an action agenda." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>

## Signs of a Bad Strategy

Bad strategy often manifests in several ways:

*   **Abstract Goals instead of Action:** Mistaking high-level goals (e.g., increasing growth and profit) for a strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:37">[00:17:37]</a>. Goals are ambitions, not a plan of action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:59">[00:17:59]</a>.
*   **Lacking Diagnosis:** Failing to articulate *why* a situation is problematic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:50">[00:18:50]</a>. Without understanding the root causes, actions become ineffective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:22">[00:19:22]</a>.
*   **Fluff and Word Salad:** Using overly complex or jargon-filled language that obscures the lack of clear thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a>.
*   **Incoherent Actions:** Proposing actions that contradict each other or do not logically follow from the stated goals or diagnosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:59">[00:20:59]</a>.

## The Role of Power and Asymmetry

A successful [[strategy_and_its_four_critical_choices | strategy]] often leverages a source of "power" or "asymmetry" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:17">[00:29:17]</a>. This means identifying something unique about your organization or situation that gives you an advantage over competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:56">[00:29:56]</a>. Examples include:

*   Being first to market or first to recognize an opportunity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:38">[00:29:38]</a>.
*   Having a strong reputation or unique institutional skill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:04">[00:42:04]</a>.
*   Established relationships, like IBM's deep connections with large corporate clients <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:13">[00:30:13]</a>.
*   Network effects, where the value of a product or service increases with the number of users, seen in social media or e-commerce platforms like Amazon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:16">[00:35:16]</a>.
*   Access to unique data, which is increasingly vital in the age of generative AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:34">[00:38:34]</a>.

Identifying your asymmetry involves asking: "In what way is my company different than other companies?" or "What do we know that other people don't know? What do we possess that people don't possess?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:52">[00:32:52]</a>.

## Overcoming the Crux: The Hardest Challenge

The "crux" in strategy is the hardest part of the problem you are facing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:17">[01:04:17]</a>. Like a difficult move in mountain climbing, if you can't address the crux, the entire endeavor may be doomed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:46">[01:03:46]</a>.

Solving the crux requires "insight" – a deep understanding of the problem that reveals a path forward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:52">[01:08:52]</a>. Insight isn't magical; it comes from "immersing yourself in the nature of the problem" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:24">[01:09:24]</a>. Examples include:

*   I.M. Pei's transparent pyramid design for the Louvre, addressing the need for an entrance without obscuring the historic building <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:35">[01:05:35]</a>.
*   Elon Musk's [[developing_engineering_strategy | engineering strategy]] insight for SpaceX rockets to land vertically, despite initial fuel concerns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:50">[01:06:50]</a>.
*   The Voyager mission's method of taking pictures of Jupiter against distant stars to precisely triangulate its position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:12">[01:08:12]</a>.

## Organizational Dynamics and Leadership

Even with a clear [[strategy_and_its_four_critical_choices | strategy]], organizational dynamics can be a significant hindrance to [[the_importance_of_execution_over_strategy | execution]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:40">[00:41:40]</a>. Complex organizations often struggle with:

*   **Diffusion of Effort:** Different departments or individuals pursuing disparate interests, leading to a lack of focus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:51">[00:27:51]</a>.
*   **Disagreement and Fear of Action:** Senior executives may know the [[challenges_in_business_strategy | challenges]], but personal or departmental interests, combined with a fear of disrupting power relationships, can lead to inaction or a refusal to make tough choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:17">[01:15:17]</a>.
*   **Lack of Decisiveness:** A reluctance from leaders to explicitly tell people what to do, often masked by a focus on "visionary leadership" over clear direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:19">[01:17:19]</a>.

To counter this, there needs to be a mechanism for making choices and prioritizing actions, ideally with senior executives taking ownership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:06">[01:11:06]</a>.

## Strategy for Startups

For founders and startups, especially those pre-product-market fit, strategy is inherently a "bet" in the face of uncertainty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:05">[02:21:05]</a>. The key is to:

*   **Be Clear About the Bet:** Understand the underlying assumptions about the market and technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:17">[02:21:17]</a>.
*   **Focus on an Achievable Problem:** Identify an "important and achievable problem" to solve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:26">[02:26:26]</a>.
*   **Adapt Quickly:** Startups often need to "switch" their target market or solution as reality is revealed through bits and pieces of information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:35">[02:22:35]</a>. This requires a "double-jointed exercise" of both commitment and willingness to change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:27">[02:27:27]</a>.
*   **Look for "Value Denied":** Identify things that people *should* be able to buy or experience but cannot, or imagine how things *ought* to be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:46">[02:46:46]</a>. This can uncover new business opportunities.

## The Importance of History and Analogy

Since there is no "science of [[strategy_and_its_four_critical_choices | strategy]]" like physics, effective strategic thinking relies heavily on understanding human experience through history and analogy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:57">[02:47:57]</a>. Reading history, biographies of business leaders, and world history can provide valuable insights into complex situations and how they were navigated in the past <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:29">[02:47:29]</a>. This helps in developing the diagnostic skill necessary to understand what is truly happening and form your own opinions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:08">[02:51:08]</a>.
