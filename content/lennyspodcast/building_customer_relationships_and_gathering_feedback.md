From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Jeff Weinstein, a product leader at Stripe, emphasizes that building strong customer relationships and effectively gathering feedback are fundamental to developing successful products and achieving product-market fit. He believes that "the business comes from customers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:53">[00:24:53]</a> and that companies should actively seek out and respond to customer input.

## Direct Engagement and Rapid Response

Weinstein advocates for a direct and immediate approach to customer interaction, breaking down traditional barriers between product managers and customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:01">[00:00:01]</a>.

He consistently shares his email on platforms like Twitter and engages directly with users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>. This approach generates "so much direct signal that is infectious" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>.

The speed of response is crucial. When a customer takes the time to communicate a problem, it's an "unbelievable gift" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:25">[00:30:25]</a>. Weinstein prioritizes responding quickly, even leaving meetings, to acknowledge feedback, which helps foster a sense of a "Secret portal" between the customer and the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:55">[00:30:55]</a>.

### Turning Detractors into Promoters
Even in situations where a company makes mistakes, a quick and empathetic response can turn a detractor into a promoter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:33">[00:31:33]</a>. Weinstein recounts an incident at Stripe Atlas where a crucial legal document error was quickly fixed, and the customer who reported it became a close "product friend for life," offering to brainstorm and review documents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:40">[00:32:40]</a>.

## [[techniques_for_effective_customer_communication_and_empathy | Techniques for Effective Customer Communication and Empathy]]

Weinstein emphasizes several [[interview_techniques_for_uncovering_customer_needs | interview techniques for uncovering customer needs]] and [[effective_feedback_and_listening_strategies | effective feedback and listening strategies]] beyond direct communication:

*   **The Power of Silence**: During customer interactions, avoid pitching your product. Instead, sit in silence and allow the customer to "open their heart out about what's the most burning thing" on their mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:20">[00:22:20]</a>. This approach helps uncover their top problems and allows the product roadmap to emerge organically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:02">[00:24:02]</a>.
*   **Open-ended Questions**: Ask customers about their daily life, what "grinded their gears" last week, or what they wish they "could just like have off your plate immediately" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:39">[00:22:39]</a>.
*   **"Magic Wand" Scenarios**: Inquire about a "massive opportunity you wish was just one door away" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:57">[00:22:57]</a>.
*   **Self-Selection through Effort**: When overwhelmed with feedback, ask for screenshots or detailed bullet points. Those willing to provide more information are typically the ones to prioritize for deeper engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:05">[00:35:05]</a>.
*   **"Bugfinder Programs"**: Create structured, time-bound programs to encourage specific types of feedback, like bug reports with videos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:50">[00:35:50]</a>. This gives customers "permission" to engage in a focused way <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:22">[00:36:22]</a>.

## Focusing on Paying Customers
A critical rule is to "discount all of that feedback from our friends to zero" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:58">[00:38:58]</a> and focus solely on actual paying customers. This ensures that the product addresses a "burning problem" for which people are genuinely willing to exchange value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:18">[00:39:18]</a>.

Weinstein even encourages new founders to practice charging him a dollar, demonstrating the "night and day" difference between someone who is "willing to pay" and someone who has actually "paying" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:53">[00:44:53]</a>. This practice helps validate whether a problem is severe enough for a customer to pay for a solution.

## Metrics Driven by Customer Value
Metrics should be "a numerical representation of the value we're providing for the for the customer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:50">[00:46:50]</a>. They should measure success from the customer's perspective, not just internal usage metrics like logins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:15">[00:47:15]</a>.

### Companies with Zero Support Tickets
For Stripe Atlas, a key metric was "companies that have no support tickets through the incorporation service" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:28">[00:50:28]</a>. This metric directly correlated with customer satisfaction and referrals, increasing from 15% to 85% in 18 months and mirroring market share growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:48">[00:51:48]</a>. This focused metric allowed engineers to act as product managers, solving specific customer pain points identified in support tickets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:57">[00:52:57]</a>.

### Users Having a Bad Day
A universal metric that can be applied to any business is "users having a bad day" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:05">[01:01:05]</a>. This involves emitting a log line whenever a user encounters a significant problem (e.g., a 404 error, delayed payout, multiple payment declines) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:09">[01:01:09]</a>. Tracking these "bad day" reasons allows teams to visualize problem frequencies and prioritize their eradication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:40">[01:01:40]</a>. The most critical "bad days" are those the company is unaware of <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:17">[01:03:17]</a>.

## [[developing_user_empathy_and_gathering_insights_for_product_development | Developing User Empathy and Gathering Insights for Product Development]]

### Stripe Study Groups
To combat the natural "entropy in the world that causes these things to go bad" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:07">[01:14:07]</a> and for product experience to rot, Weinstein initiated "study groups" at Stripe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:49">[01:16:49]</a>. These groups involve 4-8 random employees (not just PMs) who "pretend to be some company with some outcome problem" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:16">[01:17:16]</a>.

The two main rules of study groups are:
1.  **"You do no longer work at stripe"**: Participants must fully embody the customer, avoiding internal knowledge or shortcuts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:46">[01:17:46]</a>.
2.  **"We're not here to solve any problems"**: The sole purpose is "just about practicing empathy for the customer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:55">[01:19:55]</a>.

These sessions are non-punitive, fun, and provide "deeply eye openening" insights into the customer experience, often surprising participants with how difficult seemingly simple tasks can be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:12">[01:20:12]</a>. The findings are funneled into existing bug tracking processes, and the direct observation of teammates struggling motivates action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:19">[01:32:19]</a>.

### Customer Involvement in Design
The Atlas team has even started inviting customers to actively design products using visual diagramming tools, directly "drawing what they want their dashboard to be like" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:46">[02:17:46]</a>. This "right access" for customers is incredibly powerful, even if not every customer is a designer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:11">[02:18:11]</a>.

## Experience as a Business Differentiator
Weinstein asserts that making the product experience better directly drives business growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:40">[02:00:40]</a>. He argues that the entire customer journey, including sales, support, and even physical interactions, should be considered part of the "product experience" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:57">[01:29:57]</a>. By focusing on [[automating_customer_feedback_and_interaction | automating customer feedback and interaction]] and streamlining administrative burdens, companies can free up entrepreneurial energy to focus on building and shipping <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:47">[01:44:47]</a>.

Ultimately, by deeply understanding and responding to customer needs, product teams can build [[building_credibility_and_customer_engagement | credibility and customer engagement]], which directly translates to sustainable business success.
