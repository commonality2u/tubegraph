## The State of Product Management: Over-Hiring and "Product Management Theater"

During the pandemic, many companies over-hired, often lowering the bar for talent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This led to a proliferation of "ridiculous roles" like agile coaches, product owners, product operations, and business analysts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>. Marty Cagan describes this as "product management theater," where individuals are "dramatically overpaid for the value they provide" because they are essentially performing project management roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>.

Several macro factors contribute to this situation:
*   **Pandemic Over-Hiring** Companies hired extensively and sometimes lowered hiring standards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:48">[01:28:48]</a>.
*   **Financial Changes** The increased cost of funds impacts company spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:02">[01:03:02]</a>.
*   **Anticipated Impact of Generative AI** AI creates uncertainty and will have a real impact on roles, particularly administrative ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:10">[01:13:10]</a>.
*   **Bloated Team Sizes** Especially outside Silicon Valley, team sizes have grown excessively, with an overabundance of assistant product manager types <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:14">[01:14:14]</a>. Smaller teams often produce more and better results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:39">[01:14:39]</a>.
*   **Impact of Remote Work** Remote work has "taken a real hit" on both velocity and innovation, causing companies to struggle with the level of innovation they once achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:10">[01:35:10]</a>.
*   **Reliance on Processes** Many companies, particularly outside Silicon Valley, have invested in rigid processes like SAFe, often misunderstanding simpler ones like Scrum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>.

This leads to little in the way of outcomes for significant cost and waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:22">[01:04:22]</a>.

## Feature Teams vs. Empowered Product Teams

A key distinction Cagan highlights is between "feature teams" and "empowered product teams" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:59">[02:09:59]</a>.

### Feature Teams (Output-Driven)
*   **Focus:** Given a roadmap of features or projects (output) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:07">[02:10:07]</a>.
*   **Task:** Design, build, test, and deploy specific features, often with given dates and timeframes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:00">[02:12:00]</a>.
*   **Role of PMs:** Often act as project managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>, not bringing sufficient value to justify their high salaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:02">[02:20:02]</a>. Engineers or designers may even prefer to manage tasks themselves rather than deal with ineffective product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:30">[02:29:30]</a>.

### Empowered Product Teams (Outcome-Driven)
*   **Focus:** Given problems to solveâ€”customer or business problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:50">[02:21:50]</a>.
*   **Measure of Success:** Solving the problem, not just shipping a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:07">[02:22:07]</a>.
*   **Goal:** Deliver outcomes, which is harder than delivering output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:00">[02:24:00]</a>.
*   **Need for PMs:** A "real" product manager is necessary to ensure solutions are valuable and viable, in addition to being usable and feasible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>.

## Skills of a "Real" Product Manager

A "real" product manager is a "creator, not a facilitator" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:17">[02:55:17]</a>. Their primary responsibility is to ensure **value** (for the customer) and **viability** (for the business) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:58:00">[02:58:00]</a>.

Key skills and areas of expertise include:
*   **Deep Customer Understanding** Becoming an expert on users and customers through direct engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:02:01">[03:02:01]</a>.
*   **Data Expertise** Understanding product usage, how it changes over time, and purchase patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>.
*   **Business Acumen** Representing compliance, sales, marketing, financial costs, monetization, and legal constraints within the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:00">[03:09:00]</a>.
*   **Market Understanding** Deep knowledge of the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:17:00">[03:17:00]</a>.

Many product owners who learned to "manage a backlog in Jira" are simply doing "project management," not true product management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:27:00">[03:27:00]</a>.

Much of the advice and content available online (e.g., certifications, articles, books) for product managers often propagates the "feature team world" model, which can be misleading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:00">[03:36:00]</a>. Well-meaning individuals in product communities may share practices from their "crappy company," inadvertently perpetuating ineffective methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.

## The Impact of Generative AI on Product Management

Generative AI is expected to significantly disrupt product management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:10">[01:13:10]</a>.
*   **Vulnerable Roles:** Roles focused on backlog administration or purely project management are most vulnerable to automation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>.
*   **Increased Importance of Viability:** For empowered product managers, AI will make the "viability question" even more crucial, as they deal with ethical, legal, and mission-critical implications of probabilistic software <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:39:00">[05:39:00]</a>.
*   **AI Usage:** It's recommended to "think through the answer first" and then use AI tools like ChatGPT to improve, challenge, or tighten arguments, rather than relying on AI for initial ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:17:00">[05:17:00]</a>.

## Advice for Individual Product Managers

Product managers often feel "trapped" in feature teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:44:00">[03:44:00]</a>. However, Cagan believes they have "a lot more agency than they realize" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>.

[[strategies_for_career_growth_and_breaking_into_product_management | Strategies for career growth and breaking into product management]] include:
*   **Self-Assessment and Skill Development:** Identify areas to "raise your game" from a product owner or feature team PM to a "real" product manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:32:00">[03:32:00]</a>. This will likely lead to appreciation and promotion within the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a>.
*   **Push for Change:** Individuals can drive change from the ground up, encouraging their company to adopt better practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:00">[03:19:00]</a>.
*   **Critical Thinking:** Learn to evaluate information and advice, especially when seeking guidance online <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>. Research potential managers and companies to understand their working models before joining <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>.
*   **Take Ownership of Your Career:** Be proactive in shaping your role and contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>.

## Organizational Transformation: The "Product Operating Model"

Marty Cagan's book, *Transformed*, aims to address the common question of "how in the world do you transform to work like this?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:57:00">[05:57:00]</a>. It provides techniques for change, including piloting new approaches and spreading successful practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>.

The book intentionally uses examples from companies *outside* Silicon Valley, many pre-Internet, to demonstrate that transformation is possible for any organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:08:00">[05:08:00]</a>.

### What is the "Product Operating Model"?
The "product operating model" is a conceptual model, not a process, based on a set of 20 consistent principles found in the best product companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:01">[01:04:01]</a>. It avoids terms like "product-led" or "product-driven" to prevent misinterpretation as a power grab <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:45">[01:03:45]</a>.

It has three key dimensions:
1.  **How you decide what to work on (Product Strategy)**: This involves leaders making strategic decisions and "bets" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:28">[01:05:28]</a>.
2.  **How you solve problems (Product Discovery)**: This involves teams having the skills to come up with valuable, viable, usable, and feasible solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:46">[01:05:46]</a>.
3.  **How you build, test, and deploy (Product Delivery)**: This focuses on reliable and demonstrable delivery that proves outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:02">[01:06:02]</a>.

### Four Key Competencies
Most companies need to develop or institutionalize four key competencies:
1.  **Real Product Manager:** Responsible for value and viability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:02">[01:07:02]</a>.
2.  **Real Product Designer:** Covering service, interaction, visual design, and user research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:09">[01:07:09]</a>.
3.  **Real Tech Lead:** Crucial for technical direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:16">[01:07:16]</a>.
4.  **Real Product Leader:** A manager who coaches their people and crafts a genuine product strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:19">[01:07:19]</a>.

[[product_management_and_leadership_insights | Product leadership]] is essential, as teams need strong leaders to set strategy, not just do their job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:11">[01:08:11]</a>.

### Core Principles
Examples of principles within the product operating model include:
*   Innovation is more important than predictability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:51">[01:08:51]</a>.
*   Learning is more important than failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:56">[01:08:56]</a>.
*   Principles are more important than process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:58">[01:08:58]</a>.
*   Teams are empowered with problems to solve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:05">[01:09:05]</a>.
*   Emphasis on real ownership within teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:14">[01:09:14]</a>.
*   Addressing product risks and embracing quick experimentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:23">[01:09:23]</a>.
*   Small, frequent, uncoupled releases, often 20 times per day in good companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:35">[01:09:35]</a>.
*   Instrumentation and monitoring of everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:40">[01:09:40]</a>.

### Product Ops
Product Ops is analogous to Dev Ops and Design Ops, focusing on centralizing functions like user research and data analysis under one leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>. These are "small, high-leverage groups" that help teams rather than adding unnecessary layers of "process and governance" or serving as product manager assistants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:06">[01:13:06]</a>.

## Advice for Startup Founders on Hiring PMs

Founders often believe they don't need product managers early on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:20">[01:15:20]</a>. Cagan advises against hiring product managers "too soon" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:56">[01:15:56]</a>.
*   **Founder's Role:** Initially, the founder's job is to manage value and viability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:31">[01:16:31]</a>. Bringing in a "real" product manager too early can cause conflict <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:38">[01:16:38]</a>.
*   **Timing:** It's generally better to hire a product manager after achieving product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:03">[01:17:03]</a>, or when the company reaches a certain scale, typically around 20-25 engineers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:27">[01:17:27]</a>.
