## Focus on Quality and Remarkableness

At Superhuman, the value of "remarkable quality" is baked directly into the company's core values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:02">[01:30:02]</a>. This means creating something "so striking, so compelling and worthy of attention that people can't but help tell others about it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:02">[01:30:02]</a>. This commitment to detail helps to build a strong brand and fosters [[the_importance_of_customer_proximity_in_product_design | word-of-mouth virality]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:02">[01:30:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:04">[01:13:04]</a>.

## Case Study: Font Selection for Superhuman

An example of this extreme [[importance_of_design_and_functionality_in_product_development | attention to detail]] is the selection and modification of Superhuman's font <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:01:12">[03:01:12]</a>. Rahul found that none of the major font families were "truly excellent" for email <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:30:53">[03:30:53]</a>. The process involved:
*   **Initial Evaluation** The Superhuman team laid out their UI in about 15 different styles using major font families, printing them out and leaving them in the office to "marinate" and "percolate" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:39:56">[03:39:56]</a>.
*   **Specific Criteria** Rahul optimized for four key aspects when choosing a font:
    1.  **Aesthetics:** The font needed to be "gorgeous" and each character a "work of art" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:33:04">[03:33:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a>.
    2.  **Emotional Conveyance:** It had to convey any message without overpowering its sentiment (e.g., suitable for both a party invitation and a notification of someone's passing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:02">[03:40:02]</a>.
    3.  **Readability:** It needed to facilitate fast reading speed and comprehension. This led to favoring narrow fonts like Adele Sands to maintain an optimal line length (90-120 characters) even in small windows, preventing eyes from losing track of the next line <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:43:03">[03:43:03]</a>.
    4.  **Email Address Presentation:** The font needed to make email addresses look great, specifically noting Adele Sands' unusual treatment of the "@" symbol, which places the 'A' on the same baseline as the rest of the text, making it look "incredibly natural" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:02">[03:52:02]</a>.
*   **Refinement:** After selecting Adele Sands, the team worked with a type designer to modify specific glyphs that were "a little pincory" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:44:02">[03:44:02]</a>.

This meticulous approach was undertaken when Superhuman only had about 10-15 users, well before launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:04">[03:57:04]</a>.

## Balancing Speed and Quality

Rahul believes that the level of attention to detail and time spent perfecting a product before launch depends on the market and business model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:02">[03:59:02]</a>. While some markets, like greenfield marketplaces with strong network effects (e.g., Uber or Lyft), demand extreme speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:02">[03:59:02]</a>, this isn't universally true <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:02">[03:59:02]</a>.

For a mission-critical product like email, which interfaces with customers, candidates, and investors, it's crucial to have a product that "just works" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:22:04">[04:22:04]</a>. Releasing a "half-baked product" can lead to a loss of face and trust if critical functionalities fail (as exemplified by the Mailbox story where sent emails didn't go through or were duplicated) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:22:04">[04:22:04]</a>. This highlights that for certain products, [[balancing_speed_and_quality_in_product_development | attention to quality and detail]] is paramount, even if it delays launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:30:41">[04:30:41]</a>.

## Investing in User Experience

Superhuman's decision to manually onboard every single new user for years was another demonstration of their [[importance_of_integrating_ux_research_in_product_development | commitment to user experience]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:00">[04:47:00]</a>. While eventually transitioning to a self-service model, this initial hands-on approach ensured excellent user metrics for engagement, retention, product market fit score, NPS, and virality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:54:00">[04:54:00]</a>. It allowed the company to focus engineering efforts on [[product_market_fit | finding product market fit]] and [[the_importance_of_design_and_user_experience_in_building_software | solution deepening]], rather than activation flows, because humans were handling the activation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:54:00">[04:54:00]</a>. This meticulous [[the_importance_of_customer_proximity_in_product_design | customer proximity]] helped kickstart a strong brand through word-of-mouth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:54:00">[04:54:00]</a>.

The focus on [[the_importance_of_design_and_user_experience_in_building_software | game design]] (not just gamification) in Superhuman's product also illustrates this attention to detail. By designing "fun toys" like the time autocompleter, which indulges playful exploration and elicits pleasant surprises, the product inherently becomes more engaging and delightful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:02">[01:03:02]</a>. This approach makes the product fun, which in turn encourages users to tell their friends and fall in love with it, contributing to brand building and word-of-mouth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.
