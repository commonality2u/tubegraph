From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

The distinction between **Feature Teams** and **Product Teams** is a critical philosophical concept in modern product development, with significant implications for how companies innovate and deliver value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. Marty Cagan, founder of Silicon Valley Product Group and author of *Empowered* and *Inspired*, highlights this difference as a "chasm" between how successful companies operate and the rest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.

While superficially both might be referred to as "squads," their operational models and underlying philosophies are "radically different" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>. In fact, Cagan suggests that the job description for a "product manager" in a feature team is so different from that in a true product team that the same title is misleading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>. An anecdotal guess suggests only about 10-15% of commercial product companies operate as true product companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:03">[00:16:03]</a>.

## Identifying a Feature Team

A [[feature_teams_vs_empowered_product_teams | Feature Team]], also sometimes referred to as a "feature factory," is characterized by its focus on output rather than outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:08">[00:10:08]</a>.

Key signs you're working on a feature team include:
*   **Roadmap-Driven** You are handed a roadmap that is a prioritized list of features and projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>. These features are often determined quarterly by stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>.
*   **Solution Hand-off** You are given specific solutions (features) to implement, meaning your role is primarily to design a little, code a lot, and then perform QA and deploy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:38">[00:08:38]</a>.
*   **Serving the Business** The primary goal is to serve the business by cranking out features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>.
*   **Product Manager as Project Manager** The [[role_of_product_managers_in_tech_companies | product manager's role]] is largely to "herd the cats," organizing, gathering, and documenting requirements (PRDs, user stories), and ensuring items reach sprint planning and are delivered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>. This is essentially a [[challenges_with_project_management_roles_in_product_teams | project management role]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>.
*   **Idea vs. Craftsmanship** Executives often believe "an idea is 90% of the work" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:28">[00:23:28]</a>, underestimating the "craftsmanship of going from an idea to a product," which involves constant change and trade-offs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:42">[00:23:42]</a>.
*   **Ineffectiveness** Only about 20% of features developed this way generate any positive return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:18">[00:24:18]</a>.

## Characteristics of a Product Team

In contrast, a true [[feature_teams_vs_empowered_product_teams | Product Team]] is empowered to solve problems and deliver valuable outcomes.

Key characteristics of a product team:
*   **Problem-Centric** Instead of features, the team is given problems to solve, which can be customer problems or company problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:55">[00:08:55]</a>.
*   **Empowered Solutioning** The team is given the skills and autonomy to come up with the *best* solution to a given problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. This aligns with the "Netflix principle" of pushing decisions down to those with the most knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.
*   **Outcome-Oriented** The focus is on outcomes (solving the problem) rather than just outputs (releasing features) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>. Celebration happens when the problem is truly solved and results are achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>.
*   **Collaborative Peers** Engineers and designers are "true peers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:48">[00:06:48]</a>, not just implementers of ideas. They care as much about *what* is built as *how* it's built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>.
*   **Product Manager's Core Role** The [[role_of_product_managers_in_tech_companies | product manager's role]] is more challenging, focusing on discovering solutions that are valuable, usable, feasible, and viable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>. The product manager is specifically responsible for ensuring value and viability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:24">[00:11:24]</a>.

### The Product Manager's Essential Contributions

On an empowered product team, the product manager must bring specific skills and knowledge that the designers and engineers do not possess <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>. These include:
1.  **User and Customer Expertise:** Deeply understanding users and customers, sometimes requiring extensive customer visits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:27">[00:39:27]</a>.
2.  **Data Proficiency:** Being an expert in how the product is used, sales analytics, and user analytics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:14">[00:40:14]</a>.
3.  **Business Acumen:** Understanding various business aspects: marketing, sales, monetization, compliance, regulatory, privacy, and security issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:38">[00:40:38]</a>. This builds trust with stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:55">[00:40:55]</a>.
4.  **Industry and Competitive Knowledge:** Knowing the competitive landscape, industry trends, and what's possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:15">[00:41:15]</a>.

These four areas are crucial for a product manager to contribute meaningfully to an empowered team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:30">[00:41:30]</a>. A common reason empowered teams fail is that the product manager is "ill-equipped" or "incompetent" in these areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:48">[00:41:48]</a>.

## Shifting from Feature Team to Product Team

For companies or teams looking to transition from a feature factory to a product team model:

### Experimentation and Manager Buy-in
*   Suggest an experiment to your manager, proposing to run like a product team for a quarter or two <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:09">[00:36:09]</a>. This is less risky on a team-by-team basis than changing an entire business unit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:30">[00:36:30]</a>.

### Key Steps for the Team
1.  **Shift to Problem-Solving:** Ensure the team has a problem to solve, rather than just a feature to build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:11">[00:37:11]</a>. If given a feature, "reverse engineer" the problem by asking stakeholders about their success metrics (KPIs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:26">[00:37:26]</a>.
2.  **Product Manager Preparation:** The product manager must develop the four key areas of expertise mentioned above <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:15">[00:39:15]</a>. This can take two to three months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:36">[00:43:36]</a>.
3.  **Strategic Context:** The team needs clear strategic context, including the product vision, product strategy, and how their work relates to other teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:02">[00:42:02]</a>.
4.  **Discovery Skills:** Learn and apply modern [[understanding_work_for_product_development | product discovery]] techniques <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:31">[00:42:31]</a>. Resources like *Continuous Discovery Habits* by Teresa Torres or *Sprint* by Jake Knapp can be helpful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:14">[00:44:14]</a>.

### The "How" is Critical
Product teams recognize that "people don't buy the problem, they buy your solution" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. The focus should be on *solution discovery* â€“ ensuring the solution is valuable, usable, feasible, and viable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:53">[00:26:53]</a>. Product managers are "very responsible for the how" of the solution, not just the "what" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:48">[00:27:48]</a>.

### The Dangers of Delegation in Product Management
While it's beneficial to offload certain responsibilities like project management, quality assurance, product marketing, or runtime operations as a company grows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:31">[00:52:31]</a>, there are three "sacred" areas that should **never** be delegated by a product manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:35">[00:50:35]</a>:
1.  **Unencumbered Access to Users and Customers:** Direct interaction is crucial; anyone trying to mediate this access harms the product manager's effectiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:49">[00:50:49]</a>.
2.  **Unencumbered Access to Engineers:** Product managers must work daily with engineers to solve problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:15">[00:51:15]</a>. Intermediary roles like product owners or project managers who block this direct interface lead to a lack of innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:32">[00:51:32]</a>.
3.  **Unencumbered Access to Stakeholders:** This ensures the product works for the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:54">[00:51:54]</a>.

Delegating these core responsibilities, often under the guise of making the [[role_of_product_managers_in_tech_companies | product manager's job]] "easier," inadvertently creates significant damage to product success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:57">[00:52:57]</a>.

## [[common_problems_faced_by_product_teams | Common Problems]] and Trends

Cagan expresses concern about certain trends in product management:
*   **Unqualified Product People:** The prevalence of product owners taught by agile coaches who have no practical product experience, leading to "the blind leading the blind" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:17">[00:49:17]</a>. This results in a high proportion of "completely unqualified product people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:54">[00:49:54]</a>.
*   **Misguided Product Ops:** While some [[product_ops | product ops]] definitions are beneficial (e.g., managing runtime responsibilities, creating tools for PMs), others cause issues by mediating access to critical data or customer insights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a>.
*   **The "Disease of Process People":** Scaling with process (e.g., SAFe) instead of scaling with leaders is a dangerous trend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:49">[00:55:49]</a>. Process-heavy approaches are seen as repackaged waterfall and the "antithesis of agile," ultimately destroying companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:37">[00:56:37]</a>. Leaders like Steve Jobs, Jeff Bezos, and Elon Musk have warned against this "disease" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:20">[00:57:20]</a>.

Overall, the distinction between feature teams and true product teams lies in their fundamental approach to problems, solutions, and the empowerment of their members, with a clear focus on delivering valuable outcomes through collaborative [[understanding_work_for_product_development | product discovery]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:55">[00:08:55]</a>.
