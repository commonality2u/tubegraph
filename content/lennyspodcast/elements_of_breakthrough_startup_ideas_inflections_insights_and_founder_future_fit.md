From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

According to Mike Maples Jr., a legendary early-stage startup investor, most breakthrough startup ideas share three core elements: [[the_role_of_inflections_in_enabling_startup_success | inflections]], [[founders_insights_and_their_importance_in_startup_success | insights]], and [[the_concept_of_future_founder_fit_and_its_impact_on_innovation | founder future fit]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. These elements enable a startup to "wage asymmetric warfare on the present" and play an unfair game by unfair rules that favor them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:09">[00:00:09]</a>. A startup never beats a big company by executing better; instead, it wins by proposing a radically different future that disorients incumbents and moves people to that new reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>.

## Uncovering the Patterns

Maples's research involved reviewing notes and decks from thousands of startups over two decades to understand where great startup ideas originate and what differentiates successful founders and startups from those that fail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:25">[00:01:25]</a>. He noted that 80% of his biggest returning investments came from pivots, suggesting that initial implementations often aren't correct, but a strong underlying insight allows for navigation to the right product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. He emphasizes studying the "time capsule" of early-stage companies to remember how things truly happened, rather than the often-misremembered success stories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.

## The Three Elements

### 1. Inflections
An [[the_role_of_inflections_in_enabling_startup_success | inflection]] is an external event that creates the potential for radical change in how people think, feel, and act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:31">[00:15:31]</a>. It is a "turning point" where something new is introduced, enabling new empowerment for the first time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:08">[00:16:08]</a>.

*   **Examples**:
    *   **iPhone 4S with GPS chip**: Enabled ride-sharing companies like Lyft and Uber, allowing riders and drivers to locate each other accurately <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:52">[00:15:52]</a>.
    *   **Improved smartphone cameras and broadband adoption**: Enabled Instagram by making it possible to take and share high-quality photos easily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:12">[00:17:12]</a>.
    *   **Shift to user-generated content and improved CDN/broadband**: Enabled Twitch (originally Justin.tv) by making broad live video streaming possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:02">[00:23:02]</a>.
    *   **Proliferation of online customer reviews and Facebook Connect**: Combined with the Great Financial Crisis, these enabled Airbnb by building trust and providing income opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:27">[00:24:27]</a>.
    *   **Regulatory changes for telemedicine during COVID**: Made virtual doctor visits legal and reimbursable, empowering both patients and doctors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:14">[00:25:14]</a>.

*   **Stress Tests for Inflections**: To evaluate an [[the_role_of_inflections_in_enabling_startup_success | inflection]], ask:
    *   What is the specific new thing that was just introduced? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:36">[00:21:36]</a>
    *   How does it empower people? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:40">[00:21:40]</a>
    *   Under what conditions might that empowerment be realized or not realized? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:47">[00:21:47]</a>

*   **Categories of Inflections**:
    *   **Technological**: Such as new hardware or software capabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:30">[00:26:30]</a>.
    *   **Regulatory**: Changes in laws or policies that enable new behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:27">[00:26:27]</a>.
    *   **Belief/Social**: Permanent shifts in people's attitudes or patterns of behavior, often accelerated by external events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:32">[00:26:32]</a>.

### 2. Insights
An [[founders_insights_and_their_importance_in_startup_success | insight]] is a "non-obvious truth" that comes from the founders, detailing how one or more [[the_role_of_inflections_in_enabling_startup_success | inflections]] can be harnessed to change people's behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:12">[00:28:12]</a>.

*   **Non-Consensus and Radical Difference**: The best startup ideas are non-consensusâ€”most people might dislike or be indifferent to them, but a subset of people will "fall irrationally in love" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:27">[00:30:27]</a>. This is because consensus ideas are usually extensions of the present, whereas startups win by being radically different and denying the premise of existing rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:06">[00:30:06]</a>.
*   **Backcasting**: Great founders "backcast" by envisioning a radically different future and working backward, rather than forecasting from the present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:07">[00:31:07]</a>.
*   **Savoring Surprises and Earning Secrets**: Breakthroughs cannot be created with a recipe; they are discovered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:00">[00:37:00]</a>. Founders should actively seek and savor surprises, structuring experiments to learn something new rather than just validating existing beliefs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:49">[00:37:49]</a>. This process involves "earning the secret" by getting hands dirty and being awake to possibilities most people overlook <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:45">[00:41:45]</a>.
    *   **Example**: Chegg created a fake site (Textbookflick) to test textbook rental prices. The surprise was that students were willing to pay significantly more than anticipated, revealing a crucial insight about demand elasticity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:14">[00:39:14]</a>.
*   **Insight vs. Implementation**: An [[founders_insights_and_their_importance_in_startup_success | insight]] can be correct even if the initial implementation is wrong, allowing for pivots towards product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:50">[00:42:50]</a>. For example, Octa initially focused on cloud systems management, but customer feedback led them to pivot to identity management, which was a higher priority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:21">[00:43:21]</a>.
*   **Desperation in Customers**: A startup needs customers who are so desperate for a solution that they are "irresistibly drawn" to the new offering, even if it's imperfect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:00">[01:01:00]</a>. This allows the startup to "force a choice and not a comparison" against existing solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>.

### 3. Founder Future Fit
[[the_concept_of_future_founder_fit_and_its_impact_on_innovation | Founder future fit]] refers to a set of traits that make a founder ideally suited to a certain type of future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:23">[00:49:23]</a>. It's not about being a "canonical best founder," but about authenticity and being the most authentically matched for a radical future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:25">[00:54:25]</a>.

*   **Prepared Mind**: "Chance favors the prepared mind" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:27">[00:51:27]</a>. Founders with future fit attract luck and are better positioned to notice breakthroughs because they are constantly thinking about their subject <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:37">[00:51:37]</a>.
*   **Living in the Future**: Instead of trying to think of startup ideas, founders should live in the future and notice what's missing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:05">[00:52:05]</a>. If you're not living in the future, your opinion about it is less valid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:52">[00:56:52]</a>.
    *   **Examples**:
        *   **Mark Andreessen (Netscape)**: A programmer making minimum wage, he was tinkering with the worldwide web and building what was missing for himself and his team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:02">[00:48:02]</a>.
        *   **Applied Intuition**: Founders had grown up in Detroit, worked at car companies, and then at Google (Waymo, Google Maps), making them ideally suited to sell simulation software for autonomous vehicles to car CEOs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:42">[00:48:42]</a>.
        *   **OCTA**: Todd McKinnon's background at Salesforce made him trusted for identity management for cloud customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:18">[00:50:18]</a>.
        *   **Justin Kan (Justin.tv)**: Built a product he wished he had, despite it initially being a "terrible idea," because it embodied inflections that eventually led to Twitch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:34">[00:53:34]</a>.
        *   **Maddy Hall (Living Carbon)**: By proactively getting out of the present and serving as Sam Altman's chief of staff, she gained exposure to various futures, leading her to the idea of genetically modified trees for carbon capture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:19">[00:57:19]</a>.
*   **Lighthouse Customers**: Not all customers are equal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:19">[00:59:19]</a>. Some are "Lighthouse customers" who are already living in the future, and solving their problems can lead a startup to the "promised land" because their needs represent future market demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:35">[00:58:35]</a>.

## Pattern-Breaking Actions

Beyond idea generation, successful [[attributes_of_successful_founders_and_startups | startup success]] involves "pattern-breaking actions" that persuade others to embrace a different future. These actions often create a stark dichotomy between the present and a desired future, making the new offering irresistibly desirable to early believers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:35">[01:04:35]</a>.

### 1. Movements
A movement leverages the grievance of a minority against the tyranny of a majority, animating early believers to commit emotionally to a different future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:33">[01:05:33]</a>. Early customers, employees, and investors are driven by belief and aesthetic reasons, not just pragmatism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:52">[01:05:52]</a>.
*   **Examples**:
    *   **Lyft**: The "pink mustache" and fist-bump culture helped make ride-sharing seem less scary and more of a movement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:40">[01:14:40]</a>.
    *   **Seed Investing**: Floodgate helped establish seed investing as a permanent feature of the venture landscape, appealing to key LPs who believed in the concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:52">[01:07:52]</a>.
    *   **Clarence Birdseye (Frozen Food)**: Had to start a movement to convince trains to have refrigerated cars and supermarkets to have refrigerated aisles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:41">[01:08:41]</a>.
    *   **Tesla**: Mission statement "accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" appeals to a higher purpose, not just building better cars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:51">[01:09:51]</a>.
    *   **Airbnb**: Created a movement around "living like a local," turning the hotel industry's strength (consistent experience) into a weakness for those seeking authenticity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:42">[01:10:42]</a>.

### 2. Storytelling
Storytelling is crucial for articulating the new future. Founders should describe "the world that is" and "the world that could be," using frameworks like the hero's journey where the early believers are the heroes, and the founder is the mentor (Obi-Wan, not Luke) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:30">[01:12:30]</a>.
*   **Example**: Steve Jobs's iPhone launch speech masterfully contrasted the limitations of existing phones with the revolutionary potential of the iPhone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:33">[01:17:33]</a>.
*   **Tailored Pitches**: The story told to an employee, investor, or customer must differ because each audience has a unique "hero's journey" and definition of self-actualization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:20">[01:16:20]</a>.

### 3. Disagreeableness
A startup is a fundamentally disagreeable act, as it challenges the status quo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:38">[01:25:38]</a>. Great founders are willing to pursue their obsession, even if it means sacrificing status or being disliked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:05">[01:25:05]</a>. This allows them to "stand out" and make a difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:32">[01:25:32]</a>.
*   **Courage to be Disliked**: Before achieving [[attributes_of_successful_founders_and_startups | startup success]], founders must have the courage to be disliked because people may find their idea stupid or lonely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:09">[01:30:09]</a>. After success, criticism often shifts to accusations of cheating or breaking rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:20">[01:30:20]</a>.

## Applying Principles in Large Companies

Even within large companies, the principles of pattern-breaking can apply <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:43">[01:34:43]</a>.
*   **Separate Initiatives**: Pattern-breaking ideas should be treated differently from normal ideas. They should ideally be autonomous, discreet, and somewhat hidden from the "mother ship" to prevent them from being pulled back into the corporate "tractor beam" of pattern-matching and conventional thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:09">[01:37:09]</a>.
*   **Tolerating Failure**: Companies should be willing to invest a portion of profits in projects likely to fail but with wildly asymmetric upside if they succeed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:22">[01:38:22]</a>. The willingness to fail is what enables breakthrough success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:44">[01:38:44]</a>.

Ultimately, starting a company or pursuing a breakthrough idea is a lonely existence, and it's okay if most people don't like the idea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:46">[01:39:46]</a>. Founders must stick their neck out for what they believe they see before others, knowing that every great founder faces this challenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:02">[01:40:02]</a>.
