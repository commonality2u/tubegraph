## Signs of Lacking Strategy

A common problem for product teams, especially in scaling companies, is the absence of a clear [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. Signs that a team or company lacks or isn't using its [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] include:

*   **Overwork, Stagnant Metrics** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:54">[00:21:54]</a>: Teams are working excessively (80 hours a week), constantly releasing features, but company metrics aren't improving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:56">[00:21:56]</a>.
*   **Lack of Visibility** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:13">[00:22:13]</a>: Executives view product and tech as a "black box," unsure what progress is being made or if goals are being achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:13">[00:22:13]</a>. If executives and the board don't know what the product team is doing, it's a significant issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:51">[00:10:51]</a>.
*   **Disconnected Work** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:25">[00:22:25]</a>: While individual teams might know what they're working on (features, enhancements, bug fixes), the connection to overarching business value or the company [[longterm_vision_and_future_casting_in_business_strategy | vision]] is unclear to executives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:26">[00:22:26]</a>.
*   **Misalignment on Goals** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:48">[00:23:48]</a>: Different answers across the organization when asking about goals or [[longterm_vision_and_future_casting_in_business_strategy | vision]] indicate a lack of strategic alignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:50">[00:23:50]</a>. This often means the [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] exists only in people's heads, not formally documented or communicated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:04">[00:23:04]</a>.
*   **Absence of "Why"** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:28">[00:28:28]</a>: Product teams frequently ask "why are we building this?", signaling a failure in leadership to clearly explain objectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:28">[00:28:28]</a>.
*   **Executive Infighting** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:44">[00:28:44]</a>: Executives having different goals for the company leads to internal conflict, rather than operating as a unified team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:51">[00:28:51]</a>.

## Components of a Product Strategy

A clear, well-deployed [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] ensures that all teams understand how their tactical work ladders up to business metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:10">[00:28:10]</a>. Key components include:

*   **Vision** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:05">[00:31:05]</a>: A clear, concrete picture of where the company is going, how it differs from today, and its unique value proposition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:14">[00:36:14]</a>. It should be aspirational but understandable, not just a tagline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:09">[00:24:09]</a>. It should define future customers, different value provided, how it's better than competitors, and what the company *won't* do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:47">[00:37:47]</a>.
*   **Strategic Intents / Priorities** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:53">[00:24:53]</a>: Big business movers for the next 2-3 years, such as expanding geographically, going up/down market, or innovating into new areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>. These need to be clearly prioritized at the executive level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:13">[00:25:13]</a>.
*   **Product Initiatives** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:49">[00:31:49]</a>: Problem-oriented efforts, often made up of multiple epics, that align with strategic intents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:51">[00:31:51]</a>. Product leadership (directors, VPs) typically defines these <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:40">[00:31:40]</a>.
*   **Solutions / Epics** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:06">[00:32:06]</a>: The actual features or functionalities built by product teams to address the product initiatives and achieve strategic intents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:12">[00:32:12]</a>.

### Documenting Strategy

The recommended approach to documenting [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] involves a concise, layered structure:

*   **Two-Pager Memo** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:29">[00:29:29]</a>: CEOs should write a two-page document outlining the company's origin, current state, external threats, competition, what to care/not care about, and prioritized strategic intents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:29">[00:29:29]</a>.
*   **Layered Documentation** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:33">[00:31:33]</a>: Each level (executive, product leadership, product teams) should create a one- or two-pager that defines their part of the strategy, explaining "what we're doing and why" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:37">[00:32:37]</a>.
*   **Connected Documents** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:44">[00:32:44]</a>: These documents should be linked (e.g., in Google Docs or a wiki) so anyone can trace the strategy from tactical work up to the company [[longterm_vision_and_future_casting_in_business_strategy | vision]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:44">[00:32:44]</a>.

## Crafting a Compelling Product Vision

A [[crafting_a_compelling_product_vision | product vision]] should be concrete enough for people to visualize <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:14">[00:36:14]</a>. It should be aspirational and not merely describe the current state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:37">[00:36:37]</a>.

Tips for improving [[crafting_a_compelling_product_vision | vision]] development:

*   **Writing and Visualization** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:46">[00:34:46]</a>: A combination of writing (brain-dumping ideas) and visual presentations (prototypes, diagrams, customer interactions, ecosystem maps) is powerful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:03">[00:34:03]</a>. Designers can be instrumental in making [[longterm_vision_and_future_casting_in_business_strategy | vision]] clear and compelling through visuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:53">[00:35:53]</a>.
*   **Focus on Differentiation** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:09">[00:37:09]</a>: Explicitly discuss how the company will be different from competitors and why it will be better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:18">[00:37:18]</a>.
*   **Define What Not To Do** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:16">[00:38:16]</a>: A strong [[crafting_a_compelling_product_vision | vision]] also clearly states what the company will *not* do or be, preventing misguided efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:19">[00:38:19]</a>.

## Becoming More Strategic

To [[becoming_more_strategic_in_product_management | become more strategic]] in product management:

*   **"Pretend You're the CPO" Exercise** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:18">[00:39:18]</a>: Even without the formal role, imagine what decisions you would make as a CPO. This builds experience in asking strategic questions and analyzing data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:20">[00:39:20]</a>.
*   **Talk to Market and Financial Experts** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:43">[00:39:43]</a>: Engage with people who deeply understand the market and financials, including your Chief Product Officer, to learn their thought processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:45">[00:39:45]</a>.
*   **Cross-Departmental Conversations** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:24">[00:40:24]</a>: Talk to other departments (e.g., sales for win/loss analysis) to gather a wealth of knowledge on market trends, competitor activity, and customer behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:24">[00:40:24]</a>.
*   **Leverage Data and Research** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:06">[00:41:06]</a>: Leaders need data from everywhere—internal systems, user analytics, customer research, and market research—to make informed decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:17">[00:41:17]</a>. Product operations can help surface this data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:26">[00:45:26]</a>.
*   **Embrace Decision-Making and Iteration** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:17">[00:42:17]</a>: It's crucial to make the best informed decisions possible at the time, even without 100% certainty, and be willing to correct course if data indicates it was the wrong one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:20">[00:42:20]</a>. This iterative approach to [[developing_longterm_strategic_approaches_in_product_development | strategy]] is key to success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:46">[00:42:46]</a>.
*   **Learn by Doing and Adapting** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:22">[00:51:22]</a>: The most effective learning comes from execution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:34">[00:49:34]</a>. Analyze your work, identify areas for improvement, and deep dive into those specific topics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:40">[00:49:40]</a>. Adapt frameworks and processes to fit your company's culture and needs, rather than adhering rigidly to dogmatic methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:47">[00:51:47]</a>.

## The Role of a Chief Product Officer (CPO)

A Chief Product Officer (CPO) is essential when a company needs to:

*   **Scale from Single to Multi-Product** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>: This phase requires rethinking the entire [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]], prioritizing, and rapid scaling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>.
*   **Formulate Comprehensive Product Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:20">[00:10:20]</a>: A CPO pulls together an all-encompassing [[developing_a_product_strategy | product strategy]] and ensures strong communication between product and the executive team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>.
*   **Manage Complex Portfolios and Rapid Scaling** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>: As a company expands geographically, into new markets, or undergoes major transformations/pivots/mergers, the increased complexity necessitates CPO leadership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a>.
*   **Bridge the Strategy Gap** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:20">[00:11:20]</a>: A CPO ensures that tactical work is connected to the overall company [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.
*   **Provide Product Leadership and Training** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:57">[00:11:57]</a>: If there's trouble hiring or developing product managers, a CPO provides essential leadership, training paths, and processes for growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>.
*   **Understand Financials and Board Interface** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</a>: CPOs deeply understand financials, create revenue projections from roadmaps, and confidently present to the board <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:08">[00:15:08]</a>. They act as executive navigators <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:35">[00:15:35]</a>.
*   **Oversee Multiple Functions** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:40">[00:15:40]</a>: A CPO often oversees not just product, but also design, product operations, analytics, and sometimes even engineering, bringing these areas together under a singular leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:42">[00:15:42]</a>.

The decision to hire a CPO usually correlates with company strategy rather than a specific number of Product Managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:33">[00:12:33]</a>, often when annual recurring revenue (ARR) reaches around $20-$30 million in a high-growth company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:41">[00:12:41]</a>. Before a CPO, a Head of Product or VP of Product typically acts as a functional leader, implementing processes and managing roadmaps for one or two products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:41">[00:14:41]</a>.

## Product Operations

[[product_strategy_stack | Product operations]] is an emerging role vital for scaling product management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>. It addresses challenges such as lack of standardization in processes and roadmaps, absence of career ladders, inefficient meetings, and difficulty accessing data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:27">[00:44:27]</a>.

Key functions of product operations:

1.  **Internal Data and Insights** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:34">[00:45:34]</a>: Helps surface data from financial systems, user analytics, and other internal sources to monitor [[developing_a_product_strategy | strategy]] progress, track OKRs, and inform decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:38">[00:45:38]</a>.
2.  **Customer Research and User Insights** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:03">[00:46:03]</a>: Manages external data and market research, standardizes approaches for product managers to talk to customers (avoiding over-contact), and ensures user interviews are recorded and searchable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:06">[00:46:06]</a>. It helps scale user research without centralizing the practice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:49">[00:46:49]</a>.
3.  **Process Standardization and Cadence Management** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:31">[00:47:31]</a>: Standardizes processes and meeting cadences for strategic check-ins (e.g., monthly roadmap reviews, quarterly executive planning sessions) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:33">[00:47:33]</a>. It also standardizes product management processes that interact with other divisions, like updating sales teams on roadmaps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:53">[00:47:53]</a>.

A new book on product operations, co-authored by Melissa Perry, is expected by the end of 2022 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:34">[00:48:34]</a>.
