From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Dalton Caldwell, a managing director and group partner at Y Combinator with over a decade of experience across 21 YC batches, emphasizes the critical role of direct customer interaction in a startup's journey toward [[product_market_fit_techniques | product/market fit]] and sustained success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:00">[00:55:00]</a>. He has worked closely with numerous successful companies including Instacart, Retool, Brex, Deel, DoorDash, Webflow, and others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>.

## The "Don't Die" Mantra

A core mantra shared by Dalton Caldwell is simply "don't die" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. This applies to keeping the startup going even when challenges arise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. Founders often experience near-death moments where, rationally, they might give up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>. The ability to push through these periods, fueled by an "irrational intention to keep going," is a common theme among successful YC companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. This persistence is often rooted in a deep connection to the product and its users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:46">[00:09:46]</a>.

### When to Give Up
While perseverance is key, there are times when giving up might be the right move. Dalton suggests asking:
*   Are you still having fun? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>
*   Do you still enjoy spending time with your co-founders? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>
*   Do you love your customers and the problem you're solving? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:12">[00:10:12]</a>

If the answer to these questions is no, and the work is profoundly affecting personal relationships or mental health, it might be time to stop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>. No one will remember a company shutting down in 10 or 20 years, so prioritizing well-being is important <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:52">[00:10:52]</a>.

## Why Startups Fail: Losing Hope
One of the most common reasons startups fail, according to Dalton, is that founders lose hope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:32">[00:37:32]</a>. It's less common for a well-resourced, positive team to simply run out of money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:23">[00:38:23]</a>. More often, founders quit because their idea isn't working, they have conflicts with co-founders, or they simply run out of ideas and desire to continue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:45">[00:38:45]</a>. This underscores the need for genuine engagement with the product and customers.

## The Importance of Talking to Customers
Gustaf, a YC colleague, highlights that startups often fail because they don't talk to customers and thus don't find [[product_market_fit_techniques | product/market fit]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:04">[00:37:04]</a>. Dalton strongly agrees, emphasizing that genuine [[understanding_and_establishing_candidate_market_fit | customer understanding]] is fundamental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:22">[00:32:22]</a>.

### Avoid Over-Delegating Early On
Founders should actively stay close to their product and avoid over-delegating in the early stages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:43">[00:32:43]</a>. Hiring "super senior people with fancy resumes" too early can cause founders to lose touch with the core product and customer needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:51">[00:32:51]</a>. The founders who excel are "always deeply in the weeds on product and still care a lot and are still talking to customers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:16">[00:33:16]</a>.

> "You can't delegate caring about your users and you can't delegate caring that the product is great that is so critical." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:30">[00:33:30]</a>

### Practical Advice for Talking to Customers
*   **Do it in person**: Don't just hide behind a keyboard or rely on landing pages and ads for early feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:23">[00:41:23]</a>. It might feel awkward, but pushing through that "social anxiety" is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:40">[00:42:40]</a>.
*   **Dedicate significant time**: Roughly 20-30% of a founder's time should be dedicated to in-person meetings or calls with potential customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:20">[00:43:20]</a>.
*   **Focus on conversations, not just metrics**: Staring at analytics dashboards is not the same as talking to customers, especially when you have few users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:59">[00:43:59]</a>.

### Early-Stage Growth: A Contrarian View
Dalton holds a "contrarian" belief that for very early-stage startups, [[growth_strategies_and_achieving_productmarket_fit_in_startups | growth hacking]], complex analytics, and A/B testing are often a "total waste of time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:58">[01:05:58]</a>. This advice, often catered to later-stage companies, confuses seed-stage founders who haven't yet acquired their first customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:19">[01:06:19]</a>. The focus should be on getting that very first customer and understanding their needs deeply <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:57">[01:06:57]</a>.

### Examples of Effective Customer Interaction
*   **Airbnb**: The founders continued to "grind" and tried "all sorts of stuff including cereal and conventions" to find their path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:35">[00:18:35]</a>. They genuinely loved their product and the first hosts they met <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:53">[00:09:53]</a>.
*   **Brex & Retool**: Initially struggling, both companies pivoted to ideas that built on their founders' existing expertise and close customer interactions within the YC network <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:41">[00:13:41]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>.
*   **Zip**: The founders were "beasts at getting companies on calls with them" to understand procurement needs, pre-selling their product before it was built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:28">[00:44:28]</a>. They would cold DM people on LinkedIn to ask for advice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:23">[00:45:23]</a>.
*   **Stripe (The Collison Install)**: The Collison brothers would actively show up at customers' offices to help them implement Stripe, ensuring the product was actually used after a "yes" to a sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:17">[00:46:17]</a>. This "white glove service" was highly effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:02">[00:47:02]</a>.

## [[pivoting_ideas_in_startups_for_productmarket_fit | Pivoting Ideas]] and Customer Insight
A good [[pivoting_ideas_in_startups_for_productmarket_fit | pivot]] usually involves moving "closer to home" or to something the founders are already experts at <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:46">[00:14:46]</a>. This expertise is often gained through direct experience with customers or the problem space, even if the initial idea failed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a>.

*   **Segment**: The founders learned about analytics by running their first, unrelated idea (software for university professors), which then informed their pivot to a successful analytics company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:38">[00:16:38]</a>. Their final product emerged from solving their own implementation issues, rather than starting with a grand vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:02">[00:17:02]</a>.

When considering a [[pivoting_ideas_in_startups_for_productmarket_fit | pivot]], Dalton advises looking at how many new ideas the founder has to make the current product grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:15">[00:18:15]</a>. If out of ideas, it's a good time to pivot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:20">[00:18:20]</a>.

## Avoiding "Tarpit Ideas"
"Tarpit ideas" are common startup concepts that seem promising and receive positive feedback, but are ultimately difficult to succeed with <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:27">[00:24:27]</a>. These ideas often appear to be "unsolved problems" and can draw founders in with initial validation, leading them to get stuck <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:30">[00:24:30]</a>.

Examples include apps to coordinate friends for social outings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:01">[00:25:01]</a> or music discovery services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:38">[00:25:38]</a>. The danger is that while people might say they "would love that," they don't actually use it or pay for it consistently <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:15">[00:25:15]</a>. True customer validation goes beyond polite agreement to actual usage and demonstrable demand.

## Building Founder Conviction
Founders gain conviction not by starting with a perfect idea, but through a virtuous cycle of building, engaging with customers, and seeing positive data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:36">[00:51:36]</a>. This feedback loop strengthens belief in the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:39">[00:51:39]</a>.

> "The better the product does the more obsessed you get with your own company." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:08">[00:51:08]</a>

## Conclusion
Ultimately, Dalton Caldwell's advice for startups boils down to simplicity and pragmatism: "sell, make money, don't run out of money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. This is achieved by deeply engaging with customers, understanding their needs, and building a product they truly want <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:05">[00:31:05]</a>. He encourages aspiring founders to "talk to potential customers and try to pre-sell something before you write code" as a first step to validation and starting a startup <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:31">[01:11:31]</a>.
