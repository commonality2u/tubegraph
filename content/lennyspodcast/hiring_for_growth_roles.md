Hiring for growth roles, particularly a first growth lead, is a critical decision for any company. Elena Verna, a renowned expert in growth strategy, emphasizes that growth is an evolution, not a revolution, and advises a strategic approach to building a growth team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:17">[52:17]</a>.

## When to Hire Your First Growth Person

Before considering a dedicated growth hire, a company must establish a strong product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:28">[55:28]</a>. This means:
*   **Demonstrated Retention:** Customers are consistently retaining with the product, ideally measured over six months to a year post-initial offering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:36">[55:36]</a>.
*   **Ability to Acquire & Monetize:** There are "green shoots" indicating the company's ability to drive product distribution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:50">[55:50]</a>.

> [!CAUTION] Delegation Mistake
> Hiring a growth person to *figure out* the growth model from the beginning is a mistake. The founding team should first establish the initial growth model themselves, just as they would the initial product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:10">[56:10]</a>. Delegating this crucial aspect can set the company up for failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:32">[56:32]</a>.

## Finding Your First Growth Hire

Verna strongly advocates for looking internally for a first growth hire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:27">[52:27]</a>. Potential candidates might include:
*   A financial planning and analysis (FP&A) analyst <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:30">[52:30]</a>
*   An existing product manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:32">[52:32]</a>
*   A data analyst <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:34">[52:34]</a>
*   An engineer who is business-minded and technically inclined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:36">[52:36]</a>

Elena Verna, widely recognized as one of the most experienced and knowledgeable individuals in growth strategy, offers significant insights into [[hiring_and_roles_in_product_management | hiring]] for growth roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54">[00:54]</a>. ## When to Hire Your First Growth Person

A company should only consider a dedicated growth hire once it has achieved a strong product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:28">[55:28]</a>. This means:
*   **Proven Retention:** There must be demonstrable customer retention, ideally measured over a period of six months to a year after the initial product offering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:36">[55:36]</a>.
*   **Acquisition & Monetization Ability:** The company should show initial "green shoots" indicating its capacity to drive product distribution and monetize customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:50">[55:50]</a>.

> [!CAUTION] Avoid Delegation at the Outset
> It is a mistake to hire a growth person with the expectation that they will "figure out" the growth model from scratch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:10">[56:10]</a>. The founding team must first establish the initial growth model themselves. Delegating this foundational aspect can set the company up for failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:31">[56:31]</a>.

## Finding Your First Growth Hire

Verna strongly advocates for seeking the first growth hire internally within the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:27">[52:27]</a>.

### Why Internal Hires are Preferred
An internal hire is generally recommended for the first growth role because:
*   **Familiarity:** They are already familiar with the product and how its unique growth model should function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:55">[52:55]</a>.
*   **Authenticity:** A growth model needs to be authentic and specific to the product offering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:03">[53:03]</a>.
*   **Quick Wins:** Internal candidates are more likely to deliver quick wins due to their existing context and understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:36">[53:36]</a>.

Potential internal candidates could include:
*   An FP&A analyst <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:30">[52:30]</a>
*   An existing product manager <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:32">[52:32]</a>
*   A data analyst <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:34">[52:34]</a>
*   An engineer who is business-minded and technically inclined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:36">[52:36]</a>

### Risks of External Hires
While not impossible, bringing in an external hire for the very first growth role carries significant risks:
*   **Lack of Product Context:** External hires are unfamiliar with the company's product and its specific growth model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:51">[52:51]</a>.
*   **"Copy-Paste" Tendency:** Under pressure to accelerate growth, external hires often default to replicating [[growth_strategies_for_saas_businesses | growth strategies]] or [[growth_hacking_as_a_sustainable_business_strategy | growth hacking]] models from their previous companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:07">[53:07]</a>. This "copy-paste" approach rarely works, as growth models are highly dependent on the product and market context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:40">[53:40]</a>.
*   **Short Tenure:** This approach often leads to a separation within one to two years, with no significant step-function change in growth trajectory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:56">[53:56]</a>.

> [!TIP] Educate and Sponsor
> If a company chooses to hire externally, they must be willing to invest time in educating and sponsoring the growth hire, allowing them the necessary three to six months (or even longer) to understand the product's unique growth model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:39">[53:39]</a>.

## Hiring Growth Leaders: The "Try Before You Buy" Model

Verna critiques the traditional methods of [[hiring_and_team_management_philosophy_in_tech_startups | hiring for leadership roles]] through recruiting agencies or direct CEO outreach, calling it "the most broken way" to hire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:04">[12:04]</a>. She advocates for a "try before you buy" approach, especially for leadership positions, to optimize for retention in the role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:57">[12:57]</a>.

This model involves:
*   **Advising Engagements:** Starting as an advisor to gauge the fit and understand the company's challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:02">[13:02]</a>.
*   **Interim Gigs:** Taking on an interim leadership role to gain accountability and help the company identify the ideal long-term leader, while keeping operations moving and facilitating a smooth transition for their successor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:55">[10:55]</a>.
*   **Mutual Evaluation:** This process allows both the candidate and the company to assess compatibility, address existing problems, and ensure a better "match made in heaven" than traditional interviews, which often oversell the role and company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:33">[12:33]</a>.

This non-traditional path helps leaders like Verna to make [[navigating_career_growth_and_advancement_in_tech | strategic pivots]] in their careers, optimize for retention, and ensure they can apply their "superpowers" to make a significant impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05">[05:05]</a>.

## General Advice for Driving Growth

For any founder or product leader focused on driving growth, Verna's overarching advice is to understand how to find patterns in the problems being solved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:07">[57:07]</a>. Instead of treating each problem individually, developing frameworks allows for repeatable, sustainable, and competitively defensible growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:23">[57:23]</a>. This approach minimizes context switching and fosters a strong growth culture and mindset across the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:57">[57:57]</a>.
