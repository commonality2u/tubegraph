## Core Philosophy: Velocity and Empowerment

Velocity is central to Ramp's operations, influencing product development, team incentives, hiring, promotion, decision-making, and organizational structure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:30">[00:07:30]</a>. This focus originated from a small team facing a large market opportunity during the pandemic, where execution speed was paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>. High velocity allows teams to achieve impact through rapid iteration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:12">[00:08:12]</a>, attracts top talent seeking to "build and ship things" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>, and de-risks decision-making by lowering the cost of incorrect choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>.

An example of Ramp's velocity:
*   With approximately 8 engineers, they built an MX competitor in three months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:01">[00:00:01]</a>.
*   Six months later, they built an Expensify competitor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>.
*   A team of three engineers, one designer, and one PM built a Bill.com competitor for accounts payable in three months, which now processes billions of dollars annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>.

The "recipe" for this speed involves:
*   Small, [[building_effective_product_teams | single-threaded teams]] with focused goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:54">[00:09:54]</a>.
*   Providing necessary resources for execution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58">[00:09:58]</a>.
*   Setting big, lofty goals with tight timelines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:01">[00:10:01]</a>.
*   Shielding teams from organizational chaos and distractions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.

## Operationalizing Context Over Control

Ramp's leadership, notably the CTO, practices extreme hands-off management regarding specific product decisions, enabled by a deep alignment on goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:47">[00:15:47]</a>. This approach is termed "context over control" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>, where the focus is on debating:
*   The ultimate goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:03">[00:16:03]</a>.
*   The underlying hypotheses to reach that goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:05">[00:16:05]</a>.
*   The data supporting those hypotheses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:07">[00:16:07]</a>.
*   The potential solutions to test hypotheses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:12">[00:16:12]</a>.

Leadership's role is to provide context and coach teams by sharing firsthand data and insights from higher-level meetings, rather than being prescriptive about solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:58">[00:16:58]</a>. Solutions are best derived from teams closer to the ground <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:53">[00:16:53]</a>. Mistakes happen when leaders dictate solutions without explaining the context, goals, hypotheses, and data upstream <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:41">[00:16:41]</a>.

### Aligning on Goals and Hypotheses
The "contract" between leadership and product teams is defined by their strategy and roadmap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:14">[00:18:14]</a>. This strategy document includes:
*   Goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:10">[00:38:10]</a>
*   Hypotheses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:13">[00:38:13]</a>
*   Unique company positioning to achieve goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:19">[00:38:19]</a>
*   Metrics for measuring success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:23">[00:38:23]</a>
*   Initiatives, risks, and long-term outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:28">[00:38:28]</a>

Each product pod drafts its own strategy document, which is then aligned with the broader product and financial strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:37">[00:38:37]</a>. This process fosters a culture where every team member thinks strategically, similar to how a leader would <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:12">[00:39:12]</a>.

### Shielding Teams and Distributing Work
To maintain single-threaded focus, Ramp employs "protective tissue" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:53">[00:12:53]</a>:
*   **Rotational production engineering programs** shield core teams from escalations, bugs, and issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:25">[00:12:25]</a>.
*   [[the_emerging_role_of_product_operations | Product operators]] protect PMs from chaos like documentation, escalations, release management, enablement, and customer requests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:42">[00:12:42]</a>.
*   For big bets, sub-teams are formed by pulling individuals from different areas, ensuring they don't have responsibilities for existing products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:58">[00:12:58]</a>.

This distribution of traditional PM tasks across other roles means:
*   Designers are empowered to think about specs, priorities, and scope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:40">[00:58:40]</a>.
*   [[empowering_engineers_in_leadership_roles | Engineers are empowered]] to take high-level specs and think deeply, even creating their own tickets and breaking down work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:44">[00:58:44]</a>.

### Minimizing Meetings and Status Updates
Ramp avoids traditional status meetings. Updates are done asynchronously within the systems where teams operate, ideally in real-time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:34">[00:26:34]</a>. Meetings are reserved for collaboration, ideation, and decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:44">[00:26:44]</a>. This helps [[advice_for_product_managers_and_organizational_transformation | avoid demotivating people]] with excessive process and documentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:27">[00:26:27]</a>.

### Focusing on Trade-offs
To maintain velocity, leaders must be clear about trade-offs and present a "menu of items" to leadership, forcing prioritization rather than trying to do "all of it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>. This clarity prevents overcommitment and enables faster execution on fewer, more critical items <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:42">[00:27:42]</a>.

## Importance of Talent for Empowerment
Ramp's velocity is less about culture amplification and more about the exceptional quality of its engineering and design talent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:06">[00:25:06]</a>. [[advice_for_product_managers_and_organizational_transformation | Advice for product managers]] seeking to increase velocity includes:
1.  **Investment in R&D:** Companies must position R&D as a first-class citizen, paying top market rates, hiring the best talent, and fostering a strong engineering and tech brand that attracts empowered individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:19">[00:25:19]</a>. This also means the CEO is less prescriptive about product specifics, trusting the empowered tech organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:52">[00:25:52]</a>.
2.  **Engineers' Proactiveness and Accountability:** Ramp seeks engineers who want to win in the market, understand business context, execute independently, set the pace for PMs, proactively engage in discussions, fix bugs, and take accountability for product performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:11">[01:02:11]</a>. This includes them actively asking questions about the business outside of just engineering domains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:30">[01:02:30]</a>. While this can lead to engineers challenging PMs and designers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:06">[01:05:06]</a>, this culture of challenge and deep thinking is preferred over passive acceptance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:21">[01:05:21]</a>.
3.  **Hiring for Impact and Deep Thinking:** Ramp emphasizes a strong desire for impact and the ability to think deeply over mere experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:08">[01:06:08]</a>. Candidates are assessed on how they make decisions and the depth of their thought processes, especially when faced with complex, unprecedented business challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:44">[01:06:44]</a>.

## Preventing Burnout Through Velocity
Counterintuitively, velocity can help avoid burnout <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:04">[00:30:04]</a>. Burnout often stems from expending effort on things that aren't moving forward, leading to a feeling of low impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:55">[00:29:55]</a>. By eliminating distractions and bureaucracy, teams can achieve a "flow state" where work becomes thrilling and impactful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:11">[00:30:11]</a>. The key is for work to be meaningful to the individual, not just the boss, fostering a sense of ownership over their "mini-company" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:44">[00:31:44]</a>.

## The Product Manager's Role in an Empowered Team
At Ramp, with minimal PMs (13 PMs for over 100 engineers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:17">[00:57:17]</a>, the core PM job is:
*   **Team building:** Creating a strong team culture within the pod, which might have cross-functional reporting lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:31">[01:00:31]</a>.
*   **Focus management:** Ensuring the team stays on track with their focus areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:04">[01:01:04]</a>.
*   **Stakeholder protection:** Shielding the core team from external opinions, update requests, and unnecessary meetings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:10">[01:01:10]</a>.
*   **Central point of contact:** Being the primary point of contact for questions and requests, and bringing in the right people at the right time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:21">[01:01:21]</a>.

PMs at Ramp essentially distribute many traditional PM responsibilities across engineering, design, and product operations, reducing low-leverage work like extensive ticket writing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:31">[00:59:31]</a>.

## Support Reporting to Product: A First Principles Approach
A unique aspect of Ramp's structure is having the support team report directly into product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:02">[00:47:02]</a>. This decision, driven by [[the_role_of_context_and_outcome_in_product_development | first principles]] thinking, asserts that "every support ticket is a failure of our product" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:07">[00:47:07]</a>. By holding the product team accountable for support issues, it fosters a focus on product quality and self-service solutions. The company also believes deep customer understanding and acting on feedback is a core value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:37">[00:47:37]</a>.

This structure incentivizes support agents to decrease ticket volume over time and increase "deflection" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:53">[00:47:53]</a>. This strategy has resulted in an extremely low contact rate; with over 400,000 users, Ramp maintains a support team of under 30 agents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:17">[00:48:17]</a>. This challenges industry benchmarks by prioritizing outcomes (reduced support tickets) over scaling traditional support functions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:08">[00:48:08]</a>.

In essence, Ramp's model demonstrates that by empowering teams with clear context, shielding them from distractions, and trusting in top-tier talent, organizations can achieve extraordinary velocity and impact.
