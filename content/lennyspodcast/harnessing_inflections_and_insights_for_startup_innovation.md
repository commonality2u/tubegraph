From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

According to veteran early-stage startup investor Mike Maples Jr., breakthrough startup ideas share three core elements: inflections, insights, and founder future fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>. Mike Maples Jr., a legendary early-stage startup investor and founder of Floodgate over 20 years ago, pioneered seed-stage investing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:06">[01:03:06]</a>. He has made early bets on transformative companies like Twitter, Lyft, Twitch, Okta, Rappi, and Applied Intuition, appearing on the Forbes Midas list eight times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:14">[01:14:14]</a>.

Maples' research into where great startup ideas come from began after realizing that 80% of his firm's biggest returning investments came from pivots, not original ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:22:00">[04:22:00]</a>. He observed that successful startups didn't always follow conventional best practices, and many that did still failed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:30:00">[04:30:00]</a>. This led him to uncover three ways that breakthrough founders think and act differently <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>.

Business is never a fair fight, as incumbents start with an inherent advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>. Startups win not by executing better, but by proposing a radically different future that disorients the incumbent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:00">[00:37:00]</a>. This involves fighting "unfair" by harnessing different powers than are obvious to most people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>.

## Elements of Breakthrough Startup Ideas

### Inflections
An inflection is an external event that creates the potential for radical change in how people think, feel, and act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>. It's a turning point where something new is introduced, enabling new empowerment for the first time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:00">[01:19:00]</a>.

**Examples of Inflections**:
*   **Technological**: The iPhone 4S shipping with a GPS locator chip enabled ride-sharing companies like Lyft, allowing riders and drivers to locate each other precisely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:00">[01:52:00]</a>. Similarly, improved smartphone cameras, increased smartphone adoption, widespread Wi-Fi connectivity, and faster upload speeds converged to enable Instagram <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:00">[01:17:00]</a>. The widespread adoption of large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT could be considered an inflection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>.
*   **Regulatory**: Changes in laws, such as those during the COVID-19 pandemic that legalized and reimbursed tele-medicine visits across state lines, created a significant inflection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:00">[02:14:00]</a>.
*   **Belief/Behavioral**: The pandemic also led to a permanent change in people's belief about and willingness to engage in tele-medicine visits and work from home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:00">[02:44:00]</a>.

Maples proposes a "stress test" for inflections:
1.  What is the specific new thing that was just introduced? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:36:00">[02:36:00]</a>
2.  How does it specifically empower specific people in specific ways? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:00">[02:44:00]</a>
3.  Under what conditions might that empowerment be realized or not realized? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:00">[02:47:00]</a>

Inflections are all around us, but most people don't notice them; they tend to follow established patterns and assume tomorrow will resemble yesterday <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:00">[01:31:00]</a>. Great founders are skilled at recognizing these turning points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>.

### Insights
Unlike inflections, insights come from the founders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>. An insight is a non-obvious truth about how one or more inflections can be harnessed to change people's behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:00">[02:17:00]</a>. For Lyft, the insight was "you could do Airbnb for cars" by leveraging the iPhone 4S's GPS <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:32:00">[02:32:00]</a>.

Key characteristics of insights:
*   **Non-consensus and Right**: For an idea to be a breakthrough, it must be non-consensus but still right <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:45:00">[02:45:00]</a>. If an idea is consensus, it's likely not radical enough and too similar to existing solutions or incumbents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:02:00">[03:02:00]</a>. The best startup ideas often face initial hostility or indifference, but resonate deeply with a small subset of people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:00">[03:26:00]</a>.
*   **Backcasting**: Great founders backcast, assuming a radically different future and working backward from it, rather than forecasting by extending the present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:07:00">[03:07:00]</a>.
*   **Importance of Surprise**: Breakthroughs cannot be created from a recipe; they are discovered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:00">[03:19:00]</a>. Founders should actively "savor surprises" and construct experiments to be surprised, as true learning comes from discoveries that challenge existing assumptions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>.
*   **Earned Secret**: Insights often come from "earned secrets" – knowledge gained by getting one's hands dirty and exploring something at the cutting edge for its own sake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:15:00">[04:15:00]</a>. This deep engagement allows founders to notice things others miss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:29:00">[04:29:00]</a>. This connects to the observation that 80% of Mike Maples' biggest investments came from a pivot, where continuous learning and adapting to new information led to success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:22:00">[04:22:00]</a>.
*   **Iteration and Product-Market Fit**: An insight needs to be correct, but the initial implementation might be wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. For example, Okta initially focused on cloud systems management, but after showing it to customers, they realized identity management was the top priority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:38:00">[04:38:00]</a>. Founders need to iterate with the right early believers to find product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>.

### Founder Future Fit
Founder future fit refers to the specific traits a founder possesses that make them ideally suited for a certain type of future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:23:00">[04:23:00]</a>.

*   **Intrinsic Motivation and Knowledge**: These founders have deep, intrinsic motivation to pursue a specific future and possess the best knowledge and network within that emerging ecosystem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37:00">[05:37:00]</a>.
*   **Beginner's Mind vs. Experience**: Sometimes, these are young, first-time founders (like Mark Andreessen or Mark Zuckerberg) who have a "beginner's mind" and aren't encumbered by old ways of thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:00">[04:42:00]</a>. Other times, like with Applied Intuition selling to car companies, founders with deep industry experience and credibility are better suited <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>.
*   **Authenticity**: Founder future fit is ultimately about authenticity—who is most authentically matched for that different, radical future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:28:00">[05:28:00]</a>. For example, Justin Kan of Justin.tv (which later became Twitch) was building the product he wished he had, even if it seemed like a terrible idea at first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:39:00">[05:39:00]</a>.

## Actions for Pattern Breaking
Thinking differently is only half the battle; founders must also act differently to persuade others to embrace their vision. This involves three key actions: movements, storytelling, and disagreeableness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.

### Movements
A movement is a different way of developing a market, leveraging a grievance of a minority against the tyranny of a majority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>. Early customers, employees, and investors are not motivated by pragmatism, but by belief in the higher purpose and aesthetically better future a movement promises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:00">[01:06:00]</a>.

**Examples of Movements**:
*   **Lyft**: The "pink mustache" and concept of ride-sharing created a movement by offering a new, communal experience as an alternative to traditional taxis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>.
*   **Floodgate**: Mike Maples Jr. and Ann Miura-Ko saw seed investing as a movement, aiming to establish seed funds as a permanent feature of the venture landscape when others didn't believe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:00">[01:57:00]</a>.
*   **Clarence Birdseye**: He started a movement to convince trains and supermarkets to adopt refrigerated infrastructure to enable the widespread consumption of flash-frozen foods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:00">[01:08:00]</a>.
*   **Tesla**: Their mission "accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" appeals to a higher purpose beyond just being a car company, differentiating them from traditional automakers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>.
*   **Airbnb**: Brian Chesky created a movement around "living like a local," turning the hotel industry's strength (consistent experience) into a weakness by offering a radically different, authentic travel experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>.

Movements create a stark dichotomy between the world that is and the world that could be, making early believers emotionally committed to moving to that different future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.

### Storytelling
Effective storytelling is crucial for attracting early believers. Startups should leverage the "Hero's Journey" framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>.

*   **Founder as Mentor**: The founder is the "Obi-Wan," not Luke Skywalker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>. The early believers (customers, employees, investors) are the heroes, and the founder's job is to tell a story that emotes them to join and co-create the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:00">[01:18:00]</a>.
*   **World That Is vs. World That Could Be**: Like [[steve_jobs_insights_on_product_innovation | Steve Jobs's iPhone launch speech]], compelling stories constantly draw a contrast between the current painful reality and the empowering, radically different future the startup offers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:00">[01:17:00]</a>.
*   **Tailored Narratives**: The story must be tailored to different audiences, as each group has a different "Hero's Journey" and definition of success (e.g., investors seeking financial returns, employees seeking impactful work, customers solving a critical problem) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:00">[01:20:00]</a>.
*   **Concise Pitching**: For early-stage pitches, Maples suggests:
    1.  **Slide 1**: Clearly state what you do (e.g., "We let you rent an extra room in your house," for Airbnb) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:00">[01:21:00]</a>.
    2.  **Slide 2**: Present your non-obvious insight (e.g., "We believe that 500,000 is the new 5 million because of lean startups" for Floodgate) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:00">[01:22:00]</a>. This sets the context for future discussions and handles objections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:00">[01:29:00]</a>.
    3.  **Slide 3**: Provide proof points (customers, team, early traction) to validate the insight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>.

### Disagreeableness
A startup is a fundamentally disagreeable act, representing a disagreement with the status quo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:00">[01:25:00]</a>. Founders must be willing to break free from the conformity trap and pursue their obsession, even if it means sacrificing status or being disliked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:00">[01:29:00]</a>.

*   **Courage to Be Disliked**: Great founders are often disagreeable; they listen to advice but believe a different truth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:00">[01:26:00]</a>. Examples like Bill Gates and Elon Musk show that high standards and an unwavering focus on mission can lead to greatness, even if it means not being universally liked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:00">[01:27:00]</a>.
*   **Embrace Chaos**: Internally, successful startups often operate in a state of "capitalist mutation" and ambiguity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:00">[01:31:00]</a>. This messiness is normal for movements and dynamic growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:00">[01:32:00]</a>.
*   **Willingness to Fail**: The willingness to take risks and potentially fail is what enables breakthrough success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>. Asymmetric upside requires a willingness to fail in a big way <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:00">[01:39:00]</a>.

## Practical Advice for Early-Stage Founders
For those looking to generate startup ideas or already building a company, Mike Maples Jr. offers [[practical_advice_for_earlystage_founders | practical advice for early-stage founders]] drawn from [[lessons_from_successful_founders_and_startups | lessons from successful Founders and startups]] and [[challenges_and_strategies_for_creating_successful_startups | challenges and strategies for creating successful startups]].

*   **"Get Out of the Present"**: Great ideas come from "living in the future" – immersing oneself in emerging trends and technologies, noticing what's missing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:52:00">[05:52:00]</a>. For example, Mati Hall, founder of Living Carbon, spent a year as Sam Altman's chief of staff, actively sampling different futures before finding her idea for genetically modified trees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:32:00">[05:32:00]</a>.
*   **Identify Lighthouse Customers**: Seek out "lighthouse customers" who are already living in the future and actively adopting new technologies or behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>. Solving their unique problems can lead to solutions that eventually become universally desired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:00">[05:53:00]</a>.
*   **Force a Choice, Not a Comparison**: Don't aim to be a "10 times better apple"; instead, be "the world's first banana" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. The product should be so unique that customers are forced to choose it based on its inherent value, rather than comparing it to existing solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:00">[01:59:00]</a>. Find customers desperate for your unique offering, even if it's imperfect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:00">[03:00:00]</a>.

## Applying Principles to Large Companies
The principles of pattern breaking can also be applied within large organizations to foster [[inventiveness_and_innovation_in_business | inventiveness and innovation]].
*   **Treat Breakthrough Ideas Differently**: Don't expect new, pattern-breaking ideas to immediately contribute significantly to the core business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:00">[01:35:00]</a>.
*   **Create Autonomy and Obscurity**: Like Lockheed's Skunk Works or IBM's PC division, new ventures should be separate and autonomous, led by Mavericks, and initially kept less visible from the main organization to avoid being pulled back into "pattern-matching corporation" behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:00">[01:37:00]</a>. This aligns with [[effective_team_building_and_leadership_in_startups | effective team building and leadership]] by allowing teams to operate outside the constraints of traditional corporate structures.
*   **Embrace Failure**: Allocate a portion of profits (e.g., 10%) to projects likely to fail but with wildly asymmetric upside <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>. The willingness to fail is essential for breakthrough success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>.

Founders often face a lonely existence, as most people may not initially like their ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:00">[01:39:00]</a>. However, this doesn't mean the idea is wrong; it just means the founder has the courage to pursue a vision that others don't yet see <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:00">[01:40:00]</a>.
