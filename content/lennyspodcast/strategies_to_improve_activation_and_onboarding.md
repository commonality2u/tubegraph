From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Improving user activation and onboarding is a critical initial focus for startups aiming for sustainable growth. Often, challenges in retention are more a function of poor onboarding to the right user experience than tactical efforts to improve retention itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:59">[01:39:59]</a>.

## The Sean Ellis Test: A Leading Indicator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:09">[01:11:09]</a>

The [[strategies_for_finding_and_retaining_satisfied_customers | Sean Ellis Test]], also known as the Product-Market Fit test, is a simple question designed to determine if a product is a "must-have" for users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:13">[03:36:13]</a>. Users are given choices: "very disappointed," "somewhat disappointed," or "not disappointed/not applicable" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:41:40">[03:41:40]</a>.

The goal is to find users who would be "very disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:19">[03:54:19]</a>. A high percentage of users (commonly cited as 40% or more) indicating "very disappointed" is a leading indicator of product-market fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:39:10">[06:39:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:11:03">[07:11:03]</a>. This question helps assess resonance even with a first Minimum Viable Product (MVP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:58:04">[08:58:04]</a>.

### When to Use the Test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:07:37">[09:07:37]</a>

The test is ideally administered to a random sample of users who have:
*   Really used the product, not just signed up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:40">[02:22:40]</a>.
*   Hit an activation moment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:49">[02:47:49]</a>.
*   Used it two or more times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:49">[02:47:49]</a>.
*   Used it recently (e.g., within the last week or two) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:58">[02:57:58]</a>.
A sample size of at least 30 responses is recommended for reliable data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:30:17">[03:30:17]</a>.

### Interpreting and Acting on Results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:48:40">[03:48:40]</a>

*   **Focus on the "Very Disappointed" Users**: The most crucial step is to deeply understand the segment of users who would be "very disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:31">[01:50:31]</a>.
    *   **Identify Primary Benefit**: Ask "What is the primary benefit that you get?" from the product. Initially, this can be an open-ended question to crowdsource different benefits. Then, convert it into a multiple-choice question for a subsequent survey to force users to pick one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:30">[01:51:30]</a>.
    *   **Understand *Why* it's Important**: Follow up with "Why is that benefit important to you?" This provides critical context, revealing the underlying problem the product solves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:35">[01:53:35]</a>. For example, users of an email product said, "I'm drowning in email" when asked why finding things faster was important, which helped create effective marketing hooks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:00">[01:59:00]</a>.
*   **Don't Dilute for "Somewhat Disappointed" Users**: Initially, Sean Ellis suggested ignoring users who would be "somewhat disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:07">[00:27:07]</a>. This group views the product as a "nice-to-have" and focusing on their feedback might dilute the product's core value for the "must-have" users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:08">[00:30:08]</a>.
*   **Superhuman's Nuance**: Superhuman found a way to leverage feedback from "somewhat disappointed" users without diluting the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:23:05">[04:23:05]</a>. They focused on "somewhat disappointed" users who were also interested in the *same core benefit* as the "must-have" users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:29:05">[04:29:05]</a>. They then asked what was missing to make the product a "must-have" for them, staying true to the core benefit while trying to move fence-sitters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:04">[04:42:04]</a>.

## Strategies to Improve Activation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:02">[00:54:02]</a>

### 1. Focus on "Speed to Value" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:26">[01:42:26]</a>
Reducing the time it takes for a user to experience the core value of the product is paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:29:00">[05:29:00]</a>.
*   **Lookout Example**: When mobile security company Lookout had only 7% of users indicating "very disappointed," they analyzed the feedback and found that the 7% highly valued the antivirus functionality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:51:00">[09:51:00]</a>.
    *   **Repositioning**: They repositioned the product to focus on antivirus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:00">[01:57:00]</a>. This created a filter, attracting users who specifically cared about antivirus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:00">[01:59:00]</a>.
    *   **Streamlined Onboarding**: They streamlined the onboarding so the first action users took was setting up antivirus and receiving an "You're now protected" message <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>. This combination of "set the right expectations" and "speed to value" increased the "very disappointed" score to 40% in just two weeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.
*   **LogMeIn Example**: At LogMeIn, 95% of users signing up for free remote access never performed a remote control session, severely limiting monetization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:49:00">[05:49:00]</a>. A complete freeze on product development for three months was implemented, with all product, engineering, design, and marketing teams focusing solely on improving the sign-up to usage rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:54:00">[05:54:00]</a>. This effort increased the usage rate by 1,000% (from 5% to 50%) in three months, leading to a massive increase in profitable acquisition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>.

### 2. Understand and Address Friction Points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:00">[05:53:00]</a>
*   **Ask "Why?"**: When facing high drop-off rates (e.g., 90% at the download step for LogMeIn), directly ask users who bounced why they didn't complete the step <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>. This qualitative insight (e.g., "This seemed too good to be true, I didn't believe this was free") can reveal unexpected skepticism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>.
*   **Increase Desire, Reduce Friction**: These are the two primary levers for driving conversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:00">[01:36:00]</a>. Marketing should create the right desire, and onboarding should eliminate friction points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>. Remind users of the benefits they will receive along the way <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>.

### 3. Define the "Aha Moment" and Activation Metric <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>
*   **Qualitative Start**: Begin by qualitatively determining when users have had a "good enough experience" to understand the product's value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:00">[03:00:00]</a>.
*   **Correlation to Retention**: Look for a metric that correlates with long-term retention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:00">[03:19:00]</a>.
*   **Actionability**: The chosen activation moment should be something that can be experienced early in the user journey, ideally in the first session or day, to be actionable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>. If it's too far down the user experience (e.g., after 100 uses), it's not very actionable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:15:00">[04:15:00]</a>.
*   **Example**: For LogMeIn, the "aha moment" was a user completing a remote control session <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:00">[03:09:00]</a>. For Eventbrite, it could be weekly tickets sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:18:00">[04:18:00]</a>.

### 4. Optimize the Entire "Value Delivery Engine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:00">[04:47:00]</a>
Beyond initial activation, optimize the entire journey to deliver value repeatedly:
*   **Engagement Loops**: How often do users return and use the product? The "Hooked model" can provide a framework for improving engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:34:00">[03:34:00]</a>.
*   **Referral Loops**: If the product already has strong word-of-mouth, a referral program with incentives for both referrer and referee can accelerate growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:12:00">[04:12:00]</a>. However, a referral program cannot fix a product that people don't already want to talk about <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:19:00">[04:19:00]</a>.
*   **Freemium Strategy**: For a freemium model to work, the free product must be so good that it naturally generates word-of-mouth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>. The premium product then needs to be sufficiently better and differentiated to encourage upgrades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>.
*   **Northstar Metric**: A Northstar Metric captures "units of value being delivered" to the customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:59:00">[04:59:00]</a>. It should:
    *   Reflect the "must-have" value identified by the [[strategies_for_finding_and_retaining_satisfied_customers | Sean Ellis Test]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:51:00">[05:51:00]</a>.
    *   Be an aggregate number that can consistently go "up and to the right" over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a>.
    *   Correlate to revenue growth, but not *be* revenue itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:47:00">[06:47:00]</a>. For example, Airbnb used "Nights Booked" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:50:00">[06:50:00]</a> and Amazon uses "monthly purchases" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:51:00">[06:51:00]</a>.
    *   Be time-capped (e.g., daily active users, weekly rides) to encourage regular engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:22:00">[07:22:00]</a>.

### 5. Leverage Qualitative and Quantitative Research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:11:00">[06:11:00]</a>
*   **Balanced Approach**: Effective [[strategies_for_finding_and_retaining_satisfied_customers | customer research]] combines both qualitative insights (from surveys, interviews) and quantitative data (analytics, A/B testing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:16:00">[06:16:00]</a>. Qualitative insights inform better experiments, while quantitative data validates impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:19:00">[06:19:00]</a>.
*   **Ask How Users Found You**: Ask users: "How did you find this product?" and "How do you typically find products like this?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a> This can reveal viable [[networking_strategies_and_benefits | customer acquisition channels]] and user behavior patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:39:00">[06:39:00]</a>.
*   **Cross-Functional Collaboration**: Moving beyond just customer acquisition to focus on activation, engagement, and retention requires collaboration between marketing, product, engineering, and sales teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:18:00">[07:18:00]</a>. This cross-functional alignment is crucial for an effective testing program across the entire growth engine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:26:00">[07:26:26]</a>.

### 6. The Right Questions at the Right Time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:31:00">[07:31:00]</a>
Ultimately, solving growth challenges often comes down to asking the right questions at the opportune moment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:33:00">[07:33:00]</a>. Instead of jumping to solutions, focus on understanding "why" users behave a certain way or encounter specific problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:38:00">[07:38:00]</a>.
