Nancy Duarte, a best-selling author, speaker, and CEO of Duarte Incorporated, emphasizes that [[the_importance_of_storytelling_in_business | effective storytelling]] and presentation skills are crucial in various contexts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>. ## Core Principles for Effective Presentations

Nancy Duarte outlines three essential tips for creating impactful presentations:

1.  **Make the Audience the Hero** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:11">[00:17:11]</a>
2.  **Infuse Your Talk with Story** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:16">[00:17:16]</a>
3.  **Ask Yourself: "Can They See What I'm Saying?"** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:20">[00:17:20]</a>

### 1. The Audience as the Hero: An Empathy-Centric Approach

[[effective_storytelling_in_interviews | Empathy]] is at the heart of Duarte's methodology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:40">[00:17:40]</a>. She states that it is crucial to always consider "Who am I speaking with?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:30">[00:18:30]</a>.

In a presentation, the audience holds the power, as they choose whether to accept or reject an idea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:05">[00:19:05]</a>. The presenter's role, therefore, is akin to a mentor in myths and movies, who comes alongside the hero (the audience) to help them overcome challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:16">[00:19:16]</a>. This means addressing both the audience's internal conflicts (providing soothing information) and their external actions (the call to action) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:48">[00:19:48]</a>.

For instance, Duarte Incorporated conducts listening tours, including surveys and interviews, before major internal presentations to understand employee perspectives and align with their goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:56">[00:20:56]</a>. This allows for gap analysis between the current state and what will be asked of them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:05">[00:21:05]</a>.

### 2. Infusing Presentations with Story

[[how_storytelling_creates_memorable_communication | Storytelling]] goes beyond just anecdotes; it involves utilizing inherent story structures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:54">[00:23:54]</a>. Research shows that when a story is told, the speaker's and listener's brains fire in the same order and place, creating alignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:16">[00:24:16]</a>.

A key [[techniques_for_better_storytelling | technique for better storytelling]] is the "what is, what could be, new bliss" framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:52">[00:26:52]</a>.
*   Begin by stating "what is" – the current reality or problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:02">[00:27:02]</a>.
*   Then, transition to "what could be" – the imagined future state or solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:04">[00:27:04]</a>.
*   This creates a pattern of tension (what is) and release (what could be), building longing for the future state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:34">[00:27:34]</a>.
*   The final "new bliss" paints a picture of a world with the idea adopted, serving as a happy ending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>.

This structure works for various contexts, from major speeches (like Steve Jobs's iPhone launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:21">[00:28:21]</a> and the Gettysburg Address <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:59">[00:30:59]</a>) to casual conversations and influence moments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:22">[00:31:22]</a>. For instance, explaining the current state of busy schedules and the benefit of doggy daycare (what could be) can lead to a spouse assisting with chores (new bliss) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:19">[00:33:19]</a>.

### 3. "Can They See What I'm Saying?" The Role of Visuals

For the audience to truly grasp the message, presenters must help them visualize it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:20">[00:36:20]</a>. This can involve:
*   **Visual Tools:** Utilizing presentation software with intentional graphics and data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:24">[00:36:24]</a>.
*   **Live Sketching/Drawing:** In meetings, drawing on a whiteboard or napkin can create immediate alignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:43">[00:37:43]</a>. It helps everyone see the same steps and insights, preventing questions and ensuring clarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:07">[00:38:07]</a>.
*   **One Point Per Slide:** Each slide should convey a single idea that supports the overall "big idea" of the presentation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:38">[00:41:38]</a>. This prevents information overload <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:03">[00:42:03]</a>.
*   **Slide Docs:** For circulating presentations via email without a presenter, slide docs include more words, stronger pictures, and potentially a large appendix to provide all necessary context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:53">[00:42:53]</a>.

It's important to adapt to the audience's communication culture; some audiences may prefer more dense slides, while others respond better to cinematic visuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:12">[00:49:12]</a>.

## Evolution of Presentations

Historically, presentations involved 35mm slides in carousels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:52">[01:11:52]</a>. Apple was a pioneer in connecting computers to projectors at scale, albeit with initial black-and-white, bit-mapped visuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:02">[01:12:02]</a>.

Nancy Duarte's company pushed the boundaries of early digital presentations, even when graphic designers dismissed the Macintosh as a "toy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:59">[01:09:59]</a>. A pivotal moment was a 1992 sales conference where a slide with a single, hot-pink "BIG" word on a black background dramatically lit up the audience's faces, demonstrating the power of simple, impactful visuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:13">[01:13:13]</a>.

## Overcoming Nerves

Public speaking often triggers a fight-or-flight instinct <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:45">[00:56:45]</a>. Nancy Duarte suggests several [[public_speaking_tips_for_storytellers | public speaking tips]] to manage nerves:
*   **Visualize the Audience:** Sit in the audience, look at the stage, and imagine friendly, smiling faces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:00">[00:57:00]</a>.
*   **Breathing Exercises:** Practice deep, slow breaths <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:50">[00:59:50]</a>.
*   **Change Your Chemical State:** Watch short, funny videos or listen to transportive music right before going on stage to induce laughter and shift your body's chemistry from nervous to calm <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:26">[00:58:26]</a>.
*   **Focus on Content:** Avoid distractions in the green room and focus on the message <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:12">[00:58:12]</a>.

## Movements and Torchbearers

Presentations are often part of larger [[the_role_of_storytelling_movements_and_disagreeableness_in_startup_success | movements]] aimed at guiding people toward an alternate future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:42">[01:01:42]</a>. These movements typically follow a five-act story structure:
1.  **Dream:** Verbalize the future destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:05">[01:02:05]</a>.
2.  **Leap:** The audience chooses to commit to the journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:09">[01:03:09]</a>.
3.  **Fight & Climb:** The messy middle, where challenges and roadblocks are overcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:29">[01:03:29]</a>. This ongoing struggle requires emotional fuel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:06">[01:04:06]</a>.
4.  **Arrive:** Reaching the desired outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:56">[01:03:56]</a>.

Leaders act as "torchbearers" in these movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:26">[01:02:26]</a>. Like a torch in a cave, they may only see a few feet ahead, but it's enough to dissipate fear for those following <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:37">[01:02:37]</a>. Throughout these phases, leaders must use speeches, stories, ceremonies, and symbols to provide the necessary emotional fuel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:58">[01:03:58]</a>.

## Storytelling in Product Management

[[product_management_and_storytelling | Storytelling]] plays a significant role in [[role_of_storytelling_and_drawing_in_product_management | product management]] and development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:45">[01:07:45]</a>. A notable example is Airbnb's "Snow White" project <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:01">[01:08:01]</a>. To understand their customers, the Airbnb team hired an illustrator to map out a customer's "day in the life" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:12">[01:08:12]</a>. By literally walking through the customer's shoes (e.g., "her alarm goes off, what happens next?"), they discovered a critical strategic flaw and realized the need to pivot to a mobile-first approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:23">[01:08:23]</a>. These visualized keyframes became a central part of the company's strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:36">[01:10:36]</a>.

Similarly, product teams can use storytelling to convey meaning and passion behind their creations, ensuring their product is understood and adopted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:46">[01:09:46]</a>.

## Adapting to Remote Audiences

[[adapting_presentations_for_remote_audiences | Remote presentations]] require specific considerations:
*   **Eye Contact:** Presenters should practice looking directly into the camera lens to maintain virtual eye contact with the audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:30">[00:53:30]</a>.
*   **Presence:** Without the ability to see hands, body language, or room presence, speakers need to adapt their communication to convey information effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:09">[00:54:09]</a>.

The shift to remote and hybrid work environments has led to new challenges in communication, such as remote participants struggling to have their voices heard when mixed with in-room attendees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:11">[00:55:11]</a>.

## Final Advice

To become a better presenter, choose a topic you are deeply passionate about <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:20">[01:15:20]</a>. By working on a talk that makes you feel passionate, you will learn to present from your "soul," and this feeling can then be tapped into for less passionate business presentations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:45">[01:15:45]</a>. Continuously working on communication skills and clarity benefits everyone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:39">[01:16:39]</a>.
