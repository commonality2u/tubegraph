From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Notebook LM, described as one of the "most delightful and inspiring new AI products out there" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:47">[01:04:47]</a>, was incubated within Google Labs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:46">[01:07:46]</a>. Risa Martin, the Product Lead for Notebook LM, shared insights into its origins, team structure, and unique development approach within Google.

## Origins and Early Team
Notebook LM began as a "20% project" at Google, though Risa Martin noted it was "way more than that" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>. The initial concept, known internally as "talk to small Corpus," was about using a large language model (LLM) to interact with a specific piece of content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>.

The project started with a very small team: Risa Martin, an engineer, and eventually Stephen Johnson <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>. Initially, only the engineer working on the core technology was full-time, with others "chiming in" on the side <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:03">[00:07:03]</a>. When it was first announced as Project Tailwind, the team had only three engineers, a designer, Risa, and Stephen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>. This lean team grew to about eight engineers by the time of the interview <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>.

## Incubation within Google Labs
The product's development environment within Google Labs was crucial to its unique process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:07">[02:10:07]</a>. Google Labs itself is a relatively new division, established only about three years prior to the interview <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:23">[01:53:23]</a>. Risa joined Labs because her former boss, Josh Woodward (VP of Google Labs), started it with a mission to "ship AI products and... build businesses out of them" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:48">[02:08:48]</a>. Having previously worked at startups, Risa was keen to undertake a "zero to one" project again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:09">[02:07:09]</a>.

## The 'Startup-like' Operational Model
Unlike traditional Google product development, the Notebook LM team operates with significantly fewer processes, enabling them to move quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:49">[02:10:49]</a>. They would often have meetings with product managers, engineers, and designers simultaneously, "cranking on the mocks and the PRDs at the same time" and even beginning implementation during meetings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:55">[02:21:55]</a>.

Key aspects of this startup-like approach include:
*   **Clear Expectations**: Senior leadership provided clear mandates to operate differently, bypassing regular pre-roll processes and building in public <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:04:47">[02:04:47]</a>.
*   **Small Team**: The deliberately small team was seen as key to their agility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:05">[02:21:05]</a>.
*   **Public Engagement**: The team actively engaged with users through channels like Discord, which now boasts about 60,000 members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:43">[02:29:43]</a>, and Twitter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:08">[02:26:08]</a>. This contrasts with Google's traditional internal communication methods, leading to initial skepticism from some within Google about using external platforms like Discord <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:37">[02:37:37]</a>.

## Technology and the [[role_of_ai_and_content_studio_in_notebook_lm | Content Studio]]
Notebook LM is powered by Google's Gemini 1.5 Pro model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:04">[02:18:04]</a>, with a powerful audio model layered on top for features like the audio overview (podcast generation) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:23:43">[02:23:43]</a>.

The "real secret sauce" that makes the product "really good" is a proprietary component called [[role_of_ai_and_content_studio_in_notebook_lm | Content Studio]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:55">[02:29:55]</a>. [[role_of_ai_and_content_studio_in_notebook_lm | Content Studio]] is designed to take an "opinionated approach" to the user's content, making it as helpful as possible by providing summaries and generating new content with a single button click <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:55">[02:33:55]</a>. Usama, a "super talented engineer" on the team, is credited as the "real Craftsman" behind [[role_of_ai_and_content_studio_in_notebook_lm | Content Studio]] and its ability to create relatable and engaging outputs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:40:47">[02:40:47]</a>.

For the audio overview feature, the team continuously refined the audio model through extensive listening to ensure natural inflections, laughter, and interruptions, aiming for a delightful and magical experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:39">[02:51:39]</a>.

## Key Influences: Stephen Johnson
Stephen Johnson, a New York Times best-selling author and speaker with 14 books to his name <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:47">[03:40:47]</a>, played a significant role in shaping Notebook LM. Risa Martin initially viewed him as "the product" herself, learning from his curious nature, respectful approach, and extensive ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:35">[03:25:35]</a>.

His workflow, particularly his meticulous research and ability to "de-densify information," inspired the team to "make people really really good at de-densifying information" through technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:39:55">[03:39:55]</a>. Although Johnson's personal workflows (e.g., 8,000 quotes in Readwise) were "extreme" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:48:07">[03:48:07]</a>, the goal was to distill that power for "everyday people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:35">[03:59:35]</a>. Despite occasional disagreements, Risa and Stephen always reached alignment on the next steps, which was crucial for product development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:39:24">[04:39:24]</a>.

## Future Vision and Continued Development
The long-term vision for Notebook LM is to become an "AI editor surface" that is "fully remixable, any input any output" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:35">[02:50:35]</a>. This aligns with the broader goal of [[building_productivity_tools_with_a_vision | building productivity tools with a vision]] that allow users to transform any input (video, audio, emails, social media) into various outputs (blog posts, tutorials, chatbots) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:35">[02:51:35]</a>. This embodies the idea of [[the_journey_of_building_a_horizontal_software_platform | building a horizontal software platform]] that adapts to user needs across different media <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:01:21">[03:01:21]</a>.

More tactically, the team is focused on bringing Notebook LM to mobile, aiming to create a distinct and interesting mobile experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:10">[02:54:10]</a>. They are also exploring how to give users more control over the output, beyond simple "knobs" or sliders, to maintain the product's "magical and delightful" feel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:54">[03:00:54]</a>.

### Red Teaming and Safety
Google has "huge teams" dedicated to red teaming Notebook LM, testing for as many potential issues as possible to ensure the product's safety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:07">[04:26:07]</a>. While unexpected uses may arise, the team aims to respond publicly and learn from them, adding new test cases as needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:29:56">[04:29:56]</a>. A notable instance involved AI-generated hosts in an audio overview appearing to realize they were AI, which was later clarified to be based on user-provided show notes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:41:20">[04:41:20]</a>.

### User Engagement and Feedback
The team is committed to continuous learning from users, actively encouraging feedback through their Discord server and X (Twitter) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:34:07">[04:34:07]</a>. This feedback loop is essential for [[improving_developer_experience | improving the developer experience]] and ensuring the product evolves to meet user needs, particularly for educators, learners, and professionals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:07">[04:57:07]</a>.
