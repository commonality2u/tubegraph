## From Policy to Pixels: A Pivot to Consumer Apps

Before his notable success in [[consumer_app_development_and_growth_tactics | consumer app development]], Beer's first major product was "Potify," a web app built during his college years that helped voters understand the financial impact of political policies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:36:06">[06:36:06]</a>. This app went "super viral," attracting around four million users during an election season <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:40:19">[07:40:19]</a>. This initial success led to a spin-off company, Outline, which secured government contracts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:00:26">[08:00:26]</a>. However, a government shutdown and a realization that he disliked [[sales_strategies_for_startups | selling software to governments]] prompted a significant pivot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:01">[08:42:01]</a>.

He decided to focus on [[consumer_app_development_and_growth_tactics | consumer apps]], a field where his core competency of "making things that go viral on the internet" could shine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:58:34">[08:58:34]</a>. This pivot involved a four-to-five-year period of intense experimentation, during which his team, Midnight Labs, built approximately 15 "duds" across various categories like mapping, chat, and event apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:30:26">[09:30:26]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:04:47">[10:04:47]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:04:02">[15:04:02]</a>. This iterative process was crucial for [[building_and_evolving_a_software_product | building a muscle]] to understand user desires and [[strategies_for_effective_marketing_in_a_tech_startup | how to make things grow]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:37:05">[10:37:05]</a>.

## The Strategic Focus on Teens

A key insight emerged from their extensive experimentation: teens are the ideal demographic for building viral social apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:47:00">[10:47:00]</a>. This is due to several factors:
*   **Malleable Habits**: As people age, their habits with communication products become fixed, making them less likely to adopt new apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:55:00">[10:55:00]</a>.
*   **High Invitation Rate**: Adults don't invite people to new apps at the same rate as teens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:13:00">[11:13:00]</a>. Beer observed that "the number of invitations sent per user drops 20% for every additional year of age from 13 to 18" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:58:00">[11:58:00]</a>.
*   **Daily Interaction**: Teens see each other every day, facilitating organic spread within schools and social circles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:28:00">[11:28:00]</a>. This creates a higher "urgency to connect" and add new social connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:05:00">[18:05:00]</a>.

For companies targeting older cohorts, significant venture capital and advertising are often required for user acquisition, and achieving network effects becomes "basically impossible" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:31:00">[12:31:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:01:00">[17:01:00]</a>.

## TBH: A Viral Phenomenon

### The Core Insight and Product-Market Fit
The idea for TBH (To Be Honest) stemmed from observing "latent demand" â€“ people trying to achieve a specific value through a "very distortive process" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:39:00">[26:39:00]</a>. Beer noticed two key trends:
1.  **Snapchat TBH Trend**: Teens were playing a game on Snapchat where they'd post emojis and receive positive feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:24:00">[25:24:00]</a>.
2.  **Saraha's Popularity**: The number one app in the U.S. App Store was Saraha, an anonymous messaging app, despite its entire interface being in Arabic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:06:00">[26:06:06]</a>. This was a "strongest signal that you could ever have that people want something" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:32:00">[26:32:00]</a>.

The crucial insight was that people wanted positive affirmations without the bullying prevalent in other anonymous apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:22:00">[27:22:00]</a>. TBH addressed this by only allowing positive responses through pre-authored polls (e.g., "who has the best smile?") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:37:00">[27:37:00]</a>. This led to "explosive adoption," with one school sending 450,000 messages in seven days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:33:00">[28:33:00]</a>.

### [[strategies_for_effective_marketing_in_a_tech_startup | Growth Tactics]] and Scaling Challenges
TBH's initial spread involved seeding the app into a specific school in Georgia that had the earliest start date <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:14:00">[21:14:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:17:00">[21:17:00]</a>. Their testing strategy involved saturating a localized area with various [[strategies_for_effective_marketing_in_a_tech_startup | marketing efforts]], including targeted ads and a dedicated Instagram account that followed students whose bios indicated their school affiliation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:56:00">[29:56:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:14:00">[30:14:00]</a>. > "If your product's working, you'll know. And if there's any uncertainty, it's not working." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:26:00">[36:26:00]</a>

Beer also noted the difficulty for large companies to launch truly innovative zero-to-one products because it's hard to "verbalize" or "align the company" around ideas that aren't already validated by market signals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:19:00">[41:19:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:57:00">[44:57:00]</a>. ## Gas: A Reinvention in a New Climate

After leaving Facebook, Beer was motivated to revisit the anonymous polling app concept, partly to answer the question of whether TBH could have been more successful if monetized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:21:00">[56:21:00]</a>. He and a new team rebuilt the app, naming it "Gas" (originally "Crush," which boys were reluctant to invite friends to due to its feminine branding) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:54:00">[59:54:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:50">[01:15:50]</a>.

Despite similar concepts, Gas was a "zero to one development cycle" in terms of [[building_and_evolving_a_software_product | growth systems]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:32:00">[59:32:00]</a>. Regulatory changes meant they couldn't send text invites from servers as with TBH; invites now had to be sent from the user's device, requiring a complete redesign of the invite flow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="58:28:00">[58:28:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:16:00">[59:16:00]</a>. This involved nine launches and a total reinvention of how the product would spread <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:22:00">[59:22:00]</a>.

### Combating a Human Trafficking Hoax
Gas faced an unusual and severe challenge: a widespread hoax claiming the app was used for human trafficking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:42">[01:04:42]</a>. This phenomenon, which can plague any viral app, leads to negative attention and account deletions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:04">[01:05:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:22">[01:07:22]</a>. Beer and his team fought the hoax relentlessly:
*   Meeting with journalists to ensure headlines clarified the hoax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:28">[01:06:28]</a>.
*   Calling school superintendents and police chiefs to retract public warnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:51">[01:06:51]</a>.
*   Getting false App Store reviews removed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:02">[01:07:02]</a>.
*   Having his girlfriend create a TikTok video debunking the claims, which was shown to users attempting to delete their accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:10">[01:07:10]</a>. This effort reduced daily account deletions from 3% to 0.1% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:22">[01:07:22]</a>.

Gas became a "blowout success," growing larger than TBH, generating significant sales (with 10 million downloads), and running almost entirely on startup credits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:04">[01:10:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:13">[01:11:13]</a>. It was eventually sold to Discord <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:23">[01:11:23]</a>.

## Core Lessons for Building Viral Consumer Apps

Beer's extensive experience has yielded several key insights:

### 1. Reproducible Testing Process
It's crucial to "develop a reproducible testing process" to maximize the probability of success, given the unpredictability of consumer products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:11">[01:19:11]</a>. This involves:
*   **Segmented Validation**: Validate core user flow, then spread within peer groups, then spread across peer groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:52">[01:11:52]</a>.
*   **100% Execution on Core**: "Execute at 100% for the thing you're trying to validate at that specific stage" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:13">[01:12:13]</a>, even if it requires manual, unscalable work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:20">[01:32:20]</a>. This eliminates confounding variables and provides clear signal on what's working <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:50">[01:31:50]</a>.

### 2. Prioritize Time to Value and the "Aha Moment"
In 2024, user attention spans are extremely short, around three seconds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:27">[01:27:27]</a>. Products must demonstrate value immediately to avoid churn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:41">[01:27:41]</a>. This concept, "inverting the time to value," was critical for the success of Dupe.com, a shopping app that allowed users to find the cheapest product version by simply typing "dp.com/" in front of a URL <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:02">[01:27:02]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:40">[01:28:40]</a>.

This requires "extraordinarily creative" use of available tools and APIs, including unconventional methods, to activate a user in as few taps as possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:23">[01:30:23]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:06">[01:31:06]</a>.

### 3. Live Chat for Feedback and User Experience
Integrating "live chat customer support" 24 hours a day directly into the app provides a "white glove experience" for users and eliminates uncertainty about problem-solving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:45">[01:32:45]</a>. More importantly, it's the best "vehicle for getting feedback" and [[cocreating_products_with_early_users | doing user research]], as users will directly communicate their problems and ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:16">[01:33:16]</a>.

### 4. Align Marketing and Product Growth
Beer stresses that marketing and in-product growth mechanisms are intertwined and should be treated as the same <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:55">[01:33:55]</a>. Everything from ads to the in-app experience and invitation flows must be aligned to facilitate user acquisition and flywheel spin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:55">[01:33:55]</a>.

### 5. Ethical Growth Systems
He adheres to a "Gia hypothesis" for the internet: if you treat users poorly by using their data unethically or inviting people on their behalf without consent, the internet will "come back and get even" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:25">[01:01:25]</a>. Therefore, always operate "above board" with how growth systems are designed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:08">[01:02:08]</a>.

### 6. Impact of iOS 18 Contact Permissions
The upcoming iOS 18 contact permission changes will make it significantly harder to find and invite friends on apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:02">[01:24:02]</a>. The new flow, which requires users to manually select contacts from an alphabetical list, is predicted to make social graph building "very difficult" and "entrench incumbents even more" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:33">[01:25:33]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:28">[01:26:28]</a>. This creates an opportunity for new apps to "reinvent what contact sync is" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:51">[01:25:51]</a>.

## Viral Hits vs. Durable Products

Beer acknowledges that building a "durable consumer app" with lasting network effects is a "Black Swan event," happening roughly "one every decade" due to the tremendous randomness involved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:50">[01:18:50]</a>. However, he asserts that "growing a product can be a science," and a skilled individual can "make an app grow and go viral" with certainty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:26">[01:19:26]</a>.

His personal motivation lies in the "thrilling drug" of building something from scratch and watching it "take over the Internet," rather than managing a large, long-term organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:50">[01:19:50]</a>. While he doesn't rule out building a venture-scale business one day, he values the quality of life and financial benefits of his current approach, which yields comparable proceeds to some IPOs for significantly less time commitment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:10">[01:22:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:32">[01:22:32]</a>.
