The distinction between [[the_role_of_product_managers | product managers]] and project managers is crucial for understanding effective product development. According to Marty Cagan, a common issue within companies is the misinterpretation and misapplication of these roles, leading to what he terms "product management theater" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>.

## Over-hiring and Misaligned Roles

Many companies over-hired during the pandemic, leading to a proliferation of roles like agile coaches, [[the_role_of_a_product_owner_versus_a_product_manager | product owners]], product ops, and business analysts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:03">[00:15:03]</a>. These roles, while seemingly related to product, can sometimes function more like project management, delivering output rather than outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:20">[00:00:20]</a>.

Cagan notes that many individuals in positions titled "product manager" are, in essence, simply project managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>. These individuals are often "dramatically overpaid for the value they provide" because their role is fundamentally a project management one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:02">[00:20:02]</a>.

> "People that aren't real product managers they're dramatically overpaid for the value they provide because it's a project management role" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>

## Feature Teams vs. Empowered Product Teams

A key differentiator lies in the type of team structure:

### Feature Teams (or Feature Factories)
*   **Focus**: Given a roadmap of *output* (features or projects) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:07">[00:21:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:40">[00:38:40]</a>.
*   **Tasks**: Asked to design, build, test, and deploy specific features, often with given dates and timeframes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:25">[00:21:25]</a>.
*   **PM Role**: In a feature team, a dedicated [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]] is often unnecessary because it's a project management role, and other team members (like senior engineers) can often cover the administrative aspects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:16">[00:19:16]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:18">[00:28:18]</a>. The value brought by a [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]] in such a team is minimal, leading engineers and designers to prefer handling tasks themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:30">[00:19:30]</a>.

### Empowered Product Teams
*   **Focus**: Given *problems to solve* (customer or business problems), typically one or two per quarter, in addition to maintenance work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:50">[00:21:50]</a>.
*   **Measure of Success**: Solving the problem and delivering value, rather than simply shipping a feature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:07">[00:22:07]</a>.
*   **PM Role**: A "real" [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]] is essential here <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:06">[00:20:06]</a>. They are responsible for ensuring the solution is valuable and viable (works for the business) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:10">[00:23:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:55">[00:25:55]</a>. This requires a different set of skills that engineers and designers typically do not possess <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:19">[00:23:19]</a>.

### Output vs. Outcomes
A fundamental difference is the shift from delivering *output* (features, projects) to delivering *outcomes* (solving problems, creating value) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:40">[00:21:40]</a>. Strong product companies prioritize outcomes, understanding that "you just don't get points for shipping, you get points for delivering the value" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:23">[00:22:23]</a>.

## Key Skills and Responsibilities of a "Real" [[the_role_of_product_managers | Product Manager]]

A "real" [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]] is a *creator*, not merely a *facilitator* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:17">[00:25:17]</a>. Their job involves side-by-side creation with design and engineering to develop solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:48">[00:25:48]</a>. Key responsibilities and skills include:
*   **Expertise in Users and Customers**: Deep understanding of user needs gained through extensive interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:01">[00:26:01]</a>.
*   **Data Analysis**: Being the expert on how the product is being used and how usage is changing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:27">[00:26:27]</a>.
*   **Business Representation**: Understanding and representing compliance, sales, marketing, financial cost, monetization, legal constraints, and go-to-market issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:37">[00:26:37]</a>.
*   **Market Understanding**: Bringing deep knowledge of the market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:18">[00:27:18]</a>.

These are distinct from the tasks learned in certifications like CSPO or PSPO, which often focus on backlog management in tools like Jira – a skill that is not the core job of a [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:32">[00:27:32]</a>.

## The Spectrum of Roles

Cagan identifies a spectrum of roles:
*   **[[the_role_of_a_product_owner_versus_a_product_manager | Product Owner]]**: At one extreme, this is a role in a delivery process that generally "has no business being a dedicated person" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:07">[00:28:07]</a>.
*   **Feature Team [[the_role_of_product_managers | Product Manager]]**: Somewhere in the middle, they do more than administer the backlog but primarily focus on project management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:30">[00:28:30]</a>.
*   **Empowered [[the_role_of_product_managers | Product Manager]]**: At the other end, responsible for value and viability, and essential when a team is tasked with solving problems and delivering outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:23">[00:28:23]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:55">[00:25:55]</a>.

## The Future of the [[the_role_of_product_managers | Product Manager]] Role

The field of [[the_evolution_and_future_of_product_management | product management]] is undergoing changes due to factors like over-hiring, increased cost of funds, the impact of generative AI, and unsustainable team sizes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:00">[00:15:00]</a>. The predicted impact of generative AI creates uncertainty, with some even suggesting that programming might become less crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:22">[00:13:22]</a>.

For [[the_role_of_product_managers | product managers]], skills related to backlog administration are highly vulnerable to AI automation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>. Roles focused on administrative tasks or basic project management are at risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:20">[00:53:20]</a>. However, the core responsibilities of an empowered [[the_role_of_product_managers | product manager]]—especially ensuring value and viability—become even *more* critical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:51">[00:53:51]</a>. This includes navigating complex issues like the implications of probabilistic software, legal, and ethical considerations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:26">[00:54:26]</a>.

> "If you are fundamentally a backlog administrator, good luck protecting that because already people are doing that" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>

Product leaders also play a critical role, as they are responsible for product strategy and setting strategic bets, allowing empowered teams to then solve those problems autonomously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:51">[00:45:51]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:56">[00:46:56]</a>. This is distinct from a "top-down" feature factory approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:18">[00:45:18]</a>.

## Self-Improvement and Transformation

Despite the challenges, individuals can take agency to improve their skills and shift their company towards an empowered product model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:04">[01:01:04]</a>. Developing critical thinking skills and seeking out reliable resources are crucial, as much of the online [[the_art_and_role_of_product_management | product management advice]] still reflects the "feature team world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:39">[00:33:39]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:16">[00:37:16]</a>.

Companies outside of Silicon Valley, often steeped in older methods, face a harder journey to transformation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:31">[00:58:31]</a>. However, transitioning to a product operating model, which involves adopting 20 core principles, can lead to dramatic improvements and innovation, even for established non-tech companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:40">[00:58:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:30">[01:03:30]</a>.

> "People need to take more control of their career and really use their judgment" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:26">[00:34:26]</a>

## Product Operations (Product Ops)

Product Ops is analogous to Dev Ops and Design Ops <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:57">[01:11:57]</a>. In effective product organizations, Product Ops often centralizes user research and data analysts, bringing these functions under a single leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:16">[01:12:16]</a>. However, a "huge red flag" is when companies interpret Product Ops as being about "process and governance," or use it to justify providing [[the_role_of_product_managers | product managers]] with "assistants" to do their work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:06">[01:13:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:58">[01:13:58]</a>. These roles are often vulnerable to cuts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:09">[01:14:09]</a>.
