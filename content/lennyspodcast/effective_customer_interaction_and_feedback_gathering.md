## No Trade-off Between Speed and Quality
One core principle at Linear is the belief that there isn't a trade-off between speed and quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. This contrasts with common perceptions that equate speed with rushing or sloppiness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>. Instead, speed in a high-quality context reflects competence and expertise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:00">[00:15:00]</a>. Just as a master craftsman's output quality is evident in their speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:00">[00:18:00]</a>, efficient software development allows for more iterations, leading to a better product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:15:00">[09:15:00]</a>.

To achieve this, Linear advocates for quickly developing a "workable solution" within the first 10% of a project's time budget <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:36:00">[09:36:00]</a>. This early version tests key hypotheses and uncovers incorrect assumptions before significant investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:55:00">[09:55:00]</a>. The expectation is not for perfection in the first version, but for a functional prototype that can validate or invalidate major assumptions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:03:00">[11:03:00]</a>.

### Iterative Feedback Loops
Linear employs a tiered approach to [[integrating_customer_feedback_loops_in_product_development | integrating customer feedback loops in product development]]:
1.  **Internal Testing:** New features are first used by internal Linear employees, who use the product daily for their own work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:19:00">[14:19:00]</a>.
2.  **Beta Customer Groups:** Once internally validated, features are released to select beta customer groups as early as possible, ensuring data integrity and usability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:29:00">[14:29:00]</a>. This helps gauge engagement and identify if the feature truly addresses a problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:47:00">[14:47:00]</a>.
3.  **Gradual Release:** Different beta audiences are used, gradually expanding the circle of users before a general release <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:54:00">[14:54:00]</a>.

This strategy prevents wasted time building features that no one wants or uses, by getting directional feedback quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:19:00">[15:19:00]</a>.

## Avoiding Software Bloat
Linear proactively combats the common issue of enterprise software bloat by establishing clear guidelines for feature requests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:59:00">[15:59:00]</a>.

### Prioritizing Individual Contributors (ICs)
A strict rule is to say "no" to customization features requested by middle managers primarily for easier reporting, especially if they worsen the workflow for individual contributors (ICs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:50:00">[16:50:00]</a>.
This policy reflects a core promise of Linear: not to make this specific trade-off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:27:00">[17:27:00]</a>. The rationale is that if the IC experience is poor, they will disengage, leading to inaccurate data and ineffective reporting anyway <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:16:00">[18:16:00]</a>.

> "The core promise of Linear is that we will not make this particular tradeoff." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:27:00">[17:27:00]</a>

While this means sometimes turning down deals, the long-term vision is that buyers ultimately care about their team's effectiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:50:00">[20:50:00]</a>. By adhering to this principle, Linear can convince potential customers that features focused on IC productivity are more beneficial than extensive customization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:57:00">[20:57:00]</a>.

## [[best_practices_for_customer_interviewing | Best Practices for Customer Interviewing]]
Effective customer interaction involves deep digging to uncover underlying problems and emotions, rather than just fulfilling stated feature requests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:31:00">[30:31:00]</a>.

### Feeling the Customer's Pain
The goal in customer calls is to "feel bad in the same way that customers feel bad" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:11:00">[31:11:00]</a>. This means going beyond analytical questions about goals and personas to understand the emotional valence motivating a request <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:24:00">[31:24:00]</a>. By building rapport and seeing things from their perspective, customers are more willing to open up about moments when they "felt bad" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:02:00">[32:02:00]</a>. This [[how_to_effectively_interview_customers | deep empathetic understanding]] is crucial for building truly impactful solutions.

### Identifying "Schlepp Blindness"
In competitive industries, the obvious, goal-oriented problems are often already addressed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:45:00">[34:45:00]</a>. The opportunity lies in exploring "emotional hooks"â€”the unnoticed frustrations or areas where users "feel bad" during their workday <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:10:00">[35:10:00]</a>. This is akin to Paul Graham's concept of "schlepp blindness," where people are unaware of the daily burdens they carry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:51:00">[35:51:00]</a>. An outsider's perspective can help identify these subtle rhythms of feeling and pinpoint opportunities for significant improvement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:02:00">[36:02:00]</a>.

**Example: Triage Management**
Linear's triage management feature stemmed from two emotional pain points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:04:00">[37:04:00]</a>:
1.  **Overwhelmed Manual Triage:** Users felt "underwater" managing tickets manually, despite doing it effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:09:00">[37:09:00]</a>.
2.  **Lack of Control/Visibility:** Other users felt "totally out of control" with tickets "thrown over the wall" and no visibility into their status <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:33:00">[37:33:00]</a>.

Automating the triage queue addressed both feelings, making the process organized and restoring confidence for ICs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:48:00">[37:48:00]</a>.

## [[incorporating_user_feedback_into_product_development | Incorporating User Feedback into Product Development]]
When considering feature requests, Linear focuses on tying them back to "real people" and specific use cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:49:00">[24:49:00]</a>.

### Connecting to Specific Users
Instead of general feature requests, the team wants to understand the workflow of a specific person (e.g., "Bob from company X") and how a feature would impact them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:34:00">[24:34:00]</a>. This prevents building "beautiful solutions that don't match with reality" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:53:00">[25:53:00]</a>.

**Example: Customer Requests Feature**
Initially, many customers requested fully customized fields to track customer-specific needs (e.g., "Walmart wants this feature") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:40:00">[28:40:00]</a>. Instead of adding generic custom fields that would burden ICs, Linear developed a "customer requests" feature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:23:00">[28:23:00]</a>. This feature automatically brings in feedback from CRM and customer support tools, tagging escalated engineering tickets with the customer's name. This provides the desired reporting without manual tagging, making ICs more confident because they know exactly "who's asking for it" and the "real life use cases" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:33:00">[29:33:00]</a>.

### The "Annealing" Process for Feedback
When an early feature doesn't quite fit reality, Linear uses a process called "annealing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:27:00">[26:27:00]</a>. This involves asking if a specific aspect of the feature was adequately tested against reality. If not, even a small amount of feedback can indicate a need for adjustment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:45:00">[26:45:00]</a>. For big, general feature requests, the focus shifts to identifying "concentrated pools of use cases" that can be solved exceptionally well, rather than trying to cover every long-tail scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:22:00">[27:22:00]</a>.

## Systemized Creativity: Exploring Extremes
To foster creativity and develop non-obvious solutions, Linear employs a method of exploring extreme versions of ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:05:00">[45:05:00]</a>. This involves asking: "How extreme can you take it?" along a specific trait, ignoring constraints like cost or practicality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:31:00">[45:31:00]</a>. This approach expands the "possibility space" and helps overcome inherent cognitive biases or "defaults" that limit conventional thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:19:00">[46:19:00]</a>.

**Example: Draft Saving Feature**
For an issue draft saving feature, Linear explored two extremes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:01:00">[49:01:00]</a>:
1.  **Fastest Version (No Interruptions):** Closing an issue draft would instantly discard changes without prompts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:08:00">[49:08:00]</a>. This felt "super unsafe" to users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:39:00">[49:39:00]</a>.
2.  **Safest Version (Autosave Everything):** Every character typed would trigger an autosave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:02:00">[50:02:00]</a>. While safe, this led to a "paper trail" of unintended or discarded drafts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:07:00">[50:07:00]</a>.

The final solution balances these extremes: new issues prompt to save drafts, but existing drafts are automatically saved upon modification. This provides safety without cluttering the user's workspace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:39:00">[50:39:00]</a>. The key is to build and "feel through" these extreme versions, gaining deep insight into what truly works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:34:00">[50:34:00]</a>. This is directly tied to the first principle of getting things out quickly, even if they are extreme versions that won't work long-term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:47:00">[53:47:00]</a>.

## B2B Software as a Teacher
Linear believes that B2B software not only solves problems but also "teaches them how to work" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:29:00">[54:29:00]</a>. When a company adopts a tool, they also adopt the underlying processes and "best practices" embedded within it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:06:00">[55:06:00]</a>. For example, marketing software comes with built-in processes for campaigns, metrics, and cost calculation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:24:00">[55:24:00]</a>.

This perspective reinforces Linear's opinionated stance against excessive customization. By providing a tool that enforces proven workflows, Linear aims to provide a "baseline level of marketing competency" or efficient product management practices to its users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:51:00">[55:51:00]</a>. The goal is to identify practices common among high-performing teams and automate them into the product, allowing other companies to easily adopt these beneficial ways of working <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:41:00">[57:41:00]</a>.

## The Double Triangle: Product, Sales, and Marketing Collaboration
Linear's product management function operates as a "go-to-market discipline" alongside sales and marketing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="58:52:00">[58:52:00]</a>. While the traditional "Triad" of engineering, product, and design is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:02:00">[59:02:00]</a>, collaboration between product, sales, and marketing is often "underexamined" and can even be antagonistic in other organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:14:00">[59:14:00]</a>.

At Linear, product managers actively help the marketing team select the precise language and phrasing that resonates with their expert practitioner customers, ensuring the messaging sounds "native to the language that our customers speak" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:57:00">[59:57:00]</a>. Product marketers on the PM team craft change logs, release notes, and language for upcoming releases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:27:00">[01:00:27:00]</a>. Sales, in turn, validates this messaging in the field, creating a robust feedback cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:54:00">[01:00:54:00]</a>.

This "double triangle" model positions the product manager in the middle, connecting the "building side" (engineering, product, design) with the "selling side" (sales, marketing, product management) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:21:00">[01:01:21:00]</a>. This ensures that product development aligns with commercial goals and market opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:39:00">[01:01:39:00]</a>. For B2B PMs, leaning into the sales and marketing aspects of the role offers a significant opportunity for impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:50:00">[01:02:50:00]</a>.

## Deadlines and Commitment
Linear takes deadlines "seriously" but advocates for having "not too many of them" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:02:00">[01:10:02:00]</a>. When a deadline is set, it becomes a "P-zero problem," meaning everything else is secondary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:04:00">[01:10:04:00]</a>. The PM's role is to ruthlessly cut scope to ensure the deadline can be met <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:32:00">[01:10:32:00]</a>.

Deadlines are primarily for external marketing launches or campaigns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:32:00">[01:11:32:00]</a>. Missing a marketing opportunity means it's lost forever; there's a limited number of opportunities to communicate with the audience in a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:25:00">[01:12:25:00]</a>. Unlike many companies that spend a lot of time estimating to hit deadlines, Linear focuses on shipping early. By having a "working thing" 10% into the allocated time, the team can spend the remaining time deciding on iterations or polishing, allowing them to make an informed go/no-go decision at the deadline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:20:00">[01:13:20:00]</a>.

By adopting these principles, Linear demonstrates a holistic approach to [[improving_user_experience_through_customer_feedback | improving user experience through customer feedback]] and [[effective_customer_engagement_and_validation | effective customer engagement and validation]], prioritizing deep understanding and strategic implementation over reactive feature accumulation.
