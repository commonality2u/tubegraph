From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Creating a successful startup involves identifying and leveraging specific elements and adopting [[Challenges in Business Strategy|pattern-breaking]] actions. Mike Maples Jr., a renowned early-stage investor, has identified three key elements for breakthrough startup ideas: inflections, insights, and founder future fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:17">[00:14:17]</a>. These elements combine to allow startups to wage asymmetric warfare on the present, rather than competing directly with incumbents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>.

## Elements of Breakthrough Startup Ideas

### Inflections

An inflection is an external event that creates the potential for radical change in how people think, feel, and act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:31">[00:15:31]</a>. It is a turning point where something new is introduced that offers new empowerment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>. Inflections are not merely improvement curves like Moore's Law <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:01">[00:16:01]</a>.

Examples of inflections include:
*   **Technological:** The iPhone 4S shipping with a GPS locator chip enabled ride-sharing services like Lyft <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:52">[00:15:52]</a>. Improved smartphone cameras, increased smartphone adoption, Wi-Fi connectivity, and better upload speeds converged to enable Instagram <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:12">[00:17:12]</a>. Broad broadband penetration and improving CDNs (Content Delivery Networks) facilitated the rise of user-generated content and live-streaming platforms like Twitch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:37">[00:23:37]</a>. The proliferation of online customer reviews and Facebook Connect, combined with the 2008 financial crisis, created conditions for Airbnb's success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:27">[00:24:27]</a>.
*   **Regulatory:** Changes during COVID-19, such as legalizing and reimbursing tele-medicine visits across state lines, created new empowerment for patients and doctors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:14">[00:25:14]</a>.
*   **Belief/Behavioral:** The surge in tele-medicine visits during COVID-19 permanently changed people's beliefs about its convenience, shifting attitudes towards virtual doctor appointments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:34">[00:26:34]</a>. The acceptance of working from home as a permanent condition due to COVID-19 is another example <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:26">[00:27:26]</a>.

To stress test an inflection, ask:
1.  What is the specific new thing that was just introduced? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:36">[00:21:36]</a>
2.  How does it specifically empower specific people in specific ways? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:44">[00:21:44]</a>
3.  Under what conditions might that empowerment be realized or not realized (e.g., regulation, customer adoption)? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:47">[00:21:47]</a>

### Insights

An insight is a non-obvious truth about how one or more inflections can be harnessed to change people's behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:19">[00:28:19]</a>. Unlike inflections, insights originate from the founders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:12">[00:28:12]</a>.
Key characteristics of a valuable insight:
*   **Non-consensus and Right:** A great startup idea is often disliked or met with indifference by most, but deeply loved by a subset of people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:06">[00:30:06]</a>, [00:30:30]. If an idea is widely liked, it might be too similar to existing solutions and incumbents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:15">[00:30:15]</a>.
*   **Backcasting:** Successful founders "backcast," working backward from a radically different future they envision, rather than forecasting from the present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:07">[00:31:07]</a>.
*   **Earned Secrets:** Breakthroughs are discovered, not created from a recipe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:00">[00:37:00]</a>. Insights are "earned secrets," gained by getting hands-on with new technologies and actively savoring surprises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:49">[00:37:49]</a>, [00:41:10]. For example, Chegg's founders, while testing textbook rentals, discovered students were willing to pay much more than expected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:14">[00:39:14]</a>.
*   **Pivot Potential:** 80% of Mike Maples Jr.'s biggest returning investments came from a pivot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:30">[00:31:30]</a>, [00:42:41]. If a customer doesn't respond to an idea, the insight might be wrong, the implementation might be wrong, or the founder might be talking to the wrong customer (not a "future-living" customer) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:10">[00:43:10]</a>.
*   **Customer Desperation:** A startup's offer must create "irresistible desire" in the customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:00">[01:01:00]</a>. The product should force a choice, not a comparison <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:49">[01:01:49]</a>. For example, the Tesla Cybertruck is not compared to a Ford F-150; it evokes strong reactions and appeals to a specific group <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:41">[01:02:41]</a>.

### Founder Future Fit

Founder future fit refers to a set of traits that make a founder ideally suited to a certain type of future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:23">[00:49:23]</a>. There is no single "best" founder profile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:57">[00:47:57]</a>.
*   **Beginner's Mind vs. Expertise:** Sometimes it's young, first-time founders with a beginner's mind at the cutting edge of new technology (e.g., Mark Andreessen with Netscape, Mark Zuckerberg, Bill Gates) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:34">[00:49:34]</a>. Other times, it's experienced individuals whose backgrounds align perfectly with the problem (e.g., Applied Intuition founders from Detroit, car companies, and Google's Waymo) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:42">[00:48:42]</a>.
*   **Living in the Future:** Founders must immerse themselves in the future, noticing what is missing and building it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:08">[00:52:08]</a>, [00:56:52]. Sam Altman, who "visits the future multiple times a day," influenced Mdty Hall to found Living Carbon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:32">[00:57:32]</a>.
*   **Lighthouse Customers:** Engage with "lighthouse customers" who are already living in the future and whose problems, when solved, will lead the way for others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:35">[00:58:35]</a>. Industrial Light & Magic was a lighthouse customer for Silicon Graphics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:03">[00:53:03]</a>.

## Pattern-Breaking Actions

Successful startups engage in "pattern-breaking actions" that distinguish them from conventional companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:45">[00:31:45]</a>.
*   **Movements:**
    *   **Leveraging Grievance:** A movement develops a market by leveraging the grievance of a minority against the tyranny of a majority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:33">[01:05:33]</a>. Early customers are driven by belief, not pragmatism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:52">[01:05:52]</a>.
    *   **Crystallizing Choice:** A movement crystallizes a choice, making it clear there's no middle ground (e.g., civil rights movement, ride-sharing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:30">[01:06:30]</a>.
    *   **Higher Purpose:** Great movements appeal to a higher purpose rather than merely claiming to be "better" than competitors (e.g., Tesla's mission to accelerate sustainable energy) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:51">[01:09:51]</a>.
    *   **Weakening Incumbents:** They don't criticize incumbents directly but highlight the weakness in the incumbent's strength. Airbnb, for instance, offered "living like a local" instead of criticizing hotels, turning the hotel's uniformity into a disadvantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:48">[01:10:48]</a>.
*   **Storytelling:**
    *   **Hero's Journey:** Startups apply the "hero's journey" narrative, where the early believers (customers, employees, investors) are the heroes, and the founder is the mentor (like Obi-Wan Kenobi) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:48">[01:12:48]</a>, [01:15:57]. The story describes the "world that is" (problem) and the "world that could be" (solution/future) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:50">[01:15:50]</a>.
    *   **Languaging and Metaphor:** Clever naming and metaphors can help overcome resistance (e.g., Lyft's pink mustache, Twitter's "tweeting birds" metaphor) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:40">[01:14:40]</a>.
    *   **Audience-Specific Narratives:** The story told to an employee, investor, or customer should differ, as each has a unique "hero's journey" and motivation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:20">[01:16:20]</a>.
    *   **Pitch Deck Advice:** A startup pitch deck should clearly state what the company does on slide one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:47">[01:21:47]</a>. Slide two should articulate the non-obvious insight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:27">[01:22:27]</a>. Slide three should present proof points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:48">[01:23:48]</a>. Avoid a "Franken deck" by trying to anticipate every objection from non-believers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:50">[01:20:50]</a>.
*   **Disagreeableness:**
    *   **Conformity Trap:** Startups are fundamentally disagreeable acts, challenging the status quo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:19">[01:04:19]</a>, [01:25:38]. Great founders are willing to pursue their obsessions and sacrifice their status for the mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:05">[01:25:05]</a>.
    *   **Independent Thinking:** Founders should listen to advice but be willing to disregard it if they believe in a different truth (e.g., Ev Williams of Twitter) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:51">[01:25:51]</a>.
    *   **Courage to be Disliked:** Before they win, founders must have the courage to be disliked because their idea is often seen as stupid or crazy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:09">[01:30:09]</a>. After success, they are often criticized for breaking rules or being too powerful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:22">[01:30:22]</a>.

## Applying Principles in Large Companies

These principles can also apply within larger companies to foster [[Strategies for fast product development in startups|pattern-breaking]] ideas:
*   **Separate Value Delivery Systems:** Treat new, pattern-breaking ideas as distinct from the core business. Do not force them to be a large portion of the business too quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:03">[01:35:03]</a>.
*   **Autonomy and Discretion:** New ventures should be autonomous, headed by Mavericks, and kept "not visible" or separate from the main organization in their early stages (e.g., Apple's iPhone, Amazon Web Services, Lockheed's Skunk Works, IBM PC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:25">[01:35:25]</a>, [01:37:01]. This protects them from the "tractor beam" of corporate pattern matching <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:21">[01:37:21]</a>.
*   **Willingness to Fail:** Companies should invest a portion of their profits in projects that are likely to fail but have wildly asymmetric upside if they succeed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:20">[01:38:20]</a>. The willingness to fail enables breakthrough success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:44">[01:38:44]</a>.

## Conclusion

The journey of a startup founder is often lonely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:46">[01:39:46]</a>. It's crucial for founders to stick their necks out for what they believe in, even if their ideas are initially disliked by many <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:02">[01:40:02]</a>. Success in the startup world isn't about being "better" at existing games but about changing the rules entirely and leading people to a radically different, compelling future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:06">[00:39:06]</a>, [01:31:37].
