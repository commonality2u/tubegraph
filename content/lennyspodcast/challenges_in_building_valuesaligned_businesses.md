Building a values-aligned business is often fraught with challenges, yet it is considered crucial for long-term success and positive impact. Eric Ries emphasizes the importance of explicit value embodiment within organizational structures to ensure trustworthiness and alignment with human flourishing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:13">[01:35:13]</a>.

## The Core Challenge: Misaligned Intentions and Structures

A significant problem arises when companies claim to "change the world" but fail to explicitly define if it's "for the better" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:29">[00:26:29]</a>. Ries notes that the technology industry has "severely underinvested and neglected in creating governance and alignment structures" that explicitly embody their stated values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:40">[01:19:40]</a>. This oversight leads to companies becoming disappointments, betraying the trust of those who helped build them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:23">[01:20:23]</a>.

Founders often assume good intentions are sufficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:15">[01:34:15]</a>. However, individual good intentions are not enough because founders are replaceable, and organizational promises cannot be reliably made by a single person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:34">[01:34:34]</a>. Organizations are seen as "super organisms with their own soul and their own life," which founders birth and nurture, but do not own <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:47">[01:34:47]</a>.

## Psychological and Practical Barriers

*   **Fear of Failure vs. Worse Outcomes:** While a startup failing is bad, Ries argues it's "not even in the top 10" worst things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>. Far worse is being trapped in a "zombie undead company" that you hate and cannot leave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>, or building a company that becomes a "malign force in the world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>. This often contributes to a mental health crisis among founders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:00">[00:19:00]</a>.
*   **Difficulty Admitting Problems:** Many founders struggle to admit when their initial approach isn't working, even when evidence points to it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:33">[00:43:33]</a>. They may tell themselves "it's always darkest before the dawn," even when the company is effectively dead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:39">[00:56:39]</a>. This denial is a psychological defense mechanism, as the company often becomes deeply intertwined with the founder's ego <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:07">[00:59:07]</a>.
*   **"Always Too Early Until It's Too Late":** Founders often defer establishing strong governance and values-aligned structures, believing they can implement them later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:00">[01:52:00]</a>. However, as the company grows and takes on more financing, it becomes difficult to introduce changes that might be seen as counter to shareholder interests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:26">[01:45:26]</a>. This is particularly true when standard Articles of Incorporation mean a fiduciary duty to maximize shareholder returns, potentially even at the expense of ethical considerations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:05">[01:47:05]</a>.

## Strategies for Building Values-Aligned Businesses

### 1. Embed Values in Governance and Structure
To make promises believable and ensure the organization is trustworthy, values must be embodied in the company's structure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:13">[01:35:13]</a>. This can include:

*   **Legal Designations:** Adopting legal designations like Public Benefit Corporations (PBC) or Social Purpose Corporations (SPC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:01">[01:44:01]</a>.
*   **Foundation Structures:** Establishing a foundation, which can hold a portion of the company's equity and have trustees (e.g., tribal leaders for companies working with indigenous cultures) who oversee governance and ensure the mission is upheld <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:17">[01:44:17]</a>. This is common in some European countries and seen in companies like Open AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:00">[01:52:00]</a>.
*   **Board Mission Pledges:** Requiring board members to pledge their business judgment to support the company's mission, not just narrow shareholder interests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:31">[01:44:31]</a>.
*   **LTSV (Long-Term Stock Exchange Vehicles):** A financing instrument where every LP signs a contract to support the company's mission and trustees oversee shares to ensure long-term stakeholder interests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:45">[01:44:45]</a>.
*   **Founder Preferred Shares:** Implementing specific share classes or rights for founders to protect the company's mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:08">[01:48:08]</a>.

### 2. Prioritize Purpose Over Short-Term Gains
Ries argues that focusing on human flourishing can actually lead to greater profitability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:15">[01:41:15]</a>. Companies dedicated to creating net new value and avoiding extractive practices can outperform competitors who are "value destroying" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:08">[02:12:08]</a>. This is because they can attract high-quality talent aligned with the mission and build extreme customer loyalty and retention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:59">[01:41:59]</a>.

### 3. Personal Courage and Self-Reflection
Founders should be willing to "get fired for doing it the way you think is right" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:01">[00:54:01]</a>. This commitment attracts like-minded individuals and leads to greater personal satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:13">[00:54:13]</a>. It's crucial for founders to introspect and ask if they genuinely want to continue working on their current company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:26">[00:58:26]</a>. If not, it's "okay to move on" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:41">[00:58:41]</a>.

### 4. Continuous Alignment
Building a values-aligned company is a "relentless and constant process" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>. It requires ensuring that at "every level, at every moment, every decision the company makes, somebody in the room making that decision feels like they have a fiduciary duty to do the right thing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:57">[01:49:57]</a>. This means constantly creating alignment between governance, management structure, and the actual operations and culture of the company, recognizing they are "one being" working together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:16">[01:49:16]</a>.

### 5. Leveraging Uncertainty
AI presents a new era of uncertainty. Ries suggests taking actions that "make ethical sense in a wide variety of future scenarios" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:01">[01:26:01]</a>. This includes promoting transparency, building capacity, fostering competent leaders, and establishing companies committed to explicit values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:17">[01:26:17]</a>.

By proactively addressing these challenges and embracing strategies for embedding values, founders can build organizations that not only succeed financially but also genuinely contribute to human flourishing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:15">[01:41:15]</a>. This approach can be a significant [[Developing and Implementing Company Values | competitive advantage]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:11">[01:41:11]</a>.
