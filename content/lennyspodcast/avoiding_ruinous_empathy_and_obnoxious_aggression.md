From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 
Kim Scott's framework of Radical Candor emphasizes the crucial balance between [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | caring personally]] and challenging directly. When this balance is lost, communication can fall into one of three problematic quadrants: [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | obnoxious aggression]], [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | ruinous empathy]], or manipulative insincerity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:06:00">[04:06:00]</a>. Understanding and avoiding these pitfalls is key to building strong relationships and achieving better work outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:58:00">[08:58:00]</a>.

### Obnoxious Aggression

[[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | Obnoxious aggression]] occurs when you challenge directly but fail to show that you [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | care personally]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:31:00">[04:31:00]</a>. This is often what people mistakenly associate with "radical candor" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:43:00">[04:43:00]</a>.

**Problems with Obnoxious Aggression**:
*   **Harmful to others**: It hurts the recipient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:06:00">[05:06:00]</a>.
*   **Inefficient**: When you are a jerk to someone, they enter "fight or flight" mode and cannot truly hear what you are saying, making your efforts wasteful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:10:00">[05:10:00]</a>.
*   **Self-defeating**: Realizing you've acted like a jerk can lead you to overcorrect by avoiding direct challenge, resulting in manipulative insincerity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:39:00">[05:39:00]</a>.

An extreme example of [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | obnoxious aggression]] is seen in the "dots" system at Bridgewater, where public, often personal, criticism was recorded and shared, even leading to tears <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:25">[01:01:25]</a>. This approach lacks genuine personal care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:39">[01:03:39]</a>.

### Ruinous Empathy

[[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | Ruinous empathy]] is the most common mistake people make <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:12:00">[07:12:00]</a>. It happens when you show that you [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | care personally]] but fail to tell someone something they would be better off knowing in the long run, usually out of a fear of hurting their feelings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:25:00">[06:25:00]</a>.

**Problems with Ruinous Empathy**:
*   **False Harmony**: It creates a superficial sense of peace that is ultimately detrimental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:00">[02:01:00]</a>.
*   **Unfair to the Individual**: Not providing clear feedback prevents people from improving and reaching their full potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:00">[02:29:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:00">[02:31:00]</a>.
*   **Detrimental to the Team**:
    *   Leads to delayed deliverables and rework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:38:00">[02:38:00]</a>.
    *   Frustrates high-performing team members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:34:00">[02:34:00]</a>.
    *   Causes top performers to leave, as they desire to do their best work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:00">[02:49:00]</a>.

**The "Bob" Story Example**:
Kim Scott recounts a personal experience with [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | ruinous empathy]] involving an employee named Bob. Bob was well-liked, charming, and brought levity to the office, but his work was consistently riddled with sloppy mistakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>. Because Kim liked him and didn't want to hurt his feelings, she would give him vague, overly gentle feedback like, "Oh Bob, this is such a great start... maybe you can make it just a little bit better" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:00">[02:41:00]</a>. This went on for 10 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>.

Eventually, Kim realized that if she didn't fire Bob, she would lose her best performers who were tired of his late and poor-quality work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:40:00">[02:40:00]</a>. When she finally had the difficult conversation, Bob asked, "Why didn't you tell me? I thought you all cared about me" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:00">[02:50:00]</a>. This experience highlighted that what seemed "nice" at the moment was actually "ruinous" in the long run, and led to Bob's termination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:00">[02:31:00]</a>.

### Manipulative Insincerity

This is considered the "worst place of all" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:44:00">[05:44:00]</a>. It occurs when a person neither [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | cares personally]] nor challenges directly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:47:00">[05:47:00]</a>. It can arise when someone has been obnoxiously aggressive and, upon realizing it, overcorrects by pretending to agree or not addressing issues, leading to a state of self-protective insincerity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:39:00">[05:39:00]</a>.

### Strategies for Avoiding the Pitfalls and Cultivating Radical Candor

To avoid [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | ruinous empathy]] and [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | obnoxious aggression]], and move towards radical candor, consider the following:

1.  **Solicit Criticism First**: The most important first step is to actively ask for feedback from others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:20:00">[03:20:00]</a>. This builds trust and makes it easier for you to both give and receive feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:70:00">[03:70:00]</a>.
    *   **Don't say**: "Do you have any feedback for me?" â€” this is a waste of breath <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>.
    *   **Ask a question that elicits a response**: Examples include:
        *   "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?" (ensure it sounds authentic to you, not a script) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:00">[03:11:00]</a>.
        *   "Tell me why I'm wrong" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:00">[03:46:00]</a>.
        *   "What's one thing I could be doing better?" or "What's one way I could be helping you be more successful?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:01:00">[04:01:00]</a>.
        *   "What could I have done this week to better support you in your work?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:21:00">[04:21:00]</a>.
    *   **Regularity**: Managers should solicit feedback weekly from each direct report, typically for five minutes at the end of a one-on-one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>. Also ask cross-functional peers and your boss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:13:00">[04:13:00]</a>.
    *   **Embrace Discomfort**: There's no "emotional Novocain" for giving or receiving feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:34:00">[04:34:00]</a>.
        *   **Silence**: After asking a question, close your mouth and count to six; the discomfort of silence often prompts a response <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:00">[04:50:00]</a>.
        *   **Follow-up questions**: Listen with the intent to understand, not to respond. Ask clarifying questions to ensure you truly grasp what they are saying <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:41:00">[04:41:00]</a>.
    *   **Reward the Candor**: If you agree with the feedback, fix the problem and make your listening tangible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>. Be "loud" about the change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>.
    *   **Handling Disagreement**: If you disagree, find 5-10% of what they said that you *can* agree with, acknowledge it, and then state you need to think about the rest and will get back to them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>. Follow up with a respectful explanation of why you disagree <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:21:00">[04:21:00]</a>. Unspoken disagreement harms relationships more than open, respectful disagreement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:39:00">[04:39:00]</a>.

2.  **Give Feedback Effectively (HIP CORE)**:
    *   **H**umble: Go into conversations acknowledging you might be wrong; candor is a dialogue, not a monologue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:59:00">[05:59:00]</a>.
    *   **I**ntention: State your intention to be helpful; you're doing this to help them, not to dominate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:16:00">[06:16:00]</a>.
    *   **P**ersonally and **P**rivately: Praise in public, criticize in private <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a>.
    *   **I**mmediately: Give feedback right away; delaying usually means never saying it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a>. Exceptions are if you're too upset or they are too upset to hear you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:40:00">[06:40:00]</a>.
    *   **S**ynchronously: Have conversations in person or synchronously (e.g., phone calls are preferred over video due to less "noise" from facial expressions/body language) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:48:00">[06:48:00]</a>. Avoid email, text, or Slack for feedback, as you cannot gauge how it's landing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:53:00">[06:53:00]</a>.
    *   **P**roblem, not Personality: Never give praise or criticism about someone's personality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:09:00">[07:09:00]</a>.
        *   Use **CORE**: **C**ontext, **O**bservation, **R**esult, **E** (Next Step) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:28:00">[07:28:00]</a>.
            *   *Example (Criticism)*: "In the meeting [context], when you said 'um' every third word [observation], it made you sound stupid [result]. Go to the speech coach [next step]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:34:00">[07:34:00]</a>.
            *   *Example (Praise)*: "In the meeting [context], when you offered both sides of the argument [observation], it earned you credibility [result]. Do more of that [next step]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:50:00">[07:50:00]</a>.

3.  **Gauge How it's Landing**: When giving feedback, start in a neutral place and be prepared to move further on the "challenge directly" axis if they brush you off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:05:00">[08:05:00]</a>. If they look sad or mad, move up on the "care personally" axis (e.g., "Maybe I didn't say that in the best way; how could I have said it differently?") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:05:00">[08:05:00]</a>. Don't pull back the words you said, as that makes things worse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:09:00">[08:09:00]</a>. Get curious, not furious <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:14:00">[08:14:00]</a>.

4.  **Manage Your Need to Be Liked**: Realize that your job is not to be liked, but to [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | care about other people]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:00">[02:22:00]</a>. Being "nice" in the short term (ruinous empathy) is unkind in the long run <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:00">[02:57:00]</a>. Focus on doing the right thing for others and their future, more than your own discomfort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:00">[02:29:00]</a>.

5.  **Build a Culture of Care**: When leaders treat their direct reports with genuine [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | care]], it fosters a culture of caring throughout the organization, which scales even if individual relationships do not <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:00">[01:43:00]</a>.

6.  **Invest in Career Conversations**: Managers should have real, meaningful [[the_role_of_empathy_in_leadership | career conversations]] with direct reports. This includes discussing their past, dreams for the future, and creating a career action plan to develop necessary skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

By proactively soliciting feedback, giving it effectively, managing personal reactions, and fostering a culture of genuine care, individuals and organizations can move away from [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | ruinous empathy]] and [[Avoiding ruinous empathy and obnoxious aggression | obnoxious aggression]] towards the more productive and humane approach of Radical Candor. Remember, you do not have to choose between being successful and being a jerk; you can be a successful, kind person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:54">[01:16:54]</a>.
