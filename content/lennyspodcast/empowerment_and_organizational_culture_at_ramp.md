## Core Principles of Ramp's Culture

At Ramp, [[velocitycentric_product_development_at_ramp | velocity]] is paramount, influencing every aspect of how the company operates, including product development, team incentives, hiring, promotions, and decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:30">[00:07:30]</a>. This focus on speed is seen as a key metric for team performance, enabling faster iteration and ultimately leading to greater impact over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>. It also helps attract top talent, as many people are drawn to companies that build and ship products quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>.

Examples of Ramp's rapid execution include:
*   Building a competitor to MX within three months with approximately 10 people (eight engineers) when the speaker, Jeff Charles, joined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.
*   Developing a competitor to Expensify six months later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
*   Expanding into accounts payable with a team of three engineers, one designer, and one PM, delivering a product within three months that now processes billions of dollars annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>.

The "recipe" for this velocity involves:
*   Small, [[velocitycentric_product_development_at_ramp | single-threaded focus]] teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:54">[00:09:54]</a>.
*   Empowering teams with resources to achieve lofty goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58">[00:09:58]</a>.
*   Shielding these teams from internal chaos and distractions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.

### Single-Threaded Teams
Single-threaded teams at Ramp have one primary goal, ensuring that individual contributors can focus solely on that objective without being sidetracked by other tasks or responsibilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>. An example is the Flex product team, which was solely dedicated to addressing the needs of e-commerce companies for cash flow conversion and smoothing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>. Leaders protect these teams by:
*   Having dedicated individuals, like production engineers, handle escalations, bugs, and issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:25">[00:12:25]</a>.
*   Utilizing product operators to manage documentation, release management, customer requests, and other chaotic tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:42">[00:12:42]</a>.
*   Forming sub-teams for big bets by pulling individuals from different areas, ensuring they don't have responsibilities for existing products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:58">[00:12:58]</a>.

### Lofty Goals
Ramp uses ambitious goals to motivate teams, often benchmarked against publicly traded competitors like Bill.com or Expensify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:50">[00:13:50]</a>. Design also plays a significant role in motivation, with designers crafting visionary prototypes to illustrate the future of a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>. This combination of clear market opportunities, revenue goals, and inspiring designs helps teams stay focused and achieve high velocity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:50">[00:14:50]</a>.

### Context Over Control
A key aspect of Ramp's [[building_an_entrepreneurial_culture | entrepreneurial culture]] is "context over control," where leaders align teams on goals, hypotheses, and supporting data, rather than prescribing solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:56">[00:15:56]</a>. This allows solutions to emerge from the teams closest to the ground, fostering empowerment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:51">[00:16:51]</a>. The leadership's role is to constantly share context, especially information from high-level meetings, to help teams make better decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a>. The "contract" between leadership and product teams is primarily their strategy and roadmap, with teams highlighting risks and one-way decisions for leadership input <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:14">[00:18:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:43">[00:18:43]</a>.

## Quality and Speed
Ramp's high velocity doesn't compromise quality; instead, it often enhances it. Because fixes can be deployed quickly, issues are addressed rapidly, leading to higher overall quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:21">[00:21:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:33">[00:21:33]</a>. Control mechanisms ensure that high velocity does not negatively impact the business:
*   **Voice of Customer Process**: Every negative product review is shared monthly with the relevant tech lead, PM, and designer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:01">[00:22:01]</a>.
*   **NPS and CSAT Reporting**: Key customer satisfaction metrics are regularly reported <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:16">[00:22:16]</a>.
*   **Operational Overhead**: Teams are measured by the percentage of support tickets originating from their product area, normalized by user count, with a goal to maintain low operational burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:19">[00:22:19]</a>.
*   **Direct Bug Assignment**: Bugs are directly assigned to the engineer on call, ensuring they "feel the pain" and are motivated to fix issues quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:37">[00:22:37]</a>.
*   **User Experience Metrics**: If support tickets due to customer confusion are elevated, teams are halted from shipping new features until these issues are resolved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:37">[00:23:37]</a>.

## Preventing Burnout
Despite the emphasis on velocity, Ramp aims to prevent burnout by fostering a culture where impact and meaningful work are prioritized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>. Burnout, in this view, often occurs when efforts don't translate into tangible progress. High velocity, by contrast, helps individuals achieve a "flow state" where work feels thrilling and impactful, rather than a chore <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:04">[00:30:04]</a>. The goal of management is to make every team member feel ownership over their goals and problems, creating a sense of a "mini company" within their team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:50">[00:31:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:03">[00:32:03]</a>.

## Planning and Strategy
Ramp's approach to planning is characterized by Jeff Charles's quote: "Any second you spend planning is a second you don't spend doing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:25">[00:32:25]</a>. They openly acknowledge that this high-velocity environment can be "somewhat chaotic," with rapid product changes and constant adjustments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:14">[00:33:14]</a>. However, this is accepted as a trade-off for innovation and providing value to customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:32">[00:33:32]</a>.

Planning is primarily focused on high-value, high-accuracy moments like major product marketing launches that occur quarterly or bi-annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:56">[00:33:56]</a>. Beyond these, individual teams operate with autonomy and a degree of internal chaos, but with extreme clarity on their specific goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:20">[00:34:20]</a>. Ramp has moved away from overly bureaucratic OKRs, finding that financial plans and product roadmaps serve as sufficient "contracts" for accountability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:34">[00:39:34]</a>.

Product teams operate with a "strategy document" that outlines:
*   Goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:03">[00:38:03]</a>
*   Hypothesis (why it will work) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:13">[00:38:13]</a>
*   Unique positioning to achieve the goal (right to win) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:19">[00:38:19]</a>
*   Metrics for success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:23">[00:38:23]</a>
*   Initiatives, risks, and long-term outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:27">[00:38:27]</a>

An example of "right to win" was Ramp's expansion into bill payments, seeing it as a natural extension of their corporate card platform due to existing capabilities in money movement, liability processing, accounting integrations, and risk management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:07">[00:41:07]</a>.

## Thinking from First Principles
Ramp emphasizes thinking from first principles due to the unique hybrid nature of its business (credit card, payments, and software company serving both SMBs and enterprises) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:12">[00:45:12]</a>. This means avoiding pattern matching from past experiences and instead returning to the fundamental nature of the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:47">[00:45:47]</a>.

A notable example of this approach is Ramp's support team structure. Reporting directly to product management, the underlying principle is that "every support ticket is a failure of our product" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:02">[00:47:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:07">[00:47:07]</a>. This direct accountability motivates the product team to minimize issues. The company also believes that deep customer understanding comes from listening to feedback, so they incentivize support agents to decrease ticket volume over time rather than just resolving them, leading to an extremely low contact rate (over 400,000 users supported by a team of under 30 agents) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:44">[00:47:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:17">[00:48:17]</a>.

## Empowering Product Teams
To achieve so much with a lean product management team (e.g., 13 PMs for over 100 engineers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:17">[00:57:17]</a>, Ramp:
*   **Makes everyone a "PM"**: Other roles like engineers and designers are empowered to think about specs, priorities, and problem-solving, taking on responsibilities traditionally held by PMs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:11">[00:58:11]</a>.
*   **Invests in Product Operations**: A dedicated team handles operational functions such as project management, issue management, release management, enablement, and customer research, freeing up PMs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:01">[00:59:01]</a>.
*   **Cuts low-leverage work**: PMs avoid tasks like writing detailed tickets or spending excessive time in ticket management systems. Their contract is the vision, priority, and high-level spec, with execution details left to engineering teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:31">[00:59:31]</a>.

The core job of a product manager at Ramp primarily involves [[building_and_maintaining_company_culture | team building]] within their "pod" (as managers might be external to the team's direct reporting lines), ensuring the team remains focused, and protecting the team from external stakeholders and chaos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:31">[01:00:31]</a>.

## Hiring for the Culture
Ramp's [[ramps_rapid_growth_strategy | rapid growth strategy]] relies heavily on hiring A-plus engineers and designers, which forms the bedrock of their [[understanding_company_cultures_impact_on_product_development | product development culture]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>. The CTO, Kareem, initially focused almost exclusively on recruiting top talent in the company's first year, which had compounding positive effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:42">[01:09:42]</a>.

When interviewing, Ramp looks for individuals with a strong desire for impact and the ability to think deeply from first principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:02">[01:06:02]</a>. They prioritize hunger and deep thinking over traditional experience, as Ramp's unique business model often renders past patterns less relevant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:05">[01:07:05]</a>. Key indicators of an A-plus engineer or designer include:
*   Desire to win in the market and against competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:11">[01:02:11]</a>.
*   Understanding of business context and curiosity about how the company operates, makes money, and customer needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:26">[01:02:26]</a>.
*   Ability to execute independently without constant oversight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:48">[01:02:48]</a>.
*   Proactiveness in seeking out and solving problems, even outside their direct responsibilities (e.g., jumping into Slack channels to help customers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:14">[01:03:14]</a>.
*   Accountability for their product's performance and feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:02">[01:04:02]</a>.
While such individuals might be "harder to manage" due to their strong opinions, their challenging of ideas ultimately leads to deeper thinking and faster growth for the entire team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:06">[01:05:06]</a>.

## Cultivating a Culture of Velocity
For other companies seeking to [[creating_a_culture_of_experimentation_in_a_company | foster a similar culture of velocity]], Jeff Charles advises:
*   **Investment in R&D**: Ensure top-down investment in R&D as a first-class citizen, paying competitively and focusing on the engineering and tech brand to attract top talent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:19">[02:25:19]</a>.
*   **Empowerment of the Tech Organization**: This means the CEO is less prescriptive about product specifics and trusts the empowered tech team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:25:34">[02:25:34]</a>.
*   **Eliminate Meetings and Status Updates**: Meetings should be for collaboration and decision-making, not status updates, which should be done asynchronously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:09">[02:26:09]</a>.
*   **Be Clear on Trade-offs**: Leaders must communicate clearly about what the team *is* doing and, crucially, what it *is not* doing due to focus. Presenting a "menu of items" forces difficult but necessary prioritization that increases velocity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>.
*   **Publicly Recognize Success**: Leaders should actively comment on projects and give engineers opportunities to present their work, fostering excitement and motivation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:28:34">[02:28:34]</a>.
*   **Foster Deep Work**: Block out dedicated time for deep thinking and problem-solving, minimizing distractions like constant notifications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:16">[02:51:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:52:21">[02:52:21]</a>.

Jeff Charles emphasizes that [[balancing_startup_mentality_within_larger_organizations | asking for velocity requires trade-offs]] like empowerment, trust, process elimination, and increased focus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:28:09">[02:28:09]</a>. Leaders must be comfortable with these shifts to truly enable a high-velocity culture. While there is no mention of a specific [[building_a_culture_of_radical_respect | culture of radical respect]] by name, the themes of transparency, direct feedback, and empowerment align with its principles.
