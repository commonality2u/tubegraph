## Overview of Interview Questions and Communication
Interviews are a critical opportunity for candidates to demonstrate their skills and personality, often through [[the_power_of_storytelling_in_communication | storytelling]] and effective [[improving_communication_skills | communication]] [00:09:50]. Podcast guests emphasize that the way candidates answer questions, beyond just the content, reveals crucial insights into their self-awareness, problem-solving abilities, and values.

## Key Interview Questions and Their Communication Insights

### Understanding Self-Awareness and Growth
*   **"To what do you attribute your success, and you can't say luck?"** (Akash Dimano, Head of Product at Roll)
    *   This question aims to gauge a candidate's [[importance_of_introspection_and_selfawareness_in_interviews | self-awareness]] and curiosity [00:03:35]. The response reveals how reflective they are about their journey [00:03:50].
*   **"Fast forward three years, what's different about you?"** (Ben Williams, Former VP Product at Snyk)
    *   Beyond aspirations for roles or titles, this question seeks signals of humility and [[importance_of_introspection_and_selfawareness_in_interviews | self-awareness]] regarding personal and professional growth areas [00:13:31]. Interviewers look for candidates who can be open about where they need to improve [00:13:40].
*   **"What would your siblings say about you?"** (Melanie Quan Burwitz, Head of Growth at Deel)
    *   This question, or "what would your parents say about you" if no siblings, is highly revealing of what a candidate thinks others perceive of them [00:14:49]. Interviewers look for sincerity and [[importance_of_introspection_and_selfawareness_in_interviews | self-awareness]], recognizing that family members might offer less flattering, but honest, perspectives [00:15:04].
*   **"Tell me something you've learned about yourself that revealed a limitation in how you work."** (Scott Belsky, Chief Strategy Officer at Adobe)
    *   This question tests [[importance_of_introspection_and_selfawareness_in_interviews | introspection]] and whether the candidate can be open and self-reflective when they hit limits or struggle, rather than blaming others [00:18:32].
*   **"Do you consider yourself lucky?"** (Scott Belsky, Chief Strategy Officer at Adobe)
    *   This query is considered fascinating as it can reveal if someone is comfortable admitting luck, which often signifies confidence and humility, rather than insecurity [00:18:50]. Acknowledging luck suggests an understanding of privilege and external factors [00:19:08].

### Assessing Problem-Solving and Resilience
*   **"What's the hardest thing you've ever done?"** (Jeff Charles, Head of Product at Ramp)
    *   This question helps interviewers understand the candidate's definition of "hard," why it was difficult, how they overcame it, how they collaborated with others, and their level of agency in the resolution [00:04:06]. This provides insight into their resilience and work ethic [00:04:26].
*   **"Tell me about a time something went wrong."** (Paige Castello, Co-Head of Product Management and Head of AI at Asana)
    *   This question focuses on product failures or team dysfunctions [00:15:35]. The interviewer evaluates the candidate's mindset and how they discuss and relate to difficult situations, revealing their thought process and self-perception [00:15:58].

### Evaluating Strategic Thinking and Product Acumen
*   **"A group of scientists has invented a teleportation device. They've hired you to bring it to market. What do you do?"** (Shashir Marocha, CEO and Co-Founder of Coda)
    *   This "coded question test" reveals a candidate's ability to prioritize and ask incisive questions under constraints [00:05:02]. Candidates initially ask many questions, but are then told they can only ask two more before presenting a plan [00:05:50]. The ability to quickly identify the most impactful questions (e.g., "Is it safe enough for humans?" and "Is it more expensive to buy or run?") demonstrates strategic thinking and simplification skills [00:06:00]. This abstract scenario makes the method low-stakes and easier to learn for candidates [00:07:41].
*   **"Describe to me a time when you were part of a controversial product decision."** (Yuki Yamashita, Chief Product Officer at Figma)
    *   This question is revealing if candidates can clearly articulate the conflict, represent both sides, and maintain an "even-keeled way," demonstrating their ability to understand and navigate different perspectives [00:09:02].
*   **"Talk about a big problem that you worked on."** (Yuki Yamashita, Chief Product Officer at Figma)
    *   The goal is to see if the candidate can make the problem compelling, regardless of its surface-level appeal, and "rally the troops" around it [00:09:36]. This highlights their [[the_power_of_storytelling_in_communication | storytelling]] and [[improving_communication_skills | communication]] skills [00:09:50], which are vital for product managers [00:09:54].
*   **"What unfair secrets have you learned to improve the velocity and energy level of a product team?"** (Noah Weiss, Chief Product Officer at Slack)
    *   This question probes for unique, non-obvious insights that candidates have learned and how they apply them, often revealing innovative thinking [00:12:48].
*   **Behavioral questions about navigating ambiguity.** (Jiaona Zhang, Head of Product at Webflow)
    *   Given the inherent ambiguity of a Product Manager role [00:11:47], interviewers look for candidates who can provide structure and a clear path forward [00:11:55]. Good answers also involve [[improving_communication_skills | seeking help]] and inputs rather than rigidly asserting one way [00:12:07]. Candidates should outline milestones to assess progress and adapt decisions [00:12:33].

### Discerning Motivations and Values
*   **"Tell me what work you are most proud of."** (Katie Dill, Head of Design at Stripe; Ki Sarin, CEO of Linear; Camille Hurst, Product Leader at Spotify)
    *   This question helps understand a candidate's taste, judgment, motivations, and what they consider a good outcome [00:10:17]. It reveals what the person values and how they think about their achievements, offering a positive discussion point [00:10:48].
*   **"What's something that everyone takes for granted that you think is essentially hogwash or inaccurate?"** (Nikhyl Singal, VP of Product at Facebook)
    *   This question is designed to break the "interview mindset" where candidates try to predict desired answers [00:17:01]. It forces candidates to be genuinely opinionated and authentic, revealing their true thoughts and breaking down communication barriers [00:17:19].
*   **"Tell me something you did that worked out but not for the reason that you thought it would work," or "Tell me something you did that was a good decision that didn't work."** (Onyeka Omalu, Chief Product Officer at Carbon Health)
    *   This question emphasizes [[importance_of_introspection_and_selfawareness_in_interviews | introspection]], assessing if a candidate reflects on their decisions, understands why they succeeded or failed, and incorporates these learnings into future actions [00:18:10].
*   **"Tell me about a time that you delivered something that was impactful."** (Lauren Isord, Head of Growth at Notion)
    *   This aims to understand how candidates define and measure impact [00:19:30]. For growth practitioners, a good answer often highlights intrinsic motivation to have a business impact [00:19:42].

### Leveraging Reference Calls for Communication Insights
*   **"What feedback will I be giving this person in their first performance review?"** (Paul Adams, Chief Product Officer at Intercom)
    *   When conducting reference calls, this question is powerful because it's difficult for a referee to dodge [00:20:11]. It elicits specific, actionable feedback that can reveal potential growth areas or challenges, providing a realistic perspective on the candidate's performance and communication style [00:20:15].
