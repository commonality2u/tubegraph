## Early Career: Setting the Foundation

### Avoiding Short-Term Thinking

A common mistake for early-career PMs is focusing too much on short-term dissatisfactions, such as disliking a boss or perceiving a company as stagnant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>. While these issues might be real, they are often transient and can lead to lateral career moves rather than forward progression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>.

Instead, a more effective approach is to adopt a long-term perspective, working backward from an ideal "end state" or desired career outcome, much like planning a product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. This means considering the job *after* the next one, or a "skip" in the career path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. For example, if the goal is to found a company someday, current and next roles should serve that ambition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>.

Another short-term trap is equating career progression solely with promotions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>. While promotions signify movement within a company's system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>, focusing too narrowly on them can lead to frustration if one leaves immediately after a promotion, questioning its true purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>.

### The "Ex-Growth Company" Trap

A significant [[common_challenges_faced_by_product_teams_and_how_to_overcome_them | challenge]] for product managers, especially in the current economic climate, is being in or joining "ex-growth companies" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:52">[00:14:52]</a>. These are companies that may have raised significant capital during a period of hyper-growth (e.g., blitzscaling), but have since lost their momentum, often due to changes in market conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:12">[00:15:12]</a>.

**Characteristics of an Ex-Growth Company** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:07">[00:18:07]</a>:
*   Reframing the core product to find product-market fit *after* having a high valuation (hundreds of millions or more) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:10">[00:18:10]</a>.
*   The absence of a "sucking sound" or natural pull from customers, indicating a struggle to acquire users without significant marketing efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:37">[00:20:37]</a>.

For employees, staying at such a company often means losing out on equity compensation and career opportunities, as the long-term investment may not pay off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:14">[00:19:14]</a>. The opportunity cost of remaining is high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:17">[00:20:17]</a>. While loyalty or unique learning experiences (e.g., an executive role) can be reasons to stay, fear of not finding another job is considered a poor justification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:31">[00:21:31]</a>.

### Key Focus Areas for Early PMs

*   **Develop a World-Class Skill ("Pick a Lane")**: Identify and become an expert in one of the ambiguities inherent in product management, such as <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:07">[00:23:07]</a>:
    *   Crafting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:12">[00:23:12]</a>
    *   Market ambiguity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:18">[00:23:18]</a>
    *   Organizational ambiguity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:27">[00:23:27]</a>
    *   Domain expertise (e.g., ML, hardware) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:35">[00:23:35]</a>
    *   Team management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:46">[00:23:46]</a>
    *   Growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:15">[00:24:15]</a>
*   **Cultivate a Compelling Story**: Think about the narrative you'll tell to future employers about your experiences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>. This means clearly articulating what you learned, what you built, the challenges you faced, and how you overcame them, using "I" statements rather than "we" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:02">[01:22:02]</a>.

Having a diverse set of experiences, including working at different stages of company growth (pre-product-market fit, scaling smoke to fire, turning fire into something great), enhances a PM's ability as a "builder" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>. While prestigious company names on a resume (e.g., "MAGMA" - Meta, Apple, Google, Microsoft, Amazon) can unlock opportunities, especially for executives bringing scale expertise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:02">[00:13:02]</a>, merely collecting logos without meaningful building experience can lead to dissatisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>.

## Mid-Career: Promotions and Management

[[getting_into_and_growing_in_product_management | Getting into and Growing in Product Management]] often involves navigating promotions and considering a management path.

### Reasons for Not Getting Promoted

When a PM isn't getting promoted, common reasons include <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:10">[00:26:10]</a>:
1.  **Lack of Advocacy**: No one sees or champions your "magic" within the organization, necessitating a shift in project, manager, or company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>.
2.  **No Role Exists**: Especially in periods of slower growth or restructuring (e.g., layoffs, flattening management layers), the next logical role for promotion may simply not be available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>.
3.  **Impatience**: High performers often set aggressive promotion goals (e.g., 18 months instead of 2 years), but leadership roles require more soft skills, subtlety, and often have a lagging impact, leading to frustration if progression isn't as rapid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>.
4.  **Unrecognized Development Area**: Around 50% of cases involve a substantive development area that the individual doesn't acknowledge or is unwilling to change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:57">[00:28:57]</a>. Managers might fail to identify it, or the individual might refuse to hear it, feeling it contradicts their identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:10">[00:29:10]</a>.

### Challenges and Advice for New Managers

A significant [[common_challenges_faced_by_product_teams_and_how_to_overcome_them | challenge]] in the tech industry is the lack of formal training for managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:30">[00:35:30]</a>. The assumption that being a good individual contributor (IC) automatically qualifies one to manage is flawed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:46">[00:35:46]</a>.

**Key Advice for New Managers**:
*   **Share the Steering Wheel**: Management is not about simply dividing and conquering or teaching someone to ride a bike and letting go. It's more like being in the sidecar of a motorcycle â€“ attached, offering counsel, and sharing responsibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:19">[00:37:19]</a>.
*   **Be Invited In**: Managers, despite having organizational power, must earn the right to manage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:21">[00:38:21]</a>. Start by asking how you can help, and allow your reports to invite you into their work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:57">[00:38:57]</a>. Be patient; they will eventually need assistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:21">[00:39:21]</a>.
*   **Prioritize the Meeting Operating System**: At scaled organizations, the way meetings are run is as important as the products being built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:42">[01:19:42]</a>. Treat meeting cadences, attendees, and decision-making processes like a product, iterating on them quarterly based on feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:12">[01:20:12]</a>. This can be especially impactful for new leaders with fresh eyes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:05">[01:21:05]</a>.

### The Rise of the IC Path

The Individual Contributor (IC) path, where PMs remain focused on building rather than managing people, is gaining prominence, partly due to the recent backlash against hyper-growth and the resulting restructuring of companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:17">[00:40:17]</a>. This shift is seen as a positive development for the industry because it allows PMs to become true experts in specific ambiguities (e.g., market, organizational, domain) without being forced into management roles they may not be suited for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:48">[00:41:48]</a>. The IC track is becoming a more "real" and promising career option, similar to established IC paths in engineering and design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:53">[00:42:53]</a>.

## Late Career: Senior Leadership and Beyond

### The "Shadows of Superpowers"

As PMs advance to senior leadership roles, they often encounter a phenomenon called "the shadows of superpowers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:35">[00:53:35]</a>. This refers to situations where a leader's greatest strength or "superpower" (e.g., collaboration, opinionation, growth orientation) inadvertently creates new development areas or challenges at a higher level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:48">[00:53:48]</a>. What got you *here* (to your current success) isn't necessarily what will get you *there* (to the next phase of your career) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:55">[00:53:55]</a>.

For example:
*   A "world-class collaborator" might struggle when their opinions are challenged by senior leaders, as they are used to collaboration meaning agreement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:44">[00:54:44]</a>.
*   A "great opinionator" might find that their decisiveness becomes an impediment when consensus-building and broad context are required at an executive level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:50">[00:59:50]</a>.

These are tough [[improving_product_management_skills | development areas]] to identify because they are often dismissed as anomalies by the individual or their manager, as they contradict the individual's core identity or past successes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:50">[00:57:50]</a>. Recognizing these shadows requires seeking "real" contradictory feedback from trusted sources and being willing to re-evaluate one's fundamental approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:00">[00:58:00]</a>.

### Mental Health and the "Act 3" of Career

A less-discussed [[challenges_in_goal_setting_and_execution_in_product_management | challenge]] for highly successful product leaders is the mental health impact of reaching career goals without a clear "what's next" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:25">[01:02:25]</a>. Careers can now span 60 years or more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:50">[01:03:50]</a>, meaning many reach their initial "North Star" (e.g., Vice President, company founder) in their 30s or 40s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:53">[01:05:53]</a>, with decades of work remaining.

This can lead to a feeling of being "lost" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:09">[01:04:09]</a>, similar to the analogy of a dog that catches the fake rabbit on a track and then refuses to run again because its purpose is achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:51">[01:04:51]</a>. Without a new, meaningful goal, individuals may continue working without purpose, leading to sadness, creating unnecessary conflict, or developing unhealthy habits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:20">[01:06:20]</a>.

### Finding Your Next North Star

To combat this, it's crucial to proactively think about the "Act 3" of one's career and establish a new "North Star" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:06">[01:06:06]</a>.

While one path involves pursuing scaled economics (e.g., investing, private equity) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:09">[01:09:09]</a>, another fulfilling path is centered around "giving" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:41">[01:09:41]</a>. This doesn't necessarily mean abandoning professional pursuits, but integrating a purpose of contributing to others or society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:22">[01:10:22]</a>. Examples include:
*   Content creation and sharing knowledge (like podcasts or newsletters) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:28">[01:07:28]</a>
*   Volunteering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:32">[01:10:32]</a>
*   Starting mission-based companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:32">[01:10:32]</a>
*   Coaching and mentoring others to change their trajectory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:37">[01:11:37]</a>

The "Act 3" offers liberation to pursue a powerful blend of livelihood and contribution, which may have been challenging during early career stages due to various constraints <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:03">[01:11:03]</a>.

## Overarching Opportunities

### The Power of Community and Feedback

A key opportunity for product managers at all stages is leveraging community for learning and growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:16">[00:46:16]</a>. Active communities (e.g., Slack channels, Discord, Zoom calls) provide a safe space for authentic conversations, shared experiences, and best practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:27">[00:46:27]</a>. This is particularly valuable when internal company structures (like L&D departments or managers) fall short in providing adequate training and support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:42">[00:46:42]</a>.

When seeking feedback to identify [[improving_product_management_skills | development areas]], prioritize "real feedback" over formal channels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:41">[00:30:41]</a>. To encourage honest feedback:
*   Listen intently without justifying or dismissing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:31">[00:31:31]</a>.
*   Repeat the feedback back to the person to show understanding and internalization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:46">[00:32:46]</a>.
*   Create an environment of safety, assuring people there will be no retaliation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:53">[00:31:53]</a>.
*   Publicly acknowledge and appreciate specific individuals for their valuable feedback in team settings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:11">[00:34:11]</a>.

The ability to effectively receive and act on feedback is crucial for [[improving_product_management_skills | improving product management skills]] and career advancement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:04">[00:32:04]</a>.
