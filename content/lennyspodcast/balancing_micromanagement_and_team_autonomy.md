## Failure Modes in Leadership

There are two common failure modes for individuals taking on their first [[coaching_and_leadership_in_product_management|leadership]] role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:16">[01:06:16]</a>:

1.  **Direct Micromanagement**: This occurs when leaders don't grant their team members the necessary autonomy to find a path forward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:20">[01:06:20]</a>. This approach can make team members feel untrusted and limits the number of people a leader can effectively manage due to bandwidth constraints <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:31">[01:06:31]</a>. It's often characterized by treating direct reports as an extension of oneself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:47">[01:06:47]</a>.
2.  **Completely Hands-Off Leadership**: In this mode, a leader assumes trust and grants autonomy but fails to provide essential context, guardrails, or frameworks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:52">[01:06:52]</a>. While seemingly avoiding micromanagement in the short term, this can lead to the team moving in the wrong direction or not achieving necessary goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:51">[01:08:51]</a>.

## Effective Leadership Strategies

Rather than seeing micromanagement as universally bad, it's about understanding when and how to apply different levels of engagement. Leaders should aim to increase their "dynamic range" â€“ the ability to think strategically while also being able to zoom into specific details when necessary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:54">[01:07:54]</a>.

The following matrix framework helps to categorize effective leadership:

### 1. Scalable Leadership

This is the ideal state where the leader feels confident in the team's direction, and the team has the autonomy to move effectively in that direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:12">[01:08:12]</a>. This is achieved when leaders provide enough framework and context, allowing the team to arrive at solutions that might even surpass the leader's initial ideas, fostering a sense of empowerment and confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:42">[01:10:42]</a>.

### 2. Selective Micromanagement

Micromanagement is not inherently bad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:21">[01:07:21]</a>. Some of the most innovative leaders, such as Steve Jobs, Elon Musk, and Mark Zuckerberg, have been known as micromanagers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:25">[01:07:25]</a>. As product builders and innovators, the details matter, and sometimes a leader needs to engage at a granular level, like the text on a specific button <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:33">[01:07:33]</a>.

> "If you don't feel confident in the direction that your team is moving, the right answer is not to be hands-off and to let them go in that wrong direction. The right answer is to micromanage, but do it in a very tactical, in a very temporary way, so that you can help them understand what is the right direction moving forward so that you can then pull back." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:25">[01:08:25]</a>

The purpose of selective micromanagement is to:
*   **Get the team back on track** by any means necessary, including temporary, detailed guidance on decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:03">[01:10:03]</a>.
*   **Teach frameworks**: As the leader intervenes, they should actively articulate the frameworks they are using for decision-making, helping the team internalize these principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:21">[01:10:21]</a>.
*   **Aim for a temporary state**: The ultimate goal of selective micromanagement is to enable the team to understand the right direction, allowing the leader to pull back and return to a scalable [[Product Leadership and Management Techniques|leadership]] mode <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:45">[01:08:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:37">[01:09:37]</a>.

### Alignment and Confidence

A useful framework for [[mentorship_and_team_leadership|managers]] and their reports involves considering two factors:
*   **Alignment**: The degree to which you are aligned with your manager's direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:33">[01:11:33]</a>.
*   **Confidence**: The degree to which your manager has confidence in your abilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:36">[01:11:36]</a>.

In an ideal scenario, there is a high degree of both alignment and confidence, leading to full support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:40">[01:11:40]</a>. However, even if there isn't complete alignment on direction, having your manager's confidence can grant you permission and support to pursue a different path <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:45">[01:11:45]</a>. If both alignment and confidence are low, it indicates a critical situation where either alignment must be pursued, or confidence must be built to allow for alternative paths <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:08">[01:12:08]</a>. The [[Product Leadership and Management Techniques|product leader]]'s role is to ensure the team is heading in the right direction to avoid wasting time and resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:17">[01:11:17]</a>.
