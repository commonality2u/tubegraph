Developing products with artificial intelligence (AI) presents unique challenges and opportunities due to the rapidly evolving nature of the technology itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. Unlike traditional product development built on a fixed technology base, AI capabilities are constantly expanding, with computers able to perform new tasks every few months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. This necessitates a continuous re-evaluation of how products can be designed and how users interact with them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:00">[02:02:00]</a>.

## Challenges in AI Product Development

### Rapidly Evolving Technology Base
One of the primary challenges in [[the_future_of_ai_in_software_development | AI product development]] is the dynamic nature of the underlying technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. New capabilities emerge frequently, making it difficult to plan product roadmaps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:00">[01:58:00]</a>. Researchers themselves may not fully know the extent of a new model's capabilities until it's developed, or how effective it will be (e.g., 60% good vs. 99% good), which significantly impacts product design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:31:00">[06:31:00]</a>.

### Designing for Non-Deterministic Systems
AI models introduce a non-deterministic element to user interfaces, meaning the same inputs may not always yield the exact same outputs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:00">[02:24:00]</a>. This contrasts sharply with traditional computer interactions where predictable outcomes are expected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:32:00">[02:32:00]</a>. Product design must adapt to this inherent unpredictability, focusing on feedback mechanisms, graceful failures, and understanding aggregate user behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:32:00">[02:32:00]</a>.

### Enterprise vs. Consumer Applications
While consumer products can quickly adapt to new user behaviors, enterprise applications face additional complexities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:00">[02:47:00]</a>. Enterprise deployments involve existing organizational processes, diverse user technical skills, and a need for predictable, often long-term, outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:05:00">[04:05:00]</a>. Success in enterprise often hinges on fulfilling the buyer's goals, which may not always align directly with product usage satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:02:00">[05:02:00]</a>. Educational materials and power users within organizations become crucial for broader adoption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:00">[02:55:00]</a>.

## Key Skills for Product Managers in AI

The role of a Product Manager (PM) is evolving in the age of AI, requiring new and deeper skill sets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:00">[01:40:00]</a>.

### The Importance of Evaluations (Evals)
The quality of an AI-powered feature is increasingly "gated on how well you have done the evals and the prompts" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:00">[01:20:00]</a>. Many models are currently "eval limited," meaning they have more intelligence than what can be effectively utilized due to a lack of precise evaluation methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>. Writing good evals is becoming a core skill for PMs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:00">[01:37:00]</a>. This involves:
*   **Defining Success:** Clearly articulating what problem the AI is solving and what success looks like <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>.
*   **Iterative Improvement:** Continuously refining models based on evaluation feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>.
*   **Data Analysis:** Directly reviewing model outputs to understand failures and successes, rather than relying solely on aggregate metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:00">[01:28:00]</a>.
*   **Adapting Evals:** As models handle more ambiguous, long-form, and personalized tasks, evaluations may shift from "right or wrong" to more nuanced "performance review" style assessments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>.

### Prototyping with AI Models
Product managers can use AI models themselves to rapidly prototype user interfaces and compare design options, accelerating the iteration cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>. This allows for a much greater variety of experimentation and faster evaluation than traditional design methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>.

### Deeper Understanding of the Tech Stack
PMs will need to go deeper into the technical stack to understand how AI models work, even if they don't become researchers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>. An appreciation for the language and intuition behind AI development is crucial for effective product building <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>.

## The Future of AI Applications

The [[the_future_of_ai_in_software_development | future of AI]] is moving towards more intelligent, interactive, and integrated experiences across both consumer and enterprise domains.

### Proactive AI Models
Future AI models will become more proactive, capable of monitoring information (e.g., emails with user authorization) and offering timely assistance without explicit prompts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:50:00">[03:50:00]</a>. Examples include:
*   Summarizing daily activities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:50:00">[03:50:00]</a>.
*   Preparing research for upcoming meetings or presentations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:50:00">[03:50:00]</a>.
*   Generating first drafts of documents or presentations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:50:00">[03:50:00]</a>.

### Asynchronous Interactions
AI will facilitate more asynchronous interactions, allowing users to initiate complex tasks and return to them later, rather than expecting immediate responses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:21:00">[03:21:00]</a>. This enables AI to undertake "mini project plans" like fleshing out a document, fixing bugs, or adapting strategies to new market conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:00">[03:46:00]</a>.

### Multimodal AI and Universal Translation
The ability for AI to interact in all human modalities (speaking, seeing, typing) will become commonplace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:00">[03:40:00]</a>. Advanced voice modes will enable universal translation, removing language barriers in travel and business interactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

## User Adaptation and Model Personality

Despite the unintuitive nature of some AI interactions, people are remarkably quick to adapt to new AI capabilities, rapidly normalizing what once felt like magic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:00">[02:39:00]</a>. This swift adaptation is evident in the rapid adoption of tools like ChatGPT <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:00">[02:59:00]</a>.

AI products are also increasingly designed to be educational, informing users about their features and how to best interact with them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:52:00">[02:52:00]</a>. Furthermore, AI models are beginning to develop distinct "personalities" or "behaviors," fostering a sense of relationship and two-way empathy with users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:43:00">[03:43:00]</a>. This emerging "model behavior" will be a key aspect of [[future of product management with ai | AI product design]], with questions arising about customization versus a singular personality, much like human relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:43:00">[03:43:00]</a>. Young generations, in particular, are already demonstrating a "native" understanding and expectation of these advanced AI interactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:00">[03:29:00]</a>.
