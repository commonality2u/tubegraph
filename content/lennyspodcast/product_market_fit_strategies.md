[[Product Market Fit and Growth Strategy | Product market fit]] is a critical aspect for any startup, as it dictates whether a product truly resonates with its target audience. Rahul Vora, founder and CEO of Superhuman, emphasizes that the goal is to make something people want, but it cannot be for "all people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>. His approach to [[finding_productmarket_fit_in_startups | finding product market fit]] involves a combination of strategic listening, disciplined execution, and even contrarian thinking.

## Measuring and Optimizing Product Market Fit

Rahul Vora states that [[framework_for_improving_productmarket_fit | product market fit]] can be measured, optimized, and systematically increased <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:17">[00:53:17]</a>. His method, often referred to as the "Superhuman [[framework_for_improving_productmarket_fit | product market fit engine]]", allows companies to algorithmically generate a roadmap guaranteed to increase their [[product_market_fit_and_growth_strategy | product market fit]] score <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:30">[00:53:30]</a>.

### The Sean Ellis Test
The core of this measurement is [[the_sean_ellis_test__understanding_product_market_fit | The Sean Ellis Test]], which asks users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:13">[00:55:13]</a>. The response options are:
*   "Very disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:18">[00:55:18]</a>
*   "Somewhat disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:21">[00:55:21]</a>
*   "Not disappointed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:22">[00:55:22]</a>

Sean Ellis found that companies struggling to grow typically had less than 40% "very disappointed" users, while the fastest-growing companies consistently had more than 40% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:40">[00:55:40]</a>. This metric is considered more predictive of success than Net Promoter Score (NPS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:55">[00:55:55]</a>.

### Focusing Feedback for Impact
To improve the [[product_market_fit_and_growth_strategy | product market fit]] score, the strategy is to focus on the "somewhat disappointed" segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:35">[00:56:35]</a>.
*   **Do not** act excessively on feedback from "very disappointed" users, as they already love the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:20">[00:56:20]</a>.
*   **Do not** act on feedback from "not disappointed" users, as they are essentially a lost cause for the current product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:25">[00:56:25]</a>.

Instead, within the "somewhat disappointed" group, identify those for whom the product's *main benefit* resonates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:46">[00:56:46]</a>. Disregard feedback from those who like the product for other reasons, as they are pulling the product in a different direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:15">[00:57:15]</a>.

At the start of each planning cycle, it's advised to dedicate half the time to doubling down on what users already love and the other half to systematically overcoming the objections of the "somewhat disappointed" users, specifically those who resonate with the product's core benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:01">[00:58:01]</a>.

## Contrarian Product and Company Building Approaches

### Strategic Investment in Detail
Superhuman adopted a high level of attention to detail, even in the early stages before broad launch. For example, they customized the Adele Sands font for their UI, optimizing for aesthetics, message conveyance, reading speed, and how email addresses look <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:01">[00:33:01]</a>. This deliberate focus on minute details, even before widespread adoption, highlights a confidence that the product *would* work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:36">[00:38:36]</a>, and that for a mission-critical product like email, a "half-baked" solution is unacceptable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:24">[00:42:24]</a>.

### Manual Onboarding for Brand & Focus
For years, Superhuman manually onboarded every new user with a one-on-one concierge experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:47">[00:47:47]</a>. At its peak, about 20 people were dedicated to manual onboarding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:36">[00:48:36]</a>. This approach was chosen because:
1.  **Superior User Metrics:** It led to excellent engagement, retention, [[product_market_fit_and_growth_strategy | product market fit]] scores, NPS, and virality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:56">[00:49:56]</a>. This built an early cohort of "super fans" vital for brand building and word-of-mouth growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:15">[00:50:15]</a>.
2.  **Strategic Resource Allocation:** During periods of abundant funding (e.g., zero interest rate phenomenon), manual onboarding allowed engineers to focus entirely on [[product_market_fit_and_growth_strategy | finding product market fit]], solution deepening, or market widening, rather than building a first-time user experience or activation loops <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:33">[00:50:33]</a>. This avoided spending engineering resources on self-service flows for products that might not ultimately find [[product_market_fit_and_growth_strategy | product market fit]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:07">[00:51:07]</a>.

Manual onboarding ceased when a significant portion of potential users expressed a preference against it, necessitating a world-class self-service option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:05">[00:49:05]</a>.

### Game Design over Gamification
Rahul Vora advocates for making business software like games, not by merely adding points or badges (gamification), but by integrating principles of "game design" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:30">[01:00:30]</a>. Gamification often fails because rewards undermine intrinsic motivation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:04">[01:02:04]</a>, as demonstrated by the Stanford drawing study <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:47">[01:00:47]</a>.

Superhuman focuses on five key areas of game design: **Goals, Emotions, Toys, Controls, and Flow** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:56">[01:02:56]</a>. A key principle is "make fun toys and then combine those into games" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:06">[01:03:06]</a>. A "toy" in product design is a feature that:
*   Indulges playful exploration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:12">[01:05:12]</a>.
*   Is fun even without a specific goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:14">[01:05:14]</a>.
*   Elicits moments of pleasant surprise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:17">[01:05:17]</a>.

An example is Superhuman's "time autocompleter," which allows users to type natural language (e.g., "2D" for 2 days, "in a Fortnite and a day") to set reminders, even handling time zone math or allowing "never" to snooze an email <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:41">[01:03:41]</a>.

### Pricing Strategy
Superhuman's pricing strategy is based on its positioning as the "best email tool on the market" for "high performing teams and high performing individuals" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:06">[01:06:06]</a>. They validated this through data, showing users process email twice as fast, respond one to two days faster, and save four hours or more every week <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:26">[01:06:26]</a>.

They used the **Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:04">[01:07:04]</a>, asking four questions:
1.  Too expensive (not considered) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:15">[01:07:15]</a>
2.  Too cheap (worried about quality) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:22">[01:07:22]</a>
3.  Starting to get expensive (requires thought, but still considered) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:29">[01:07:29]</a>
4.  A bargain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:40">[01:07:40]</a>

Most startups price at the "bargain" point (Number 4) to maximize top-of-funnel sign-ups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:46">[01:07:46]</a>. However, Superhuman chose the "starts to feel expensive" price point (Number 3), which was $30 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:07">[01:08:07]</a>. This price supports their "best-in-class" positioning and implies a significant ROI for users due to time savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:13">[01:08:13]</a>.

## Navigating Growth Stages

Companies often experience an "unavoidable slowdown" as they transition from **solution deepening** (making the product better for existing users) to **market widening** (making the product available to more users) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:16">[01:15:16]</a>. For example, Superhuman initially focused on Gmail on the web, then had to invest years in building support for iOS, Mac, Windows, Android, and Office 365, which often goes unnoticed by existing users but is crucial for broader market reach and enterprise sales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:06">[01:16:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:15">[01:17:15]</a>.

An "avoidable slowdown" can occur if the CEO's time becomes too fragmented by management responsibilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:55">[01:17:55]</a>. Rahul Vora used a "switch log" to track his time, realizing he spent only 6-7% on product, design, technology, and marketing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:54">[01:18:54]</a>. By hiring a president, he reduced his direct reports from eight to two, freeing up 60-70% of his time for his "zone of genius" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:39">[01:19:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:49">[01:19:49]</a>. This highlights that organizational design can be a powerful lever to regain velocity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:15">[01:30:15]</a>.

## Decision Making: Single Decisive Reason (SDR)

Rahul Vora employs a thinking tool called the **Single Decisive Reason (SDR)**, learned from Reid Hoffman at LinkedIn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:17">[01:19:17]</a>. For important decisions, there should be *one reason* that, on its own, is sufficient to support the decision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:31">[01:19:31]</a>. This prevents relying on a collection of weak reasons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:41">[01:19:41]</a>, which can be misleading, especially in group settings where consensus or risk aversion might bias decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:09">[01:20:09]</a>. If a team cannot isolate the SDR, it suggests they haven't fully figured out why they want to make the decision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:33">[01:20:33]</a>.
