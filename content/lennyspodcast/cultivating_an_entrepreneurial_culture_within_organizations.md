From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

To [[innovating_within_large_organizations | innovate within large organizations]] and remain competitive, a company needs to stay entrepreneurial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:39">[01:16:39]</a>. This involves constantly thinking about what's next, defining industry standards, and anticipating competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:47">[01:16:47]</a>. An [[entrepreneurial_mindset_and_leadership_insights | entrepreneurial mindset]] facilitates the development of new products and the improvement of internal processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:08">[01:18:08]</a>. Figma, for instance, explicitly encourages a "run with it" culture, allowing individuals to pursue ideas that are seen as a manifestation of company values rather than a distraction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:07">[01:17:07]</a>.

## The Role of the "Keeper of the Flame"
Bringing a zero-to-one idea to fruition within an organization is akin to being the "Keeper of the Flame" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:11">[01:19:11]</a>. Much like Hestia in Greek mythology, whose job was to keep the hearth burning, the individual or team driving a new idea must constantly stoke the flames and embers to prevent them from dying out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:25">[01:19:25]</a>. It's also their responsibility to ensure the idea spreads like wildfire, building momentum and hype <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:51">[01:19:51]</a>.

## Key Elements for Cultivating an Entrepreneurial Culture

### 1. Developing a Compelling Vision
A strong vision is paramount because the product development cycle is often chaotic, with extreme highs and lows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:28]</a>. An anchored vision ensures that any step, even a pivot, feels like forward progress and a [[the_role_of_organizational_culture_in_experimentation_success | learning opportunity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>.

#### Crafting the Vision
*   **User and Team Immersion**: A compelling vision cannot be created in a vacuum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. It requires being "fundamentally inseparable" from both users and the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:32">[00:09:32]</a>.
*   **Cross-Pollination of Functions**: Integrate research insights, beautiful designs and prototypes, and engineering feasibility from the start <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:42">[00:09:42]</a>. This ensures the vision is achievable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>.
*   **Visual Communication**: Words can only go so far <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:28">[00:11:28]</a>. Pitches should combine pain points, solutions, and proof points with visuals like prototypes and mocks, rather than just traditional timelines and costings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. Figma, for example, leans heavily into designing and prototyping even before a project gets a green light, subscribing to a "see to believe and see to feel" philosophy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:02">[00:18:02]</a>.
*   **Unified Artifact**: Create a single artifact, like a Figma deck, that the entire team contributes to and feels ownership over <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:13">[00:17:13]</a>.

#### Getting Buy-in
*   **Develop Strong Conviction**: Possess an "almost like reality distortion field" where "no" is interpreted as "not yet" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:22">[01:23:22]</a>. This [[entrepreneurial_mindset_and_leadership_insights | strong conviction]] allows navigating the messy journey from zero to one and rallying the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:05">[00:22:05]</a>.
*   **Generate Momentum**: Constantly create forward progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:37">[00:23:37]</a>. Adopting a "two-way door" mindset (as in Jeff Bezos's framework) allows for flexibility and iteration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:48">[00:22:48]</a>.
*   **Have an Opinion**: Even if potentially wrong, putting out an idea is a better catalyst for a good solution than starting from nothing, as people are more likely to react to something than nothing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:15">[00:25:15]</a>. Aim for an "A-minus idea" to take into user research, which can then evolve into an A-plus idea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:51">[00:25:51]</a>.
*   **Strong Opinions, Weakly Held**: Be willing to "kill your darlings" if external signals or user feedback suggest a different direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:22">[00:26:22]</a>.
*   **Direct Communication**: Be explicit about the level of confidence in an opinion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>. Foster a culture where everyone feels comfortable speaking up and providing feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:19">[00:29:19]</a>.

### 2. Hacking Hype and Spreading Ideas
Hype is crucial for both internal buy-in and external user excitement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:54">[00:38:54]</a>.
*   **Leverage Large Forums**: Utilize company-wide events like sales kickoffs or annual showcases (e.g., Figma's Config) to present products and generate excitement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:55">[00:36:55]</a>.
*   **Show, Don't Tell**: Demos, even if the product is rough around the edges, can create significant hype by allowing people to *see* what you envision for the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:56">[00:35:56]</a>. Early visibility, even beyond the product's current stage, can provide incredible learnings and fuel for the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:18">[00:37:18]</a>.
*   **Emotional Connection**: Hype is tied to emotion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:14">[00:41:14]</a>. Create moments that make users feel special and happy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:30">[00:41:30]</a>. For example, FigJam celebrated its anniversary by hiding new features as "birthday presents" for users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:35">[00:40:35]</a>.
*   **Strategic Dogfooding**: Figma uses a multi-month dogfooding process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:10">[01:27:10]</a>. This transparent approach, where products are put on staging early, provides critical feedback and makes people invested in the product because they feel they "shaped that part of the product" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:23">[01:28:23]</a>. This fosters a collective investment in success, as product development is a team sport <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:55">[01:28:55]</a>.

### 3. Embracing Bottoms-Up Initiatives
Many of Figma's monumental launches, like multi-edit, Jambot, and the widgets platform, originated from hackathons and bottoms-up projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:24">[01:17:24]</a>.
*   **Maker Week / Hackathons**: These events provide "breathing space" for people to look ahead and be ambitious <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:09">[00:33:09]</a>. It's a mistake to think hackathons are only for engineers; anyone with an idea can participate, gather a team, and build momentum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:58">[02:00:58]</a>.
*   **Overcoming Skill Gaps**: Utilize readily available tools (like AI for coding or design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:55">[01:59:55]</a>) or simply ask for help from colleagues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:21">[02:01:21]</a>. Never let personal skills hinder the pursuit of an idea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:16">[02:21:16]</a>.

### 4. Deep User Immersion and Empathy
Understanding users is key to developing the "right idea" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:17">[02:21:17]</a>.
*   **Constant Conversations**: Engage in frequent conversations with users, even in informal settings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:34:41">[02:34:41]</a>. These anecdotes build an intuition and a "repository or library" of insights for product decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:59">[02:39:59]</a>.
*   **Interview Non-Users**: Asking non-users *why* they aren't using a product can yield the most insightful conversations, revealing perceptions and marketing challenges beyond just product features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:39">[02:44:39]</a>.
*   **Leverage Sales Teams**: For larger companies, maintain a tight relationship with the sales team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:41">[02:46:41]</a>. Participate in sales calls to understand customer pain points and inform the product roadmap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:52">[02:46:52]</a>. Create artifacts (like "weekend in FigJam" videos) that the sales team can use to evangelize products and their diverse use cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:01">[02:49:01]</a>.
*   **Operationalize Insights**: Store feedback in an actionable system (e.g., Slack integration to Asana tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:12">[02:50:12]</a>) for weekly grooming and prioritization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:30">[02:50:30]</a>.

### 5. [[creating_and_maintaining_team_culture_and_passion | Creating and Maintaining Team Culture]]
A strong [[leadership_and_company_culture | company culture]] builds trust among team members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:07">[01:05:07]</a>.
*   **Passion for People**: Beyond passion for the product vision, being passionate about the people you work with makes the day-to-day more enjoyable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:32">[01:05:32]</a>.
*   **Invest in Connection**: Roadmaps and products change, but a connection to colleagues makes a team more durable when times get tough <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:36">[01:05:36]</a>. This connection fosters rallying and collaboration rather than fracturing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:47">[01:05:47]</a>.
*   **Rituals and Celebrations**: Implement fun rituals like "Hot Seat," where team members ask personal questions to build connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:46">[00:59:46]</a>. Organize celebrations like "Figgies" (Oscar-style awards) to appreciate team members' contributions and quirks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:45">[01:02:45]</a>. These efforts can be low-cost and scrappy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:45">[01:06:45]</a>.
*   **Play as a Value**: Embrace "play" as a core value, emphasizing that work and gatherings should be fun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:50">[01:03:50]</a>.

### 6. Dealing with Constant Change and Adaptability
Successfully [[entrepreneurship_lessons_and_strategies | launching new initiatives]] often requires adapting to unforeseen circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:12">[01:11:12]</a>.
*   **Scrappiness**: Be highly adaptable when resources or plans change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:43">[01:14:43]</a>. For example, when an external organization declined to fund a conference, its founder proactively sought funding and speakers herself, building it from the ground up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:06">[01:10:06]</a>.
*   **High Agency**: Possess the mindset of "I will make this happen myself" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:03">[01:12:03]</a>. This involves being proactive in seeking solutions and driving initiatives forward even without formal backing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:12">[01:11:12]</a>.

### 7. Understanding and Catering to Individual Motivations
Running a team successfully and driving an idea forward hinges on understanding motivations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:09">[01:30:09]</a>.
*   **Individual Contribution Styles**: Recognize that different team members (engineers, designers, researchers, data scientists) have varying desires for involvement in product visioning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:27">[01:30:27]</a>. Some prefer solutions handed to them, while others need to contribute to feel passionate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:33">[01:30:33]</a>.
*   **Cater to the Individual**: Constantly cater to the individual rather than the average, allowing decisions to be made in the open where team members can voice their perspectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:50">[01:30:50]</a>.

## Potential Downsides
While strong conviction and scrappiness are powerful, they can have downsides:
*   **Skepticism of Conviction**: Initial skepticism may arise if team members are unfamiliar with a PM's strong convictions, potentially leading to questions about whether an idea is driven by personal belief or user need <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:58">[01:13:58]</a>. This necessitates consistently highlighting user proof points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:12">[01:14:12]</a>.
*   **Team Preference for Planning**: Scrappiness and a "last-minute" approach, while enabling agility, may not be preferred by all team members who might value more structured planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:42">[01:14:42]</a>.
*   **Getting Consumed by Details**: A focus on details can be beneficial, but knowing when to defer decisions is also important <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:55">[01:14:55]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Superpowers have shadows: "Every superpower has a shadow," meaning that every strength can also have a corresponding liability or downside that needs to be managed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:20">[01:15:20]</a>. It's crucial to focus on leveraging strengths while being self-aware of blind spots, and to hire for complementary skills to build a well-rounded team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:51">[01:15:51]</a>.

## The Importance of Self-Awareness
Ultimately, cultivating an entrepreneurial culture and driving new ideas requires an [[entrepreneurial_mindset_and_leadership_insights | entrepreneurial mindset]] and a deep understanding of human dynamics, both individual and organizational <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:09">[01:30:09]</a>. If an individual has an insight that others aren't seeing, it is their responsibility to evangelize it and bring others onto the same page, which in turn fosters a more entrepreneurial spirit throughout the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:33">[01:29:33]</a>.
