From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

[[importance_of_productmarket_fit_for_startups | Finding Product Market Fit]] is the single most crucial endeavor for a startup during its initial three years, yet it remains an underexplored and underexplained topic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. Many startups, particularly B2B sales-led ones, find themselves stuck at early stages of [[levels_of_productmarket_fit_and_their_significance | product market fit]], often burning through resources without making progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>.

First Round Capital, drawing from data across hundreds of startups, has developed a practical and actionable framework to help founders navigate this journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:00">[01:30:00]</a>. This framework outlines four distinct levels of [[the_concept_of_product_market_fit_and_its_evolution | product market fit]] and emphasizes the importance of understanding which level you are in, recognizing signs of being stuck, and knowing how to adjust your strategy through **pivoting** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>.

## The Four Levels of Product Market Fit
The ultimate goal for any company is to achieve **Extreme Product Market Fit**, defined as a state of widespread demand for a product that satisfies a critical need and can be delivered repeatably and efficiently to each customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:00">[01:18:00]</a>. This involves balancing three key ideas: demand, satisfaction, and efficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:00">[01:40:00]</a>. However, startups don't pursue all three simultaneously from the outset <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:00">[01:53:53]</a>. [[the_concept_of_product_market_fit_and_its_evolution | Product Market Fit]] unfolds in a sequence of levels over multiple years, typically taking 4 to 6 years for the best Enterprise companies to reach the extreme stage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:00">[02:01:00]</a>.

The four levels are:
1.  **Nescent** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:00">[02:16:00]</a>
2.  **Developing** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>
3.  **Strong** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>
4.  **Extreme** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:00">[02:22:00]</a>

## Signs of Being Stuck and How to Pivot

### Level 1: Nescent Product Market Fit
At this earliest stage, your company is likely pre-seed or seed-stage with fewer than 10 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:00">[02:46:00]</a>.
*   **Focus:** Your primary job is to identify 3-5 customers who share an **important and urgent problem** and deliver a **satisfying solution** that fulfills a promise they deeply care about <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:00">[02:54:00]</a>.
*   **Priorities:** Focus on **satisfaction first**, then demand, with efficiency being the least important at this stage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>. It's acceptable to be inefficient if it helps uncover what delivers insane customer satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:00">[02:24:00]</a>.
*   **Benchmarks:**
    *   0 to 500k ARR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:00">[02:42:00]</a>.
    *   Demand largely comes from your personal network (friends, family, VCs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:00">[02:13:00]</a>.
    *   It's hard to find customers; it might take 20 warm intros to get one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:00">[02:26:00]</a>.
    *   Metrics like burn multiple, gross margin, and NRR are not yet applicable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:00">[02:54:00]</a>.
*   **Signs You're Stuck (12+ months in):**
    *   Your product disappearing overnight wouldn't deeply disappoint customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>. They "like" you, but don't "love" or "need" you; you're "friendzoned" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:00">[02:37:00]</a>.
    *   You have a few happy customers, but the most important feature is different for each of them (more like a consulting business than a product business) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:30:00">[02:30:00]</a>.
    *   It's incredibly hard to find the next new customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:43:00">[02:43:00]</a>.
    *   Product usage is low and not growing much <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:00">[02:49:00]</a>.
*   **How to Pivot:** This is a common stage to get stuck <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:00">[02:14:00]</a>. If you're stuck for 6-12 months, it's time to consider a pivot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:00">[02:18:00]</a>. This involves using the **Four Ps** framework:
    *   **Persona:** Who are you targeting?
    *   **Problem:** What critical problem are you solving for them?
    *   **Promise:** What value are you delivering or pitching?
    *   **Product:** What are you building to deliver on that promise?
    *   **Example (Lattis):** Jack Alman's Lattis started as an OKR tool, but customers weren't satisfied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:54:00">[02:54:00]</a>. He kept the **Persona** (Heads of HR) but changed the **Problem** (Performance Management) and the **Promise** and **Product** to address this new, more urgent need <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:07:00">[03:07:00]</a>. This pivot, made even with Figma mockups, unlocked significant demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:00">[03:11:00]</a>.
    *   **Example (Vanta):** Christina Copo's Vanta pivoted completely, changing all four Ps from early ideas like "B2B Alexa" to compliance automation, driven by listening to customer pain points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:03:00">[03:03:00]</a>.
    *   **Example (Plaid):** Plaid started as a consumer budgeting app. When it struggled, founders realized the banking connection component they built (the **Product**) was valuable to other developers. They pivoted their **Persona** (from consumers to fintech developers), **Problem** (from budgeting to connecting bank accounts), and **Promise** (API for bank accounts) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:00">[03:22:00]</a>.

    The advice is to focus on changing the Persona, Problem, and Promise first, as these are generally easier to iterate on than rebuilding your entire product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>. Look for a more burning pain or a more valuable promise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. Psychologically, it's hard to throw away past efforts, but a "200% pivot" might be necessary instead of just a 10% tweak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:00">[03:11:00]</a>.

### Level 2: Developing Product Market Fit
At this stage, you're likely a seed or Series A company with up to 20 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:01:00">[04:01:00]</a>.
*   **Focus:** Your job is to grow from 5 satisfied customers to 25 satisfied customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:24:00">[03:24:00]</a>.
*   **Priorities:** You must start thinking about **demand** in addition to satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>. The product needs to do a lot of the heavy lifting for you to scale beyond 10 customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:53:00">[03:53:00]</a>.
*   **Benchmarks:**
    *   500k to 5 million ARR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>.
    *   Developing scalable demand channels beyond warm intros (e.g., cold outreach, content, community events) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:10:00">[04:10:00]</a>.
    *   Sales conversion (first call to close) around 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:35:00">[04:35:00]</a>.
    *   Magic Number (New ARR / CAC) between 0.5 and 0.75 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>.
    *   Regretted churn not worse than 10-20% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:17:00">[04:17:00]</a>.
    *   NRR (Net Revenue Retention) at least 100% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:21:00">[04:21:00]</a>.
    *   Gross margin not worse than 50% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:25:00">[04:25:00]</a>.
    *   Burn multiple not worse than 5x <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:33:00">[04:33:00]</a>.
*   **Signs You're Stuck (12-18+ months in):**
    *   Current customers are happy, but you're struggling to open the demand floodgates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:00">[04:37:00]</a>.
    *   Regretted churn is greater than 20% (a satisfaction warning sign) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:07:00">[04:07:00]</a>.
    *   Sales cycles are too long, you're losing deals late in the funnel, or struggling to hit your desired price point (customers say "no budget" or "bad timing") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:22:00">[05:22:00]</a>.
    *   You're not getting known for your product or receiving much organic inbound interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:48:00">[04:48:00]</a>.
*   **How to Pivot:** If you're stuck at this level, it's often because your demand source isn't repeatable, or your product isn't solving a sufficiently urgent problem. You need to adjust your approach to demand and potentially re-evaluate your Four Ps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:22:00">[05:22:00]</a>.
    *   **Example (Looker):** Looker struggled at Level 1 initially because its product required customers to see their own data to understand its value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:38:00">[04:38:00]</a>. They cracked Level 2 by developing a "forward deploy process" where they'd spend significant time (20-40 hours) setting up the product with a customer's data before even closing the sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>. This high-touch, inefficient process delivered immense customer satisfaction and led to rapid sales growth once they found a repeatable way to deploy it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:49:00">[04:49:00]</a>.
    *   **Example (Ironclad):** Ironclad began as an "AI legal assistant" but struggled to find buyers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:12:00">[04:12:00]</a>. They pivoted their **Promise/Positioning** from creating a new category to playing within an existing one ("CLM" - Contract Lifecycle Management) but offering a **better Product** (AI-powered automation) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:17:00">[04:17:00]</a>. This change clicked because customers were already looking for CLM solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>.

### Level 3: Strong Product Market Fit
This is where [[the_concept_of_product_market_fit_and_its_evolution | product market fit]] starts to feel real, with increased momentum and organic demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:33:00">[05:33:00]</a>.
*   **Focus:** Your job is to achieve **repeatability**, moving from 25 to 100+ satisfied customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:43:00">[05:43:00]</a>. The marginal customer should be much easier to acquire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:45:00">[05:45:00]</a>.
*   **Priorities:** You've cracked at least one scalable demand channel (marketing and sales) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:28:00">[05:28:00]</a>. Now, **efficiency** must come into focus, in addition to maintaining satisfaction and demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:49:00">[05:49:00]</a>.
*   **Benchmarks:**
    *   30-100+ people in your company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:14:00">[05:14:00]</a>.
    *   5 million to 25 million ARR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>.
    *   At least 10% of inbound leads come from referrals and word-of-mouth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:18:00">[05:18:00]</a>.
    *   Gross margin above 60% (ideally 70%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:29:00">[05:29:00]</a>.
    *   Burn multiple below 3x (ideally 1-3x) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:35:00">[05:35:00]</a>.
    *   Regretted churn less than 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:43:00">[05:43:00]</a>.
    *   NRR greater than 110% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:48:00">[05:48:00]</a>.
*   **Signs You're Stuck:**
    *   A "leaky bucket" where NRR is below 90% or regretted churn is above 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:34:00">[06:34:00]</a>.
    *   Growth is slowing down (e.g., struggling to do 2x after 3x growth in prior years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:40:00">[06:40:00]</a>.
    *   Saturation of your first scalable channel; you need to find new channels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:46:00">[06:46:00]</a>.
    *   Growing, but spending too much money to do so, pushing your burn multiple above 3x <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:54:00">[06:54:00]</a>.
*   **How to Pivot:** At this stage, it's about optimizing efficiency, deepening market penetration, or potentially expanding into new product lines or markets to maintain growth. The challenge is navigating increased competition in a tougher market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:51:00">[06:51:00]</a>.

### Level 4: Extreme Product Market Fit
Congratulations! At this level, you have a valuable company, likely a unicorn, aiming to become a decacorn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:27:00">[07:27:00]</a>.
*   **Focus:** Maintain high levels of satisfaction, demand, and efficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:29:00">[07:29:00]</a>.
*   **Priorities:** The main challenge is sustained growth by expanding your Total Addressable Market (TAM). This often means bringing your product into new markets or developing multiple products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:42:00">[07:42:00]</a>.
*   **Benchmarks:**
    *   Team size greater than 100 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:44:00">[07:44:00]</a>.
    *   Series C/D or beyond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:48:00">[07:48:00]</a>.
    *   More than 100 customers, aspiring to reach thousands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:51:00">[07:51:00]</a>.
    *   Beyond 25 million ARR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:56:00">[07:56:00]</a>.
    *   Sales conversion (first call to close) better than 15% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:06:00">[08:06:00]</a>.
    *   Magic Number greater than 1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:10:00">[08:10:00]</a>.
    *   CAC payback less than 12 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:12:00">[08:12:00]</a>.
    *   Gross margin above 80% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:15:00">[08:15:00]</a>.
    *   Burn multiple less than 1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:19:00">[08:19:00]</a>.
    *   Churn less than 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:22:00">[08:22:00]</a>.
    *   NRR greater than 120% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:23:00">[08:23:00]</a>.
*   **Challenges:** The "never-ending journey" requires finding [[the_concept_of_product_market_fit_and_its_evolution | product market fit]] again and again for new products or market expansions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:44:00">[08:44:00]</a>. Customer expectations are constantly rising, adding another layer of challenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:24:00">[09:24:00]</a>.

## Timelines for Product Market Fit
The entire journey to Level 4 typically takes 4-6 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:11:00">[10:11:00]</a>.
*   **Level 1:** Ideally, 12 to 18 months. This is arguably the most important stage, as it involves choosing the right Persona and Problem to focus on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:19:00">[10:19:00]</a>.
*   **Level 2:** Roughly 1 year. The goal is to move from 5 to 25 customers quickly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00">[10:31:00]</a>.
*   **Level 3:** Approximately 2 years. This involves scaling revenue from 5 million to 25 million ARR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:36:00">[10:36:00]</a>.
*   **Level 4:** Ongoing, often several years to get from 25 million to 100 million ARR and beyond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:48:00">[10:48:00]</a>.

Approximately 60-70% of companies never progress beyond Level 1 or Level 2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:53:00">[10:53:00]</a>. If a founder has been working on a product for 4-5 years and hasn't felt any significant pull from the market, it's often wise to consider returning money to investors or seeking a soft landing, as [[the_concept_of_product_market_fit_and_its_evolution | product market fit]] is incredibly difficult to achieve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:21:00">[11:21:00]</a>.

## The Four Ps for Pivoting (When Stuck)
When facing [[signs_of_a_lacking_or_poorly_implemented_product_strategy | signs of being stuck]], especially at Level 1 or 2, the **Four Ps** provide a structured approach to identifying what needs to change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:00">[03:22:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:28:00">[11:28:00]</a>. These are:
1.  **Persona:** Your target customer. Think of the market as a collection of people with problems and money to spend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:36:00">[11:36:00]</a>. Get deeply into their mind, understanding their challenges, goals, and how they succeed at their job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:42:00">[11:42:00]</a>.
2.  **Problem:** The critical pain point you are solving for your Persona.
    *   Employ [[developing_customer_discovery_methods_for_productmarket_fit | dollar-driven customer discovery]] to test the dollar potential of your hypothesis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:48:00">[11:48:00]</a>.
    *   Identify "extreme value" by asking non-leading questions about their top goals and what makes them hard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:59:00">[11:59:00]</a>. Look for "wow" statements ("Does that really work? If so, I'd sign up for a waitlist today!") or demonstrated interest (e.g., asking for a follow-up meeting or to share the deck) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:59:00">[11:59:00]</a>.
    *   Confirm "ability to pay" by asking if they are currently looking for such a product, building an internal solution, or if there's an existing budget (e.g., for a competing tool) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:59:00">[11:59:00]</a>. Understand their decision-making process for third-party tools <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:20:00">[12:20:00]</a>.
    *   Gauge "willingness to pay" by asking about their budget, what they pay for similar tools, and fair, expensive, and prohibitively expensive prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:20:00">[12:20:00]</a>.
3.  **Promise:** How you describe or position your product to your Persona, addressing their Problem. This is often where a simple change in framing can unlock demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:40:00">[04:40:00]</a>. It's often easier to pivot within an existing category with a better product than to create an entirely new category <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>.
4.  **Product:** What you are building to deliver on the promise and solve the problem for the Persona. The fidelity of your early product or demo should align with what's needed to secure a sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:44:00">[12:44:00]</a>.

Founders must be willing to iterate on these Four Ps, especially the Persona, Problem, and Promise, as they are easier to change and test than rebuilding the Product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>. If you can predict 70-80% of what the next person will say during customer interviews, you've talked to enough people and likely found patterns to guide your pivot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:51:00">[12:51:00]</a>.

This comprehensive approach helps founders identify [[challenges_in_early_startup_growth_and_product_pivots | challenges in early startup growth and product pivots]], avoid being stuck, and increase their odds of achieving strong and eventually extreme [[product_market_fit_strategy | product market fit]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>.
