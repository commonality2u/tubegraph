## Why Use Metaphors and Imagery?

*   **Rallying Teams:** Metaphors and imagery can unite a team around a common vision, making abstract concepts more tangible and relatable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45">[01:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:28">[26:28]</a>.
*   **Creating Shared Narrative:** A compelling [[the_impact_of_storytelling_on_memory_and_communication | narrative]] or story allows team members to internalize an idea and make independent decisions that remain consistent with the overall vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:01">[31:01]</a>.
*   **Simplifying Complexities:** Analogies help break down difficult subjects, making them easier to grasp and remember <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:52">[29:52]</a>.
*   **Guiding Product Development:** By defining the desired "feeling" of a product through a metaphor, designers and engineers can intuitively make choices that create a cohesive and consistent user experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:30">[31:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:36">[34:36]</a>.

## Key Metaphors and Their Applications

### The "Dinosaur Brain" in Product Reviews

To encourage product teams to be more concise and make clear recommendations in product reviews, Amy Vora uses the "dinosaur brain" metaphor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:24">[17:24]</a>.

*   **Concept:** Executives and senior leaders often have to deal with a vast breadth of information across many areas, making it difficult for them to go deep on every single detail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:42">[18:42]</a>. Their "dinosaur brain" can only hold about three facts at a time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:18">[18:18]</a>.
*   **Application:** The best service a team can provide in a product review is to simplify information and present a clear recommendation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:32">[18:32]</a>, rather than overwhelming with data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:40">[17:40]</a>. The manager owns the context, while the team owns the recommendation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:39">[19:39]</a>. This fosters a complimentary relationship where different views and new information are valued <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:09">[20:09]</a>.

### The "Hill Climb" for Strategic Shifts

The "hill climb" metaphor describes the journey from a local optimum to a global optimum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:55">[26:55]</a>.

*   **Concept:** Organizations often optimize their current systems, reaching a "local optimum" (the top of a hill) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:07">[27:07]</a>. However, a "global optimum" (a distant mountain) might exist, requiring a risky descent into a "valley" (unknown territory) before climbing to a higher peak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:23">[27:23]</a>. This often involves giving up something that is working well without a guarantee of success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:28">[28:28]</a>.
*   **Application:** This metaphor helps to set expectations for periods of difficulty or stagnation during major strategic shifts, such as moving from desktop to mobile platforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:19">[29:19]</a>, or even personal life changes like new jobs or relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:19">[28:19]</a>. Remembering the feeling of the "summit" (the ultimate success) helps endure the "slog" of the valley <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:03">[29:03]</a>.

### "Putting on the Coat of the Job" for Career Decisions

This metaphor helps in evaluating potential job roles by imagining the day-to-day experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:56">[09:56]</a>.

*   **Concept:** Instead of relying solely on "spreadsheet math" about career progression, this approach involves an emotional simulation of the job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:38">[08:38]</a>.
*   **Application:** Questions like "What would it be like if I were doing this job?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:12">[09:12]</a>, "What would I think about on my commute?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:16">[09:16]</a>, and "Who would I have lunch with?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:17">[09:17]</a> elicit an emotional response that can be more telling about fit and satisfaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:23">[09:23]</a>. This often leads to choosing roles where one feels "at home" due to trust with colleagues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:35">[09:35]</a>.

### "Toddler Soccer" for Goal Setting

This metaphor describes a common pitfall in goal alignment within organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:53">[56:53]</a>.

*   **Concept:** When all teams are given the same broad goal (e.g., "get revenue"), they tend to "run to the same surface" or customer segment, tripping over each other without coordination, much like toddlers playing soccer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:56">[56:56]</a>.
*   **Application:** To avoid this, it's crucial to decouple goals, assigning each internal team a different objective that ladders into an overall framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:23">[57:23]</a>. This creates "plenty of room for people to make progress on their lane" and ensures the full customer journey is addressed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:56">[57:56]</a>. Goals can be based on input metrics that lead to the desired output, allowing teams to optimize for their specific area <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="58:48">[58:48]</a>.

## How to Develop the Skill of Using Metaphors

1.  **Focus on User Feeling:** When creating a metaphor, think about how you want users to feel when interacting with the product and what real-world experiences evoke that same feeling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24">[00:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:36">[34:36]</a>.
    *   **Example: WhatsApp** The product metaphor for WhatsApp was "face-to-face [[techniques_for_effective_customer_communication_and_empathy | communication]]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:00">[33:00]</a>. The goal was to make users feel connected to others, even when geographically separated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:17">[32:17]</a>. This guided small details like typing indicators, checkmarks, and the visual design of calls, all contributing to a sense of intimacy and presence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:10">[34:10]</a>. Another example was "sitting around in your family room" for video calling, emphasizing lightweight, joinable calls over formal conference calls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:39">[36:39]</a>.
2.  **Build "Emulators" of Other Thinkers:** Learn from leaders who excel in different areas by mentally simulating how they would approach a problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:19">[30:19]</a>.
    *   **Example Emulators:**
        *   **Eric Antino:** Focuses on the emotional "feeling" a product evokes, leading to analogies for user experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:47">[29:47]</a>.
        *   **Bos:** Prioritizes [[communication_frameworks_for_clarity_and_influence | principle-based decision making]] and explores the long-term ramifications of those principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:53">[35:53]</a>.
        *   **Rob Goldman:** Emphasizes looking at the dashboard and metrics to guide decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:03">[36:03]</a>.
    *   **Benefits:** This mental exercise provides new lenses and toolkits for solving problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:05">[35:05]</a>, making work more interesting and preventing boredom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:54">[34:54]</a>.

### The Power of Curiosity in Disagreement

Amy Vora is known for her profound curiosity in moments of disagreement, a trait praised by Meta's CTO, Bos <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:47">[10:47]</a>. Instead of reacting with defensiveness, she responds with genuine interest, often saying, "Fascinating, you have to tell me more why you think that" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05">[00:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:35">[10:35]</a>.

*   **Sublimating Ego:** This approach requires setting aside the desire "to be right" and prioritizing the best outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15">[00:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:05">[11:05]</a>. Others often possess information that one doesn't <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:54">[11:54]</a>.
*   **Learning Opportunity:** Disagreements become opportunities to learn new things and gain a deeper understanding of how others perceive the world, even when looking at the same facts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21">[12:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:16">[15:16]</a>.
*   **Positive Feedback Loop:** By being curious, one learns more, leads to better outcomes, and fosters positive relationships, outweighing the initial desire to be right <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:12">[14:12]</a>.
*   **Technique for Controlling Visceral Reactions:** When faced with a strong disagreement, take a pause <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:29">[16:29]</a>. This allows the body to calm down and the mind to process, leading to a more constructive, open response rather than a primal, protective one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:33">[16:33]</a>. Acknowledge healthy tension and differing information as a sign of knowledge, and agree on the ultimate outcome to guide the conversation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:21">[01:00:21]</a>.
