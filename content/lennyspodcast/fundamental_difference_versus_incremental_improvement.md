From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

NuBank, a company larger than Coinbase, Robinhood, Affirm, Sofi, and Lemonade combined, attributes 80-90% of its growth to word-of-mouth referrals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>. This success stems from a core philosophy: "We're not trying to be incrementally better, we are trying to be fundamentally different" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>. This principle guides their product development, aiming for customers to "love us fanatically" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>.

## Why "Fundamentally Different" Matters

According to Jag Dugall, Chief Product Officer at NuBank, while many companies spend vast sums on marketing, investing in making the product great is a "much better scenario" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a>. This approach ensures that customers will enthusiastically tell their friends, leading to a strong viral acquisition loop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:13">[00:18:13]</a>.

Great execution, when paired with a poor strategy, results in a "waste of everyone's time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:48">[00:44:48]</a>. A clear [[improving_strategic_thinking_through_practice_and_betterment | strategy]] that aims for fundamental difference prevents diffused execution and allows for a rapid assessment of focused bets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:24">[00:48:24]</a>.

> [!quote] The Problem with Incrementalism
> "Incrementally better doesn't get you there unless you have that hook." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:04">[01:22:04]</a>

## Operationalizing "Fundamentally Different"

### Deep Pain Points & Customer Obsession
NuBank's initial success in Brazil was built on tapping into a "very deep pain point" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>: the traditional Brazilian banks were highly profitable but also "amongst the most hated in the world" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:16">[00:07:16]</a>.

A core value at NuBank, deeply ingrained in its employees, is the desire for customers to "love us fanatically" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>. When faced with difficult decisions, the company biases "in favor of the customer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>, prioritizing customer love over short-term financial or operational convenience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>.

### High Bar for Product Success
NuBank rigorously measures customer satisfaction to ensure fundamental difference:

*   **Shaun Ellis Score**: The company is a "fanatical follower" of the Shaun Ellis methodology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>. They rarely scale a product until at least 50% of Brazilian customers (adjusting for cultural politeness) report they would be "very disappointed" if the product went away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:53">[00:15:53]</a>.
    *   This metric is used during iteration and before full launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:49">[00:17:49]</a>.
    *   It helps identify specific user segments that *do* love the product, allowing for targeted improvements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:12">[00:23:12]</a>.
*   **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: NuBank is "addicted" to its NPS score, aiming for "world-beating" results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:51">[00:16:51]</a>. Scores typically range from 70s to 90s, with any dip causing alarm <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:27">[00:17:27]</a>.
*   **Churn**: Low churn rates are crucial for a viral customer acquisition loop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:01">[00:18:01]</a>.

> [!quote] "Good Enough Isn't Good Enough"
> During product reviews, NuBank leadership asks, "Is it great enough?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:51">[00:20:51]</a>. The objective is to redefine the category on dimensions of quality, complexity, and price, often simultaneously breaking traditional tradeoff constraints <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:56">[00:13:56]</a>.

### Product Design & Iteration
The development process emphasizes:

1.  **Mock Press Releases**: Before engineering work begins, teams must articulate "why the intended customer should care" in a two-paragraph press release <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:18">[00:13:18]</a>.
2.  **Customer Discovery**: Focus on innovating for customers, not just asking them what they want <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>. Observing customer pain points, even when they don't notice them, is key <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:42">[00:10:42]</a>.
3.  **Direct Customer Contact**: Product managers are encouraged to "pick up the phone yourself" and call 10 customers directly to understand their feedback, rather than relying solely on summarized research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:28">[00:26:28]</a>. This direct feedback is critical for rapid iteration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:02">[00:27:02]</a>.
4.  **Hypothesis-Driven Research**: Teams must articulate a very clear hypothesis before conducting research. The goal of research is to validate or invalidate this hypothesis, not to simply gather data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:10">[00:36:10]</a>. It's important to avoid becoming a "lawyer for my hypothesis" and instead act as a judge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:43">[00:36:43]</a>.
5.  **Strategic Patience**: Products are not scaled aggressively until they demonstrate strong product-market fit, sometimes waiting two to two-and-a-half years before widespread launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:59">[01:02:59]</a>. This prevents scaling "a small problem and scale it" into "a big mess" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:43">[00:29:43]</a>.

## Strategic Implications

This focus on fundamental difference aligns with principles of [[improving_strategic_thinking_through_practice_and_betterment | strong strategy]]. Strategy is defined not as an ambitious goal or aspiration, but as "a coherent plan for how you going to apply your strengths in a leveraged way against a core important problem" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:15">[00:46:15]</a>.

For startups, "concentration is what builds wealth, diversification is what preserves wealth" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:39">[00:48:39]</a>. This means making "concentrated bets" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:50">[00:48:50]</a> that redefine categories, rather than incrementally improving existing ones, even if it feels "desperate" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:49">[00:50:49]</a>.

NuBank's initial success was built on creating a new category: a branchless bank built for the rise of smartphones in Brazil and Latin America <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:43">[00:54:43]</a>. This allowed them to offer a "no fee credit card" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:10">[00:55:10]</a>, which was an emotional differentiator in a market with high fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:10">[00:55:10]</a>. Even when entering an existing market, the mindset should be to "reinventing that category" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:13">[00:57:13]</a>, not just [[copying_competitors_vs_innovation | copying competitors]].

## Future Vision: AI-Native Products

The "fundamentally different" mindset extends to future technological shifts. Just as companies needed to become "mobile native" during the shift from desktop to mobile, today companies need to figure out "what does AI native mean" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:16">[01:11:16]</a>. This involves designing products where AI is at the "heart" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:51">[01:11:51]</a>, rather than just appended to existing features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:19">[01:11:19]</a>.

NuBank envisions a future where banking is "fundamentally different" than in the last 100 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:44">[00:59:44]</a>. They believe that social and AI technologies can democratize access to a "personal banker" for all 8 billion people on the planet, helping them navigate their "harshly unoptimized financial lives" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:49">[01:07:49]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:06">[01:00:06]</a>.
