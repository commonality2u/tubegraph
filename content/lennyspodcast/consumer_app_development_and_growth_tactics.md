## Early Ventures and the Pivot to Consumer Apps

Before his success in consumer apps, Nikita developed a web app called **Potify** during college, which aimed to help American voters understand the financial impact of presidential policy proposals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:36:00">[06:36:00]</a>. This tool went "super viral," attracting around four million users during an election season <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:39:49">[07:39:49]</a>.

The success of Potify led to the formation of a company called **Outline**, securing government contracts, including one with the Obama Administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:04:00">[08:04:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:33:00">[08:33:00]</a>. However, a government shutdown caused contract cancellations, leading Nikita to realize his true competency lay in making things go viral on the internet, not selling software to governments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:00">[08:42:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:57:00">[08:57:00]</a>.

This led to a pivot towards consumer apps, a challenging transition from web development. Nikita quickly learned that mobile apps have a "low margin for error" where "every tap on a mobile app is a miracle" due to users' high frequency of switching between apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:21:00">[14:21:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:41:00">[14:41:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:52:00">[14:52:00]</a>. Over four to five years, his team built approximately 15 different mobile apps—including mapping, chat, and event apps—many of which were "Duds" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:01:00">[15:01:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:48:00">[15:48:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:52:00">[15:52:00]</a>. This iterative process helped them develop a "muscle" for understanding user desires and how to make things grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:39:00">[10:39:00]</a>.

## Strategies for Targeting Teen Users

Nikita and his team eventually focused on **[[building_apps_for_teens_and_market_insights | building apps for teens]]** due to several key insights:
*   **Malleable Habits** Teenagers' habits are more adaptable; older users are "fixed into our habits of using certain communication products" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:55:00">[10:55:00]</a>.
*   **High Invitation Rate** Younger users (13-18) are significantly more likely to invite others to new apps. The number of invitations sent per user drops by 20% for every additional year of age from 13 to 18 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:18:00">[11:18:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:00:00">[12:00:00]</a>. This means if you build for adults, you'll likely need to acquire every user with ads, requiring significant venture capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:07:00">[12:07:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:31:00">[12:31:00]</a>.
*   **Daily Interaction** Teens see each other daily at school, facilitating in-person social spreading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:28:00">[11:28:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:29:00">[11:29:00]</a>. This "highest urgency to communicate" makes them an ideal target for social products with network effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:23:00">[18:23:00]</a>.

## TBH: Insight, Viral Growth, and Scaling Challenges

Nikita's major breakthrough came with **TBH**, an anonymous polling app sold to Facebook for over $30 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:00">[02:11:00]</a>.

### The Insight: Latent Demand and Positive Interactions
The idea for TBH stemmed from two observations:
1.  **A Snapchat Trend (TBH)** Teens were playing a game on Snapchat where they'd post images with emojis and positive affirmations, asking friends to reply with an emoji indicating their feelings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:22:00">[25:22:00]</a>. This showed a "venue for disclosure" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:49:00">[25:49:00]</a>.
2.  **Saraha's Popularity** The #1 app in the U.S. App Store was Saraha, an anonymous messaging app entirely in Arabic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:06:00">[00:26:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:21:00">[26:21:00]</a>. This was a "strongest signal" of "latent demand" – people seeking a particular value (anonymous feedback) but using a "distortive process" (a foreign language app, often leading to negativity) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:28:00">[26:28:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:36:00">[26:36:00]</a>.

Nikita realized that users wanted to "feel good about themselves" without the bullying associated with anonymous apps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:27:00">[27:27:00]</a>. TBH's innovation was to use pre-authored, positive-only polls ("who has the best smile," "most likely to be president"), ensuring anonymity while delivering exclusively positive affirmations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:37:00">[27:37:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:10:00">[28:10:00]</a>. This led to "explosive adoption," with one school sending 450,000 messages in seven days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:33:00">[28:33:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:38:00">[28:38:00]</a>.

### Viral Growth and Seeding Strategy
TBH was launched in a Georgia school with the earliest start date to accelerate testing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:14:00">[21:14:00]</a>. The app spread to 40% of the school in 24 hours <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:26:00">[21:26:00]</a>.
Their "seeding" strategy involved:
*   **Saturation Marketing** Running ads targeted at a specific school and creating dedicated Instagram accounts that followed students, knowing high schoolers often put their school in their bio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:00:00">[30:00:00]</a>.
*   **Synchronous Adoption** The goal was to get an "intensity of adoption and density" within a small group to kickstart network effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:58:00">[30:58:00]</a>.
*   **Reproducible Testing** This manual, non-scalable method was used to "eliminate confounding variables" and quickly determine if the product "had legs" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:11:00">[31:11:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:50:00">[31:50:00]</a>. The advice is to "test the best possible version" even if it requires manual work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:20:00">[32:20:00]</a>.

### Hyper-Growth Challenges
At its peak, TBH received 360,000 daily downloads <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:43:00">[24:43:00]</a>. However, hyper-growth created "absolute chaos" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:13:00">[35:13:00]</a>:
*   **Server Crashing** Servers immediately started crashing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:39:00">[21:39:00]</a>.
*   **Rapid System Failure** Systems broke every few days, requiring constant replacement and "ruthless prioritization" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:53:00">[34:53:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:00:00">[35:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:20:00">[35:20:00]</a>.
*   **Geofencing** They had to geofence the app, limiting its availability to specific states, to "control growth" and allow time to scale servers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:58:00">[28:58:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:42:00">[35:42:00]</a>.
*   **Product-Market Fit**: Nikita defines product-market fit in consumer apps as "abundantly obvious" where "people are going to be fighting to get into it," leading to metrics like "hourly actives per day" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:18:00">[36:18:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:52:00">[36:52:52]</a>.

## Product Management in Large Tech Companies vs. Startups

After selling TBH, Nikita worked at Facebook as a product manager for four years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:13:00">[37:13:00]</a>. His experience revealed a significant difference in the role of product management in large tech companies compared to startups:
*   **Detachment from Design** In large companies, PMs are "completely detached from the design process," mainly writing documents and acting as "team secretary" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:39:00">[39:39:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:55:00">[39:55:00]</a>. Nikita, who considers himself primarily a designer, found his skills "atrophied" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:09:00">[40:09:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:39:00">[40:39:00]</a>.
*   **Difficulty in Innovation** Large companies struggle to launch "completely new product zero to one stuff" because it's hard to intellectually justify unconventional ideas to VPs and align the company around them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:19:00">[41:19:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:31:00">[42:31:00]</a>.
*   **Risk Aversion** Incentives in big tech prioritize defensible projects with existing market signals, making it difficult to pursue hunches. It can take "12 to 24 months to respond to competitive threats" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:57:00">[45:57:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:02:00">[47:02:00]</a>.
*   **Products Live and Die in Pixels** For consumer products, especially standalone apps, the product manager should be deeply involved in "designing the hierarchy, the pixels, the flows" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:17:00">[51:17:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:28:00">[51:28:00]</a>.

### Combating the Human Trafficking Hoax
Gas faced a widespread hoax claiming it was used for human trafficking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:42">[01:04:42]</a>. Nikita's relentless response included:
*   Meeting with journalists to ensure headlines debunked the hoax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:28">[01:06:28]</a>.
*   Calling school superintendents and police chiefs to retract false public statements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:54">[01:06:54]</a>.
*   Asking Apple to remove false App Store reviews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:02">[01:07:02]</a>.
*   Creating a TikTok video explaining the truth, which users saw when attempting to delete their accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:10">[01:07:10]</a>. This effort reduced account deletions from 3% to 0.1% daily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:22">[01:07:22]</a>.
*   Even contacting the CEO of TikTok to have false videos removed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:11">[01:09:11]</a>.

### The Importance of Naming and Branding
During Gas's development, alternative names like "Crush" and "Melt" were tested. "Crush," with a pink icon, significantly reduced invitation rates, especially among boys <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:01">[01:16:01]</a>. By renaming to "Gas" with a black icon and a flame, the invite rate jumped, demonstrating how naming and branding can impact user acquisition by influencing user behavior and perception <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:49">[01:16:49]</a>.

Nikita acknowledges the challenge of building "durable" consumer social apps that last beyond a "summer song" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:47">[01:18:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:03">[01:19:03]</a>. He considers finding durability in communication/social products a "Black Swan event" with tremendous randomness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:55">[01:18:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:19">[01:19:19]</a>.

However, he firmly believes that "growing a product can be a science with certainty," where a skilled individual can make an app "grow and go viral" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:26">[01:19:26]</a>. His personal motivation lies in the thrill of watching a product "take over the Internet" as a small team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:50">[01:19:50]</a>.

## Key Lessons for Consumer App Development

### 1. Inverting Time to Value
Users have extremely short attention spans (around 3 seconds in 2024) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:27">[01:27:27]</a>. Products must demonstrate value immediately. Nikita emphasizes the concept of "inverting the time to value" so users experience the "aha moment in seconds" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:06">[01:27:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:10">[01:27:10]</a>. An example is the Dupe app, where users type "dp.com" in front of a URL to find the cheapest version of a product, providing instant value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:40">[01:28:40]</a>. This requires "extraordinarily creative" use of available tools and APIs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:23">[01:30:23]</a>.

### 2. Live Chat Customer Support
Placing 24/7 live chat customer support directly in the app provides a "white glove experience" and helps eliminate confounding variables related to user problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:45">[01:32:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:04">[01:33:04]</a>. Crucially, it's the best vehicle for user research, as users "literally tell you the problem they're having" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:18">[01:33:18]</a>.

### 3. Incremental Validation
For zero-to-one product development, it's essential to "compartmentalize" validation steps:
1.  Will people use the core flow?
2.  Will people spread it within their peer group?
3.  Will it hop peer groups? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:52">[01:11:52]</a>
"Execute at 100% for the thing you're trying to validate at that specific stage" and "half-ass the rest" to get clear signal on one part <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:12">[01:12:12]</a>. The product should be designed with "layers of conditional statements," ideally condensing to about four critical "must-be-true" elements for success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:54">[01:12:54]</a>.

### 4. Holistic Marketing and Product Growth
Founders often wrongly separate marketing and product growth. The entire user funnel, from ads to in-app experience and invitation mechanisms, needs to be aligned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:55">[01:33:55]</a>. If an ad shows a specific community, the app must allow users to join that community, and invites should mention it to maintain consistency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:55">[01:33:55]</a>.

### 5. Understanding User Motivations
Nikita categorizes primary reasons for app downloads into three main groups:
1.  **Make or save money** (e.g., WhatsApp for free texting) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.
2.  **Find a mate** (e.g., Tinder, Snapchat) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>.
3.  **Unplug from reality** (e.g., Netflix, Fortnite) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>.
There are also utilitarian categories like movement (Uber) or shelter (Airbnb) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:00">[01:23:00]</a>.
