## The Pitfalls of "Ex-Growth Companies"

Nikhail Singal, who has led large product teams at companies like Meta and Credit Karma, identifies a category he calls "ex-growth companies" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:00">[01:16:00]</a>. These are organizations that have raised substantial capital during periods of hyper-growth but are now struggling to find actual product-market fit in a changed economic environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>.

### Characteristics of Ex-Growth Companies
*   **Capitalized but Stagnant:** They possess significant capital but are not raising new rounds or actively experiencing organic growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:00">[01:25:00]</a>.
*   **"Scaled Ocean Liner" Mentality without Market Fit:** They are structured like large, established companies (e.g., hundreds of employees) but are still trying to discover what their core product is or who their customers are <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>.
*   **High Acquisition Effort:** A key indicator is the difficulty in acquiring users or customers, suggesting a lack of natural "pull" for their product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>.
*   **Misaligned Valuation:** Their valuation might be in the hundreds of millions or more, yet they are still in a phase where a pre-product-market fit valuation would be more appropriate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:00">[01:44:00]</a>.

For employees, especially product managers, staying in an "ex-growth company" can lead to significant opportunity costs and potentially worthless equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>. While loyalty or a unique learning opportunity (like an executive role) might be reasons to stay, it's generally advised to consider other options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:00">[02:17:00]</a>.

## Challenges in Management Structure and Growth

A significant issue identified in the tech industry's organizational dynamics is the lack of proper training for managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:32:00">[03:32:00]</a>. Often, individuals are promoted to management based on their technical or product-building skills, not their ability to lead or teach others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:00">[03:46:00]</a>. This creates an "epidemic" of challenges, including issues with bias, coaching, and addressing development areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:00">[03:19:00]</a>.

### Redefining Managerial Roles
New managers often face two common pitfalls:
1.  **Sharing the Steering Wheel:** They struggle to effectively share responsibility and decision-making with their team members, often defaulting to a "divide and conquer" or "teach to ride a bike" approach rather than a collaborative "sidecar" model where the manager provides counsel while the team drives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>.
2.  **Earning the Right to Manage:** Managers assume their organizational power grants them the right to intervene, rather than being "invited in" by their team members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a>. Earning trust and understanding where specific help is needed is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>.

### The Rise of the Individual Contributor (IC) Path
The increasing focus on efficiency and a reduction in management layers has led to a stronger emphasis on the Individual Contributor (IC) path in product management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:02:00">[04:02:00]</a>. This is seen as a positive development, correcting a long-standing "bug" in the industry where product managers were almost exclusively funneled into management roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:00">[04:26:00]</a>.

The IC path allows individuals to focus on building deep expertise in specific areas, such as:
*   Crafting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:00">[02:12:00]</a>
*   Market ambiguity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:00">[02:18:00]</a>
*   [[the_role_of_organizational_dynamics_in_strategy | Organizational dynamics]] (solving internal problems with complex teams) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>
*   Domain expertise (e.g., ML, hardware) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:35:00">[02:35:00]</a>
*   [[effective_growth_frameworks | Growth frameworks]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:00">[02:37:00]</a>

This specialization allows product professionals to become "world-class" in a particular "lane" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:21:00">[02:21:00]</a>, which is highly valuable for companies seeking specific expertise for growth, rather than just general management experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>. This shift is bringing product roles more in line with engineering and design, where strong IC paths have long existed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:43:00">[04:43:00]</a>.

## The Role of Community in Organizational Learning and Growth

Given the challenges in formal management training, community learning is emerging as a critical component for professional development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:16:00">[04:16:00]</a>.

### Benefits of Community
*   **Safety and Authenticity:** Communities provide a safe space for open, authentic conversations, allowing professionals to realize they are not alone in their struggles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>.
*   **Sharing Best Practices:** Members can share their experiences, solutions, and learn from how others have navigated similar challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:36:00">[04:36:00]</a>.
*   **Empowerment:** Tools like Slack, Discord, and Zoom have made it easier to create and sustain vibrant online communities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:40:00">[04:40:00]</a>.

Nikhail created "The Skip CPO Community" for Chief Product Officers to connect, share experiences, and support each other through the unique challenges of their roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>. This community helps leaders navigate complex situations and avoid feeling isolated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:00">[04:26:00]</a>.

## Optimizing Organizational Operations

Effective meeting management is a crucial aspect of [[the_role_of_organizational_dynamics_in_strategy | organizational dynamics]] and efficiency, especially in scaled organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:49">[01:19:49]</a>. Treating the "meeting operating system" as a product can significantly improve a team's ability to execute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:55">[01:19:55]</a>.

### Elements of an Effective Meeting OS
*   **Structured Cadence:** Define recurring meetings, their purpose, attendees, and decision-making processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:25">[01:20:25]</a>.
*   **Regular Review and Iteration:** Conduct quarterly reviews of the meeting structure, gather feedback, and iterate to improve efficiency and effectiveness, much like product versions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:41">[01:20:41]</a>.
*   **Delegation and Conversation Focus:** Ensure meetings foster the right degree of delegation and facilitate productive conversations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:00">[01:20:00]</a>.

This approach ensures that precious meeting time, which is often the most expensive time in a company, is used effectively and supports decision-making and acceleration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:49">[01:20:49]</a>. It's a key area for new leaders to focus on, as it allows them to identify inefficiencies with fresh eyes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:05">[01:21:05]</a>.
