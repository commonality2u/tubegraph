## Defining Product Positioning
April Dunford defines [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] as defining how a product is the best in the world at delivering specific value that a well-defined set of companies care a lot about <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:54">[00:12:54]</a>. It encompasses several key elements:
*   Alternatives to the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:11">[00:13:11]</a>
*   How the product is different <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>
*   Unique value delivery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:16">[00:13:16]</a>
*   The target audience that values this unique offering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:21">[00:13:21]</a>
*   The definition of the market category the product intends to win <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:25">[00:13:25]</a>

[[importance_of_proper_product_positioning_in_b2b_tech_companies | Proper product positioning]] is crucial because if a product isn't doing well, the problem may not be the product itself, but its [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>.

## Identifying Weak Positioning
Weak [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] can negatively affect every stage of the sales pipeline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:09:50]</a>. It's difficult to measure with a single metric, but common indicators observed in sales calls include:
*   **Confusion from prospects**: When customers ask the sales representative to "back it up and pitch it to me again" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>.
*   **Misconceptions about the product**: Prospects believing they know what the product is, but they're wrong (e.g., "Oh, you're just like Salesforce") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a>.
*   **Lack of perceived value**: Prospects understanding what the product does but not why anyone would pay for it (e.g., "I can do that in a spreadsheet") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:52">[00:11:52]</a>.
*   **Customer churn**: High churn rates due to customers realizing the product isn't what they thought it was or doesn't meet their expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a>.

A major cause of weak [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] is a lack of alignment across company teams (founder, marketing, sales, product) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:56">[00:13:56]</a>.

## The Positioning Process
Dunford's methodology for [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | effective product positioning strategies]] involves a structured approach, often a week-long sprint, to achieve [[common_challenges_and_solutions_in_aligning_company_teams_on_positioning | alignment]] across the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:40">[00:48:40]</a>. It's a "team sport" requiring input from marketing, product, sales, customer success, and the CEO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:45">[00:14:45]</a>.

The five key components are developed sequentially:
1.  **Competitive Alternatives**: Identify what the product needs to beat to win a deal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:38">[00:23:38]</a>. This includes direct competitors and the "status quo" (e.g., spreadsheets, manual processes), as roughly 40% of B2B deals are lost to "no decision" (meaning the status quo won) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:57">[00:23:57]</a>.
2.  **Differentiated Capabilities**: List the features, functions, or company attributes that the product offers that alternatives do not <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>.
3.  **Value**: Translate these differentiated capabilities into concrete value for the customer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:32">[00:25:32]</a>. This often results in two or three core value themes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:49">[00:25:49]</a>.
4.  **Best-Fit Customers**: Define the characteristics of target accounts that care deeply about the unique value offered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:27">[00:26:27]</a>.
5.  **Market Category**: Determine the context in which the product is positioned to make its value obvious to the target audience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:06">[00:27:06]</a>.

The output of this process is not just a document with bullet points, but a sales narrative – an actual pitch that can be tested and adopted by all customer-facing teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:59">[00:33:59]</a>.

### Example: Help Scout
April Dunford illustrates the process using Help Scout, a customer success software company competing against giants like Zendesk, but also against email or shared inboxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:57">[00:29:57]</a>:
*   **Alternatives**: Zendesk, other competitors, and the status quo (email, rudimentary shared inboxes) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:25">[00:30:25]</a>.
*   **Differentiated Capabilities**: Features focused on delivering amazing service, such as a proper service-oriented shared inbox, personalized customer interactions (no ticket numbers, assigned persons), and intelligent chat features that only appear when a human is available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:42">[00:30:42]</a>.
*   **Value**: Delivering amazing customer service that deepens customer relationships and drives growth through loyalty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:31">[00:31:31]</a>.
*   **Best-Fit Customers**: Direct-to-consumer and e-commerce brands that view customer service as a growth driver, not merely a cost center, unlike, for example, phone companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:52">[00:31:52]</a>.
*   **Market Category**: Positioned as a platform for modern e-commerce companies that prioritize customer loyalty and growth through exceptional service, contrasting with alternatives that treat service as a cost center <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:00">[00:33:00]</a>.

## Positioning vs. Messaging vs. Branding
Dunford emphasizes the distinction between these often-confused terms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:15">[00:40:15]</a>:
*   **[[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | Positioning]]**: A foundational input for messaging. It defines how a product wins in the market by understanding its competitive alternatives, differentiation, value, target, and market category <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:27">[00:40:27]</a>.
*   **Messaging**: The actual text on a homepage or in communications. It flows *from* the positioning; you can't write effective messaging until you understand who the message is for and what the unique value is <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:53">[00:40:53]</a>.
*   **Branding**: Defines what a brand stands for. Like messaging, it requires a clear understanding of [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] (target buyer and differentiation) to be distinct and effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:21">[00:41:21]</a>.

## Segmentation vs. Personas in B2B
Dunford clarifies the difference between market segmentation and buyer personas, especially in B2B contexts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:43">[00:54:43]</a>:
*   **Segmentation**: How a market is divided <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:24">[00:55:24]</a>. In B2B, this is typically based on "firmographics" (company size, revenue, geography) or specific characteristics like using a particular software package or having a certain team structure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:21">[00:56:21]</a>. Effective segmentation is actionable, allowing sales and marketing to identify and target specific companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:59">[00:56:59]</a>.
*   **Personas**: Focus on individual people and their characteristics, hopes, dreams, fears, and goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:51">[00:55:51]</a>. In B2B, a purchase involves multiple people (often 5-7) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:11">[00:58:11]</a>. Dunford argues that the most important persona is the "champion" – the individual whose job it is to build consensus and champion the deal internally, including selling it to the economic buyer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:48">[00:58:48]</a>. If the [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] doesn't resonate with the champion, the deal is dead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:12">[01:01:12]</a>. While other personas exist, the primary focus should be on arming the champion to sell to those other stakeholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:58">[01:01:58]</a>.

## When to Address Positioning
It's important to continuously check in on [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] as products and markets evolve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:01">[01:19:01]</a>.

### Early Stage Companies
For super early-stage companies, Dunford advises against over-tightening [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:58">[00:49:58]</a>. Instead, they should develop a "positioning thesis" – an initial best guess based on assumptions and customer discovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:11">[00:50:11]</a>. It's crucial to keep the [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] "a little bit loose" in the early days to allow the market to pull the product in unexpected directions, similar to a fishing net designed for tuna that might discover it's amazing for grouper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:08">[00:51:08]</a>.

### Growth Stage Companies
Companies should consider tightening their [[understanding_and_executing_effective_product_positioning_strategies | positioning]] when a clear pattern emerges regarding who loves the product and why <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:49">[00:52:49]</a>. This means observing common characteristics among successful customers, even if they initially appear diverse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:17">[00:53:17]</a>. Once this pattern is clear and repeatable, the company can "smash their foot on the gas" and run at that specific market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a>.
