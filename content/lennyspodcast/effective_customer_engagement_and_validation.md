From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Dalton Caldwell, a Managing Director and Group Partner at Y Combinator, emphasizes the critical role of [[Effective Customer Interaction and Feedback Gathering | effective customer engagement and validation]] in a startup's journey. With over a decade of experience across 21 YC batches, he has witnessed firsthand how staying close to customers and validating ideas can be the difference between success and failure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:51">[01:00:51]</a>.

## The Importance of Customer Connection
A recurring theme in Caldwell's advice is the necessity for founders to genuinely care about their customers and product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:42">[09:42]</a>. This deep connection can be a crucial signal to "keep going" even when faced with difficulties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:09">[10:09]</a>. He notes that successful founders, like those of Airbnb, truly loved their product and the people using it, even during challenging times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:52">[09:52]</a>. It's about being "obsessed with product" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:09">[35:09]</a>.

### Staying Close to the Product
Caldwell advises founders against over-delegating product responsibilities, especially in the early stages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:43">[32:43]</a>. Hiring super senior people with impressive resumes too early can lead founders to "take their eye off the ball" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:51">[32:51]</a>. Founders should remain deeply involved in product development and continue to talk to customers, regardless of the company's stage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:17">[33:17]</a>.

> "You can't delegate caring about your users and you can't delegate caring that the product is great that is so critical" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:31">[33:31]</a>.

Prioritizing time effectively means focusing on customer and product obsession over excessive networking or investor relations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:52">[34:52]</a>.

## [[How to effectively interview customers | How to Effectively Interview Customers]]

A common reason startups fail is that they don't talk to customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:04">[37:04]</a>. Caldwell provides [[Best Practices for Customer Interviewing | practical advice]] on how to approach customer conversations:

*   **In-Person Interaction is Key**: Avoid hiding behind keyboards or relying solely on landing pages and ads for validation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:23">[41:23]</a>. Founders need to overcome social anxiety and actively seek out physical meetings with potential customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:24">[42:24]</a>.
*   **Time Allocation**: As a self-assessment, founders should aim for roughly 20-30% of their time to be dedicated to in-person or direct customer meetings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:08">[43:08]</a>. This direct interaction is invaluable compared to just analyzing dashboards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:57">[43:57]</a>.
*   **Embrace Awkwardness**: Early customer interactions can feel awkward, as demonstrated by the early Airbnb founders who asked people to rent out their homes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:50">[42:50]</a>. Powering through this discomfort is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:58">[42:58]</a>.

### Building Conviction Through Feedback
Conviction in a startup idea is often built through a "Network effect virtual cycle" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:34">[51:34]</a>. The more positive feedback and data received from customers, the more confident founders become that they are on the right track <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:39">[51:39]</a>.

>[!TIP] Pre-selling Before Building
> Caldwell strongly advises aspiring founders to talk to potential customers and "pre-sell something before you write code" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:33">[01:11:33]</a>. If people are genuinely excited and willing to commit, that serves as a powerful "green light" to start building the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:03">[01:12:03]</a>.

## Avoiding Premature Growth Hacking
For very early startups, Caldwell holds a contrarian view: "growth and growth hacking and doing all this analytics AB testing stuff â€” is a total waste of time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:02">[01:06:02]</a>. Much of the widely available startup advice is geared towards later-stage companies, and applying it too early can be actively unhelpful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:19">[01:06:19]</a>.

If a startup has "no users," focusing on complex growth hacking theories or elaborate split testing is counterproductive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:24">[01:07:24]</a>. The focus should instead be on getting the very first customers and engaging with them directly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:58">[01:05:58]</a>.

## Examples of Effective Customer Engagement
*   **Brex & Retool**: These companies, which underwent significant pivots, found initial success by talking to other people within the YC batch or leveraging the YC network <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:18">[44:18]</a>.
*   **Zip**: The founders were "beasts at getting companies on calls with them to ask them about procurement" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:28">[44:28]</a>. They engaged in hundreds of conversations by messaging people on LinkedIn for advice, eventually converting them into early beta testers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:23">[45:23]</a>.
*   **PostHog**: After launching an open-source product, their calendars were filled with users eager to implement it and provide feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:52">[44:52]</a>.
*   **Stripe (The Collison Install)**: The founders actively helped customers implement their product, even showing up at offices and offering to "drive" the keyboard to ensure integration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:17">[46:17]</a>. This hands-on approach ensured customers didn't churn due to lack of implementation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:02">[47:02]</a>. Patrick Collison, co-founder of Stripe, was known for being extremely available and hands-on with early customers, checking in weekly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:37">[47:37]</a>.

These examples highlight that regardless of the product type, direct and consistent engagement with customers is paramount for [[Customer engagement and product development | product development]] and ultimately, success.
