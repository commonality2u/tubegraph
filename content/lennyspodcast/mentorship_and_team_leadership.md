## Leadership Philosophy: Coaching as a Team Sport
Caba views product management and [[coaching_and_leadership_in_product_management|leading product teams]] as a "team sport" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:01">[00:52:01]</a>. He emphasizes that not everyone needs to be a "star player" like LeBron James or Kobe Bryant; strong role players are essential and need to feel valued and have the right place on the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:21">[00:52:21]</a>.

Reflecting on his background in education, Caba draws parallels between managing a classroom of students and leading a product team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:11">[00:46:11]</a>. Just as a teacher needs to be a strong communicator, have a clear vision, and influence students to get on board, a product manager must also effectively communicate, cast vision, and influence engineers, designers, researchers, and marketing teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:51">[00:46:51]</a>. This perspective highlights the importance of [[coaching_strategies_for_ceos_and_leaders|coaching]] and enabling teams rather than leading top-down <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:33">[00:47:33]</a>.

### The Coaching Tree
Caba advocates for the concept of a "coaching tree" in [[leadership_and_personal_growth_as_a_ceo|leadership]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:54">[00:52:54]</a>. Similar to how college basketball coaches take pride in the success of their assistant coaches who go on to become head coaches, product leaders should focus on building up and growing their team members for their next roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:01">[00:53:01]</a>. Their success reflects the leader's impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:53">[00:53:53]</a>. This approach reinforces the responsibility of leaders to coach people to greatness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:01">[00:54:01]</a>.

## [[mentorship_and_coaching|Mentorship Strategies]]
Caba emphasizes that true [[mentorship_and_coaching|mentorship]] is not about having a single mentor, but rather a "stable of mentors"—ideally three or four individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:51">[02:00:51]</a>. Meeting with them once a month on different Fridays ensures continuous support and learning, even if one mentor is unavailable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:25">[02:20:25]</a>.

To find mentors, Caba advises:
*   **Articulate specific challenges or learning opportunities:** Instead of asking someone to be a mentor directly, explain what you are working on or the challenges you face <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:38">[02:07:38]</a>.
*   **Seek recommendations:** Ask trusted individuals if they know someone who has expertise in that specific area or good insights into the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:47">[02:09:47]</a>. This approach creates a "common purpose" and increases the likelihood of a mutually beneficial connection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:55">[02:11:55]</a>.

He also suggests learning by observing others, describing it as "stealing things from them" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:29">[02:29:29]</a>. By watching how other product managers hone their craft, deliver, and lead teams (e.g., attending team or leadership meetings), individuals can adopt effective techniques for communication, framework application, and message delivery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:07">[02:31:07]</a>. Being a "student of practice" is as important as being a "student of theory" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:31:51">[02:31:51]</a>.

## Developing Skills and Teams with Educational Frameworks
Caba uses educational frameworks to diagnose and address team challenges.

### Bloom's Taxonomy
He applies Bloom's Taxonomy, a framework for critical thinking, to understand where teams and individual product managers are struggling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:02">[00:48:02]</a>. The taxonomy's levels (knowledge, comprehension, application, analysis, synthesis, evaluation) help identify gaps:
*   **Knowledge:** Do they have the core information needed? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:33">[00:48:33]</a>
*   **Application:** Do they understand a concept but struggle to apply it in various scenarios? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:44">[00:49:44]</a>
*   **Synthesis/Evaluation:** Can managers connect their work back to the broader business context and strategy to prioritize effectively? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:15">[00:50:15]</a>

This framework helps leaders meet people where they are and provide targeted support, rather than just expecting them to "figure things out" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:05">[00:49:05]</a>.

### Shared Mental Models
Caba emphasizes the importance of establishing shared mental models and concepts within a team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:03">[00:44:03]</a>. For example, at YouTube, he found his teams lacked a mental model for "product craft" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:24">[00:44:24]</a>. He created a presentation to define his thinking, providing a framework that established a shared language and repository of skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:32">[00:44:32]</a>.

## Key Principles for Team Building and Cultural Change
When joining a new team or seeking to improve a culture, Caba uses a "Managing Complex Change" framework <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:51">[00:41:51]</a>. This framework suggests five essential components for successful change:
1.  **Vision:** A clear direction for the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:06">[00:42:06]</a>.
2.  **Skills:** The capabilities needed to execute the vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:07">[00:42:07]</a>.
3.  **Incentives:** Motivation for the team to engage in the work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:11">[00:42:11]</a>.
4.  **Resources:** Sufficient personnel, budget, and tools <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:16">[00:42:16]</a>.
5.  **Action Plan:** A clear roadmap for execution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:16">[00:42:16]</a>.

Missing any one of these components leads to specific negative outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:39">[00:42:39]</a>:
*   Missing Vision: Confusion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:45">[00:42:45]</a>
*   Missing Skills: Anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:53">[00:44:53]</a>
*   Missing Incentives: Resistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:50">[00:42:50]</a>
*   Missing Resources: Frustration (implied by the framework, not explicitly stated in transcript <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:11">[00:42:11]</a>)
*   Missing Action Plan: False Starts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>

As a leader, Caba observes what's happening and identifies which of these components are missing, then focuses on pulling the right levers to improve the team's performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:08">[00:43:08]</a>. Action plans are often easier to institute than changing vision or skills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:25">[00:43:25]</a>.

### The Role of the Manager
Within the "impact equals environment times skills" framework (related to career growth), Caba identifies the manager as the most important environmental variable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:21">[00:16:21]</a>. A great manager, who is empathetic, aware, and a strong communicator, has the ability to adjust scope, ensure the team has the right resources, and address cultural issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:29">[00:16:29]</a>. He advises employees to clearly articulate their challenges to their managers and tie them back to how they impact their work, seeking a plan to alleviate these issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:10">[00:17:10]</a>.

### Building Relationships and Understanding Stories
When joining a new team, Caba dedicates his first 90 days to observing team meetings and getting to know every team member—not just product managers, but also engineers, content designers, and others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:06">[01:36:06]</a>. He learns their names and their stories, understanding what they care about professionally and personally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:25">[01:36:25]</a>. This investment helps him understand how the team functions from each person's perspective and makes team members more receptive to his ideas when he eventually shares them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:48">[01:36:48]</a>. His experience as a dean in a boarding school taught him the universal nature of human challenges and the importance of valuing diverse voices to build world-class products that can scale globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:17">[01:39:17]</a>.
