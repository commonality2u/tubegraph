## Unique Challenges in AI Product Development

Unlike traditional product development, where one builds upon a fixed technology base <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>, [[challenges_in_building_ai_products | AI product development]] involves a constantly shifting foundation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>. Every few months, computers gain unprecedented capabilities, forcing a re-evaluation of how products should adapt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.

Key challenges include:
*   **Emergent Capabilities:** New models develop "emergent properties" during training, meaning their exact capabilities are unknown even to the research team until they emerge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>. This makes planning difficult, as product teams don't know if a capability will be 60%, 90%, or 99% good, which dramatically alters the product design <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>.
*   **Stochastic Nature:** AI systems are non-deterministic; the same input might not always yield the exact same output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a>. This fundamentally challenges decades of intuition built on deterministic computing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:24">[00:21:24]</a>.
*   **Evaluation Limitations:** Models today are often "eval limited" rather than intelligence limited, meaning they can do more than current evaluation methods capture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>. Human-written evaluations might not fully capture a model's superior performance or nuanced behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>.
*   **Enterprise vs. Consumer Products:** Enterprise products have longer feedback loops, requisition states, and deployment cycles, requiring patience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. User feedback in enterprise is direct and financially incentivized, unlike the aggregate data science of consumer products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>. Additionally, enterprise buyers often have different goals than the end-users of the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.

## The Evolving Role of Product Managers in AI

The [[impact_of_ai_on_product_development_roles | role of product managers]] (PMs) is rapidly changing, demanding new skills and a deeper understanding of the technology.

### The Primacy of Evaluations (Evals)
Writing good evaluations (evals) is becoming a core skill for PMs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:38">[00:14:38]</a>.
*   **Importance:** Evals help bridge the gap between a model's potential and its actual product utility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>. The quality of a feature is now gated on how well the evals and prompts are done <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:20">[00:15:20]</a>.
*   **Developing Skills:** PMs can learn to write good evals by asking the models themselves, which can provide sample evals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:06">[00:16:06]</a>. It is crucial to examine the raw data and actual answers, not just metrics, to understand where a model succeeds or fails <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a>.
*   **Future of Evals:** As models handle more long-form, ambiguous tasks (e.g., "get me a hotel in New York City"), evaluations will become less about right/wrong answers and more about nuanced performance reviews, akin to assessing a competent human <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a>.

### Key Skills for Product Managers
*   **Deeper Tech Understanding:** PMs need to go deeper into the tech stack to appreciate the underlying research and model behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:46">[00:19:46]</a>.
*   [[prototyping_with_ai_in_product_development | Prototyping with AI tools]]: [[using_ai_tools_for_product_management_and_development | Using AI tools]] to quickly generate UI options and perform A/B comparisons allows for faster iteration and evaluation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:05">[00:19:05]</a>.
*   **Designing for Non-Determinism:** PMs must adapt product design to systems where they are not fully in control of the model's output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:30">[00:20:30]</a>. This involves establishing feedback mechanisms, guardrails, and methods for understanding aggregate model behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:35">[00:20:35]</a>.

## Educating Users on AI Products

Users adapt incredibly quickly to new AI technologies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:13">[00:23:13]</a>. ChatGPT, for example, was mind-blowing two years ago but would now feel "so dumb" compared to current models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:03">[00:24:03]</a>. Despite rapid adoption, effectively educating end-users, especially non-technical ones in enterprise settings, is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:17">[00:26:17]</a>.

Strategies include:
*   **AI as an Educational Tool:** Products like Claude can directly inform users about their features and how to use them, providing documentation links and step-by-step assistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:01">[00:25:01]</a>.
*   **Leveraging Power Users:** Identifying and empowering internal power users within organizations can create evangelists who teach others how to use AI for specific tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:07">[00:27:07]</a>. Custom GPTs, for instance, allow power users to create tailored AI experiences that simplify adoption for less technical colleagues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:50">[00:26:50]</a>.
*   **Designing for Graceful Failure:** When models are less than perfect (e.g., 60% accurate), the product design must account for human intervention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:52">[00:10:52]</a>. For example, GitHub Co-pilot, even if imperfect, is valuable because it saves typing and allows users to edit its suggestions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>.

## The Future of Human-AI Collaboration

The future of AI product development will focus on [[the_role_and_evolution_of_ai_models_in_product_development | more sophisticated AI models]] and deeper collaboration between humans and AI.

Key trends:
*   **Proactive AI:** Models will become more proactive, monitoring user data (with authorization) to spot trends, provide summaries, prepare for meetings, or draft presentations, moving beyond reactive Q&A <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:50">[00:33:50]</a>.
*   **Asynchronous AI:** AI will handle longer, more complex tasks asynchronously. Users can initiate a project (e.g., "flesh out this mini project plan" or "fix this bug" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:06">[00:35:06]</a>), allowing the AI to "think" for minutes or hours, and the user can return when the task is complete <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:21">[00:34:21]</a>.
*   **Orchestration of Models:** Instead of a single model, complex tasks will involve workflows and orchestrations of multiple models, each used for what it's best at <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>. For example, a model good at reasoning (like O1) might pause to "think" and form hypotheses, while another model might check outputs or handle specific tasks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:58">[00:30:58]</a>. This approach mirrors human collaboration, where different individuals with varied skill sets work together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:07">[00:33:07]</a>. This has implications for [[role_of_ai_in_software_development | software development]], such as UI testing and data manipulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:11">[00:28:11]</a>.
*   **Multimodal Interaction:** AI will interact in all the ways humans do â€“ not just typing, but also speaking and seeing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:40">[00:35:40]</a>. Advanced voice modes enable universal translators, transforming communication across language barriers for business and travel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:06">[00:36:06]</a>.
*   **Human-like Relationships:** Users are starting to form nuanced relationships with AI models, perceiving distinct "personalities" and even developing empathy for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:45">[00:38:45]</a>. The "personality" of the model becomes a key product consideration, with questions arising about customization versus a distinct brand personality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:46">[00:39:46]</a>. Younger generations, in particular, interact with AI in naturally "native" ways, like pouring their hearts out to voice mode or commanding real-time story creation with imagery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:09">[00:37:09]</a>.

Ultimately, the goal for [[creating_aipowered_product_teams | AI-powered product teams]] is to automate "drudgery" tasks, freeing humans to focus on creative work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:57">[00:28:57]</a>.
