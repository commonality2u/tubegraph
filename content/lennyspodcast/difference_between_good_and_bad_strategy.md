From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Strategy, at its core, is a design for overcoming a high-stakes challenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. It's a blend of policy and action specifically crafted to address a problem or opportunity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>. The term originates from the Greek "Stratos," referring to elected leaders in Athens who managed critical issues like invasions or plagues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>. Richard Rumelt, a renowned expert and author of *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* and *The Crux*, emphasizes that strategy is fundamentally about [[understanding the challenges of developing effective business strategies | dealing with a challenge]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:12">[00:06:12]</a>.

Instead of calling it a "strategy," Rumelt suggests thinking of it as an "action agenda" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:02">[00:00:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:09">[00:58:09]</a>. This emphasizes a focus on tangible actions to address a recognized problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>.

## The Kernel of a Good Strategy
A robust strategy, or "the kernel" as Rumelt describes it, consists of three essential components:

1.  **Diagnosis:** An in-depth understanding of the situation and the critical challenges being faced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:33">[00:01:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>. This involves deciding what aspects of reality to pay attention to and forming hypotheses about how things connect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>.
2.  **Guiding Policy:** A clear approach or logic for how to deal with the identified challenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>. This defines what the organization will do and, crucially, what it will *not* do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:07">[00:12:07]</a>.
3.  **Coherent Actions:** A set of coordinated actions designed to implement the guiding policy and address the diagnosis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:25">[00:01:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. These actions must be consistent with the diagnosis and policy, and not self-contradictory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:36">[00:12:36]</a>.

### Example of a Good Strategy
Microsoft's approach to AI illustrates these components:
*   **Diagnosis:** Recognizing the challenge of adapting to AI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>.
*   **Guiding Policy:** Deciding to invest in a major AI leader (OpenAI) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:32">[00:15:32]</a>.
*   **Coherent Actions:** Incorporating AI into their search engine and other products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</a>.

## Signs of a Bad Strategy
Rumelt identifies several common characteristics of a bad strategy, often rooted in a lack of rigor or an attempt to avoid difficult choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:49">[00:16:49]</a>. Often, these are covered under [[Signs of a lacking or poorly implemented product strategy | signs of a lacking or poorly implemented product strategy]].

### Goals Masquerading as Strategy
A common pitfall is mistaking a list of performance or profit goals for a strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:37">[00:17:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:01">[00:18:01]</a>. Goals are ambitions, not a plan for how to achieve them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:01">[00:18:01]</a>. For example, a company might state its strategy is to increase growth and profit simultaneously, which often battle each other without a clear plan to reconcile them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>. Similarly, stating "our strategy is to increase market share in China" without addressing *how* to overcome specific challenges is an ambition, not a strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:10">[00:11:10]</a>.

### Fluff and Word Salad
Bad strategies often use convoluted or abstract language to obscure a lack of clear thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:33">[00:20:33]</a>. This "word salad" attempts to sound sophisticated but provides no real direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:40">[00:20:40]</a>.

### Lacking or Incorrect Diagnosis
Without a clear understanding of the core problem, a strategy is built on shaky ground <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:54">[00:16:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a>. For example, if a company says its problem is "not grown fast enough," it's a statement of desire, not a diagnosis of what's holding them back <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:42">[00:19:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:02">[00:20:02]</a>. Similarly, a government strategy to improve education by sending more people to college fails if the root diagnosis isn't addressed (e.g., 15-year-olds struggling with elementary math) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:04">[00:22:04]</a>.

### Incoherent Actions
Actions proposed in a bad strategy may contradict each other, making effective [[Strategy formulation and execution | execution]] impossible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:54">[00:20:54]</a>. For instance, aiming to "burn less oil" while simultaneously "importing more" is self-contradictory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:55">[00:12:55]</a>. A good strategy requires actions that reinforce each other and align with the overall guiding policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:03">[00:14:03]</a>.

## The Role of Power and Focus in Strategy
A good strategy relies on exploiting an asymmetry – a unique advantage or source of [[Role of power in strategy | power]] that differentiates an organization from its competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:17">[00:29:17]</a>. This could be being first to market, having a strong reputation, or possessing unique knowledge or relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:38">[00:29:38]</a>.

Power must be focused on a specific target that can be affected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:01">[00:27:01]</a>. Trying to achieve too many disparate goals diffuses effort and weakens the strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:37">[00:27:37]</a>. The example of Microsoft's early focus on basic software and network effects, or IBM's leveraging of its customer relationships, demonstrates effective focus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:22">[00:30:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:56">[00:35:56]</a>.

## Organizational Dynamics as a Hindrance
Even with a clear understanding of good strategy, organizations often struggle due to internal dynamics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:40">[00:41:40]</a>. This relates to [[Understanding Execution Problems Strategy vs Culture | understanding execution problems]].

*   **Diversity of Interests:** Different departments or individuals within an organization often have their own interests and agendas, making it hard to coalesce around a single action agenda <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:17">[01:15:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:38">[01:15:38]</a>.
*   **Fear of Action and Change:** Implementing a strategy means changing what people do and can alter power relationships within the organization, which leaders may be hesitant to enforce <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:55">[01:15:55]</a>.
*   **Lack of a Decider:** In complex organizations, the absence of a clear authority figure to make definitive choices and enforce them can lead to strategic paralysis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:13">[01:20:13]</a>.

Nokia's decline, for instance, has been partly attributed to replacing engineers with lawyers and accountants, and adopting a matrix organization that diffused power, making it difficult to respond to the emerging smartphone market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:56">[01:19:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:13">[01:20:13]</a>.

## Crafting a Winning Strategy
[[Creating a winning product strategy | Creating a winning product strategy]] or any strategy involves a specific thought process:

1.  **State the Problem (The Crux):** Clearly define the challenge you face. Don't just list ambitions, but identify what is *hard* about achieving them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:46">[00:56:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:57">[01:02:57]</a>. This is the "crux" – the hardest part of the climb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:01">[01:03:01]</a>.
2.  **Focus on Achievable Challenges:** Of all the problems, choose one or two that are both important (close to your ambition) and addressable (you can actually do something about them) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:01">[01:01:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:44">[01:01:44]</a>.
3.  **Develop an "Action Agenda":** Frame your strategy as a set of coherent actions to deal with the identified problem, rather than abstract goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:02">[00:00:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:52">[01:02:52]</a>.
4.  **Seek Insight:** Immerse yourself in the nature of the problem, looking at all available data and historical analogies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:24">[01:09:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:28">[01:09:28]</a>. Insights often come from understanding what makes the problem *hard* and then seeing a way through it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:29">[01:06:29]</a>.

For startups, a strategy often involves making a bet on a particular product-market solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:40">[01:21:40]</a>. Successful startups are often willing to adapt and pivot when initial assumptions about customers or solutions prove incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:35">[01:22:35]</a>. This requires a unique mental flexibility: a conviction in winning while simultaneously being willing to shift if things aren't working <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:14">[01:23:14]</a>.

## Learning from History
There is no "science" of strategy like physics or engineering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:57">[00:47:57]</a>. Instead, strategy is largely based on analogy to previous human experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:10">[00:48:10]</a>. Reading widely about business history, biographies of leaders, and world history can provide valuable insights for strategic thinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:32">[00:47:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:16">[01:36:16]</a>. Understanding past events, like the Great Depression or the early days of the automobile industry, from the perspective of those living through them, can train one's mind to diagnose complex situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:56">[00:50:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:24">[01:24:24]</a>. This historical perspective can help in [[debating traditional and modern approaches to strategy taught in business schools | understanding how strategy evolves]].

## Key Takeaway
Strategy is not mysterious; it's about solving the most important, addressable problem an organization faces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:30">[01:31:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:33">[01:31:33]</a>. It requires focus on what's doable and consistent action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:46">[01:31:46]</a>. The main difference between good and bad strategy lies in a clear diagnosis of the problem, a focused guiding policy, and coherent actions, all underpinned by a deep understanding of organizational capabilities and external realities.
