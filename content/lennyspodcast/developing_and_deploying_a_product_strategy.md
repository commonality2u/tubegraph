## Common Problems with Product Strategy

Perri identifies several pervasive issues related to [[importance_of_strategy_and_decision_making_in_product_management | strategy]] in product organizations, especially in scaling SaaS companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>:

*   **Lack of Context and Direction** Many organizations believe their problems stem from a lack of training, but Perri argues that the fundamental issue is often the way goals are set and [[strategy_development_and_execution | strategy]] is deployed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>. Without clear strategic context, even trained people don't know what to work towards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>.
*   **Plateauing Growth** Companies that successfully scale quickly may eventually hit a plateau, necessitating a re-thinking and re-prioritization of their strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>.
*   **Undefined or Uncommunicated Strategy**
    *   Teams may work excessively (80 hours a week) but see no movement in key metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:56">[00:21:56]</a>.
    *   The product or tech department can become a "black box" to the executive team, who don't understand what's happening or if goals are being achieved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:37">[00:10:37]</a>.
    *   There's often a "missing middle" connecting individual team work (features, bug fixes) to broader business objectives and the company vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:25">[00:22:25]</a>. Teams may know what they're building but not *why* it ladders up to the company's goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:08">[00:28:08]</a>.
    *   Lack of consistent answers across the organization when asked about the company's vision or key objectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:50">[00:23:50]</a>.
    *   The strategy often exists only in leaders' heads and isn't effectively written down or communicated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:04">[00:23:04]</a>.
*   **Lack of Prioritization and Focus** As companies scale, they face many choices and requests from existing customers, making it critical to focus and prioritize effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:23">[00:08:23]</a>. Without a clear, prioritized strategy, teams become spread too thin across numerous initiatives, leading to insufficient progress on key metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:00">[00:26:00]</a>.
*   **Executive Misalignment** One of the biggest issues is when executives have different goals and are not aligned on shared company objectives, leading to internal conflict <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:51">[00:28:51]</a>.

## When to Hire a Chief Product Officer (CPO)

The need for a CPO often emerges at pivotal scaling phases, not necessarily at a fixed number of product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:33">[00:12:33]</a>:

*   **Transition from Single to Multi-Product**: When a company moves from a single product to managing a complex portfolio of multiple products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>.
*   **Rapid Scaling and Strategic Rethink**: After finding product-market fit, as a company scales rapidly (e.g., around $20-30 million in ARR) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:43">[00:12:43]</a>, it often needs to redefine its entire strategy, prioritize, and deploy it across a growing workforce <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>.
*   **Lack of Executive/Board Visibility**: If executives and the board are unaware of product and tech progress, it signals a need for a CPO-level communicator who can effectively convey strategy and outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>.
*   **Disconnection in Strategy Deployment**: If individual teams cannot articulate how their work connects to the overall company strategy, it indicates a lack of top-level strategic formulation and deployment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.
*   **Challenges in Hiring and Developing PMs**: Difficulty in hiring product managers or a prevalence of junior PMs without clear training paths and processes suggests a gap in product leadership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:57">[00:11:57]</a>.
*   **Complex Portfolio Expansion**: Expanding into drastically new markets, geographically, or through major transformations/mergers, often necessitates a CPO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>.
*   **Need for a Singular Leader**: When there's a need for one leader to unify product, design, and sometimes engineering, product operations, or analytics, a CPO is appropriate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>.

### What a CPO Does

Before a CPO, a Head of Product or VP of Product typically acts as a functional leader, implementing processes and managing roadmaps for one or two products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:41">[00:14:41]</a>. A CPO, however, operates at an executive level, handling:

*   **Comprehensive Product Strategy**: Pulling together an all-encompassing product [[strategy_development_and_execution | strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>.
*   **Board and Financial Interface**: Interfacing with the board, understanding financials deeply to project revenue from roadmaps, and working closely with CROs and CFOs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</a>.
*   **Executive Navigation**: Confidently projecting to the board and acting as an executive navigator, bringing together other executives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:33">[00:15:33]</a>.
*   **Overseeing Multiple Functions**: Capable of overseeing multiple functions beyond just product, such as design, product operations, and sometimes engineering or analytics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:40">[00:15:40]</a>.
*   **Multi-product and Platform Strategies**: Leading complex multi-product or platform strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:01">[00:16:01]</a>.

## Developing a Product Strategy

A well-deployed strategy leads to product teams that deeply understand how their work impacts the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:16">[00:28:16]</a>.

### Signs of an Effective Strategy

*   **Aligned Storytelling**: All teams can articulate what they're working on and *why*, connecting tactical work back to business metrics, initiatives, and vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:54">[00:27:54]</a>.
*   **Reduced Stakeholder Infighting**: Executives are aligned on common company goals, leading to calm and objective discussions about trade-offs rather than emotional conflicts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:44">[00:28:44]</a>.
*   **Clear Pushback**: Teams can easily push back on requests that don't align with company priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:18">[00:30:18]</a>.

### Key Elements of a Written Strategy

Perri recommends writing a concise two-page document for strategic clarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:03">[00:31:03]</a>. This document should cover:

*   **Vision**: Where the company is going <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:05">[00:31:05]</a>.
*   **Positioning**: How the company is positioned in the market against competition to reach that vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:09">[00:31:09]</a>.
*   **Current State**: What the product looks like today <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:14">[00:31:14]</a>.
*   **Priorities and Strategic Intents**: What actions will be taken to get there, with clearly prioritized "big business movers" for the next 2-3 years (e.g., expand into new markets, innovate into new opportunities) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:53">[00:24:53]</a>.
*   **Outcomes**: What specific outcomes are expected upon achieving these priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:30">[00:31:30]</a>.

This strategic information should be housed in a shared document (e.g., Google Docs, wiki) with linked levels, allowing teams to trace their work up the "strategy tree" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:40">[00:32:40]</a>.

### Layering the Strategy

Strategy is developed at different organizational levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:33">[00:31:33]</a>:

1.  **Executive Team**: Writes the overarching Strategic Intents for the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:35">[00:31:35]</a>.
2.  **Product Leadership (Directors, VPs, CPOs)**: Writes Product Initiatives, which are problem-oriented around significant customer problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:40">[00:31:40]</a>.
3.  **Product Teams**: Works on "options" (often epics) â€“ the specific solutions to build that address product initiatives and contribute to strategic intents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:12">[00:32:12]</a>.

### Developing a Product Vision [[developing_and_communicating_a_product_vision | Developing and communicating a product vision]]

A strong product vision is:

*   **Concrete**: People should be able to picture it clearly in their heads, not just a vague tagline like "be the backbone of healthcare" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:14">[00:36:14]</a>.
*   **Lofty but Achievable**: It should be far enough away that it requires iteration and testing, not something that can be achieved with one single build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:37">[00:36:37]</a>.
*   **Future-Oriented**: It should describe what the company will be in the future, not what it is today <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:52">[00:36:52]</a>.
*   **Differentiated**: Clearly articulate how the company will be different from competitors and why it's better, not just "the best" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:09">[00:37:09]</a>.
*   **Customer-Focused**: Describe the future customers and the unique value proposition they will receive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:47">[00:37:47]</a>.
*   **Definitive about what *not* to do**: A good vision often explicitly states what the company will *not* be or *not* do, preventing teams from blindly copying competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:16">[00:38:16]</a>.

Perri recommends a combination of writing and visual aids (like prototypes or diagrams illustrating ecosystem interactions) to communicate a vision, often leveraging UX designers for impactful presentations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:46">[00:34:46]</a>.

## Improving Strategic Thinking

For Product Managers looking to develop stronger strategic skills:

*   **Think Like a CPO**: Even without the formal role, imagine what you would do if you were the CPO. This provides practice in asking strategic questions and digging into data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:13">[00:39:13]</a>.
*   **Talk to Market and Financial Experts**: Engage with people who deeply understand the market and financials, including your CPO (if you have one). Ask about their processes, thought processes, and what data they review to set priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:43">[00:39:43]</a>.
*   **Cross-Departmental Conversations**: Speak with other departments like sales to understand win/loss analyses and market insights. Subject matter experts across the organization hold a wealth of knowledge about market movements and innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:24">[00:40:24]</a>.
*   **Leverage Data**: Strategy relies heavily on data. Work with data analysts to pull internal data (financial systems, user analytics) and surface patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:06">[00:41:06]</a>.
*   **Integrate Research**: Ensure customer research and external market research are bubbled up and inform strategic decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:47">[00:41:47]</a>.
*   **Embrace Decision-Making**: Recognize that you won't always be 100% certain. The goal is to make the best-informed decision possible at the time, be willing to make a choice, and then be prepared to correct course if new information indicates it was the wrong one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:17">[00:42:17]</a>.
*   **Study Other Companies' Strategies**: Analyze how successful companies have developed and executed their strategies, understanding that while frameworks vary, the core principles of data analysis and market understanding remain constant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:24">[00:43:24]</a>.

## The Role of Product Operations

As product organizations scale, challenges arise in [[executing_strategy_effectively_in_product_management | executing strategy effectively]]: standardization of processes breaks down, data becomes siloed, and customer interactions become uncoordinated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:09">[00:44:09]</a>. This is where Product Operations (Product Ops) comes in.

Product Ops helps:

*   **Provide Right Insights**: Ensure teams receive the necessary insights to make informed decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:16">[00:45:16]</a>.
*   **Standardize Outputs and Check-ins**: Standardize roadmaps, meetings, and check-ins to monitor strategic progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:20">[00:45:20]</a>.

Typically, Product Ops functions include:

1.  **Internal Data and Insights**: A team that surfaces internal data (financial systems, user analytics) to track OKRs, monitor strategy progress, and aid decision-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:34">[00:45:34]</a>.
2.  **Customer and User Insights**: Manages external data, market research, and standardizes customer research approaches to ensure product managers can talk to customers effectively without overwhelming them. It's about scaling user research, not centralizing it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:02">[00:46:02]</a>.
3.  **Process Standardization**: Standardizes processes and cadences for strategy check-ins (e.g., monthly roadmap reviews, quarterly planning sessions). It focuses on interactions with other divisions (e.g., sales, marketing), not internal team processes like daily stand-ups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:31">[00:47:31]</a>.

## Continuous Learning for Product Managers

To avoid burnout from information overload and continually improve:

*   **Focus on Execution First**: Prioritize doing your job effectively. The most learning comes from hands-on execution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:34">[00:49:34]</a>.
*   **Self-Retrospective**: Analyze your work regularly to identify what's working and what's not <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:40">[00:49:40]</a>.
*   **Deep Dive on Skill Gaps**: Choose specific areas you want to improve (e.g., user research, data analytics) and deep dive into those topics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:26">[00:50:26]</a>.
*   **Adapt Frameworks**: Understand that while core principles of product management (talking to users, iterative development, measuring success) remain constant, the specific frameworks can and should be adapted to fit your company's culture and needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:14">[00:51:14]</a>. If a process isn't serving you, change it; don't stick to dogmatic approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:50">[00:51:50]</a>.
*   **Build Your Own Toolbox**: Experiment with different approaches and retain what works for you, creating a personalized set of tools and methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:11">[00:52:11]</a>.
