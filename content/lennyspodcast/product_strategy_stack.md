The Product Strategy Stack is a framework designed to help product leaders separate and clearly define terms often conflated, such as goals, roadmap, and strategy. Its primary goal is to provide a clear framework for making decisions and driving business value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:43">[00:18:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:56">[00:19:56]</a>. This system helps teams understand the decision-making framework and what truly drives value for the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:02">[00:20:02]</a>.

The framework can be used in two primary ways:
*   **Top-down:** To define a new [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:32">[00:21:32]</a>.
*   **Bottom-up:** To debug issues or identify gaps in an existing strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:37">[00:21:37]</a>. For instance, if goals are not being met, the problem might stem from an ill-defined roadmap, unclear [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]], or a lack of understanding of the company's overarching [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] or mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:41">[00:21:41]</a>.

## Components of the Product Strategy Stack

The stack consists of five distinct layers, from top to bottom:

1.  **Company Mission** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:07">[00:20:07]</a>
    *   The change the company (or team/subsidiary) wants to bring to the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:10">[00:20:10]</a>.
    *   A qualitative and inspirational statement of the company's purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:15">[00:20:15]</a>.
    *   Vision is considered part of the mission, combining the future world the company sees with its role in that world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:14">[00:22:14]</a>.

2.  **Company Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:34">[00:20:34]</a>
    *   The rigorously logical plan the company will use to achieve its mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:39">[00:20:39]</a>.
    *   It must be specific and rigorous, detailing the approach or plan to make progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:45">[00:20:45]</a>.

3.  **Product Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:02">[00:21:02]</a>
    *   The connective tissue between the company's objectives and the day-to-day actions of the product team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:06">[00:21:06]</a>.
    *   A critical aspect of [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]] is making it visual. Relying on words alone can lead to different interpretations. Instead, a [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] document should include wireframes or conceptual blueprints that illustrate what the product will look like when the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] is implemented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:40">[00:24:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:05">[00:25:05]</a>. This provides concrete clarity on what is being built, how it fulfills the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]], and the necessary trade-offs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>.
    *   For Product Managers without dedicated designers, it's recommended to sketch ideas on paper or use wireframing tools like Balsamiq, as a basic visual is better than none for communicating intent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:02">[00:27:02]</a>.

4.  **Product Roadmap** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:16">[00:21:16]</a>
    *   Informed by the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]], outlining the incremental steps to achieve the desired outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:16">[00:21:16]</a>.

5.  **Product Goals** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a>
    *   Ultimately informed by the roadmap, these measure progress towards the strategy. In a contrarian view, goals should follow the roadmap, not precede it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:31">[00:42:31]</a>.
    *   The "goals first" approach can lead to teams focusing solely on moving metrics without sufficient context or strategic alignment. A better approach is to first define the desired destination (mission and strategy) and then set goals that help measure progress towards that destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:05">[00:43:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:19">[00:43:19]</a>. If achieving a goal pulls away from the destination, it prompts a critical conversation about changing either the goal or the destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:55">[00:43:55]</a>.

### Goal Setting and the Frontier of Understanding

When setting goals, it's crucial to consider the "Frontier of Understanding" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:48">[00:48:48]</a>, which refers to the boundary between what a team knows and doesn't know. Instead of always committing to outcome-based goals, teams should set goals appropriate to their current level of understanding and risk:

1.  **Understanding Risk:** If the team doesn't know what levers move a particular metric, the goal should be to increase understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:29">[00:49:29]</a>.
2.  **Dependency Risk:** If the team understands the levers but lacks the necessary tools or resources, the goal should be to acquire those tools or address dependencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:43">[00:50:43]</a>.
3.  **Execution Risk:** If resources and hypotheses are strong, the goal can be focused on the quantity of experiments or execution milestones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:51">[00:50:51]</a>.
4.  **Strategic Risk:** Once understanding and execution are solid, the goal can be tied to the ultimate strategic outcome, acknowledging that the initial hypothesis might still be incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:01">[00:51:01]</a>.

Leadership typically focuses on outcomes, but a PM can convey learning and a clear path to instil confidence, even if the immediate goal is understanding or execution, rather than a direct metric movement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:51">[00:52:51]</a>. The objective is to be in the quadrant where you hit your goals *and* know why, or if you miss them, at least understand why <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:01">[00:54:01]</a>.

                                                                                                                           ## The Product Strategy Stack

The Product Strategy Stack is a framework designed to help product leaders precisely define terms typically mixed up, such such as goals, roadmap, and strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:43">[00:18:43]</a>. It serves to clarify the decision-making framework and highlight what genuinely drives value for the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:02">[00:20:02]</a>.

This system can be utilized in two primary ways:
*   **Top-down:** For [[developing_a_product_strategy | developing a product strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:32">[00:21:32]</a>.
*   **Bottom-up:** For debugging issues or pinpointing gaps in an existing [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:37">[00:21:37]</a>. For example, if a team struggles to meet its goals, the problem might stem from an undefined roadmap, an unclear [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]], or an insufficient understanding of the company's broader [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] or mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:41">[00:21:41]</a>.

### Components of the Product Strategy Stack

The stack comprises five distinct layers, starting from the top:

1.  **Company Mission** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:07">[00:20:07]</a>
    *   Represents the desired change the company (or a specific team/subsidiary) aims to bring to the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:10">[00:20:10]</a>.
    *   It is a qualitative and inspirational statement articulating the company's core purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:15">[00:20:15]</a>.
    *   The concept of 'vision' is often integrated into the mission, encompassing both the future state the company envisions and its role within that future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:14">[00:22:14]</a>.

2.  **Company Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:34">[00:20:34]</a>
    *   This layer details the rigorously logical plan the company will employ to achieve its mission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:39">[00:20:39]</a>.
    *   It must be specific and meticulous, outlining the precise approach or plan for making progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:45">[00:20:45]</a>.

3.  **Product Strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:02">[00:21:02]</a>
    *   Serves as the crucial link between the company's overall objectives and the daily activities of the product team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:06">[00:21:06]</a>.
    *   A key aspect of [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]] is its visual representation. Relying solely on words can lead to varied interpretations. Therefore, a [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] document should incorporate wireframes or conceptual blueprints that illustrate the product's appearance upon [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]] implementation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:40">[00:24:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:05">[00:25:05]</a>. This provides concrete clarity on the build, how it aligns with the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]], and necessary trade-offs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>.
    *   For Product Managers lacking dedicated designers, it's advisable to sketch ideas on paper or use wireframing tools like Balsamiq, as any visual aid is superior to none for conveying intent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:02">[00:27:02]</a>.

4.  **Product Roadmap** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:16">[00:21:16]</a>
    *   This layer is informed by the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | product strategy]] and outlines the incremental steps required to achieve desired outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:16">[00:21:16]</a>.

5.  **Product Goals** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:18">[00:21:18]</a>
    *   Ultimately, these goals are informed by the roadmap and serve to measure progress toward the [[Product Strategy and Vision Development | strategy]]. A contrarian viewpoint suggests that goals should follow the roadmap, rather than precede it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:31">[00:42:31]</a>.
    *   A "goals first" approach can cause teams to focus solely on moving metrics without sufficient context or strategic alignment. A more effective method is to first define the desired destination (mission and strategy) and then establish goals that measure progress toward that destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:05">[00:43:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:19">[00:43:19]</a>. If achieving a goal diverts from the destination, it necessitates a critical discussion about whether to adjust the goal or the destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:55">[00:43:55]</a>.

### Goal Setting and the Frontier of Understanding

When setting goals, it's vital to consider the "Frontier of Understanding" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:48">[00:48:48]</a>, which marks the boundary between what a team comprehends and what it does not. Instead of always committing to outcome-based goals, teams should align goals with their current level of understanding and risk:

1.  **Understanding Risk:** If a team is unsure what levers impact a specific metric, the primary goal should be to deepen that understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:29">[00:49:29]</a>.
2.  **Dependency Risk:** If the team grasps the levers but lacks the necessary tools or resources, the goal should focus on acquiring those tools or resolving dependencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:43">[00:50:43]</a>.
3.  **Execution Risk:** With solid resources and strong hypotheses, the goal can shift towards the quantity of experiments or execution milestones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:51">[00:50:51]</a>.
4.  **Strategic Risk:** Once understanding and execution are robust, the goal can directly target the ultimate strategic outcome, acknowledging that the initial hypothesis might still prove incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:01">[00:51:01]</a>.

While leadership is typically outcome-driven, a Product Manager can communicate learning and a clear path forward to build confidence, even if the immediate goal is understanding or execution, rather than directly moving a metric <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:51">[00:52:51]</a>. The ideal scenario is to achieve goals *and* understand why, or if they are missed, at least comprehend the reasons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:01">[00:54:01]</a>.
