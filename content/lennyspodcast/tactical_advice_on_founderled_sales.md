[[importance_of_founderled_sales_over_hiring_salespeople_early_on | Founder-led sales]] is about bringing the founder's vision into the world and understanding which part of the market resonates with it, thereby aligning the founder's vision with market reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.

## Why Founders Should Lead Sales Early
It is a common misconception that founders should quickly hire a salesperson to handle sales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. However, [[importance_of_founderled_sales_over_hiring_salespeople_early_on | founder-led sales]] is often recommended as it provides a significant competitive advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>:
*   **Visionary Leadership** The founder is the visionary who can articulate the product's purpose and future like no one else, including a salesperson <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.
*   **Authority and Excitement** As the highest authority in the startup, the founder's involvement excites the market. People are eager to speak with a founder because they often possess knowledge or a perspective the market lacks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>.
*   **Deep Market Insight** Founders can discern budding opportunities or subtle market signals in conversations that a salesperson might miss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>. These "budding moments" are where crucial insights for refining the go-to-market strategy reside, leading to [[founder_mode_in_product_leadership | product-market fit]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>.
*   **Accountability** Founder involvement brings accountability closer to the decision-making pulse, preventing misinterpretations that can occur when relying on a salesperson's feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>.

[[importance_of_founderled_sales_over_hiring_salespeople_early_on | Founder-led sales]] is primarily about learning as rapidly as possible to understand the market's pulse, thereby earning the right to sell <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. The goal is not immediate revenue, but rather to gather critical research that informs future sales and product development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:48">[00:35:48]</a>. Founders should aim to lead sales until they achieve around $500K to $1 million in Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR), or when they reach a certain velocity point <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:16">[00:36:16]</a>.

1.  **Introduction Call** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>
2.  **Second Call / Demo** (depending on market maturity) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:33">[00:10:33]</a>
3.  **Proposal / Scope of Work Discussion** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>
4.  **Co-authoring Scope & Feedback** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:53">[00:10:53]</a>
5.  **Procurement Introduction** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>
6.  **Final Signature** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>

### Finding and Reaching Out to Leads
Before investing in tools, founders should manually [[finding_and_reaching_out_to_leads_as_a_startup_founder | find 30 potential leads]] and write personalized, thoughtful notes to each <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:28">[00:23:28]</a>. This exercise helps identify commonalities among ideal customers, such as team size, industry, role, or career history, which informs parameters for future outreach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:20">[00:24:20]</a>.

#### Crafting Effective Outreach Messages
When drafting initial outreach emails or messages (e.g., LinkedIn DMs), focus on these elements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:47">[00:14:47]</a>:
*   **Relevancy** Clearly state why the message is relevant to the recipient's role or company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:17">[00:13:17]</a>. Relevancy is often more crucial than personalization, as over-personalization can appear forced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:24">[00:13:24]</a>.
*   **Differentiation/Counterintuitive Insight** Introduce a novel insight or a counterintuitive idea that sparks curiosity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:47">[00:13:47]</a>. The goal is to make them pause and think, "What?" or "I've never thought about it that way" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:51">[00:13:51]</a>. Avoid positioning your product as simply "better" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:56">[00:29:56]</a>.
*   **Problem-Focused** Discuss the problem you aim to solve and why it needs addressing, rather than immediately presenting your solution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:36">[00:14:36]</a>.
*   **Concise** Keep messages short (3-4 sentences max) to ensure readability on mobile devices without scrolling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>.

A good example message could be: "Our belief at Jellyfish is that zero-to-one sales talent doesn't exist, which is why we built this model. I noticed you're looking to target [X, Y, Z] based on your website; I'd love to have a conversation with you" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:28">[00:16:28]</a>.

#### Conversion Rates
Conversion rates from outbound outreach can vary. While important, the [[Sales_strategies_for_startups | win rate]] (percentage of calls that convert to signed deals) is often a more telling metric <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a>. A high win rate means you need fewer initial contacts to achieve sales targets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:31">[00:17:31]</a>. Healthy conversion rates for outbound are typically 5-7%, but can range from 2-3% to 8-15% for founder-led outreach solving a deeply felt problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:21">[00:18:21]</a>. The problem a founder is solving and their insight into it significantly influences interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:42">[00:18:42]</a>.

### The First Sales Call: Engaging the Prospect
On the first call, founders should be vulnerable and honest about their early stage, stating they are deeply passionate about solving a specific problem and have much to learn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:15">[00:31:15]</a>. This encourages more raw and honest feedback from prospects, who are more likely to offer candid opinions if they believe the product is still in development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:37">[00:32:37]</a>.

Avoid direct questions like "what keeps you up at night" or "what are your pain points" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:06">[00:42:06]</a>. Instead, focus on understanding the prospect's current approach to the problem:
*   "How is this problem manifesting on your side?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:01">[00:32:01]</a>
*   "Are you measuring or managing this problem today?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:24">[00:40:24]</a>
*   "How have you tried to solve for it?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:43">[00:40:43]</a>
*   "How do you visualize this working in your head?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:05">[00:39:05]</a>

These questions help uncover if the problem is growing and widening (a good sign) and if the prospect has already invested effort into solving it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:07">[00:40:07]</a>. Signs of genuine interest include the prospect wanting to pull in other team members for a follow-up call <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:16">[00:41:16]</a>.

Crucially, **do not perform a product demo on the first call** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:30">[00:49:30]</a>. The demo is one of the few "carrots" a founder controls; once shown, the mystique is gone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:37">[00:49:37]</a>. For high-value deals (hundreds of thousands of dollars), slow down the process to ensure all relevant stakeholders are present for the demo, making it a "group's baby" rather than one individual's <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:31">[00:50:31]</a>.

### Co-Authoring and the Role of Services
In early stages, exciting prospects often involves making them feel like the solution is being built specifically for them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:21">[00:43:21]</a>. This can involve inviting them to [[the_role_of_services_in_earlystage_startup_sales | co-author the scope of work]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:37">[00:43:37]</a>.

[[the_role_of_services_in_earlystage_startup_sales | Co-authorship]] helps assess a buyer's maturity: if they lack an existing process or strategy for a problem, they may not be ready to purchase technology directly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:51">[00:43:51]</a>. In such cases, consider selling a service first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:58">[00:43:58]</a>. While investors may not prefer service-based revenue, it serves several purposes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:13">[00:44:13]</a>:
*   **Educating the Market** It allows you to educate the buyer and help them design their internal process for adopting your eventual technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:45">[00:44:45]</a>.
*   **Intent and Logo Acquisition** It demonstrates intent from the customer, secures a valuable logo, and generates initial revenue, even if not recurring <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:08">[00:44:08]</a>.
*   **Paid Research** You get paid to help the customer define their process and gather valuable insights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:49">[00:44:49]</a>.

Services contracts should be time-boxed, ideally to 90-day increments, to avoid getting locked into long-term, non-scalable work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:13">[00:45:13]</a>. Examples of services include helping a client design a custom pitch to their boss or mapping out how a new technology would integrate with their existing workflows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:09">[00:48:09]</a>. This approach turns you into a consultant, guiding them towards product acquisition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:47">[00:48:47]</a>. Approximately 40-50% of B2B SaaS startups, especially those with top-down sales models, initially sell some form of service before selling their technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:28">[00:47:28]</a>.

### Navigating Procurement and Getting the Signature
When selling to larger organizations (500-1000+ employees), founders will inevitably encounter procurement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:35">[00:56:35]</a>. Procurement teams are professional buyers, highly intelligent and focused on mitigating risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:25">[00:51:25]</a>.
*   **Simplify Your Pitch** Keep your explanation of the product simple, jargon-free, and easy for them to grasp <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:42">[00:51:42]</a>.
*   **Differentiate Clearly** Your offering must feel distinctly different from existing preferred vendors; merely being "better" is insufficient and can lead them to recommend an alternative already in their system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:50">[00:51:50]</a>.
*   **Do the Work** Be proactive in filling out forms and making their job as easy as possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:27">[00:52:27]</a>. Don't assume they will prioritize your small deal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:31">[00:52:31]</a>.
*   **Define Your Scope** Clearly articulate what your product does and does not do. Vague descriptions can lead them to classify you as high-risk, triggering burdensome master service agreements (MSAs) with extensive requirements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:57">[00:52:57]</a>.
*   **Truncate Contracts** To navigate long due diligence processes, consider separating contracts into technology and service components <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a>. The service contract allows you to onboard and educate users while the technology contract moves through slower approvals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:54">[00:53:54]</a>.

Before reaching the final signature, identify the actual signatory (e.g., CFO, Chief Legal Officer, Business Unit Head) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>. Work with procurement to prepare concise, relevant bullet points for the signatory to understand what they are approving, preventing delays <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:45">[00:58:45]</a>. Always ensure finance and procurement have approved and signed the contract before starting any work, as payment is processed through official channels like purchase orders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:24">[01:00:24]</a>.

## Sales Cycle Timeline and Discounting
*   **Timeline** The sales cycle duration depends on the founder's project management efficiency, the organization's complexity (highly regulated industries take longer), and the seniority of the contact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:26">[01:02:26]</a>. While rare, [[enterprise_sales_strategies_for_tech_startups | Enterprise sales]] can close in 90 days, but typically range from 6 to 12 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:36">[01:03:36]</a>.
*   **Discounting** Avoid discounting merely to close a deal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:58">[01:00:58]</a>. Only offer discounts if the customer provides significant value in return, such as becoming a design partner or a long-term reference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:33">[01:01:33]</a>. For Enterprise deals, an Annual Contract Value (ACV) between $50K and $200K is a good starting point <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:21">[01:05:21]</a>.

## Common Pitfalls and General Advice
The biggest pitfall in [[Sales_strategies_for_startups | sales for startups]] is **qualification** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:24">[01:10:24]</a>. Many startups believe they have a "bottom of funnel" problem (deals dying at the end), but it's almost always a "top of funnel" issue related to:
*   Talking to the wrong people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:12">[01:11:12]</a>
*   Using the wrong messaging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:16">[01:11:16]</a>
*   Not solving a sufficiently important problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:57">[01:11:57]</a>
*   Not being different enough <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:04">[01:11:04]</a>

### Tips for Founders
*   **Be Passionate and Engaging** Sales should feel exciting for the buyer. Founders should bring their passion and energy to calls, making prospects feel invigorated and like their world will change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:25">[01:11:25]</a>.
*   **Build Trust** Honesty is crucial. If your solution genuinely solves their problem, state it. If it's not a good fit, be transparent. Telling a prospect they are not the right fit can build immense trust, leading to future referrals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:47">[01:12:47]</a>. Trust is the number one currency in sales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:07">[01:13:07]</a>.
*   **Understand Your Market** Decide whether to build for Enterprise (higher value, longer sales cycles, specific procurement hurdles) or Small Business (higher volume, faster sales, but higher churn) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:13">[01:06:13]</a>. Mid-market can be challenging as it straddles both dynamics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:53">[01:06:53]</a>.
*   **Consider Coaching** Many successful founders benefit from a sales coach or consultant to help them [[the_personal_development_of_a_founder_and_leadership_lessons | learn to do sales]] early on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:51">[01:14:51]</a>.

The payoff for diligent [[enterprise_sales_strategies_for_tech_startups | Enterprise sales]] efforts can be exponential. Once "in" with an Enterprise client, you become a preferred vendor, gain access to internal conversations, receive introductions, and transition from a salesperson to a trusted partner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:03">[01:06:03]</a>.
