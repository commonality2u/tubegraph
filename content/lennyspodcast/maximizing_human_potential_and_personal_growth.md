## Pushing Potential through Direct Feedback

Toby emphasizes that everyone is capable of achieving far more than they realize <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>. He attributes underperformance to limiting ideas and environments that narrow focus on unambitious goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>. His approach to feedback, sometimes described as the "Toby Tornado," is characterized by directness and a focus on accelerating progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>.

Instead of letting projects linger if they are not working, Toby advocates for quickly identifying and stopping them to prevent wasted time and effort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>. This "compression of time" is crucial because careers are finite, and the goal is to maximize things one can be proud of <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.

### The "Toby Tornado"
The "Toby Tornado" involves:
*   **Rapid change management:** Compressing conversation, conflict, or "real talk" into a short timeframe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.
*   **Decisive action:** If a project isn't good, it's stopped, and a new version is started, often with the same team becoming "founders of the next version" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:32">[00:05:32]</a>.
*   **Accountability:** Toby feels it's "super unfair" to let people work on something that won't make it, and ignoring such issues is an abdication of his CEO responsibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>.

Toby applies the same directness to individual feedback. He believes reminding people of their own potential constantly is a wonderful thing to do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:32">[00:09:32]</a>. He holds people to a high standard, expecting them to accomplish things they didn't imagine they could <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.

## Product Philosophy and Enabling Potential

Toby sees his product as a means to help people achieve greater success and become more ambitious with their online stores than they initially intended <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:50">[01:09:50]</a>. This stems from his own journey, where starting a snowboard store unexpectedly led to building Shopify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:57">[01:10:57]</a>.

His approach to product design and [[philosophy_of_product_design_and_human_potential | human potential]] involves:

*   **100-Year Vision:** Shopify operates with a 100-year vision, not focusing on short-term software projects but on the enduring mission to make entrepreneurship more common <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:16">[01:03:16]</a>. This long-term focus helps in [[effective_growth_frameworks | decision-making]], prioritizing long-term value over short-term gains or "putting future profits forward at a discount" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:03">[01:08:03]</a>.
*   **First Principles Thinking:** Instead of imitating existing solutions, new products are derived from first principles, considering all currently available building blocks to solve problems optimally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:10">[00:30:10]</a>. This avoids "path dependence" and "overfitting" to outdated compromises <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:19">[00:29:19]</a>. Toby is suspicious of pitches that aim to do a "good version of the same thing everyone else does" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:20">[00:31:20]</a>.
*   **Comfort with Unquantifiable Things:** Shopify balances being "extremely data informed" with valuing unquantifiable aspects like taste, quality, passion, love, fun, and delight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:07">[01:18:07]</a>. He believes that focusing on these aspects, particularly fun, will eventually lead to positive metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:20">[01:21:20]</a>.
*   **Lowering Complexity:** A core insight at Shopify is that every time a complex process is simplified, more businesses are created on the platform <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:30">[01:20:30]</a>. This reduces the "courage" needed for new entrepreneurs, who often face discouragement and fear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:07">[01:21:07]</a>. Simplifying things like taxes or payments makes the journey less intimidating, enabling more people to succeed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:22">[01:24:22]</a>.
*   **Playing Positive-Sum Games:** Toby's belief is that on a long enough timeline, playing positive-sum games with customers is the "ultimate [[growth_hacking_and_strategies | growth hack]]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:37">[01:09:37]</a>. This involves continuously adding value and not engaging in value extraction or short-sighted tactics that compromise trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:17">[01:08:17]</a>.

## Cultivating Personal Growth and Career Development

Toby suggests that a [[career_growth_and_personal_development | career growth and personal development]] journey should be an exploration driven by curiosity, akin to an "infinite game" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:32">[01:13:32]</a>.

### The "Talent Stack"
He advises people to think of their career as a product or "Talent as a Service," where they are "selling a subscription" of their value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:45">[01:34:45]</a>. The key is to find an intersection of unique skills and interests where one can become "too good to ignore" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:35">[01:35:35]</a>. This "triplicate intersection of three different things" allows for greater differentiation and success than trying to compete in broad, crowded areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:54">[01:32:54]</a>.

### The Importance of Courage
Toby believes courage is a rare and highly valued trait <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:48">[00:55:48]</a>. He actively invites disagreement because it demonstrates courage and builds trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:00">[00:55:00]</a>. For products, he advocates for lowering the "net amount of courage needed" for users to engage, as seen in features like Instagram's "try run" for Reels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:10">[01:27:10]</a>.

### Being a Product Leader
A product leader's role is to ensure the team "gives a sh*t" about the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:32">[01:37:32]</a>. This involves:
*   **Building empathy:** Fostering understanding for the users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:43">[01:37:43]</a>.
*   **Infectious caring:** The product leader must exothermically care about the product, as this alone can make a 10 times better product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:10">[01:39:10]</a>.
*   **Looking around corners:** Understanding the product better than anyone else and using the team as a resource to anticipate future challenges and opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:35">[01:38:35]</a>.

This [[leadership_and_personal_growth_as_a_ceo | "founder mode"]] of pumping heat and energy into the system is crucial for a company's innovation and long-term success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:53">[01:39:53]</a>. Toby attributes his own success to not conforming to traditional CEO expectations, but instead focusing on understanding details and constantly challenging assumptions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:09">[00:51:09]</a>.
