From: [[lennyspodcast]] <br/> 

Katie Dill, Head of Design at Stripe, emphasizes that beauty and functionality are not opposing concepts in product development, but rather that beauty enhances functionality. A decline in the use of the word "beauty" in digitized books from the 1800s to the 2000s coincides with the belief that "functionality is King" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. However, Dill argues that beauty makes products easier to use, more approachable, and more compelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>. Beyond practical benefits, beautiful products increase trust, signaling that painstaking detail and care have been invested, which assures users that other unseen details are also handled with care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>.

Dill, who has built and led design teams at hypergrowth companies like Airbnb, Lyft, and Stripe, shares insights on how to operationalize quality and how design can directly contribute to business growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:11">[01:11:11]</a>.

## The Value of Design Quality

The common perception is that while product managers and founders understand the value of high-quality design, it often isn't prioritized over new features due to unclear ROI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:00">[01:11:00]</a>. Dill refutes this, stating that [[quality_improvement_leading_to_business_impact | quality is growth]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:45">[01:14:45]</a>.

### Levels of Quality
Dill identifies several levels of quality for a product or feature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:55">[01:11:55]</a>:
*   **Baseline Quality**: The product works and provides its intended value proposition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:59">[01:11:59]</a>.
*   **Well-Rounded Quality**: It is error-free and functions comprehensively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:10">[01:12:10]</a>.
*   **Exceeding Expectations**: The product surprises users and does something they weren't even seeking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:20">[01:12:20]</a>.

The ultimate levels of quality should align with user expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:27">[01:12:27]</a>. Companies that view quality as non-negotiable understand that it is functionality itself, which leads to great usability and desirability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:43">[01:13:43]</a>.

### Design as a Growth Driver
[[quality_improvement_leading_to_business_impact | Quality is growth]] because making a product easier to use and more understandable drives adoption and usage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:52">[01:15:52]</a>. At Stripe, the growth team is "maniacally focused" on [[improving_user_experience_and_product_quality | building better experiences]] because it directly ties to business metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:11">[01:16:11]</a>.

*   **Onboarding Flow**: Improvements to Stripe's onboarding flow, by making products easier to understand, have led to increased activations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:22">[01:16:22]</a>.
*   **Checkout Experience**: Research on top e-commerce sites revealed that 99% had errors in their checkout flow that hindered seamless, quicker transactions and higher conversion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:50">[01:16:50]</a>. By [[improving_user_experience_and_product_quality | improving the quality]] of the checkout experience, Stripe has seen a **10.5% increase in businesses' revenue** when transitioning from an older to a newer checkout form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:33">[01:17:33]</a>. Stripe powers checkout for millions of businesses globally, including Amazon, Hertz, Shopify, and Spotify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:00">[01:18:00]</a>.

## Principles for Operationalizing Design Quality

Dill outlines several themes for [[operationalizing_the_principle_of_meticulousness_in_product_development | operationalizing quality]]:

1.  **Quality is a Group Effort**: It cannot be the sole responsibility of one individual or team, but requires an organizational commitment and shared care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:52">[02:50:52]</a>.
2.  **Vision and Alignment**: Even with talented individuals, a shared vision is necessary to ensure individual contributions coalesce into a cohesive, high-quality product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:20">[03:22:20]</a>. This is analogous to a house where an architect and general contractor ensure all disparate parts fit together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:27">[03:26:27]</a>.
3.  **Editing and Courage**: Leaders must have the courage to say "this isn't good enough" and push for further iteration, even when teams have put in significant effort <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>.
4.  **Understanding from the User Perspective**: Quality must be understood from the user's holistic journey, not just isolated features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:41:00">[03:41:00]</a>. This fosters empathy and reveals how disparate parts of a product fit together or fall short <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>.

## "Walk the Store" Process

To address quality regression over time (where products might worsen as new features are added without considering the whole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:34:05">[03:34:05]</a>), Stripe implemented a "Walk the Store" process:

*   **Focus on Critical Journeys**: They identified 15 critical user journeys that deeply matter to users and must meet the highest quality standards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:56:00">[03:56:00]</a>.
*   **Cross-Functional Ownership**: Each of these 15 journeys has engineering, product, and design leaders responsible for its quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>.
*   **Regular Review**: Leaders regularly "walk the store" by putting themselves in the user's shoes and experiencing the full journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:32:00">[03:32:00]</a>. This includes internet searches, website interactions, documentation, and the product dashboard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:05">[03:36:05]</a>.
*   **Friction Logging**: They "friction log" their experiences, noting what works and what doesn't, filing bugs, and reaching out to relevant teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a>.
*   **Scoring**: Journeys are scored based on a rubric covering usability, utility, desirability, and whether they are "surprisingly great" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:45:00">[03:45:00]</a>. A color system (e.g., yellow, green) is used for scoring to avoid getting "tied around the axle" with precise numbers and to encourage qualitative judgment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:43:00">[03:43:00]</a>.
*   **Calibration (PQR)**: Quarterly, leaders come together in a "Product Quality Review" (PQR) to calibrate scores and discuss quality interpretations. This ensures shared understanding of the quality bar across the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:35:00">[03:35:00]</a>.
*   **Benefits**: This process increases awareness and accountability, leading to insights about bigger opportunities and process changes (e.g., improving SEO to align with later journey steps) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:58:00">[03:58:00]</a>. It converts skeptics from other functions by showing them the user experience firsthand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:45:00">[03:45:00]</a>.

## Prioritizing Quality Work

Instead of strict time allocations (e.g., "10% on fixing things" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:06">[04:47:06]</a>), Stripe relies on culture and shared values:

*   **Hiring for Judgment and Care**: Employees are hired with the expectation that they possess great judgment and care for what they build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:49:00">[04:49:00]</a>.
*   **Advocacy from Leadership**: Quality is clearly advocated for at the highest levels of the company, fueling teams' planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:00">[04:50:00]</a>.
*   **Broad Definition of Impact**: Performance rubrics at Stripe define impact broadly to include quality efforts that may be harder to measure or are longer-term, ensuring they are celebrated and recognized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:10:00">[05:10:00]</a>.
*   **Meticulous Craft**: This is an operating principle at Stripe, expected of everyone in the organization, whether designing spaces, building APIs, or handling support calls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:27:00">[05:27:00]</a>.

## Team Building and Leadership

Dill leverages a simple formula for team performance: **Performance = Potential - Interference** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a>. Leaders aim to increase potential (hiring well, developing talent) and decrease interferences that hinder great work (like "a muscle atrophying underneath a cast" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:59:00">[05:59:00]</a>).

*   **Organizational Structure and Co-location**: At Lyft, Dill addressed interference stemming from a physical separation of design, engineering, and product teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>. Designers in a separate, "beautifully designed" room led to wasted work and misalignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:00">[01:03:00]</a>. By breaking down the physical and organizational "wall" and having multi-disciplinary teams (designers, engineers, product managers) sit together with shared goals, iteration cycles became faster, and clarity improved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>. Creative spaces for critique and working sessions were still maintained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>. This approach of [[operating_models_and_collaboration_in_product_management | co-locating teams]] by product area is crucial for efficient and aligned work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>.

### Improving Awareness of Work
To combat the loss of awareness in growing companies, Dill implemented a system where designers share screenshots or prototypes of their work in a shared Google Slide deck every couple of weeks (now monthly) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:52:00">[05:52:00]</a>.
*   **Benefits**:
    *   Designers see what's happening across the team, identifying overlapping work or interesting patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:58:00">[05:58:00]</a>.
    *   Product managers, engineering leaders, and company leaders gain a clear understanding of ongoing developments, enabling a "journey approach" to see how all pieces fit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:36:00">[05:36:00]</a>.
    *   It fosters early detection of redundancies or conflicting paths, ultimately leading to better and more efficient work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:18:00">[06:18:00]</a>.

## Hiring for Design Quality

When hiring designers, prioritize the following qualities over mere tool proficiency or process knowledge:

*   **Taste and Character**: It's easier to teach tools and processes than taste and character. Look for individuals with a natural inclination for great judgment and taste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:00">[01:11:00]</a>.
*   **Humility**: A humble designer is more likely to listen to users, be curious about what's working, and strive for continuous improvement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>.
*   **Hustle/Chutzpah**: Design is an act of creation, which can be scary. Look for courage to propose new ideas and to fight for excellence, even if it means pushing back on "good enough" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>.
*   **Early-Stage Companies**: While a "doer" is often needed, consider a senior leader design advisor alongside a full-time executor to bring a user-focused organizational strategy from the start <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>.

## Bold Ideas and Vision Work

Companies often fear bold ideas, opting for incremental approaches due to easier measurement and perceived safety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:00">[01:07:00]</a>. However, this is a "dangerous tendency" because users experience products holistically, across surfaces and over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:00">[01:08:00]</a>.

*   **"Reach for the stars, land on the Moon"**: This metaphor illustrates the importance of vision work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>. This means sketching out the ideal, "11-star experience" (as Brian Chesky describes it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>) that is comprehensive and considers the entire user journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>. Without this North Star, incremental changes may not lead to the desired outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>. Vision work provides a roadmap for measured, thoughtful progress, even if the entire vision isn't shipped at once <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>.

## The Nature of Design

Ultimately, design is "simply intention" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>. It involves bringing intentionality to decisions by considering who is impacted and using the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>. While design expertise brings creative skills and taste to the table, anyone in any function can bring more care and intentionality, leading to better outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:00">[01:48:00]</a>.

### Stripe Press: A Manifestation of Quality
Stripe Press, a publishing arm of Stripe, prints books focused on "ideas of progress" that align with Stripe's mission to increase the GDP of the internet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. The meticulous care put into these books, such as "Poor Charlie's Almanac," and their accompanying interactive websites (e.g., press.stripe.com) demonstrate Stripe's commitment to [[design_and_innovation_in_software_development | creativity and excellence]] and [[building_a_brand_and_maintaining_product_quality | its identity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:00">[01:55:00]</a>. The website design, for instance, aims to replicate the physical experience of browsing a bookstore online, showcasing the "Art and Science" meld <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>. This illustrates how passion for quality extends beyond core business products to shape a brand's perception.

## Learning to Lead

Dill's personal leadership journey has been shaped by key experiences:

*   **Airbnb Intervention**: Early in her tenure as Head of Experience Design at Airbnb, half of her 10-person design team staged an intervention, presenting a list of her perceived shortcomings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a>. This difficult moment taught her that she hadn't earned their trust and wasn't bringing the team along with her <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:59:00">[05:59:00]</a>. By shifting to a listening approach and making changes collaboratively, team engagement scores significantly improved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:59:00">[05:59:00]</a>. This underscored the importance of listening first as a leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:59:00">[05:59:00]</a>.
*   **The Power of Trust**: From [[understanding_the_importance_of_understand_work_in_leading_product_development | learning to fly]], Dill recalls a moment when her instructor moved his seat back during a landing, signifying his trust in her <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:00">[01:31:00]</a>. This visceral experience taught her the importance of showing faith in her team and colleagues to take on challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:00">[01:32:00]</a>.
*   **"Tomorrow is Today"**: This life motto reminds Dill to act now on intentions rather than deferring them, combating the tendency to put off important tasks or improvements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:00">[01:27:00]</a>.
*   **Merit and Hard Work**: Her parents instilled in her the belief that accomplishment is based on merit and hard work, not gender or physical attributes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:00">[01:28:00]</a>. This foundation has given her the "chutzpah" to pursue leadership roles and feel comfortable being in rooms where she might be outnumbered by people who don't look like her <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:00">[01:29:00]</a>.

## Conclusion

[[balancing_speed_and_quality_in_product_development | Balancing speed and quality in product development]] is an ongoing challenge, but by embracing design as intentionality, fostering a culture of meticulous craft, and implementing structured processes like "Walk the Store," organizations can directly tie quality improvements to business growth and build truly great products.
