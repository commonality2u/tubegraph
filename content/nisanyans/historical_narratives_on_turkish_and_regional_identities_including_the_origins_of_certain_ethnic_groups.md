## The Identity of "Greengrocers" (Manavlar)
The term "greengrocer" (manav) refers to a local agricultural people, distinct from [[Cultural and Historical Background of Turkish Yrk Tribes | Yoruk]] communities, primarily found within the former Ottoman province of Hüdavendigar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:20">[01:00:20]</a>. This province, centered around Bursa, included Balıkesir, the southern part of Çanakkale, Bilecik, İzmit, and Adapazarı <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>. The modern Turkish word "manav" (vegetable seller) has been derived from the name of this community since the 18th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:32">[01:00:32]</a>.

The term "greengrocer" is understood to be an administrative and legal designation rather than a purely ethnic one, applied to individuals meeting certain criteria within the province <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:03">[01:02:03]</a>. Key characteristics included being Muslim, distinguishing them from non-Muslims, and being sedentary, setting them apart from the nomadic [[Cultural and Historical Background of Turkish Yrk Tribes | Yoruk]] population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:29">[01:02:29]</a>.

### Conversion to Islam and Population Dynamics
A significant portion, possibly the majority, of the Muslim agricultural population in the Hüdavendigar province were local Byzantine inhabitants who converted to Islam <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:01">[01:05:01]</a>. This conversion was often driven by an adaptation to the political and administrative conditions of the new Ottoman state, allowing them to be recognized as Muslim taxpayers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:25">[01:05:25]</a>.

The process of conversion was complex and involved several factors beyond simple force or divine revelation:
*   **Seizure of Church Properties** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:36">[01:08:36]</a>: The Ottoman state confiscated church properties, leading to the decline of monasteries, which were vital centers for education and clergy training. This weakened the institutional support for the Christian religion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:11">[01:09:11]</a>.
*   **Rise of Islamic Institutions** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:46">[01:10:46]</a>: The establishment of mosques and madrasahs by Muslim figures provided new educational and social structures, filling the void left by the declining Christian institutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:01">[01:11:01]</a>.
*   **Childhood Acquisition of Religion** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:50">[01:11:50]</a>: Religion is primarily acquired during childhood, making it difficult for adults to fully change their fundamental sensitivities and emotional elements related to faith <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:04">[01:12:04]</a>.

### Migration and Cultural Blending
The Ottoman conquest spurred significant migration, akin to a "gold rush," as new territories offered opportunities for settlement and land acquisition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:52">[01:13:52]</a>. These migrants were predominantly Muslim, but their origins were diverse:
*   **Turks** who settled in the new properties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>.
*   **Armenians** who migrated from Eastern provinces to Western Anatolia (İzmit, Sakarya, Adapazarı) for work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:36">[01:14:36]</a>.
*   **Persians** from Iran <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:14">[01:16:14]</a>.
*   **Kurds** from Eastern provinces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:19">[01:16:19]</a>.
*   **Arabs** from Syria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:25">[01:16:25]</a>.
*   **Romani/Çingene** people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:29">[01:16:29]</a>.

These diverse groups, all at least nominally Muslim, intermarried over generations, leading to a mixed population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:44">[01:16:44]</a>. The continuation of certain traditions, such as specific dishes or celebrations like Hıdrellez among "greengrocers," does not necessarily indicate a purely Greek or pure Turkish origin, but rather a blending of local cultures over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:09">[01:17:09]</a>. ## Origin of "Malazgirt"
The town of Malazgirt, known for its historical significance, was originally named Manaz Gert before the arrival of the Turks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>. The suffix "kert" (or "gerd" in Western Armenian), from old Persian, means "city" or "castle" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:27">[01:42:27]</a>. This is seen in other place names like Carthage (Kartaca) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:45">[01:42:45]</a>. Thus, Manaz Gert translates to "Manaz city" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:12">[01:43:12]</a>.

According to a 6th-century historian, the town was founded by a person named Manav <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:16">[01:43:16]</a>. This legend was later corroborated by the discovery of Urartian cuneiform tablets at the end of the 19th and beginning of the 20th centuries, which revealed the name "Menas" (or Menu in Armenian) as one of the great Urartian kings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:49">[01:43:49]</a>. Archaeological data expanded the understanding of the Urartian kingdom, showing that it extended to the Bulanık and Malazgirt areas. Therefore, the city of Manaz Gert was likely founded by the Urartian King Menu or an ancestor with the same name <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:04">[01:45:04]</a>.

## The "Byzantine Empire" Terminology
The term "Byzantine Empire" to refer to the Eastern Roman Empire is a convention invented by European intellectuals and Renaissance historians in the mid-16th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:10">[01:16:10]</a>. The empire itself never used this name; "Byzantium" was originally the name of the city of Istanbul, not an empire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:45">[01:45:45]</a>. Although a 15th-century medallion depicting Sultan Mehmet the Conqueror as "Byzantine Imperator" exists <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:30">[01:46:30]</a>, this likely refers to him as "Emperor of Istanbul" rather than of a state called "Byzantine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:04">[01:47:04]</a>. The concept became widespread gradually, and Eastern Roman emperors never referred to themselves as Byzantine Emperors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:20">[01:48:20]</a>. This highlights how external perspectives can shape [[Cultural reflections and historical narratives in Turkey | historical narratives]] and [[Perception of historical and religious narratives in Trkiye | perception of historical narratives]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:10">[01:46:10]</a>.

## Hemşinlis
The [[Historical dynamics of ethnic groups in Turkey | Hemşinli]] people, particularly in the Tortum and İspir districts north of Erzurum, are understood to have migrated there after the Russian invasion of Erzurum province in 1828-1829 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:08">[01:34:08]</a>. A large part of Erzurum's Armenian population withdrew with the Russians, leaving many villages empty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:33">[01:34:33]</a>. This led to a significant migration from Hemşin to these depopulated areas, with almost all [[Historical dynamics of ethnic groups in Turkey | Hemşinli]] villages in Erzurum tracing their origins to 1829-1830 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:55">[01:34:55]</a>. They speak Turkish with a variant of the Hemşin dialect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:56">[01:33:56]</a>.

## Nature of History's Progression
The question of whether history progresses linearly or cyclically is a long-standing debate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:10">[01:50:10]</a>. The speaker asserts that it is "both" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:25">[01:50:25]</a>.

The 19th and early 20th-century Enlightenment view, often seen in Marxist thought, assumes a linear progression from primitive to advanced stages (e.g., feudalism to capitalism to communism) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:39">[01:50:39]</a>. However, this perspective is deemed insufficient because history does not always progress in a single direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:48">[01:51:48]</a>. Civilization has experienced frequent interruptions and collapses, such as:
*   The collapse after late antiquity (5th-12th centuries), leading to a "terrible collapse" in the early Middle Ages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:03">[01:52:03]</a>.
*   The decline of Islamic civilization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:20">[01:52:20]</a>.
*   Anatolia's cultural and economic decline from the 15th to the 19th centuries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:30">[01:52:30]</a>.
*   The collapse of the Upper Bronze Age civilization around 12th century BC, where writing and stone architecture disappeared for centuries, resembling a return to the Stone Age <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:58">[01:52:58]</a>.

Despite these cyclical collapses, technology has objectively advanced throughout history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:14">[01:54:14]</a>. Knowledge and acquired skills, though sometimes covered up or rendered unusable, have never been completely lost, especially after the invention of writing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:36">[01:54:36]</a>. Thus, history involves both a linear progression and violent cycles within that progression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:28">[01:55:28]</a>.
