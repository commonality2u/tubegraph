## Early Israelite History and the Archaeological Record

Archaeological evidence indicates that around 1000 BC, Jerusalem was merely a shepherd's settlement comprising approximately 30 households <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. This contrasts with common narratives, as Egyptian sources, despite being "quite rich" and from an "extremely literate" culture, contain no information or even the slightest indication of Jews being in or leaving Egypt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>.

Furthermore, Hebrew script did not exist at this time; its emergence was much later, around 700-800 BC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:17">[00:01:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>.

### The Rise and Fall of Northern and Southern Kingdoms

Around 800 BC, a highly developed kingdom emerged north of modern-day Israel. This Northern Kingdom featured at least two significant cult centers with temples and a rich aristocratic and royal class <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:41">[00:01:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:03">[00:02:03]</a>. It was utterly destroyed by the Assyrians in 712 BC, becoming an Assyrian colony, with a significant portion of its population exiled to areas around modern Mosul <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>. Non-Jewish, non-Hebrew tribes were subsequently brought in to colonize the area <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>.

Coinciding with the Northern Kingdom's destruction, Jerusalem's population increased fivefold in 712 BC, indicating its sudden rise as an important city <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:59">[00:02:59]</a>. A kingdom centered in Jerusalem emerged during this period, playing a vital political role for approximately 120 years, acting as a buffer state between Assyria and Egypt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>. This kingdom, though small, was significant within the political framework of the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>.

## Monotheism and Religious Reform in Judah

During this period, several important kings ruled. King Hezekiah (reigned 715-686 BC) saw a significant population increase in Jerusalem, likely due to immigrants from the North <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. His reign marked the "first stages of a new cult, a religious revival," and religious reform <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:44">[00:05:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. The prophet Isaiah, a chief ideologist of King Hezekiah's government, appears during this time, introducing the concept of the Messiah <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. Much of the narrative in the Bible/Torah is understood to have emerged during this "main formation period" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>.

Manasseh, Hezekiah's son, reigned for 40 years (686-642 BC) and is portrayed negatively in the Torah for deviating from monotheism and embracing foreign gods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:58">[00:06:58]</a>. However, archaeological and historical data indicate a period of significant wealth and prosperity, stemming from an alliance with Assyria, particularly through the olive oil trade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:30">[00:07:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:33">[00:07:33]</a>.

Manasseh's grandson, Josiah, initiated a "Wahhabism movement" or radical religious reform, consolidating worship in Jerusalem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:01">[00:08:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>. He systematically eliminated provincial shrines and local temples, destroying even the Baal temple in the North and desecrating graves of Baal priests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:45">[00:08:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>. The prophet Jeremiah, described as a "complete Middle Eastern fanatic" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>, was the chief ideologist for King Josiah, advocating for the destruction of "stone temples" and the concentration of all worship in the Jerusalem temple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:24">[00:09:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:12">[00:10:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:16">[00:10:16]</a>. This centralized religion, directed by Jeremiah's divine messages, led to significant chaos and resistance in the country <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:13">[00:11:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>.

## The Formation of the Torah Narrative

The main teachings and bulk of the Torah texts were created during the reigns of Hezekiah and Josiah, responding to the needs and polemics of that period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:54">[00:12:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:56">[00:12:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:06">[00:13:06]</a>. The narrative reflects the intense conflict between the Northern and Southern Kingdoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:40">[00:15:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:44">[00:15:44]</a>.

Key elements of this "official history" include:
*   **Conquest of Canaan:** The Torah describes the Israelites' complete slaughter of the Canaanite people and destruction of their cities under Joshua's leadership, aided by God <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:21">[00:13:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:24">[00:13:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:33">[00:13:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:40">[00:13:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:01">[00:14:01]</a>. However, other parts of the text indicate ongoing conflict with Canaanite cities and kings centuries later, with kings being punished for respecting Canaanite idols <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:12">[00:14:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:14">[00:14:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:16">[00:14:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:18">[00:14:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:21">[00:14:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:23">[00:14:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:27">[00:14:27]</a>. This contradiction suggests a polemic, with "northerners" always depicted as doing things incompletely and "southerners" as doing it correctly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:32">[00:15:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:35">[00:15:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</a>.
*   **Southern Dominance in Mythical History:** While the Northern Kingdom was dominant in the known historical period, the South became dominant in mythical history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:07">[00:17:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:10">[00:17:10]</a>.
*   **The David and Solomon Narrative:** The social descriptions of David and Solomon in the Torah reflect the court customs, writing traditions, and even harem elements of King Hezekiah and Josiah's era, retrospectively applied to ancestors 350-400 years earlier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:27">[00:17:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:32">[00:17:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:40">[00:17:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:44">[00:17:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:51">[00:17:51]</a>. David and Solomon are presented as mythical ancestors of the Southern kings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:59">[00:17:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:03">[00:18:03]</a>. The story of David, starting as a bandit leader who later became king, is a plausible reflection of how small power centers might have emerged in the region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:30">[00:19:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:32">[00:19:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:38">[00:19:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:41">[00:19:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:09">[00:20:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:16">[00:20:16]</a>.
*   **Moses and the Exodus:** There is no archaeological basis for the Exodus narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:02">[00:38:02]</a>. The names of Moses, Amram, Maryam, and Aaron are likely Egyptian, suggesting a connection to Egypt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:26">[00:21:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:29">[00:21:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:33">[00:21:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:36">[00:21:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:43">[00:21:43]</a>. This narrative likely served a political purpose, reflecting past conflicts or diplomatic needs with Egypt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:53">[00:22:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:56">[00:22:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:01">[00:23:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:02">[00:23:02]</a>. The narrative could have reinforced a struggle against Egyptian hegemony <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:30">[00:23:30]</a>.
*   **Creation of a Centralized Cult:** King Josiah's "discovery" of the book of laws in the Temple, which mandated a single temple and detailed sacrificial rules, was key to establishing a "new state ideology" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:21">[00:18:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:24">[00:18:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:26">[00:18:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:31">[00:18:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:35">[00:18:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:37">[00:18:37]</a>. This marks the establishment of a new cult based on a single-centered temple, canceling previous diverse cults <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:29">[00:40:29]</a>.

### Polytheism vs. Radical Monotheism

In the ancient world, polytheism was common, often serving as a diplomatic tool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:09">[00:26:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:12">[00:26:12]</a>. Alliances and trade led to the incorporation of foreign gods, with temples built for them as a sign of respect and partnership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:15">[00:26:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:18">[00:26:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:24">[00:26:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:27">[00:26:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:38">[00:26:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:40">[00:26:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:43">[00:26:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:45">[00:26:45]</a>.

The Torah, reflecting a political movement against foreign alliances, consistently criticizes the worship of foreign gods and details the punishments for such practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:42">[00:25:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:00">[00:26:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:26">[00:27:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:30">[00:27:30]</a>. Examples include King Ahab's marriage to a Phoenician and his construction of a vineyard temple, which was opposed by Prophet Elijah <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:39">[00:27:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:43">[00:27:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:45">[00:27:45]</a>. Similarly, Manasseh's alliance with the Assyrians led to the weakening of belief in Yahweh and the introduction of idols in temples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:45">[00:28:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:48">[00:28:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:51">[00:28:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:58">[00:28:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:01">[00:29:01]</a>. Josiah's reforms were a direct rejection of all idols and a relentless fight against them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:32">[00:29:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:36">[00:29:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>.

The transition to radical monotheism was not easy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:58">[00:29:58]</a>. The idea of a single God, with no intermediaries or other deities, was a radical concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:13">[00:25:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:17">[00:25:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>.
