## Historical Narratives in Art: The Mazeppa Opera

A particularly striking example of cultural narrative in Moscow is the staging of Tchaikovsky's opera *Mazeppa* at the Bolshoi Theater during wartime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:00">[02:50:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. Despite Mazeppa's betrayal and heavy defeat at the Battle of Poltava <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:00">[02:37:00]</a>, the state-backed production portrays him not as a "vile dog," but as a complex character driven by love and lust for power, experiencing a "real human tragedy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:11:00">[03:11:00]</a>. ## The Enduring Idea of the Roman Empire

The Roman Empire, often thought to have ended in 476 AD with the dethroning of the last Western Roman Emperor, continued as an "idea," a "narrative," and a "symbol" until 1806 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:00:00">[04:00:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:08:00">[04:08:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>. The Papacy, for centuries, maintained many institutions of the Roman Empire, preserving political sovereignty, institutional continuity, and the Latin language across old Roman lands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:19:00">[04:19:00]</a>. This continuity allowed for the declaration of new Roman Emperors, such as Charlemagne in 800 AD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a> and the German King Otto I in 963 AD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:00">[04:26:00]</a>. The title of Roman Emperor, held by German rulers until its abolition by Napoleon, underscored a continuous, albeit evolving, "Germanic empire" that remained a significant political and cultural element for 850 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:22:00">[04:22:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:30:00">[04:30:00]</a>. Even after 1648, the Germanic Empire retained a "ceremonial, symbolic quality" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:12:00">[04:12:00]</a>.

## Ottoman Empire and Turkish Identity

The [[cultural_reflections_and_historical_narratives_in_turkey | Turkish]] perspective on the Ottoman Empire often suffers from a "childish fear" of old customs and images <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>. This fear stems from two main sources:
1.  **Glorification by ignorant segments**: A segment of society, described as "ignorant" and "empty," glorifies the Ottoman Empire through "mythology television series" and "bigoted discourse," leading some to entirely identify the Ottoman Empire with this negative portrayal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>.
2.  **Republic's caricature**: The Republic's narrative, inherited from the Young Turks of the 1908 revolution, portrays the Ottoman Empire as a "rotten dictatorship," "land of bigotry and ignorance," particularly focusing on Abdulhamid's reign as a 33-year "black hole" of stagnation and tyranny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:00">[01:36:00]</a>. This narrative, reinforced by school education, creates a caricature that people believe and fear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:00">[01:35:00]</a>.
