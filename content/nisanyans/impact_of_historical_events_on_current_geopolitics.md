The current [[Global political dynamics and conflicts | global political dynamics]] are deeply rooted in historical events and the shifting balance of power. The ongoing conflict in Ukraine, for instance, is not merely a war between Russia and Ukraine, but rather a proxy conflict between Russia and the Western alliance, primarily NATO, led by the United States and its satellites <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>.

## The Aftermath of the Soviet Union's Collapse

Between 1989 and 1991, the Soviet Union, effectively the Russian Empire, was liquidated almost entirely peacefully and without bloodshed, an unprecedented event in history for a great world empire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:00">[04:37:00]</a>. After its collapse, Russian society held a strong expectation of being accepted and embraced by the West, dreaming of integration into European democratic institutions and culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:53:00">[05:53:00]</a>. This period, from 1991 to 1995, was marked by a "honeymoon" with Western countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:36:00">[07:36:00]</a>.

However, this hopeful period quickly soured. The Western world, particularly Americans, Germans, and others, descended upon Russia "with an army of looters," exploiting its natural resources, industrial facilities, art collections, treasures, and even its women <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:10:00">[08:10:00]</a>. Russian society, including some former Communist Party officials, consequently experienced "the ugly face of capitalism" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:18:00">[09:18:00]</a>.

By 1998, a natural reflex emerged within Russian society to counter this plunder, leading to the rise of what the West termed "Russian oligarchs" or "mafias," who used various means, including violence, to reclaim control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:32:00">[09:32:00]</a>. This shift was met with strong Western condemnation, accusing Russia of lacking democracy and rule of law <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:52:00">[10:52:00]</a>.

## NATO Expansion and Containment of Russia

Parallel to this economic exploitation, American rulers sought to further break up Russia, akin to the Soviet Union's dissolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:23:00">[11:23:00]</a>. Chechnya became a focal point, with foreign-trained Islamic militants, some from Turkey, sent to instigate a guerrilla war for independence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:42:00">[11:42:00]</a>. The election of Vladimir Putin in 2002 was largely predicated on his duty to suppress this movement and stabilize the country, which he achieved by quelling the Chechen conflict <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:16:00">[12:16:00]</a>. The Western world "never forgave Putin" for this action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:45:00">[12:45:00]</a>. Similar destabilization efforts were attempted in Tajikistan and Georgia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:11:00">[13:11:00]</a>, with Georgia, encouraged by external forces, developing a fanatic nationalism that led to conflict with Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:03:00">[14:03:00]</a>.

### The Evolution of NATO's Role

NATO was founded in 1949 specifically to combat the Soviet Union and limit its potential hegemony <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:00">[01:07:00]</a>. After the dissolution of the Warsaw Pact in 1991, NATO, logically, had no reason to exist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>. However, it persisted as a massive organization supporting a vast arms and propaganda industry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:00">[01:47:00]</a>. Like any bureaucratic organization, its primary purpose shifted to self-preservation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:00">[01:57:00]</a>, leading to a redefinition of its enemy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:00">[01:59:00]</a>.

Starting around 1997, NATO began to expand its influence. The first expansion was the unification of East and West Germany in 1991, with Russian consent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:00">[01:50:00]</a>. Subsequently, Poland, the Czech Republic, and Hungary joined NATO, despite initial assurances that these countries would only join the European Union, not NATO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:00">[01:52:00]</a>.

The situation escalated when three Baltic republics (Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia) joined NATO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:00">[01:55:00]</a>. These republics, historically created by Germany in 1918 as a strategic spearhead against Russia, were never independent before the 20th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>. Their inclusion in NATO effectively plugged Russia's access to the Baltic Sea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:04:00">[02:04:00]</a>. Further expansion included Bulgaria and Romania, and eventually parts of Yugoslavia were incorporated into NATO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:05:00">[02:05:00]</a>.

Since 2008, the idea of including Ukraine in NATO gained traction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>. Despite resistance from the Ukrainian administration, a coup in 2014 brought a pro-NATO government to power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:00">[02:11:00]</a>. This led to a bloody conflict in 2014, with tens of thousands killed in regions like Donetsk and Lugansk, whose demands for closer ties with Russia were suppressed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:00">[02:16:00]</a>.

Russia's position since the early 2000s, and particularly since 2008, has been clear: Ukraine cannot be included in NATO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:23:00">[02:23:00]</a>. Russia views Ukraine as part of its historical homeland, with a significant Russian population and integrated economic and cultural ties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:00">[02:29:00]</a>.

### Warning Voices and Policy Makers

Even within the Western world, sane voices, such as George Kennan, the theorist of the first Cold War, warned against this expansion. In 1997, Kennan stated that NATO's expansion policy towards Russia was "suicide, madness, stupidity," with no logical defense, and would inevitably lead to war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. Many other military, diplomatic, and academic figures echoed this warning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:00">[02:59:00]</a>.

The aggressive policy towards Russia is attributed to a "bureaucratic movement" that gained prominence in Washington during the Clinton period (1995-1996) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:00">[02:13:00]</a>. By 2000, this group, characterized by prejudice, arrogance, and a "sickness of power" rooted in seventy years of anti-communist propaganda, dominated American foreign policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:00">[02:16:00]</a>. This policy directly led to the current war, with the Western side being the primary party responsible for planning and causing it knowingly and willingly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>.

## Historical Parallels and Geopolitical Gambles

The current situation is seen as a "gamble, a loss shot" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:00">[03:14:00]</a>. Historically, those who underestimated Russia met with disappointment: Napoleon in 1812, the Ottoman Empire in 1877, and Hitler in 1941 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:00">[03:36:00]</a>.

### The Role of Germany and Eastern Europe

Germany, after 1989 and 1991, significantly expanded its economic influence across Eastern Europe, from Greece to Estonia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>. German companies, investments, and supermarkets dominated these markets, pouring in money and loans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>. However, these loans are likely unrecoverable, necessitating further expansion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:02:00">[04:02:00]</a>. Germany thus had two options: capture Ukraine to exploit its resources, or "play bigger and take Russia" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:04:00">[04:04:00]</a>. Initially targeting Ukraine, they are now focused on Russia, hoping for a regime change that would turn Russia into a "population area" for exploitation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>.

## Turkey's Strategic Approach

The ongoing conflict has reaffirmed the success of Turkey's recent foreign policy concerning NATO and Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:00">[01:21:00]</a>. In a potential war, Turkey's most appropriate policy would be one that serves its own interests, reminiscent of İsmet İnönü's policy during World War II <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>. This involves engaging with both sides, showing closeness to the West while also fulfilling some demands and wishes of the other side, even purchasing weapons from both <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:00">[01:26:00]</a>. This "slippery" approach is deemed the best policy for a country that lacks the power to independently cope with its neighbors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>. The war, while a problem, also presents an opportunity for Turkey to gain concessions by leveraging its strategic position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:00">[01:35:00]</a>.

Western capitalism desires to freely commodify the industrial resources, products, human capital, and existing values of these countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>. The resistance from these two major military powers to this commodification is the core issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:00">[01:50:00]</a>.
