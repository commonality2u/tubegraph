## Historical Naming and Geography: Palestine
The term "Palestine" and its connection to "Philistine" is rooted in ancient history. In the Torah, the Philistines are mentioned as "Piştim" or "Palestine," and are described with hatred <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:46">[00:24:46]</a>. Around the 12th century BC, Philistines established city-states on the coastal part of today's Gaza strip, leading to conflicts with the Israelites <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:14">[00:25:14]</a>.

The name "Palestine" as a geographical term gained prominence after the Roman Empire, following Jewish rebellions in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD, exiled the Jews and renamed the province to Palestine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:21">[00:26:21]</a>. This was done to erase the name of Judaism from the province's geography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:38">[00:26:38]</a>. For the next 2000 years, from Roman to Byzantine and Islamic eras, the country was known as Palestine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:50">[00:26:50]</a>. Arabs, unable to pronounce the "p" sound, named it "Felestin" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>. Even during the Ottoman period, while there was no administrative unit named Palestine, the term was commonly used as a geographical reference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:33">[00:27:33]</a>. When the British took control of the region in 1918, they established a province named Palestine, which remained a British colony and administrative unit until 1948 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:07">[00:28:07]</a>.

The use of "Palestine" as an insult or term of contempt originated from the Torah, where Palestinians are belittled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:26">[00:28:26]</a>. This became an idiom in Western languages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:45">[00:28:45]</a>.

## The Armenian Question and Eastern Anatolia
During World War I, Russia occupied eastern parts of Türkiye, including Trabzon, Erzurum, Gümüşhane, Bayburt, Erzincan, and Van <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:48">[01:31:48]</a>. Russia established a temporary administration and granted some powers to a "Western Armenia government" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:04">[01:32:04]</a>. The possibility of these areas becoming part of Armenia after the war was "quite strong" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:14">[01:32:14]</a>.

However, a key factor is that the Ottoman Empire had already "cleansed the Armenian population of the region in 1915," anticipating Russia's plans to establish an Armenian state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:02">[01:33:02]</a>. This demographic engineering by Türkiye aimed to prevent an Armenian-majority region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:50">[01:33:50]</a>. Furthermore, Russia's policy in Kars, which it had taken in a previous war, involved a "divide and rule" strategy, settling Armenians, Greeks, Turks, Kurds, and Russians, rather than establishing a purely Armenian state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:14">[01:35:14]</a>.

The Western powers, allies of Russia during WWI, also had their own interests. France, for example, insisted on control over Cilicia, including Adana and Antep, to counter a strong Russian presence and an Armenian state in the east <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:53">[01:37:53]</a>. The sudden Russian Revolution and subsequent withdrawal changed these complex geopolitical calculations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:49">[01:38:49]</a>.

## Cultural and Historical Sites: The House of Virgin Mary
The House of Virgin Mary, located in the Selçuk district near Şirince, is under the responsibility of the Catholic Church for its interior, while external services are managed by the Selçuk Municipality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:16">[01:19:16]</a>. The story of its discovery dates back to the 1820s or 1830s, when a bedridden Catholic nun in Germany or Austria reportedly had visions of the Virgin Mary, describing the location of a house <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:12">[01:20:12]</a>. Decades later, Catholics found ruins on Mount Ephesus, concluding it was the described location <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:14">[01:21:14]</a>.

However, the historical basis for the site is questioned:
*   The Aegean region of Türkiye is replete with Roman or early Byzantine ruins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:52">[01:21:52]</a>.
*   The belief that Mary came to Ephesus stems from a passage in the Bible where Jesus entrusts his mother to his youngest student <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:28">[01:22:28]</a>, and a later Book of Revelation attributed to Saint John, claimed to have been written in Ephesus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:29">[01:23:29]</a>. This link requires "a high level of faith" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:10">[01:23:10]</a>.
*   The observation that the Greeks of Şirince celebrated the Virgin Mary on August 15 at the ruins is presented as evidence, but the speaker points out that such celebrations occur in "every village where the Greeks live" across the region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:27">[01:24:27]</a>.

The discovery also coincided with the Catholic Church's "great struggle to convert Ottoman Christians to Catholicism" in the 1870s and 1880s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:50">[01:24:50]</a>, suggesting a political motivation. The site was not taken "very seriously" by the Christian world generally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:25">[01:25:25]</a>.

The House of Virgin Mary gained prominence in the 1950s when Türkiye developed its first systematic tourism policy. It was identified as a key element for attracting cultural and religious tourism from Europe, allowing tourists to "satisfy their Christian feelings" while leaving foreign currency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:40">[01:26:40]</a>.

## Historical Narratives: The Name 'Orhan'
The name "Orhan" is discussed in the context of medieval Turkish and Mongol history. According to Reşidüddin's *Câmi’u’t-Tevârîh*, the name of the Mongol leader in the Ergenekon Epic is Orhan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:48">[01:00:48]</a>. Orhan is considered a deep-rooted Turkish name <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:17">[01:01:17]</a>.

Reşidüddin, the grand vizier of the Ilkhanids (Mongols) around Tabriz or Rey, undertook a massive ideological project: an official compilation of history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:24">[01:02:24]</a>. This project involved the use of wooden block printing, an early form of printing in Islamic history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:42">[01:02:42]</a>. The goal was to synthesize Mongol sovereignty ideology with Islamic, Turkic, and Iranian themes in this official historical narrative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:23">[01:03:23]</a>. The founder of the Mongol nation was named Orhan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:46">[01:03:46]</a>. Coincidentally, Osman Gazi, the founder of the Ottoman dynasty, named his son Orhan around this time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:59">[01:03:59]</a>. This naming choice suggests an approval of the Ilkhanids' ideological project <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:44">[01:04:44]</a>.

After the political change that led to the overthrow and posthumous mutilation of Reşidüddin, his historical works were banned and destroyed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:10">[01:05:10]</a>. Following this shift in power, a significant ideological transformation occurred, leading to the sudden emergence of countless books written in the Turkish language across a vast geography, from Balıkesir to the Great Wall of China, after 250 years of Persian dominance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:52">[01:05:52]</a>. Persian had been the common cultural language, while Mongolian was the language of the elite, and Arabic the language of science <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:44">[01:06:44]</a>. This period, from 1306 to 1300 onwards, saw poets who had written in Persian suddenly switch to Turkish, and Turkish works began to appear in various regions like the Volga, Anatolia, Tabriz, Mosul, Egypt, Syria, Samarkand, and Hiva <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:34">[01:07:34]</a>. This demonstrates a significant shift in [[cultural_and_ethnic_history_in_turkey_and_the_surrounding_regions | cultural and ethnic history]].
