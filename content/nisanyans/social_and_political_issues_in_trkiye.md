## Controversy over the Adhan

A significant point of contention has been remarks regarding the Adhan (call to prayer) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>. While the Adhan itself is generally not objectionable in principle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>, concerns arise when it becomes excessively loud due to amplification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:02:56]</a>. Efforts to address noise complaints with municipalities are often ineffective, as some in the community believe they are making an important statement by not lowering the volume <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>. The act is described as aggressive and rude, akin to yelling in someone's face <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:52">[00:03:52]</a>. It is argued that the amplified call to prayer should be banned, as it is seen not as an act of worship but a tradition that has become an expression of a "shantytown belief related to the New Age," becoming common after the 1980s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.

## Tribal Structures and Regional Differences

There's a significant distinction between tribal structures in Western and Eastern Anatolia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:31">[00:46:31]</a>. In Western Anatolia, tribal structures were weaker and marginal, with the majority of the population being settled peasants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:12">[00:47:12]</a>. In the East, tribal structures played a leading role in social life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:00">[00:48:14]</a>.

The Euphrates river marks an ancient cultural dividing line in Türkiye <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:39">[00:54:39]</a>. West of the Euphrates, a bureaucratic central administration system was established since the Byzantine period, with strong state authority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:47">[00:50:53]</a>. East of the Euphrates, state influence was weak, leading to chaotic and anarchic governance by local Armenian, Georgian, Kurdish, and Arab elements who formed their own political organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:02">[00:55:12]</a>.

This historical context leads to a surprising conclusion regarding democratic culture: it is suggested that a free spirit in Türkiye is more likely to be found east of the Euphrates than in the West <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:42">[00:56:46]</a>. Western Türkiye's population is seen as timid due to a long history of submission to authority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:06">[00:56:16]</a>, whereas Easterners have not bowed down for 2000 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:21">[00:56:26]</a>.

## Education and Brainwashing

The differences between education and brainwashing are discussed, noting their similar techniques and methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:14">[00:40:27]</a>. Both involve making a person accept ideas through repetition, insistence, and authority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:49">[00:40:52]</a>. The distinction lies in social acceptance: if indoctrination is based on common social acceptance, it's called education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:17">[00:41:40]</a>. If perceived as rape, attack, or lies by the majority, it's seen as brainwashing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:49">[00:41:54]</a>. A simple test for this is whether a person is willing to subject their own children to the indoctrination: if yes, it's education; if no, it's brainwashing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:08">[00:42:30]</a>.

### Religion in Society
Ottoman society was highly organized around religion, which provided life discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:27">[01:16:35]</a>. Muslims, Armenians, Greeks, and Jews had their daily, weekly, and annual rhythms determined by their religious culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:04">[01:17:08]</a>. While there was a police force for Muslims to enforce religious duties, members of the ruling class in large mansions could bypass these obligations due to their privacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:34">[01:17:53]</a>. In contrast, those living in communities faced social pressure to comply <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:57">[01:18:02]</a>.

Ottoman society also included Bektashis and other religious orders who did not adhere to formal religious rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:16">[01:18:32]</a>, as well as a large Alevi population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:36">[01:18:40]</a>. Alevis are described as those who do not comply with the rules and customs of Sunni Islam <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:50">[01:18:54]</a>, though they have their own customs and beliefs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:16">[01:19:27]</a>.

In modern Türkiye, a cultural conflict has intensified over the last hundred years, with half of society perceiving religious rules as an imposition and tyranny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:38">[01:21:00]</a>. This contrasts with places like Samos, where religion is a natural part of life and not a political or social discussion topic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:20">[01:21:27]</a>.
