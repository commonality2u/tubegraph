## Reflections on Writing a Holy Book and the Nature of Holy Texts

The discussion delves into the [[the_evolution_and_interpretation_of_religious_texts | evolution and interpretation of religious texts]] and the philosophical challenges they present, particularly concerning the existence of evil and the concept of a single, omnipotent God.

### The Origin of Satan in Religious Texts

Satan first appears in the Torah, specifically in the book of the Prophet Zechariah, written around the mid-5th century BC (c. 450-440 BC) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:26">[01:22:26]</a>. This is notable because Satan is not mentioned in the core texts of the Torah or in earlier books by prophets from the 7th century BC, nor in David's 150 psalms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:20">[01:24:20]</a>.

The emergence of the Devil concept in Zechariah's book is linked to the influence of the Persian Empire and its state religion, Zoroastrianism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:38">[01:23:38]</a>. Zoroastrianism is explicitly based on a dualism of a good God and a "dark power that constantly struggles with him" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:02">[01:25:02]</a>. This dualistic framework offers a solution to a significant [[philosophical_and_ethical_considerations | philosophical gap]] in the older Jewish religion: if God is omnipotent, why is there so much evil and suffering? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:31">[01:26:31]</a>

The Jewish thought that shaped in the 5th century BC, which later influenced Christianity and Islam, resolved this by introducing a "1 and a half God" model <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:24">[01:28:24]</a>. This model posits an almighty God alongside a "devil Racim" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:41">[01:28:41]</a> who is not a god and is destined for defeat, thus incorporating evil without fully embracing dualism.

### On Writing a Holy Book

When contemplating writing a holy book, the speaker outlines several key principles to ensure its success and longevity, touching upon [[unquestioning_faith_and_morality | unquestioning faith and morality]] and the nature of [[religious_and_philosophical_discussions | religious and philosophical discussions]]:

*   **Contradictory Moral Teachings:** A holy book's moral teachings should be "contradictory from beginning to end" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:30">[01:43:30]</a>. This allows for endless discussion and interpretation over centuries, as people can argue that conflicting verses mean different things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:54">[01:43:54]</a>. This inherent contradiction is seen as the "most successful method to sell the holy book" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:07">[01:44:07]</a>, reflecting how all basic moral principles in the human mind often contradict each other, similar to proverbs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:18">[01:44:18]</a>.
*   **Broad Appeal:** The book must appeal to the "entire spectrum as a moral teaching," providing "bottomless and endless" material for discussion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:51">[01:45:51]</a>. Examples are drawn from the Quran and the Bible, noting how every kind of opinion and moral principle can be defended by relying on these texts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:33">[01:46:33]</a>.
*   **Attribution (Not Self-Proclamation):** The author should not speak in their own voice; instead, the teachings should be attributed to a "holy man" or "great teacher" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:49">[01:46:49]</a>. This creates a sense of reverence and allows for the figure to be mythologized and elevated, as "the Sheikh does not fly, the disciple makes him fly" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:24">[01:47:24]</a>.
*   **Inconsistency is Desirable:** It's beneficial if the "teacher's says" do not consistently match each other <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:10">[01:48:10]</a>. Consistency would limit the book's audience, as those who disagree with a clear stance would be excluded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:21">[01:48:21]</a>. To satisfy "both sides of any negotiation," ambiguity and broad interpretations are essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:59">[01:48:59]</a>.

These reflections underscore the [[the_importance_of_philosophical_thinking_and_its_limitations_in_solving_existential_questions | importance of philosophical thinking]] in understanding not only religious texts but also the human and societal dynamics that shape them.
