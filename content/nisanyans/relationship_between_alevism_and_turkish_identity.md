## Demographics and Diversity of Alevi Communities

The exact number of Alevis in Türkiye is unclear, with figures possibly ranging from 8 to 10 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:17">[00:00:17]</a>. However, there are significant [[demographics_of_alevis_in_turkey | Alevi communities]] with distinct origins and characteristics, illustrating [[differences_between_various_alevi_communities | differences between various Alevi communities]]:

*   **Anatolian Turks** Approximately 4 to 4.5 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>.
*   **Zazas** Another large community, with a slight indication of Turkish roots in the past <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>.
*   **Hatay Arab Alevis (Nusayris)** A large community in the south of Hatay, holding a more radical line in their [[alevi_religion_and_beliefs | Alevism beliefs]] than the other two groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a>.
*   **Thracian Alevis** Some of Albanian origin, possibly from Southern Albania and associated with Bektashiism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>.
*   **Tahtacılar** An element in the Aegean and Mediterranean regions whose origins are "completely unknown," though they speak Turkish. A French researcher concluded they are a group that existed before the arrival of Turks in Anatolia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:36">[00:01:36]</a>.

The idea that Alevis are descendants of ancient Turks continuing an isolated religion is dismissed as "shameful" and having "nothing to do with it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>.

## Historical Origins of Alevism

[[history_of_alevism | Alevism]] is described as a rebellion movement that emerged across Anatolia, Iran, and Syria at the end of the 15th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>. This movement was a "tremendous uprising" against Islam <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.

It incorporated various groups:
*   **Old Turkmen elements** Who had been in constant rebellion since the 13th century, refusing to pay taxes or bow to the state's teachers and mosques <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>.
*   **Kurds** A large portion joined the uprising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>.
*   **Armenians and Armenian converts** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>.
*   **Nusayris** (Arab Alevis) in coastal Syria, described as having "nothing to do with Islam" and including Christian converts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>. These groups found the new Alevi model suitable and declared themselves Alevis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.

Alevism formed as a defensive position due to oppression, massacres, and bans by the state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>. This oppression fostered integration, secret codes, solidarity organizations, and a distinct culture within the Alevi community <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:56">[00:04:56]</a>.

## The Turkish Identity Connection as a Defensive Mechanism

The assertion that [[historical_origins_of_alevism | Alevism]] is connected to old Turkish roots is characterized as a "metal pathology" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>. This viewpoint suggests that Alevis, not feeling they belong to the Muslim ummah and fearing Sunnis, have sought an alternative identity in "Turkism," clinging to a "fairy tale" of Central Asian origins as a "shelter" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:35">[00:05:35]</a>. This pursuit is seen as leading to an "ugly videology" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>.
