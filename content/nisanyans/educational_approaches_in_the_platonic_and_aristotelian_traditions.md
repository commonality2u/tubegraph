## Platonic Approach: Theory and Abstraction

[[Philosophical methodologies of Plato and Aristotle | Plato]] favored discussions on abstract and high-level subjects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:00">[01:19:00]</a>. His approach to understanding the state, for example, was primarily theoretical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:00">[01:41:00]</a>.

His works were typically written as literary dialogues, often featuring Socrates and other individuals engaging in conversations about profound topics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:00">[03:25:00]</a>. These dialogues were characterized by their wit and quality discussion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:45:00">[03:45:00]</a>.

### The Florentine Academy and Theory-First Method
Towards the end of the 15th century, around 1450, the Platonic Academy was established in Florence, partly influenced by Byzantine thinkers who sought refuge in the West after the fall of Istanbul <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:51:00">[12:51:00]</a>. This institution emerged as a rival to existing universities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:27:00">[13:27:00]</a>.

The Academy adopted a "theory-first" approach to knowledge:
> "First, we need theory for language, a theory is established through logic, Then we ask nature the questions that will say this theory is wrong. In other words, we sat down according to our thoughts and made our calculations, it should be like this, now let's go and see if this step is possible. In other words, if you don't do theory first and then experiment, you will end up in the wrong places, they said." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:41:00">[13:41:00]</a>

This perspective led to "extraordinary breakthroughs in science" in the subsequent 100-150 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:11:00">[14:11:00]</a>.

### [[Aristotles impact on science and philosophy | Aristotle's Influence]] on Science and Education
For centuries, especially in the [[The role of monastic institutions in historical education | Islamic world]] and later in the Western world, [[Aristotles impact on science and philosophy | Aristotle]] was regarded as the "patron of all non-religious sciences" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:21:00">[06:21:00]</a>. In early universities (13th-15th centuries), his works were considered almost as sacred as religious texts, a view transferred from Islamic madrasahs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:41:00">[06:41:00]</a>. Many of [[Aristotles impact on science and philosophy | Aristotle's]] works were translated into Western languages from Arabic, highlighting the significant role of Arab scholars in preserving and transmitting his thought <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:00:00">[07:00:00]</a>. [[Aristotles impact on science and philosophy | Aristotle]] was seen as the "philosopher himself," an embodied form of philosophical inquiry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:21:00">[07:21:00]</a>.

His empirical approach is evident in the common modern definition of science as knowledge based on observation and experiment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:12:00">[08:12:00]</a>. However, the transcript cautions that moving from concrete observations to abstract generalizations is a difficult process fraught with paradoxes and risks, as underlying assumptions can lead to incorrect conclusions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:36:00">[09:36:00]</a>. For example, historical medical knowledge, despite extensive observation and experimentation by figures like Galen and Ibn Sina, reached wrong conclusions due to metaphysical assumptions about the human body <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:43:00">[10:43:00]</a>. It was realized by the mid-15th century that simply adding more empirical data without a theoretical framework does not necessarily advance knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:39:00">[12:39:00]</a>.

## Conclusion

The [[Differences between Plato and Aristotle | differences between Plato and Aristotle]] in their educational approaches reflect their broader philosophical methodologies. [[Philosophical methodologies of Plato and Aristotle | Plato]] championed abstraction and a theory-first methodology, inspiring a later academy that prioritized theoretical construction before empirical testing. Conversely, [[Philosophical methodologies of Plato and Aristotle | Aristotle]] emphasized meticulous observation and detailed categorization, laying the groundwork for empirical science and a comprehensive curriculum that served as the foundation for Western and Islamic educational institutions for centuries.
