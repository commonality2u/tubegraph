## Krikor Zohrab: A Key Figure

Krikor Zohrab (Evtekno Zora), born in the 1890s, was a prominent lawyer and literary figure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>. He was considered the brightest lawyer of his period in Ottoman law and was a professor at Istanbul University <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>. Zohrab authored two important works on the Civil Code during that era <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>. Later, he entered politics, serving as one of the most active members of parliament from 1908 to 1915, tirelessly working on every serious issue and striving to reform the Ottoman state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>.

## Armenians' Transition to Catholicism

The transition of some [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] to Catholicism was part of the modernization efforts within [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian society]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:48">[00:56:48]</a>. This process occurred in several stages:
*   **Early 1700s:** Headman Vartabet of Sivas initiated significant reforms within the [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian community]] after the Ottoman Empire's defeat in Europe around 1699 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:06">[00:57:06]</a>. These reforms included establishing schools for girls, modernizing the [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian language]], and creating a dictionary from the spoken language <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:24">[00:57:24]</a>. Vartabet's efforts were condemned by both the Ottomans and the [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian Church]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:50">[00:57:50]</a>. He eventually took refuge with the Venetians, who helped him establish a monastery near Venice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:09">[00:58:09]</a>. This monastery became a vital center for modern [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian culture]], publishing, and education for centuries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:36">[00:58:36]</a>.
*   **1760s:** The Catholic Church, based in Rome, systematically endeavored to convert Eastern Christian communities, including Nestorians and [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]], to Catholicism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:16">[00:59:16]</a>. This effort was centered in [[armenian_history_in_istanbul | Istanbul]] and, more importantly, in Aleppo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:33">[00:59:33]</a>. Individuals within the [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian society]] who were dissatisfied with the traditional oligarchic church structure and believed in Westernization as the sole solution embraced Catholicism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:51">[00:59:51]</a>.
*   **1828 Crisis:** This shift led to a major crisis in [[armenian_history_in_istanbul | Istanbul]], with street clashes between traditional [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian Church]] adherents and Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:16">[01:00:16]</a>. The Ottoman state viewed Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] with suspicion due to their perceived ties with Europeans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:39">[01:00:39]</a>. During this period, Mahmud II, dealing with the Greek rebellion, sought closer ties with the [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian community]], seeing them as a "loyal people" unlike the rebellious Greeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:45">[01:00:45]</a>.
*   **Deportation to Ankara:** Reportedly, at the request of a prominent [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian community]] member named Mira, all Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] in [[armenian_history_in_istanbul | Istanbul]] were deported to Ankara in a terrible manner, suffering deaths from starvation and hardship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:42">[01:01:42]</a>. Despite the hardships, they managed to take root in Ankara, making it a center for Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>. By 1915, Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] constituted 30-40% of Ankara's population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:14">[01:02:14]</a>. There was historical animosity between Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] and the local [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian Church]] adherents in Ankara, with stories of spitting on the ground when Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] passed by their churches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:28">[01:02:28]</a>. The Ankara Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian community]] played a leading role in the mohair wool trade, a significant export for Turkey at the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:08">[01:03:08]</a>.
*   **1915 Deportations:** In May 1915, the government initially decided that Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] would not be deported, believing they did not support [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian nationalism]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:29">[01:03:29]</a>. However, this policy was reversed in August, and Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] in Ankara were also deported <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:09">[01:04:09]</a>. Less information is available about the Catholic [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenian community]] in Mardin, though there was a significant Catholic Syriac community and a magnificent archbishop's building there <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:19">[01:04:19]</a>.

## Armenia-Azerbaijan Conflict and Turkish Involvement

The conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan has a significant religious dimension, as religion has enabled [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] to preserve their identity for over a thousand years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:39">[01:12:39]</a>. However, the immediate cause of the recent crisis is attributed to the insecurity of the Azerbaijani regime, which is described as an illegitimate family rule seeking to show successes to its people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:25">[01:13:25]</a>. This involves promoting the conquest of Nagorno-Karabakh and fostering intense hatred for [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:12">[01:15:12]</a>.

Turkey's involvement in the conflict appears to be driven by several factors:
*   **Financial Benefits:** There have been reports of significant financial transfers from Azerbaijan to Turkey (e.g., $8 billion in recent months) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:43">[01:15:43]</a>.
*   **Regional Dominance:** Turkey seeks to assert its role as the "elder brother" in the region, projecting a message of leadership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:02">[01:16:02]</a>.
*   **UAV Technology Showcase:** The conflict provided an opportunity to test, introduce, and advertise newly developed UAV (unmanned aerial vehicle) technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:06">[01:16:06]</a>.
*   **Geopolitical Strategy:** A long-standing Turkish national goal is to establish a land connection with Central Asian Turks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:24">[01:16:24]</a>. Armenia is perceived as a country created by Russia to obstruct Turkish expansion towards Central Asia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:44">[01:16:44]</a>. Turkey aimed to break this obstruction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:54">[01:16:54]</a>.

The conflict resulted in Russia's intervention, leading to a new arrangement where Nagorno-Karabakh's future remains uncertain, with a decision to be made in five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:05">[01:17:05]</a>. As a "consolation gift" to Turkey, a highway in Nakhchivan was promised, though its future and conditions are yet to be seen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:26">[01:18:26]</a>.

## Historical Dynamics and Cooperation

Historically, [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] and their neighbors have not always been at odds; conflicts often arose from local disputes and political sovereignty issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:57">[01:22:57]</a>. Until the early 20th century, the majority of their neighbors were tribes involved in animal husbandry, leading to specific challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:15">[01:23:15]</a>.

*   **Karabakh Principality:** The establishment of the Karabakh principality by Ibrahim Khalil Khan in 1748 was based on intricate Azeri-Armenian cooperation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:40">[01:23:40]</a>. Similar patterns of cooperation were observed in other eastern principalities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:12">[01:24:12]</a>.
*   **Division of Labor:** In regions like Sason, Kulp, and Mutki, a division of labor existed where Muslims and Kurds held authority and carried weapons, while Christian [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]] primarily engaged in agriculture and certain crafts, a system that suited both sides under specific conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:38">[01:24:38]</a>.
*   **Caucasus Tensions:** During the Soviet period, tensions always existed in the Caucasus between [[armenian_history_and_relations | Armenians]], Georgians, and Azeris <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:42">[01:25:42]</a>. However, with independence, the assertion of nation-state identities led to the collapse of this complex historical infrastructure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:09">[01:26:09]</a>.
