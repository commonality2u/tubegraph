## Ottoman Empire: Three Historical Periods

The history of the Ottoman Empire can be broadly divided into three distinct periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>:

1.  **Period of Rapid Establishment**
    *   The Ottoman Empire was established with incredible speed and dynamism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.
    *   It brought a new type of Islamic Empire model, which was extraordinarily successful in a short time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>.
    *   This "slave empire" model was based on extremely strong central control, bringing the economy under state control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>.
    *   It was a highly rational model within its own assumptions, learning from the "failed examples" of the Abbasid and Byzantine states <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>. The Ottomans systematically and rationally tried to purify themselves from these past mistakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a>.

2.  **Period of Inevitable Collapse and Destruction**
    *   Though initially successful, the Ottoman model inherently led to a great collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>.
    *   By the 16th century, global trade routes were shifting, Europe was rapidly gaining strength in technology and economy, and its population was increasing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>.
    *   The highly centralized Ottoman model, similar to the Soviet model, proved fragile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>.
    *   The [[decline_of_the_ottoman_empire | Ottoman decline]] was one of the longest and most painful destruction stories in world history, lasting approximately 350 years, from the 1570s/1580s until 1918 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.
    *   Regions like Anatolia and the Eastern Mediterranean basin, historically rich and civilized, were reduced to a "Stone Age" level of poverty, low literacy, and social destruction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.
    *   This was due to the collapse of the production and management models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:03">[00:12:03]</a>. The *timar* system, a state-controlled production system, collapsed between 1500 and 1590 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>. Villagers fled, became nomads, and agricultural lands lay uncultivated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>.
    *   By the 18th century, European travelers noted that crossing into Ottoman lands meant entering an area of isolation, abandonment, destruction, and poverty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>. This contrasted sharply with the early 1500s, when Anatolia and Rumelia were among the world's richest regions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>.

### The Problem of Surplus Value Exploitation

A core issue identified was the Ottoman Empire's unprecedented level of surplus value exploitation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:09">[00:28:09]</a>.
> "The skill is to collect surplus value neither too little nor too much. You can only collect as much tax as a society can handle, but the great success and great disaster of the Ottoman Empire and it achieved an unprecedented level of surplus value exploitation and as a natural result of this it collapsed within the Soviet Union and let me tell you the same story." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:53">[00:27:53]</a>

This over-exploitation, similar to the Soviet model, initially yielded vast wealth and allowed for magnificent projects like mosques and palaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:15">[00:27:15]</a>. However, it eventually led to widespread poverty and societal collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:27">[00:27:27]</a>.

## Modernization Efforts and Their Limitations

By the early 1800s, the Ottoman Empire was on the brink of complete collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:26">[00:13:26]</a>. This led to a period of reform, starting with Selim III and continuing under Mahmud II <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:39">[00:13:39]</a>.
*   The only perceived model for success was Europe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:56">[00:13:56]</a>. The aim was to adopt European consultants, education, languages, and systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:03">[00:14:03]</a>.
*   These reforms were "radical" but largely confined to a narrow elite circle in Istanbul, specifically within the Bosphorus, Beyoğlu, and Eminönü areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>.
*   While significant changes occurred within this elite, they failed to fundamentally transform the broader empire and led to "uncalculated and unforeseen disasters" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:41">[00:15:41]</a>.

### Comparison with Japan and Russia

The failure of Ottoman [[modernization_efforts_in_turkish_history | modernization efforts]] is often contrasted with the successes of Japan and Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:04">[00:16:04]</a>.

*   **Japan:** Possessed an extremely homogeneous society and was an island nation, allowing for a unified cultural and ideological approach to modernization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:34">[00:16:34]</a>.
*   **Russia:** Despite being an empire, Russia managed to enforce a "Russification" process, homogenizing diverse tribes into a common culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:20">[00:17:20]</a>.
*   **Ottoman Empire:** Was an empire established by conquest and tyranny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:05">[00:18:05]</a>, comprising diverse national and religious identities that resisted assimilation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:18">[00:18:18]</a>. The empire could never become a homogeneous nation-state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:32">[00:18:32]</a>.
    *   The Greek independence marked a major turning point, as it showed that national identity could form a separate political entity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:48">[00:18:48]</a>.
    *   The Ottoman system, by the 19th century, had created an economic, social, and cultural collapse across its territories <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:24">[00:19:24]</a>.
    *   Various regions, such as Egypt, Adana (Kozanoğlu), Bergama (Karaosmanoğlu), and parts of the Balkans (Tepedelenli Ali Pasha), operated as virtually independent entities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:39">[00:19:39]</a>.

## Mentality of the Ottoman Elite

The Ottoman elite, particularly from the 17th century onwards, became increasingly detached and conservative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:57">[00:29:57]</a>.
*   **Conservative Nature:** Conservatism is defined as "we cannot find a way out" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:19">[00:30:19]</a>.
*   **Economic Blockage and Internal Conflicts:** Starting from the later years of Kanuni (Suleiman the Magnificent) and deepening into the 17th century, the economic foundations weakened, leading to internal struggles and a "ruthless, bloody central dictatorship" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:34">[00:30:34]</a>. The cessation of conquest and plunder, the empire's basic mechanism, further exacerbated this <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:26">[00:32:26]</a>.
*   **"Ivory Tower" Culture:** The elite developed an "ivory tower" mentality, cultivating a sophisticated but incredibly narrow culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:54">[00:38:54]</a>.
    *   Their literature, such as Şeyhülislam Esad Efendi's dictionary *Lehçetü'l Lügat* (1732), was highly complex and understood by only a very small circle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:33">[00:39:33]</a>.
    *   The late adoption of the printing press (which came to the Ottoman Empire late <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>) reflects this elitist attitude <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:21">[00:41:21]</a>. The purpose was not widespread knowledge, but to maintain control and exploit the population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:43">[00:41:43]</a>.
    *   There was a fear that fundamental changes would threaten the power of the Muslim minority who ruled the country based on conquest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:00">[00:35:00]</a>.

## Conclusion

The [[modernization_efforts_in_turkish_history | Ottoman reforms]] were ultimately a "dead end" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:02">[00:43:02]</a>. The established structure was so fragile that any significant change risked immediate collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:11">[00:43:11]</a>. This systemic failure, particularly from 1808 to 1918, led to profound and lasting negative consequences for Turkey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:54">[00:43:54]</a>. Turkey, once one of the world's richest, most productive, and cultured countries, is now described as "a misery and a garbage dump" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:59">[00:43:59]</a>.
