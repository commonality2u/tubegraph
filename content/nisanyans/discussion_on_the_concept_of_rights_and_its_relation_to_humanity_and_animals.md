## The Human Concept of Right

The idea of "right" is presented as a basic human instinct and a core dimension of conscience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:17">00:35:17</a>. Humans constantly evaluate their actions and positions in terms of what is "right," even in daily decisions like choosing clothes or how to interact with others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:32">00:35:32</a>. This self-assessment includes recognizing when another person has a right, rather than solely insisting on one's own <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:07">00:36:07</a>.

It is suggested that the concept of right is an inherent, "biological category" for humans, present in every individual who is not mentally ill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:51">00:36:51</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:20">00:38:20</a>. While the specific "calculus of right" can vary between individuals, the underlying sense of right is universal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:56">00:37:56</a>.

### Intersubjectivity of Rights
A crucial aspect of rights is their intersubjective nature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:36">00:38:36</a>. This means the concept of rights is predicated on the assumption that another individual is also a "subject" with an inner self and feelings, just like oneself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:00">00:39:00</a>. When interacting with others—whether accepting, hiring, or even causing harm—humans make a calculation that implicitly acknowledges the other person's humanity and rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:29">00:39:29</a>. Therefore, the concept of rights always precedes a relationship with another subject who possesses rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:33">00:39:33</a>.

## Animal Rights: A Problematic Concept

The speaker argues that the concept of "animal rights" is deeply flawed, dangerous, and harmful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:03">00:40:03</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:47">00:41:47</a>.

> "When you say animal rights, we empty the concept of rights. It is a dangerous and harmful thing. Because this carries the risk of reducing people's rights and moral behaviors to the level of animals. It carries the risk of destroying them." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:03">00:40:03</a>

This perspective arises from the view that animals lack the fundamental "mechanism" to recognize or assert rights in the human sense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:44">00:36:44</a>. An animal acts based on instinct and feeling, not on a reciprocal understanding of rights with another subject <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:40">00:37:40</a>. There cannot be a "balance of rights" between humans and animals because animals do not view humans as equal, absolute subjects, nor do they recognize human rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:15">00:41:15</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:28">00:41:28</a>.

### Duty Towards Animals
While rejecting the notion of "animal rights," the speaker emphasizes that humans have a *duty* to treat animals well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:29">00:40:29</a>. This duty is not towards the animals themselves in a reciprocal sense, but rather "towards other people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:29">00:40:29</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:36">00:40:36</a>. Being a "good and compassionate person" is an important value that extends to respecting and treating animals and even inanimate beings well <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:39">00:40:39</a>. However, this benevolence does not equate to animals possessing "rights" comparable to those of humans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:45">00:41:45</a>.
