## Historical Context of Anatolian Demographics

### Muş and the Armenian Alphabet

The village of Güven (Hatrik) in Muş is historically significant as the birthplace of Mesrop Mashtots, who invented the Armenian alphabet in 405 AD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:51">[01:24:51]</a>. The alphabet's purpose was to translate the Bible into Armenian, a pivotal event in Armenian history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:05">[01:25:05]</a>. This invention allowed the Armenian nation to maintain its cultural and religious identity as a distinct entity, preventing assimilation into Iranian (Zoroastrian) or Greek (Christian) cultures, especially after the collapse of the Armenian Kingdom by 428 AD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:28:14">[01:28:14]</a>.

At the beginning of the 19th century, nearly all villages on the Muş plain were Armenian <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:56">[01:29:56]</a>. The Muş Principality was Kurdish, with Kurdish beys and soldiers governing the region <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:55">[01:30:55]</a>. Kurdish highlanders and Armenian villagers had complex relationships, including both friendships and enmities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:49">[01:30:49]</a>.

By the 1830s, the Ottomans liquidated the Kurdish principalities, establishing a more centralized administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:21">[01:33:21]</a>. This upset the balance between religions and tribes. By the end of the 19th century, the majority of the Muş plain's population had become Muslim and Kurdish <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:53">[01:33:53]</a>. Many Armenians converted to Islam for social reasons, subsequently adopting Kurdish as their language and integrating into Kurdish tribes, leading to their assimilation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:07">[01:34:07]</a>. By 1918, the Armenian population in Muş had largely disappeared <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:13">[01:35:13]</a>.
