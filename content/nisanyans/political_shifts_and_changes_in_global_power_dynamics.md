## The Rise of Neoconservatism in American Foreign Policy
A significant question in recent [[current_global_power_dynamics_and_future_prospects | world politics]] is how Neoconservatism managed to become so hegemonic in America as the Cold War ended <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:03">[00:26:03]</a>.

### Origins and Influence
In the 1980s, a movement called Neoconservatism began to emerge within academic circles and journals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:33">[00:27:33]</a>. An idea magazine called *Commentary*, published by Norman Podhoretz, served as a home for this movement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:40">[00:27:40]</a>. Most of its writers were former Marxists and even Trotskyists, who gradually shifted towards a more radical Neo-Imperialist stance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:58">[00:27:58]</a>.

Their influence began to be felt in the late 1990s during the Clinton administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:21">[00:28:21]</a>. By 2001, coinciding with George W. Bush's presidency, they gained significant power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:34">[00:28:34]</a>. Following the Twin Towers attack, this group completely took over America's internal and external security policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:07">[00:29:07]</a>. Figures like the Kristols initially dominated the American Foreign Ministry and then the Pentagon during the Rumsfeld period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:29:25">[02:29:25]</a>. They maintained their influence within the Republican Party until 2016, but after Donald Trump's takeover, they switched allegiance to the Democratic Party and are currently influencing global affairs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:56">[00:29:56]</a>.

### Ideology of Neoconservatism
The ideology of Neoconservatives is characterized by:
*   Ignorance, prejudice, arrogance, and self-importance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:37">[00:30:37]</a>.
*   A belief that America must dominate the world, as there is "no other way" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:03">[00:31:03]</a>.
*   The use of moral discourse such as "democracy, human rights, and freedom" as propaganda <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:17">[00:31:17]</a>.
*   Categorical hostility towards Russia, with the goal of its disintegration and destruction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:25">[00:31:25]</a>. This hostility is attributed to growing up in the Cold War years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:06">[00:36:06]</a>.

This ideology arose not from a calculated "deep state" plan, but from the natural progression of certain ideas gaining traction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:07">[00:32:07]</a>. Pre-1980s American foreign policy was overseen by cadres with deep intellectual bases, characterized by a "ruthless but not cowboy-like" approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:27">[00:33:27]</a>. The shift is attributed to a generational change, with those graduating after 1968 often lacking moral grounding and historical knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:29">[00:34:29]</a>. Their past as radical Marxists is seen as a symptom of this lack of foundational understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:51">[00:34:51]</a>.

### The Role of Ethnic Background
A significant observation is that many prominent Neoconservatives are of Eastern European Jewish descent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:03">[00:42:03]</a>. While dismissing the idea of an organized Jewish global conspiracy, it is suggested that cultural past, ethnic origin, and family tradition may contribute to certain handicaps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:45">[00:42:45]</a>. The *Commentary* magazine itself was described as specifically a "Jewish magazine" that dealt with issues of Jewish society and thought, from which this influential cadre was raised <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>.

### Opposition to Russia and Europe's Future
The Neoconservative hostility towards Russia is seen as irrational, rooted in the need for an "enemy" to defeat and destroy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:00">[00:38:00]</a>. Despite Russia abandoning its empire in 1991 in an unprecedented manner, seeking to integrate with Europe, the American "cowboys" perceived this as a threat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:17">[00:38:17]</a>.

A Europe united with Russia, leveraging Europe's lack of natural resources and Russia's abundance, is presented as the only way to preserve Europe's superior global position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:00">[00:39:00]</a>. A Europe deprived of Russia would be forced to exploit other regions or shrink <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:26">[00:39:26]</a>. The Neocon goal is to destroy Russia, as evidenced by American actions in Iraq, Libya, Syria, Yemen, and Serbia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:01">[00:40:01]</a>. America, in this view, lacks experience in developing or supporting countries, preferring to "pull the plug" and withdraw <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:25">[00:40:25]</a>. This approach is deemed a "crime against humanity" that must be prevented at all costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:51">[00:40:51]</a>.

## Limitations of Elected Governments and Separation of Powers
The nature of power in today's world suggests that elected politicians have limited roles in key and vital policy areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:50">[00:51:50]</a>.

### Autonomous Institutions
Security institutions (military, police, intelligence) are described as "completely autonomous" today <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:30">[00:52:30]</a>. They have guaranteed budgets, non-transparent decision-making mechanisms, and extraordinary power beyond the control of governments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:46">[00:52:46]</a>.

Monetary policies and financial institutions (Central Banks, banks) also operate beyond government control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:40">[00:53:40]</a>. They can "ruin the country" if governments attempt policies not approved by the existing financial order <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:02">[00:54:02]</a>.

International relations are another area where countries are "very intensely dependent and connected to others" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:30">[00:54:30]</a>. Even cultural policy is influenced by global organizations like UNESCO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:58">[00:54:58]</a>.

### The Illusion of Democracy
The speaker views the regime called "democracy" as "95% fraud" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:05">[00:52:05]</a>. The role of political leaders is largely to make society "feel good" and give the impression that citizens' voices are heard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:22">[00:55:22]</a>. The example of the American presidency suggests that the actual management of the country is done by others, not necessarily the elected leader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:48">[00:55:48]</a>.

### Obsolescence of Separation of Powers
The concept of separation of powers, originating from French thinkers and adopted by the U.S. Constitution, envisioned independent legislative, executive, and judicial branches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:40">[01:44:40]</a>. However, this has become an outdated concept. Today, the executive branch, represented by public bureaucracy, controls 50% of the national income and consists of millions of professionals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:52">[01:45:52]</a>. This professional bureaucracy is far more powerful than amateur legislative bodies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:54">[01:46:54]</a>. The judiciary, globally, tends to side with the state bureaucracy in disputes with citizens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:12">[01:48:12]</a>. Thus, the idea of separation of powers is now a "pleasant memory of history" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:30">[01:48:30]</a>.

### [[political_developments_in_turkey_and_global_geopolitics | Political Developments in Turkey and Global Geopolitics]]
The July 15, 2016, coup attempt in Turkey is described as a "bait," with the "real coup" occurring the next day through a massive, pre-planned purge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:30">[01:38:30]</a>. The potential outcome if the Fethullah movement had seized power is uncertain, deemed akin to "taking one and hitting the other" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:00">[01:39:00]</a>.

Regarding the possibility of a coup in Turkey today, it is suggested that a coup against the existing government, like those in 1960 and 1980, is unlikely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:55">[01:23:55]</a>. Instead, a "coup aimed at changing the cadre within the ruling structure" is always possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:01">[01:24:01]</a>. Unless one or more stakeholders within the ruling party unite to change the government through non-electoral means, a change of government is not foreseen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:20">[01:24:20]</a>.

#### Secularism in Turkey
Kemalism, the founding ideology of the Turkish Republic, is characterized as an "Islam-centric" movement that aimed to transform Turkey from a multi-religious society into an almost exclusively Muslim one by eliminating the non-Muslim population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:26">[00:58:26]</a>. In this sense, Atatürk is seen as a "representative hero of the Islamic majority" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:17">[00:59:17]</a>.

Secularism in Turkey was a policy to break the restrictive power of the traditional Islamic clergy, who often hindered state reforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:10">[01:11:10]</a>. The state asserted its role as the administrator of the Islamic community, expecting religious scholars and orders to remain silent or face suppression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:31">[01:01:31]</a>. This was driven by the need for reform and a realization that the West's power stemmed from fields like mathematics and industry, requiring a shift from traditional religious education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:03">[01:04:03]</a>.

#### The Role of Circassians in Turkish History
Following the collapse of the Ottoman Empire's outer parts, significant migrations occurred to Turkey from the 1860s onwards, notably from Rumelia (Balkans) and the Caucasus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:48">[01:09:48]</a>. These two groups, especially the Rumelian immigrants (like Enver and Talat), formed the "lifeblood" of the project to build a new Turkish state purified of non-Muslims <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:01">[01:11:01]</a>.

Caucasian migrants, including Circassians, were perceived with both admiration and suspicion by the Turkish state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:40">[01:13:40]</a>. While they were seen as warlike and aligned with the Turkish national project due to their animosity towards Armenians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:41">[01:12:41]</a>, their tradition of independence and autonomy meant they were not as easily controlled as other groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>. This led to an "ambivalent and serious attitude" towards them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:25">[01:12:25]</a>.
