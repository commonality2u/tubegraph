### Impact of the September Earthquake
A "terrible reality" was encountered along the road from the mountains to Marrakesh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:52">[00:03:52]</a>. The main epicenter of the September earthquake was along this route, in the mountainous region south of Marrakesh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. Approximately 40 villages and two large towns were completely destroyed, leveled to the ground with no buildings left standing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>. Cars were still visible under rocks from mountain slides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>, and restaurant signs remained despite old buildings being gone, creating a "very very sad scene" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>. Seven months post-earthquake, people were living in tents or containers, and there seemed to be little movement to rebuild their lives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:53">[00:04:53]</a>.

### Berber Identity and Arab Culture
Historically, the local Berber population in Morocco resisted Islamic rule for centuries when Arabs conquered these lands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:44">[00:10:44]</a>. However, subsequent dynasties and rulers of Morocco, although often of native Berber origin, consistently adopted Arab culture, identity, and the Islamic religion as their "flag" when establishing states or asserting dominance beyond their tribes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:26">[00:11:26]</a>. This was because Islam and Arab civilization became a "symbol of civilization" and a tool to gain submission beyond small tribal areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:36">[00:12:36]</a>. Berber feudal dynasties that conquered the country invariably boasted of Arab origin and being more Muslim than others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:36">[00:11:36]</a>.

### [[historical_and_cultural_connections_between_morocco_and_the_ottoman_empire | Ottoman Empire's Limited Influence and Attempts]]
Despite similarities, there is a significant difference from the Ottoman Empire: Moroccan state organization lacked the permanence and sophistication of the Ottoman state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:43">[00:16:43]</a>. Moroccan dynasties were temporary, constantly facing questions of legitimacy and rebellions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:56">[00:16:56]</a>. The Moroccan state would "disintegrate like a rosary" every 30-100 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:50">[00:17:50]</a>. In contrast, the Ottomans had a single dynasty for 600 years and a strong institutional structure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:10">[00:18:10]</a>.

In the 16th and 17th centuries, the Ottoman Empire made efforts to establish sovereignty in Morocco, even capturing the city of Fez twice and briefly holding the city of Morocco (Marrakesh) once <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:20">[00:20:20]</a>. These efforts were driven by a desperate search for a port on the Atlantic Ocean <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:22">[00:21:22]</a>. The shift in global trade routes from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic left the Ottoman Empire, which controlled the former, facing an impasse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:21">[00:22:21]</a>. However, Ottoman attempts to establish a foothold in Morocco, like their ventures in Abyssinia and India, largely ended in "fiasco" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:22">[00:24:22]</a>. They had to withdraw from Morocco after a short attack period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:29">[00:24:29]</a>.

Despite the limited direct political control, there is significant admiration for the Ottoman Empire and Turkey in Morocco and the wider Arab world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:02">[01:32:02]</a>. Turks are seen as "rich relatives" and the "whitest Muslim country" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:21">[01:32:21]</a>. Ottoman influence can be seen in the imitation of the Janissary army by the 17th-century Alawi dynasty, which established a professional army with Turkish commanders and officers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:31">[01:33:31]</a>. The Turkish title "Pasha" was also adopted and used, as exemplified by "Ba Ahmed Pasha," a prime minister and dictator in the 1890s, whose palace in Marrakesh is still called "Pasha's Palace" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:57">[01:33:57]</a>.
