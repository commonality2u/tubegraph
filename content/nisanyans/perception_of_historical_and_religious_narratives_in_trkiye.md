## Historical Narratives: Palestine and Turkish Identity

The Israelites fought significant wars against them, and these people are depicted with hatred in the Torah <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:44">[00:25:44]</a>. The southern part of the country, including Jerusalem, was then called Judea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:49">[00:25:49]</a>.

The Roman Empire, after Jewish rebellions in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD, exiled Jews from the region and renamed the province "Palestine," removing the name of Judaism from the area <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:04">[00:26:04]</a>. This name persisted for two millennia through Roman, Byzantine, and Islamic eras (where Arabs pronounced it "Felestin") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:44">[00:26:44]</a>. While there was no administrative unit called Palestine during the Ottoman period (it was the province of Jerusalem), the term was consistently used as a geographical descriptor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:13">[00:27:13]</a>. When the British took control in 1918, they established a province called Palestine, which remained a British colony until 1948 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:45">[00:27:45]</a>.

The use of "Palestine" as an insult in 19th-century German texts reflects a term of contempt, stemming from the Torah's belittling descriptions of Palestinians as stingy and despicable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:13">[00:28:13]</a>. ### Ottoman-Mongol Link and the Name "Orhan"

The name "Orhan," used by Osman Gazi for his son, reflects a [[historical_narratives_on_turkish_and_regional_identities_including_the_origins_of_certain_ethnic_groups | deeper historical narrative]] connected to the Mongol period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:32">[01:00:32]</a>. Reşidüddin, the grand vizier of the Ilkhanids (Mongols), undertook a massive official historical compilation, the *Câmi’u’t-Tevârîh* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:24">[01:02:24]</a>. This project aimed to synthesize Mongol sovereignty ideology with Islamic, Turkic, and Iranian themes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:23">[01:03:23]</a>. The founder of the Mongol nation in this narrative was named "Orhan" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:42">[01:03:42]</a>.

The fact that Osman Gazi named his son Orhan around this time, when Mongols dominated Anatolia, suggests an acknowledgment or approval of the Ilkhanid ideological project <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:21">[01:04:21]</a>. This ideological giant, however, eventually collapsed, leading to the suppression of Reşidüddin's works and a shift in [[cultural_and_historical_narratives_in_trkiye | cultural language]]. Persian had been the common language, and Mongolian the administrative language for an elite <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:01">[01:06:01]</a>. Suddenly, from 1306-1310, Turkish emerged as a new administrative and [[cultural_and_historical_narratives_in_trkiye | cultural language]], with poets who previously wrote in Persian switching to Turkish <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:48">[01:06:48]</a>.
