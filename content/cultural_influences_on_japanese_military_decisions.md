## The Importance of Understanding the "Other Side"

### "Half-Court Tennis" and Mirror Imaging
Americans often analyze international affairs and wars by focusing predominantly on American actions, a concept the speaker terms "half-court tennis" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>. This approach can lead to misunderstandings and surprises, as seen in the Iraq War with the unexpected insurgency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:20">[00:02:20]</a>, and in World War II with [[japanese_warfare_strategy_and_tactics_during_world_war_ii | Japanese actions]] like Pearl Harbor and their fighting style <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>. A common pitfall is "mirror imaging," where one projects their own likely actions onto an adversary, which often fails <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>.

### Net Assessment and Culture
Both Sun Tzu and Clausewitz advocated for "net assessment," analyzing political, military, geographic, and economic factors of all sides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>. The speaker argues for including culture in this assessment, as it can influence how actors perceive rationality and make decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>. For example, the Imperial Japanese Army War College curriculum in 1943 belatedly shifted focus from Soviet to U.S. tactics, despite the primary ongoing conflict in China, indicating a flawed net assessment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>.

## Bushido: The Soul of Japan

### Overview
Unlike Western military thought which might center on single texts like Sun Tzu's "Art of War" or Clausewitz's "On War," Japanese military thinking was heavily influenced by Bushido, the "code of the Samurai" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:59">[00:07:59]</a>. This body of literature, largely written during the peaceful Tokugawa period, focused less on military strategy and more on the deportment and conduct of the samurai, reflecting core Japanese values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:01">[00:08:01]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.

### Nitobe Inazō: A Cultural Bridge
Nitobe Inazō, author of "Bushido: The Soul of Japan" (1900), serves as a cultural bridge for understanding these concepts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:48">[00:08:48]</a>. An important figure in Japan (featured on the 5,000 yen note), Nitobe spent 18 years abroad, married a Philadelphia Quaker, converted to Christianity, and sought to explain Japanese culture to the West <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:25">[00:09:25]</a>. He posited that Japanese morality stemmed from [[impact_of_cultural_values_on_war_conduct | Bushido]], a chivalrous code of honor for the warrior class <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.

### Philosophical Origins of Bushido
According to Nitobe, Bushido has three main pillars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>:
1.  **Buddhism:** Contributed to Japanese fatalism, a "calm trust in fate, a quiet submission to the inevitable, a friendliness with death" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:02">[00:10:02]</a>. Bushido literature shows a preoccupation with death, contrasting with Western military thought which focuses on violence but not necessarily honorable death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:07">[00:10:07]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>. Buddhist tenets emphasize that existence is suffering caused by craving and attachment, with Nirvana achievable through right conduct. This fosters a focus on how life is led (deportment) rather than achievements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:32">[00:10:32]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:08">[00:11:08]</a>.
2.  **Shinto:** Fostered extreme patriotism and reverence for the emperor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:10">[00:11:10]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:15">[00:11:15]</a>.
3.  **Confucianism:** Imported from China, Confucianism provided a framework for organizing and regulating society through hierarchical, interlocking social obligations, ritual, and etiquette <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:32">[00:11:32]</a>. This system emphasizes duty over Western concepts of equality (even twins have a birth order in Japan) and freedom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:01">[00:12:01]</a>.
This value system is entirely different from the Greco-Roman, Judeo-Christian West <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:06">[00:12:06]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>.

## Key Bushido Theorists and Values

### Yamamoto Tsunetomo ("Hagakure")
Yamamoto Tsunetomo's "Hagakure" (early 18th century), written by a retainer who had not experienced actual combat, emphasizes several key values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:44">[00:12:44]</a>:
*   **Preoccupation with Death:** "The Way of the Samurai is found in death. It is not necessary to gain one's aim, but if you live on without achieving it, it's cowardice." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:59">[00:12:59]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:22">[00:13:22]</a>. This contrasts sharply with Clausewitz's focus on achieving policy objectives. This mindset helps explain Banzai charges, where remnants of Japanese forces would knowingly charge into machine-gun fire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:47">[00:13:47]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:57">[00:13:57]</a>.
*   **Honor and Suicide (Seppuku):** "The way of avoiding shame is simply death. Even if it seems certain that you will lose, retaliate." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:10">[00:14:10]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:16">[00:14:16]</a>. Suicide was a way to escape disgrace from mortifying failure, preserving honor for oneself and associates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:28">[00:14:28]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:57">[00:14:57]</a>. Nitobe explained that death involving honor was "accepted in Bushido as a key solution to many complex problems," <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:17">[00:15:17]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:23">[00:15:23]</a> such as failed battle plans or a losing war.
*   **Loyalty:** "For a warrior, there is nothing other than thinking of his master." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:40">[00:15:40]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:01">[00:16:01]</a>. This loyalty, historically to a feudal lord, translated to prioritizing one's company over family in modern times, a reverse of priorities in China <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:04">[00:16:04]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:18">[00:16:18]</a>.
*   **Willpower over Strategy:** "There is nothing that cannot be done... simply become insane and desperate, and it'll somehow work out for you." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:32">[00:19:32]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:42">[00:19:42]</a>. This emphasis on willpower denigrated strategy and tactics. Yamamoto stated, "Learning such things as military tactics is useless. The Way of the Samurai is one of immediacy, and it is best to dash in headlong." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:05">[00:20:05]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:18">[00:20:18]</a>. Thinking about tactics in peacetime was seen as leading to hesitation in wartime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:20">[00:20:20]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:40">[00:20:40]</a>.

### Daidōji Yūzan (Loyalty, Duty, Valor)
A contemporary of Yamamoto Tsunetomo, Daidōji Yūzan defined the essential samurai values as loyalty, duty, and valor, emphasizing steadfast loyalty in battle to the point of disregarding one's own life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:43">[00:22:43]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:07">[00:23:07]</a>.
*   **Group Loyalty and In-Group/Out-Group Dynamics:** In the East, the group, not the individual, is the basic unit of society, with group interests taking primacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:09">[00:23:09]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:21">[00:23:21]</a>. Japanese society is characterized by finely calibrated in-groups (e.g., based on province, education, company, military branch, family) and out-groups, with complex, sometimes conflicting, obligations to these nested groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:25">[00:23:25]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:12">[00:24:12]</a>. The Japanese language itself reflects these hierarchical and in-group/out-group distinctions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:14">[00:24:14]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:32">[00:24:32]</a>. This leads to strong group membership and stove-piping <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:53">[00:24:53]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:00">[00:25:00]</a>.

### Miyamoto Musashi (Operational Preferences)
Miyamoto Musashi, an actual master samurai, provides insights into operational preferences derived from Bushido values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:03">[00:25:03]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:18">[00:25:18]</a>:
*   **Risk Tolerance:** Musashi's military science included fighting "even five or 10 people single-handedly," suggesting long odds would not deter the Japanese <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:30">[00:25:30]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:03">[00:26:03]</a>. This reflects the thinking of Tojo and Yamamoto regarding Pearl Harbor, despite its long odds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.
*   **Surprise and Preemption:** In stalemated situations, like the China theater, Musashi's thinking implied using tactics to put the enemy off-balance, often by opening new theaters of war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:05">[00:26:05]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:40">[00:26:40]</a>. This was seen in the 1931 invasion of Manchuria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:09">[00:27:09]</a>, the 1937 wider invasion of China <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:39">[00:27:39]</a>, and the December 1941 attacks across the Pacific, including Pearl Harbor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:08">[00:28:08]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:23">[00:28:23]</a>. Preemptive attacks characterized the start of all Japan's major modern wars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:03">[00:29:03]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:12">[00:29:12]</a>.
*   **Breaking Enemy Will:** Musashi offered advice on breaking enemy will, particularly in insurgencies, to achieve psychological victory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:17">[00:29:17]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>. The Japanese may have believed atrocities like the Rape of Nanking would achieve this <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:41">[00:29:41]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:50">[00:29:50]</a>. However, this often put adversaries on "death ground," transforming failing states into lethal adversaries, similar to Nazi brutality towards Russians and Russian brutality towards Ukrainians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:53">[00:29:53]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:38">[00:30:38]</a>.

## Strategic Implications of Bushido Values

The Bushido value system led to several strategic tendencies:
*   **Focus on Offense and Military Action:** An emphasis on offensive, preemptive military action to solve problems, with fixed policy objectives and an unwillingness to adapt plans once failing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:44">[00:30:44]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:25">[00:31:25]</a>.
*   **Equating Operational and Strategic Success:** A tendency to believe that winning individual battles (operational success) would automatically lead to achieving broader war aims (strategic success) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:41">[00:16:41]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:20">[00:17:20]</a>.
*   **Insensitivity to Risk:** A willingness to undertake high-risk operations without adequate planning for contingencies or long-term costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:25">[00:31:25]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:27">[00:31:27]</a>.
*   **Lack of Grand Strategy:** "Grand strategy" involves integrating all instruments of national power (diplomatic, economic, military, informational) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:07">[00:21:07]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:22">[00:21:22]</a>. The samurai literature focused almost exclusively on the military instrument <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:31">[00:21:31]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:36">[00:21:36]</a>. This led to actions like invading French Indochina without assessing potential reactions, which triggered a [[role_of_the_us_oil_embargo_and_diplomatic_miscommunications_prewwii | U.S. oil embargo]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:41">[00:21:41]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:04">[00:22:04]</a>. There was no clear definition of when Japan would consider its territorial acquisitions sufficient to end the war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:20">[00:32:20]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:28">[00:32:28]</a>.

## Bushido in Practice: Sins and Rivalries

### Neglect of Logistics
Japan's industrial capacity was a fraction of the U.S. (e.g., 1/13th steel and coal, 1/10th munitions production) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:35">[00:33:35]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:46">[00:33:46]</a>. Each U.S. soldier had significantly more equipment (4 tons vs. 2 lbs for Japanese soldiers) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:53">[00:33:53]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:01">[00:34:01]</a>. The U.S. had an 18:1 supply-to-rifleman ratio, compared to Japan's 1:1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:12">[00:34:12]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:27">[00:34:27]</a>. Japan faced food shortages before Pearl Harbor and critical oil shortages by winter 1942-43, yet the government intended to fight on even with predictions of zero fuel by end of 1945 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:30">[00:34:30]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:12">[00:35:12]</a>. Admiral Ugaki Matome, in his diary, later acknowledged the disparity in national resources, which Japanese leaders had initially dismissed as U.S. propaganda <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:14">[00:35:14]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:35">[00:36:35]</a>. Ugaki, an honorable samurai who believed willpower could overcome material inferiority, paid with his life after the war proved him wrong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:44">[00:36:44]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:10">[00:37:10]</a>.

### Neglect of Sea Lines of Communication
Prince Higashikuni, the last Imperial Prime Minister, attributed defeat to the loss of transport shipping, reduced to 1/9th by war's end, paralyzing the empire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:16">[00:37:16]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:39">[00:37:39]</a>. The Imperial Japanese Navy (IJN) focused on Mahanian fleet-on-fleet engagements, neglecting convoy duty, which Alfred Thayer Mahan considered a "promising secondary operation" but proved primary in the Pacific <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:42">[00:37:42]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:14">[00:38:14]</a>. Admiral Ugaki initially disdained targeting merchantmen, only later lamenting the failure of supply to Guadalcanal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:41">[00:38:41]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:06">[00:39:06]</a>.

### Intra-Service Rivalries
In-group/out-group dynamics exacerbated rivalries within each service:
*   **Imperial Japanese Army (IJA):** The Kwantung Army (Japan's Army of Manchuria) independently decided to invade all of Manchuria in 1931, not Tokyo, kicking off a 15-year war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:18">[00:40:18]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:42">[00:40:42]</a>. Numerous coup attempts, often involving army factions, occurred throughout the period, including one last attempt when Emperor Hirohito was capitulating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:42">[00:40:42]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:07">[00:41:07]</a>.
*   **Imperial Japanese Navy (IJN):** Unlike the U.S. practice of rotating combat-experienced pilots to training roles, Japanese aviators trained, fought, and died together within their groups, hindering the transfer of combat lessons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:13">[00:41:13]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:50">[00:41:50]</a>. Cultural reasons also discouraged "hot washes" or critical after-action reviews common in the U.S. military <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:53">[00:41:53]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:14">[00:42:14]</a>.

### Inter-Service Rivalries (Army vs. Navy)
These rivalries were toxic and deeply rooted in pre-war budget battles for scarce resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:36">[00:39:36]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:59">[00:39:59]</a>.
*   **Organizational Issues and Separate War Plans:** The Army and Navy only began regular liaison meetings in Tokyo in 1944 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:30">[00:42:30]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:35">[00:42:35]</a>. The Navy resisted unifying high commands, fearing it would become a mere delivery service for the Army <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:44">[00:42:44]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>. Post-1905 Russo-Japanese War, the Army and Navy developed separate, secret war plans: the Army focused on Russia for a land grab in Eurasia, while the Navy targeted the [[allied_versus_axis_strategies_during_world_war_ii | U.S. and Britain]] for a Pacific empire. Each plan assumed the other service's support without coordination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:26">[00:43:26]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:05">[00:44:05]</a>.
*   **Guadalcanal:** The Army only reluctantly agreed to the Navy's "Southern Advance" after being defeated by the Russians at Nomonhan in 1939 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:20">[00:44:20]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:35">[00:44:35]</a>. The Navy, however, hadn't informed the Army that its crucial outer defensive perimeter, requiring airfields, was incomplete <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:08">[00:45:08]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:25">[00:45:25]</a>. The Army learned this in August 1942 when the U.S. landed on Guadalcanal, where one such airfield was being built. The Navy then needed Army help, leading to the disastrous Guadalcanal campaign which diverted resources from other objectives like Port Moresby and a planned assault on Chongqing, and forced the cancellation of plans to invade Australia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:25">[00:45:25]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:18">[00:47:18]</a>. Mutual distrust was rampant, with the Army accusing the Navy of sacrificing them and lying about troop numbers to secure rations, while the Navy wanted to withdraw sooner than the Army <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:09">[00:39:09]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:18">[00:39:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:02">[00:46:02]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:22">[00:46:22]</a>.
*   **The Midway Decision:** After Pearl Harbor, Admiral Yamamoto wanted to attack Midway, but the Army initially refused. The Doolittle Raid in April 1942, though causing minor physical damage, had a major strategic effect: the humiliated Army, appalled that Japanese skies could be bombed, suddenly backed the Midway operation out of a desire to retaliate and avenge honor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:57">[00:47:57]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:24">[00:49:24]</a>. After Japan lost four aircraft carriers at Midway, the Navy, due to in-group secrecy and shame, didn't inform anyone (including the Army or the public) for three to four months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:27">[00:49:27]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:42">[00:49:42]</a>.

## Consequences and Misunderstandings

### The End of the War in the Pacific
The stalemate was broken by three events in four days in August 1945: the atomic bombing of Hiroshima, the Soviet invasion of Manchuria (a nightmare scenario for the IJA), and the atomic bombing of Nagasaki <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:26">[00:50:26]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:18">[00:51:18]</a>. These psychologically shattered Japanese leadership, allowing Emperor Hirohito to break the cabinet deadlock and announce surrender via an unprecedented radio broadcast. His samurai subsequently obeyed, cooperating fully with the occupation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:21">[00:51:21]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:57">[00:51:57]</a>.

### American Miscalculations and "Half-Court Tennis"
The U.S. also engaged in "half-court tennis." The 1930 Hawley-Smoot Tariff, which damaged international trade, was a factor in Japan's perceived need for an empire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:59">[00:53:59]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:16">[00:54:16]</a>. U.S. failure to understand Japan's motivations in Manchuria (anti-communism, economic survival) and later in China (walling off communism, stabilizing China for economic growth) led to policies like the oil embargo that precipitated war rather than deterring it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:40">[00:54:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:06">[00:52:06]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:13">[00:52:13]</a>. Ironically, U.S. post-war objectives for China (stabilize, keep communists out) were similar to Japan's stated pre-war aims <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:42">[00:54:42]</a> - <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:48">[00:54:48]</a>.
