From: [[mrbeast]] <br/> 

The Beast Olympics is an extreme competition where one person from every country on Earth competes for a $250,000 gold medal to determine "which country is the best" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>. The events test individual skill and endurance, with strategic decisions and the absence of [[competitive_teamwork]] often shaping the outcomes.

## Competition Structure
The competition features multiple rounds of eliminations across various challenges, including track, gymnastics, archery, fencing, and penalty kicks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>. The primary goal is individual advancement, leading to a focus on self-preservation and strategic elimination of perceived threats.

## Individual Advancement and Self-Preservation

### Gymnastics of Death
In the "Gymnastics of Death" event, ten contestants compete in each round, with only the first five to reach a middle platform advancing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>. Despite being a group challenge, contestants prioritize their own progress:
*   Competitors were observed "stepping on him" and "stepping over his body" to advance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. This highlights a cutthroat approach, where individual success takes precedence over any form of [[group_dynamics_and_alliances_in_a_competitive_setting | group dynamics]] or cooperation.

### Archery
The archery event introduced an interesting element of meta-game strategy:
*   Competitors voted for a country to make their target harder to hit, with each vote pushing the target back ten feet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>.
*   This voting mechanism incentivized a [[competition_dynamics_and_teamwork | competitive strategy]] to hinder strong opponents. Indonesia, having performed exceptionally well in earlier rounds, became the primary target, receiving 12 votes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.
*   Indonesia's representative acknowledged this strategic targeting, stating, "I promise I was too big of a threat" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>, indicating awareness of the competitive landscape and how prior success can lead to being targeted.

## Strategic Decision-Making

### Opting Out for Money
As the competition progressed, a new strategic choice was offered to the final 16 countries: take $10,000 and withdraw, or continue competing for the $250,000 gold medal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.
*   Only two contestants, representing Fiji and Italy, chose to take the $10,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:59">[00:09:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.
*   This decision reflects a personal risk assessment; some chose a guaranteed payout over the uncertain pursuit of a larger prize, understanding the high stakes and increased difficulty of remaining challenges.

## Competitive Execution

### Fencing
In the fencing event, top finishers from previous rounds were given the strategic advantage of choosing their opponents for 1v1 matches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>. This allowed for potentially weaker opponents to be targeted, or for personal rivalries to be played out.

### Penalty Kicks
The final event, penalty kicks, progressively increased in difficulty by narrowing the goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:57">[00:15:57]</a>. The ultimate challenge involved the two remaining finalists, Libya and Slovenia, taking turns as goalkeeper and kicker <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:18">[00:17:18]</a>. This created a direct [[team_dynamics_and_strategies | head-to-head competition]] that tested both offensive and defensive skills.

Overall, the Beast Olympics demonstrated how individual competitive drives, strategic decisions, and the direct elimination of rivals underpin the entire competition, with little room for traditional notions of [[teamwork_and_alliances_during_competitive_events | teamwork]] or [[team_collaboration_and_competition_in_building | collaboration]].
