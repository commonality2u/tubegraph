## Initial Police Statements

Following the staged home invasion, Jennifer Pan was initially treated as a victim and witness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.

### First Statement (November 9th, 2010)
Jennifer was taken to the Markham police station to give a voluntary statement under oath <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a>. She was informed of her rights as a witness, including that fabricating evidence to mislead is an offense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>.
During this interview, the detective noted instances of unusual behavior, such as Jennifer finding her words easily and executing sentences perfectly, contrasting with expected emotional distress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58">[00:09:58]</a>. Her reaction to the sound of a door closing was identified as a startle reflex, potentially exaggerated by hyperarousal from the traumatic event <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>. She also hesitated and stuttered when attempting to explain how she made a 911 call with her hands tied, looking to the detective for approval <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>. The detective also observed her smiling, which she would quickly correct to a solemn stare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:21">[00:17:21]</a>.

### Second Statement (November 11th, 2010)
Jennifer was called back for another statement, ostensibly to collect more details <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:43">[00:18:43]</a>. However, the true purpose was to gather information for her culpability and begin cross-examination, catching her in previous lies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:02">[00:19:02]</a>. Jennifer was still considered a witness, but was unofficially a leading suspect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:37">[00:18:37]</a>. She reiterated her story but continued to make contradictions, such as initially forgetting to mention she was tied up before being taken downstairs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:46">[00:24:46]</a>. She also changed details about who was asking for the purse and the appearance of the assailants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:42">[00:26:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:10">[00:27:10]</a>.
A key moment involved a "clinical demonstration" where Jennifer had to physically show how she retrieved her phone while tied to a banister <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:17">[00:30:17]</a>. This demonstration, unexpectedly, made sense and was somewhat beneficial to her defense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:13">[00:32:13]</a>. The detective noted that Jennifer's tissues remained completely dry during moments of supposed grief <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:27">[00:33:27]</a>. The final part of this statement involved collecting information about Jennifer's past, including her faked grades and college degree, and her relationship with her boyfriend, to lay the foundation for a future interrogation strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:43">[00:34:43]</a>. The detective applied psychological pressure by questioning how intruders gained access without breaking in and why she was left alive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:31">[00:36:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:11">[00:38:11]</a>.

## The Interrogation and Admission

Jennifer was kept under close observation after these statements, including during her mother's funeral, where she was reportedly emotionless and never shed a tear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:37">[00:43:37]</a>.

### Father's Statement
Miraculously, Jennifer's father, Han, awoke from his induced coma on November 15th <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:52">[00:43:52]</a>. He gave an official statement in secret on November 16th, contradicting Jennifer's story <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:01">[00:44:01]</a>. He revealed that Jennifer was never tied up but was instead walking around freely and talking with the intruders as if they were friends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:10">[00:44:10]</a>. Despite suffering minor brain damage, which could be used by a defense team to refute his testimony <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:31">[00:44:31]</a>, his word was crucial.

### Interrogation (November 22nd, 2010)
York Regional Police knew they needed a confession to secure a conviction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:47">[00:44:47]</a>. Jennifer was called back for an interrogation at the Markham police station <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:57">[00:44:57]</a>. This time, she was read her rights to silence, officially making her a suspect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:51">[00:46:51]</a>.
The detective employed a "how and why solution" strategy, aiming to shift blame away from Jennifer and onto circumstances that prompted the crime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:07">[00:50:07]</a>. He built a theme around overly controlling parents and unfair limitations, linking it to her relationship with her boyfriend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:08">[00:51:08]</a>. Jennifer expressed feeling trapped and depressed due to her parents' expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:07">[00:55:07]</a>.
The detective then applied the "futility technique," asserting that he had abundant resources and even fabricating that police could use infrared satellite imagery to see inside houses to imply overwhelming evidence against her <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:52">[01:01:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:49">[01:02:49]</a>. He confronted her directly:
> "And at this point Jennifer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:15">[01:05:15]</a>... I know that you've not been truthful with the police <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:17">[01:05:17]</a>... you're involved in this I know that <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:16">[01:06:16]</a>."

He continually pressed her to admit her involvement, using broad terms that inferred guilt without directly accusing her of murder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:52">[01:09:52]</a>. Jennifer was on the cusp of admitting her involvement, asking what it would mean for her nine times throughout the interrogation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:28">[01:10:28]</a>. After 3 hours, 20 minutes, and 26 seconds, Jennifer displayed genuine emotion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:36">[01:15:36]</a> and admitted to planning the home invasion:
> "we're supposed to take you" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:16">[01:16:16]</a>
> "no just me" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:29">[01:16:29]</a>

Although she claimed she was the intended target and her parents were to be spared, this admission was enough for her arrest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:40">[01:16:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:48">[01:16:48]</a>. The detective then directly accused her of planning for her parents to be targeted:
> "and the job was for your parents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:40">[01:20:40]</a>."

## [[court_trial_and_sentencing|Court Trial and Sentencing]]

Jennifer Pan was charged with first-degree murder, attempted murder, and conspiracy to commit murder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:36">[01:24:36]</a>. Her [[court_proceedings_and_trial_statements|trial]] began on March 14th, 2014, where she pleaded not guilty to all charges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:41">[01:26:41]</a>.

Key evidence presented by the prosecution included:
*   Jennifer's interrogation tape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:46">[01:26:46]</a>.
*   116 text messages exchanged between Jennifer and her boyfriend in the six hours leading up to the murder, detailing how the crime would be carried out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:50">[01:26:50]</a>. Forensics were able to uncover these messages despite the use of burner phones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:59">[01:26:59]</a>.
*   DNA evidence and witness testimony linking the intruders to the crime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:13">[01:27:13]</a>.

Jennifer Pan, her boyfriend, and each of the intruders were found guilty of first-degree murder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:17">[01:27:17]</a>. They were all given a life sentence without the possibility of parole for 25 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:19">[01:27:19]</a>.

Jennifer Pan, now 34 years of age, is serving her sentence at the Grand Valley Institution for Women in Kitchener, Ontario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:27">[01:27:27]</a>. She will be first eligible for parole in November of 2035 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:31">[01:27:31]</a>.
