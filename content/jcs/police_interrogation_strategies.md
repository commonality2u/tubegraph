## Impact of Technology on Investigations

The evolution of technology, particularly the internet and search history, has made forensic investigations significantly more efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>. Web browser data is often critical evidence, revealing aspects of a suspect's personality that might otherwise remain hidden, sometimes being more effective than a full confession <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a>.

For instance, in the case of Stephen McDaniel, his internet search history revealed extensive viewing of violent and torture-themed pornography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>. He also conducted searches on "how to commit sexual assault" and "how to molest sleeping girl," which forensics identified as overt signs of desensitization to sexual activity, leading to a need for more potent stimuli <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>. This online behavior was seen as a precursor to his real-world actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

## Interrogation Techniques

[[interrogation_techniques | Interrogation techniques]] and strategies are employed by detectives to elicit information and confessions from suspects. These can range from subtle psychological maneuvers to direct confrontation.

### Psychological Pressure

Detectives often use psychological pressure during interrogations. This can involve:
*   **Closing distance and demanding eye contact**: Recognized as a technique to increase psychological pressure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:47:00">[09:47:00]</a>. However, a suspect's defiance, such as Stephen McDaniel's unnerving gaze, can cause the detective to look away and reset, potentially boosting the suspect's confidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:03:00">[10:03:00]</a>.
*   **"Cookie Jar" Analogy**: Likening the situation to a child caught taking a cookie, implying that lying makes one feel bad and risks being caught <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:36:00">[19:36:00]</a>.
*   **Futility Technique**: Attempting to make the suspect believe further resistance is useless due to overwhelming evidence. This is most effective when playing on existing doubts in the suspect's mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:37:00">[22:37:00]</a>.

### Shifting Approaches

Detectives frequently shift their [[criminal_interrogation_techniques | criminal interrogation techniques]] to adapt to the suspect's behavior:
*   **From Aggression to Trivial Questions**: A detective might initially use an aggressive approach, then retreat to asking trivial questions for a period before subtly increasing pressure again <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00">[10:31:00]</a>.
*   **Character Attack**: Attacking the suspect's character and dignity, especially by contrasting their current demeanor with public statements, aims to coax them into defending themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:41:00">[13:41:00]</a>.
*   **Immediate Aggression**: Some [[detective_interrogation_techniques | detective interrogation techniques]] involve immediate aggression from the outset, sometimes disregarding rapport development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:07:00">[16:07:00]</a>.
*   **Sympathetic and Understanding**: A detective might completely shift from confrontational to sympathetic, attempting to create a connection and offer a socially acceptable reason for the crime. This is typically more effective when rapport has already been built, making it difficult after a direct confrontation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:25:00">[23:25:00]</a>.
*   **Non-Confrontational Questions**: Asking non-confrontational questions for extended periods to observe if the suspect's demeanor or responses change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:09:00">[25:09:00]</a>.
*   **Inducing Mental Exhaustion**: Repetitively asking the same question or repeating statements to try and wear down the suspect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:25:00">[31:25:00]</a>.
*   **Belittling and Humiliation**: If admissions are not forthcoming, detectives may resort to belittling and humiliating the suspect, possibly out of frustration and certainty of guilt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:17:00">[32:17:00]</a>.
*   **Stating Known Facts**: Asserting knowledge of the crime and evidence ("We know you killed her," "Your hair was there") to imply the game is over and encourage confession <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:20:00">[35:20:00]</a>.

### Ethical Considerations

[[investigation_tactics_and_police_interrogation | Investigation tactics and police interrogation]] create an "ethical vacuum" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:11:00">[34:11:00]</a>. Behaviors that would be considered cruel or reprehensible in other circumstances, like bullying, are often viewed differently due to the impression of retribution, as the suspect is believed to have committed a horrific act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:17:00">[34:17:00]</a>.
