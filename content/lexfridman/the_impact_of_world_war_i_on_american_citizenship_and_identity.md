World War I was a significant turning point in shaping the identity and citizenship framework of the United States. The war not only influenced military and foreign policy but also left an indelible mark on how American citizenship was conceptualized and practiced. This article explores the multifaceted impacts of World War I on American citizenship and identity.

## Mobilization and the Redefinition of Citizenship

World War I represented a monumental shift in the demands placed on American citizens. It brought forth questions about what it meant to be an American and what responsibilities and rights came with citizenship.

The immediate demand for military personnel led to the implementation of the Selective Service Act of 1917, mandating that all men aged 18 to 45 register for the draft <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:38">[00:24:38]</a>. This act underscored a newfound obligation for citizens to serve and, if necessary, die for their country. It reinforced the idea that citizenship now required active participation in national defense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:14">[00:29:14]</a>.

> [!info] Selective Service Act
>
> The Selective Service Act of 1917 required nearly all men in the United States aged 18 to 45 to register for the draft. Over 24 million men registered, making it one of the most comprehensive mobilization efforts in U.S. history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:38">[00:24:38]</a>.

## The Legal and Cultural Citizenship

The war profoundly influenced two key dimensions of citizenship:

1. **Legal Citizenship**: It pertained to the official rights and obligations that came with being an American citizen. This included participating in civic duties such as voting and serving in the military <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:00">[00:20:00]</a>.

2. **Cultural Citizenship**: It revolved around the sense of belonging and identity as an American. The war fostered a narrative of unity, where diverse populations were brought together under the idea of a shared national project <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:27">[00:24:27]</a>.

## Ideological Shifts and Propaganda

World War I marked the beginning of using modern propaganda to shape public opinion and define national identity. Iconic imagery, such as the "I Want You" poster featuring Uncle Sam, became a tool to unify the public around the war effort and what it meant to be a patriot <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:01">[00:21:01]</a>.

> [!info] Propaganda and National Identity
>
> The "I Want You" poster from 1916 by James Montgomery Flagg became a recurring symbol of American identity and military mobilization efforts during both World Wars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:01">[00:21:01]</a>.

## Surveillance and Civil Liberties

World War I also brought about increased government surveillance, laying the groundwork for what would become the surveillance state in America <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:01">[01:31:01]</a>. The Espionage Act enacted during the war permitted broad censorship and limited freedoms that were traditionally associated with American citizenship, such as free speech and press <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:56">[00:28:56]</a>.

## Long-term Impacts on American Identity

The consequences of the war echoed through the 20th and into the 21st century. The close relationship between military service and American citizenship established during WWI continued, and the evolving narrative of national identity set during this era still influences debates regarding citizenship today <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:12">[00:29:12]</a>.

In conclusion, World War I was a pivotal event that reshaped American citizenship and identity, setting precedents for future military engagements and internal policies, while also sparking increased political and civil activism for rights and freedoms post-war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:26">[00:30:26]</a>.
