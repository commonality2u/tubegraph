## The Free Energy Principle

The Free Energy Principle is a theoretical framework proposed by [[existentialism_and_its_evolution | Karl Friston]] to explain the behavior of living systems. It suggests that organisms maintain their existence and resist entropy by minimizing a quantity known as "free energy" [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:18">[01:19:18]</a>]. This principle provides a lens for understanding the existential imperatives of living systems, framing their survival as an inference problem where entities appear to optimize their own existence by maintaining their boundary with the environment.

## Understanding Existence and Living Systems

Friston describes existence itself as an optimization process where living systems appear to maximize the evidence for their own existence [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:02">[00:44:02]</a>]. This involves engaging in actions that maintain their integrity against the environment’s chaos. The principle links existential imperatives to the minimization of surprise, where organisms act to align their predictions with sensory input [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:18">[01:19:18]</a>].

## Hierarchical and Structural Foundations

## The Markov Blanket and Autonomous Dynamics

A key concept in the Free Energy Principle is the Markov blanket, which defines the boundary that separates a system from its environment [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:57">[00:57:57]</a>]. This boundary allows a living system to interact with external states and maintain its internal organization. It is through this dynamic that a system exerts autonomous control, adapting to environmental changes and ensuring its survival.

## The Transition from Existence to Life

Friston differentiates between mere existence and life, suggesting that living systems not only maintain themselves but can actively interact with and adapt to their environments [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:03">[01:01:03]</a>]. The movement and agency inherent in living systems allow them to seek out and process new sensory inputs actively, exemplifying a higher level of existential complexity than non-living systems, such as a static oil drop.
