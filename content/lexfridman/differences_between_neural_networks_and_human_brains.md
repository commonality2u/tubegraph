## Structural Differences

### Neurons and Layers

Human brains operate with biological neurons, which communicate through electrical spikes traveling across synapses. In contrast, artificial neural networks comprise artificial neurons organized in layers, commonly using numerical weight values to transmit signals. The human brain's neurons are vast, numbering in the billions, forming intricate networks that can perform complex tasks such as recognizing objects in a fraction of a second. Artificial networks, despite having simpler structures, achieve their tasks by training a set of adjustable parameters within defined layers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.

### Spiking vs. Continuous Models

The brain's use of spikes may represent an inherent efficiency or necessary component that is not yet fully understood or utilized in [[biological_versus_artificial_neural_networks | artificial neural networks]]. Some researchers believe it might be beneficial to explore spiking models in AI to align closer to biological processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>. However, despite this potential, current models tend to simulate these spikes rather than adopt them, reflecting a key architectural difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:27">[00:08:27]</a>.
