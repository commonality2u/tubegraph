The question of what constitutes life and consciousness is a profound inquiry pondered by scientists and philosophers alike. This discussion, as observed between Sarah Walker, an astrobiologist and theoretical physicist, and Lee Cronin, a chemist, unveils the complexity behind these definitions and the inherent challenges in understanding them.

## Understanding Life

The definition of life is notably evasive and often debated. Common scientific definitions have traditionally centered around biological processes and criteria, but Walker and Cronin propose a more nuanced approach. 

> Life can be described as the manner in which information structures matter across space and time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:42">[00:16:42]</a>. It extends beyond simple biological mechanisms to encapsulate "simple machines constructing more complex machines" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:48">[00:16:48]</a> and the concept of life as a mechanism through which the universe explores possibilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:39">[00:17:39]</a>.

This perspective expands the scope of life beyond Earth's boundaries to ponder how life might manifest elsewhere in the cosmos.

## Defining and Measuring Consciousness

The discourse on consciousness is intimately tied to questions about life's origins and its manifestations. Walker and Cronin touch upon the need for a comprehensive theory to understand consciousness as more than a biological or neurological phenomenon.

Walker suggests that human intelligence is not solely based on the reflection of past experiences but rather includes the capacity to imagine future possibilities, setting humans apart in their cognitive landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:34:31">[02:34:31]</a>. This capacity for imagination might be intrinsic to understanding consciousness in both humans and potential extraterrestrial life.

## Assembly Theory

Walker and Cronin's assembly theory offers a pioneering framework that defines and measures the complexity of life. This theory quantitatively distinguishes objects based on how much information or causal history is required to assemble them. An object's assembly index signifies the amount of causal processes needed for its existence, thereby serving as a metric to explore the nature of life and consciousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:28">[00:24:28]</a>.
