Artificial intelligence (AI) systems are rapidly progressing, raising significant challenges concerning fairness and bias. These issues manifest in various aspects of AI development, deployment, and societal impact. During a discussion with Peter Norvig, Director of Research at Google, several key challenges and considerations related to fairness and bias in AI were explored.

## Evolution of AI and Resource Constraints

One of the core challenges in AI has been adapting to rapid advancements in hardware and computational power. From the early editions of "Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach" to recent works, the shift from resource constraints was significant. Initially, methods like predicate logic were limited by the memory available to handle millions of expressions. However, as computing resources have grown, the methods need constant reevaluation to leverage new capabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:01">[01:02:01]</a>.

## Redefining Utility in AI

A fundamental philosophical shift in AI is the focus on utility functions. Initially, AI was often about maximizing expected utility, based on well-defined single-agent scenarios. However, the complexity has grown to incorporate multi-agent and societal perspectives, challenging developers to determine collective utility functions. This shift pushes AI boundaries from technical optimization to encompass ethical and societal dimensions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:25">[01:02:25]</a>.

## Addressing Fairness and Bias

### Algorithmic Techniques
Unpacking fairness in AI involves analyzing how algorithms impact protected classes, such as race and sex. Developers must strive for equitable outcomes while acknowledging the theoretical limitations that prevent perfect fairness. For instance, maintaining equal opportunity across these classes can come at the cost of certain biases being inherent to algorithm performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:24">[01:06:24]</a>.

### Learning Utility Functions
Inverse reinforcement learning is one technique discussed for deriving utility functions by observing actions and inferring intent. However, inferring these intents accurately poses challenges due to suboptimal and self-destructive actions commonly observed in real-world contexts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:08">[01:04:08]</a>.

## Philosophical and Ethical Considerations

AI’s integration into decision-making processes that affect societal norms necessitates confronting ethical concerns, which includes encoding human values into utility functions. This taps into broader discussions on fairness, bias, and the aggregation of multiple perspectives and utilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:38">[01:03:38]</a>. ## Communication and Trust

AI's interaction with society involves navigating a complex web of trust. The field needs to balance between transparency, accountability, and functionality. Trust mechanisms, like explainability and validation, are paramount in ensuring AI systems are used wisely and ethically. These mechanisms help manage expectations and foster more informed discussions about AI’s role in society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:00">[01:20:00]</a>.

## The Future of Ethical AI Development

Looking forward, discussions centered around AI must prioritize the ethical considerations in AI development, fostering pathways that build societal trust and understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:52">[01:29:52]</a>. Addressing the challenge of bias and fairness requires an interdisciplinary approach, engaging ethicists, technologists, and broader society in building systems that reflect shared values rather than exacerbating existing inequities.
