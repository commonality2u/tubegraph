## Key Growth Pathways in Aging and Disease

Two major growth pathways in the body, the IGF (Insulin-like Growth Factor) pathway and the mTOR (mammalian Target of Rapamycin) pathway, are central to both positive growth and pathological processes, including [[impact_of_lifestyle_on_aging | aging]] and cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:33">[00:03:33]</a>.

### mTOR Pathway
mTOR exists in two complexes: mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) and mTOR complex 2 (mTORC2), with different activities in various tissues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. While complete absence of mTORC1 activity can lead to muscle wasting (as it plays a crucial role in [[impact_of_protein_on_aging_cancer_and_longevity | protein synthesis]]) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>, overactivity is associated with [[impact_of_lifestyle_on_aging | aging]] and diseases like cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>. mTOR is primarily driven by amino acids, with leucine identified as the highest affinity amino acid sensor for mTORC1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>. mTORC in muscle has a much higher affinity for leucine than in fat or liver cells, suggesting that in nutrient deprivation, muscle is preferentially signaled for growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:33">[00:07:33]</a>.

### IGF-1 Pathway
IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1) is also a growth factor essential for muscle growth, repair, and neuronal growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>. However, excessive IGF-1 can enable damaged cells to continue growing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>. Both IGF-1 and mTOR pathways are regulated by amino acid intake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:40">[00:05:40]</a>. While some believe IGF-1 is exclusively amino acid-driven (methionine being a key driver), it is also clear that carbohydrates, indirectly via insulin, play a role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>. Insulin levels influence IGF binding protein 3 (IGFBP-3), which determines the amount of active IGF-1 available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>.

## Dietary Strategies for Longevity

Peter Attia's conceptual approach to [[dietary_interventions_in_longevity | dietary interventions in longevity]] focuses on managing protein and carbohydrate intake to keep growth pathways in check <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:56">[00:16:56]</a>:

*   **Protein Intake:** Consume the minimum amount of [[impact_of_protein_on_aging_cancer_and_longevity | protein]] required to maintain and grow muscle mass, avoiding overconsumption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:11">[00:17:11]</a>.
*   **Carbohydrate Management:** Lower carbohydrate intake to the lowest tolerable point that allows for maintenance of low fasting insulin (ideally below 3 or 4 IU) and limits post-meal glycemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:43">[00:17:43]</a>. A standardized test (e.g., OGTT) can be used to monitor postprandial hyperinsulinemia, aiming to keep insulin below 30 within one hour of a 75-gram glucose challenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:20">[00:18:20]</a>. Individual carbohydrate tolerance varies greatly based on insulin sensitivity and muscle mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:58">[00:18:58]</a>.
*   **Fat Intake:** Fat becomes the "fill" for the remainder of caloric needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:19">[00:19:19]</a>.

This individualized approach means diets can vary significantly, from 40% carbohydrates and 20% [[impact_of_protein_on_aging_cancer_and_longevity | protein]] to 20% carbohydrates and 15% [[impact_of_protein_on_aging_cancer_and_longevity | protein]], with fat making up the rest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:25">[00:19:25]</a>.

## The Role of Gut Health and Micronutrients

Rhonda Patrick emphasizes the importance of [[nutrition_and_dietary_impacts_on_health | nutrition and dietary impacts on health]] by considering:
*   **Gut Health:** The gut profoundly regulates the immune system and is a major source of inflammation, a driver of the [[impact_of_lifestyle_on_aging | aging]] process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:07">[00:20:07]</a>. Eating fiber-rich foods and avoiding those that damage the gut is crucial for maintaining the mucin layer, which separates immune cells from microorganisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:39">[00:20:39]</a>. Diet affects gut bacteria, which generate short-chain fatty acids and signaling molecules influencing hematopoiesis and regulatory T cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:39">[00:27:39]</a>.
*   **Micronutrients:** These are vital cofactors for enzymes and proteins involved in processes like cancer cell regulation and damage repair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:50">[00:20:50]</a>.

While Peter Attia is a "late adopter" when it comes to specific gut microbiome interventions due to a lack of clear diagnostic tools and actionable insights in non-catastrophic cases, he acknowledges the phenomenon of endotoxemia (leakage of bacterial toxins into the bloodstream) as a major source of inflammation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:16">[00:21:16]</a>.

## Dietary Impacts on Cancer

Everyone carries millions of cancer cells at any given time, but the immune system usually eradicates them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:11">[00:14:11]</a>. The goal for [[impact_of_diet_on_longevity_and_healthspan | longevity]] is to delay the onset of major diseases, including cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:26">[00:16:26]</a>.

### Cancer Amplifiers
IGF-1 acts as an "amplifier" for cells that have already acquired damage, promoting their continued growth and survival rather than allowing them to die <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>. Refined carbohydrates and sugars are linked to inflammatory processes that can initiate cell damage, setting the stage for cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:13">[00:13:13]</a>. Epidemiological data suggests that the consumption of highly refined carbohydrates and sugar is more predictive of cancer prevalence in a society than variations in [[impact_of_protein_on_aging_cancer_and_longevity | protein]] content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:56">[00:11:56]</a>.

### Metabolic Strategies Against Cancer
Cancer cells often exhibit the "Warburg effect," relying on glycolysis even in the presence of oxygen, optimizing for cellular building blocks rather than efficient ATP production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:46">[00:52:46]</a>. This metabolic shift may be a strategy to avoid mitochondrial activity, which generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) â€“ a pro-death signal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:09">[00:59:09]</a>.

Strategies to exploit this include:
*   **Forcing Mitochondrial Activity:** Activating pyruvate dehydrogenase (e.g., with DCA) or using [[ketogenic_diets_impact_on_aging_and_healthspan | ketogenic diets]] (forcing cells to use oxidized fats, which require mitochondria) can generate ROS and push cancer cells towards death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:53">[00:58:53]</a>. This approach may be safer than chemotherapy, as normal cells (not primed to die) are less affected by increased mitochondrial activity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:57">[01:00:57]</a>.
*   **Avoiding Antioxidants:** Taking supplemental dietary antioxidants during cancer treatment can be dangerous, as they blunt the pro-death ROS signaling pathway in cancer cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:53">[01:01:53]</a>.

A concern with [[ketogenic_diets_impact_on_aging_and_healthspan | ketogenic diets]] for cancer is that cancer cells also utilize lipids for building new cells, which could potentially offset the beneficial effects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:45">[01:00:45]</a>.

## Diet and Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the only neurodegenerative disease on the top 10 list of causes of death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:44">[00:43:44]</a>. Its prevalence has increased significantly over the last 50 years (around 2.5% per year), suggesting an environmental driver rather than simply a natural consequence of [[impact_of_lifestyle_on_aging | aging]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:27">[00:42:27]</a>.

### "Brain Diabetes" Hypothesis
There is a strong association between AD, Type 2 diabetes, and hyperinsulinemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:48">[00:43:48]</a>. Peter Attia views AD as "brain diabetes," hypothesizing that it is fundamentally a neuronal energy problem caused by difficulty in getting glucose through pyruvate dehydrogenase into the Krebs cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:01">[00:44:01]</a>. Tau plaques and synapse issues are seen as byproducts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:29">[00:44:29]</a>. Maintaining high insulin sensitivity is believed to reduce AD risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:04">[00:46:04]</a>.

Neurons primarily use lactate supplied by astrocytes (brain cells that use glucose to generate lactate) and can also utilize beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:25">[00:48:25]</a>. Both lactate and BHB are thermodynamically favorable energy sources that can bypass glucose metabolism issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:44">[00:48:44]</a>. Animal models show that simultaneous injection of glucose and insulin or administration of exogenous BHB can transiently overcome deficits and reverse symptoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:41">[00:44:41]</a>.

### ApoE Genotype and Phenotype
The ApoE4 allele significantly increases the risk of Alzheimer's disease (e.g., 2x for 3/4 genotype, 10-20x for 4/4 genotype) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:01">[00:31:01]</a>. However, the majority of AD cases are still 3/3 genotype, and having ApoE4 does not guarantee AD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:29">[00:31:29]</a>. Some research suggests the phenotype (actual serum level of ApoE) may be more predictive of AD than the genotype <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:01">[00:32:01]</a>. ApoE4 has a loss of function in cholesterol transport to neurons and a dominant negative effect, leading to aggregate accumulation and chronic inflammation in the brain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:53">[00:37:53]</a>.

### Lifestyle and [[impact_of_diet_on_aging_and_brain_function | Dietary Impacts on Brain Function]]
[[impact_of_lifestyle_on_aging | Lifestyle]] plays a significant role in Alzheimer's risk, especially for those with ApoE4 alleles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:43">[00:36:43]</a>. Factors like alcohol consumption, refined carbohydrates, and sugars contribute to inflammation that can cross the blood-brain barrier and cause damage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:01">[00:37:01]</a>. Traumatic brain injury (TBI) also increases AD risk, particularly for ApoE4 carriers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:29">[00:53:29]</a>. Exogenous lactate or BHB are being explored to treat TBI, as they can bypass energy shortages and aid in glucose sparing, allowing glucose to be used for glutathione synthesis to combat oxidative stress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:25">[00:54:25]</a>.

Richard Isaacson, a neurologist at Cornell, uses very-low-glycemic-index diets coupled with MCT (medium-chain triglycerides) to induce ketosis without a full [[ketogenic_diets_impact_on_aging_and_healthspan | ketogenic diet]] for early cognitive decline, with promising results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:12">[00:45:12]</a>.
