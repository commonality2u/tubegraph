Research into aging has significantly advanced, moving from early studies on simple organisms to complex investigations in mammals and humans. A key focus is understanding the biological mechanisms that regulate the aging process and how to slow or even reverse them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:10">[00:00:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a>.

## Early Discoveries: Longevity Genes and Sirtuins

A major breakthrough in the 1980s and early 1990s was the identification of genes that control aging, often referred to as longevity genes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. These genes were first found in organisms like nematode worms and yeast cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>. Notably, these same longevity genes are present in humans and nearly all life forms on Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>.

Lifestyle factors such as exercise and fasting are beneficial because they activate these longevity genes, which then help repair the body and slow down the aging process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>. For instance, studies with nematode worms have shown that decreasing insulin IGF-1 signaling can extend lifespan by 100% and maintain youthful characteristics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>.

### Sirtuins: Key Regulators of Longevity

Sirtuins are enzymes that control gene expression, acting like "traffic cops" by turning genes on and off <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. Their dysregulation over time, often due to accumulating DNA instability, affects lifespan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>. A significant discovery was that overexpressing one of these genes, SIR2, in yeast extended their lifespan by 30% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>.

Sirtuins mimic the benefits of calorie restriction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>. If the SIR2 gene is removed, the benefits of calorie restriction are lost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:50">[00:06:50]</a>. In mammals, calorie restriction, leading to low insulin and IGF-1 levels, significantly increases sirtuin levels (e.g., SIRT1 in liver and muscle) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:57">[00:08:57]</a>. While different pathways like insulin, IGF-1, mTOR, and AMPK were once thought to be distinct, they are now understood as an interconnected network that influences longevity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:08">[00:10:08]</a>.

## NAD and Sirtuin Activity

Sirtuins require Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+) to function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:42">[00:14:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:55">[00:14:55]</a>. NAD+ levels fluctuate throughout the day, rising during fasting or exercise, and decreasing after a large meal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>. These cycles are linked to circadian rhythms, with NAD+ levels influencing the body's internal clock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:29">[00:12:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:34">[00:12:34]</a>.

NAD+ is crucial for metabolism, mitochondrial function, and activating sirtuins and DNA repair enzymes like PARP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:18">[00:13:18]</a>. As individuals age, NAD+ levels decline, potentially by half between ages 20 and 50 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:02">[00:15:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</a>. This reduction impairs sirtuin activity, leading to reduced repair mechanisms and energy production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:19">[00:15:19]</a>. Restoring NAD+ levels can "trick" the body into thinking it's younger, allowing sirtuins to function optimally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:29">[00:15:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:41">[00:15:41]</a>.

*   **NAD+ Decline and its Consequences**:
    *   Immune system activity and DNA repair (PARPs) are major drains on NAD+ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:30">[00:17:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:35">[00:17:35]</a>.
    *   Accumulating DNA damage overwhelms sirtuins, distracting them from their normal function of maintaining proper gene expression and cellular identity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:01">[00:18:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:34">[00:18:34]</a>. This loss of cellular identity is observed in aging mice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:44">[00:18:44]</a>.
    *   Sirtuins are among the first proteins to respond to a broken chromosome, playing a role in DNA repair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:05">[00:19:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:11">[00:19:11]</a>.

## Epigenetic Reversal and the Information Theory of Aging

The concept of [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | epigenetic reversal]] is deeply intertwined with the **information theory of aging**. This theory posits that aging is primarily a loss of information regulation over time, particularly in the [[epigenetics_and_aging | epigenome]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:56">[00:19:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:57">[00:31:57]</a>. While the genome (DNA) is digital information (A, T, G, C), the epigenome is an analog reader, highly susceptible to accumulating "noise" over decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:44">[00:31:44]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:53">[00:31:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:55">[00:32:55]</a>. This "noise" leads cells to lose their identity and fail to turn on the correct genes as they did when young <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:17">[00:20:17]</a>.

### The Epigenetic Clock and its Reversal

Dr. Steve Horvath's work on the [[epigenetic_clocks_and_their_significance | epigenetic clock]] demonstrates a distinct epigenetic aging pattern in various cell types, including human cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:32">[00:20:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:37">[00:20:37]</a>. This clock, based on DNA methylation patterns, not only predicts chronological age but also healthspan and longevity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:53">[00:22:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:57">[00:22:57]</a>. Lifestyle factors like smoking and obesity can accelerate this clock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:03">[00:23:03]</a>.

Recent breakthroughs link sirtuin movement to Horvath's methylation clock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:49">[00:20:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:53">[00:20:53]</a>. The distracted protein DNA repair system leads to methylation changes that the clock measures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:59">[00:20:59]</a>. Researchers believe they have identified the signal to "reset" the proteins and reverse the Horvath clock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:29">[00:21:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:42">[00:21:42]</a>.

### Yamanaka Factors and Age Reversal

The key to [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | epigenetic reversal]] lies in the Yamanaka factors. Shinya Yamanaka discovered a set of four genes that, when introduced into adult cells (like skin cells), can reprogram them back into [[impact_of_technological_advances_like_crispr_and_pluripotent_stem_cells_on_aging_research | pluripotent stem cells]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:41">[00:24:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:55">[00:24:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:59">[00:24:59]</a>. This process literally resets the epigenome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:40">[00:31:40]</a>, providing evidence that aging is not a one-way street <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:03">[00:36:03]</a>.

Initial attempts to activate all four Yamanaka factors in mice led to death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:50">[00:25:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:57">[00:25:57]</a>. However, a pulsed activation, turning the genes on for a few days then off, showed promising results in premature aging mouse models, extending their lifespan by over 40% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:06">[00:26:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:21">[00:26:21]</a>. This approach has also been shown to improve wound healing and kidney healing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:28">[00:26:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:32">[00:26:32]</a>.

Current research indicates that using only three of the four Yamanaka factors (excluding the oncogene Myc) can be effective and safer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:17">[00:27:17]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:21">[00:27:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:26">[00:27:26]</a>. These three genes have been shown to protect neurons from dying in mice and in cell cultures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:29">[00:27:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:35">[00:27:35]</a>. Critically, the gene capable of restoring the Horvath clock was required for these benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:38">[00:27:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:44">[00:27:44]</a>.

This research, though largely unpublished as of the interview <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:45">[00:21:45]</a>, is highly promising. Scientists are working towards using molecules, rather than viruses, to achieve this [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | age reversal]] in a pill or drip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:50">[00:27:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:55">[00:27:55]</a>.

*   **Applications and Safety**:
    *   [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | Epigenetic reversal]] has shown benefits on damaged neurons and even cancer cells <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:33">[00:22:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:37">[00:22:37]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:40">[00:22:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:43">[00:22:43]</a>.
    *   Restoring eyesight and regrowing optic nerves in old mice has been achieved safely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:38">[00:24:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:43">[00:24:43]</a>.
    *   Clinical trials for glaucoma and blindness in old mice are underway, as localized treatments minimize risks to the rest of the body <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:32">[00:30:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:36">[00:30:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:42">[00:30:42]</a>.
    *   However, [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | epigenetic resetting]] is a powerful intervention that requires extreme caution; over-reprogramming cells can lead to tumor formation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:53">[00:29:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:58">[00:29:58]</a>. Extensive clinical testing is needed before widespread human use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:05">[00:30:05]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:22">[00:30:22]</a>.

## Comparison with Lifestyle and Supplements

While [[epigenetic_age_acceleration_and_reversal | epigenetic reversal]] offers a potent new approach, lifestyle factors like fasting and exercise remain crucial. They activate the body's natural defenses, such as sirtuins, AMP kinase, and mTOR, which can slow down and stabilize epigenome decay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:51">[00:28:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:56">[00:28:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:01">[00:29:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:09">[00:29:09]</a>. These pathways are considered relatively safe to tweak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:30">[00:29:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:35">[00:29:35]</a>.

NAD+ boosters, such as Nicotinamide Mononucleotide (NMN) and Nicotinamide Riboside (NR), are precursors to NAD+ <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:42">[00:37:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:51">[00:38:51]</a>. They have shown promise in raising NAD+ levels in animals and humans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:20">[00:39:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:27">[00:39:27]</a>. In mice, NMN has been shown to improve various health parameters over a year of treatment and is currently being studied for its effects on frailty and heart function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:56">[00:59:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:01">[01:00:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:23">[01:00:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:26">[01:00:26]</a>.

NAD+ boosters also show promise for brain health, with mouse studies indicating improved memory and slower Alzheimer's progression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:02">[00:51:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:08">[00:51:08]</a>. They can also improve blood flow and regenerate the vascular system, which is critical for cognitive function and combating vascular dementia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:29">[00:51:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:33">[00:51:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:42">[00:51:42]</a>.

Resveratrol, a molecule found in stressed plants, activates sirtuins and other stress response pathways (xenohormesis), helping the body deal with stress and age better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:51">[00:39:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:18">[00:40:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:24">[00:40:24]</a>. Animal studies, including in rhesus monkeys, have shown that resveratrol can significantly improve cardiovascular health, reducing aortic stiffness and fat accumulation in arteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:30">[00:42:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:09">[00:43:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:55">[00:45:55]</a>. It also possesses anti-inflammatory properties and helps with DNA repair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:39">[00:46:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:48">[00:46:48]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Practical Considerations for Supplements
> *   NAD+ boosters (NMN, NR) and resveratrol should be stored in a cool, dark place (e.g., freezer or fridge) to prevent degradation, especially NMN, which can degrade into nicotinamide if it gets wet or humid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:55">[00:57:55]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:58">[00:57:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:02">[00:59:02]</a>.
> *   High doses of nicotinamide can inhibit sirtuins and interfere with DNA repair <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:04">[00:58:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:07">[00:58:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:13">[00:58:13]</a>.
> *   Resveratrol absorption is significantly increased when consumed with a bit of fat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:39">[00:44:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:43">[00:44:43]</a>. The *trans* form is more effective in activating sirtuins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:19">[00:45:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:25">[00:45:25]</a>.
