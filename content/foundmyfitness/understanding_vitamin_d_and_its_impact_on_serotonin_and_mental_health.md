## Challenges in Defining [[Vitamin D Supplementation and Cognitive Improvements | Optimal Vitamin D Levels]]

The current Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for [[Vitamin D Supplementation and Cognitive Improvements | vitamin D]] is 600 International Units (IUs) daily <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:16">[00:34:16]</a>. Deficiency is defined as 25-hydroxy [[Vitamin D Supplementation and Cognitive Improvements | vitamin D]] levels below 20 nanograms per milliliter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:33">[00:34:33]</a>. However, it takes 1000 IUs per day to raise blood levels by only 5 nanograms per milliliter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:38">[00:34:38]</a>, meaning that even the RDA may not be sufficient to bring a deficient individual to a sufficient level (30 nanograms per milliliter or above) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:48">[00:34:48]</a>.

One major difficulty in determining optimal [[Vitamin D Supplementation and Cognitive Improvements | vitamin D]] amounts stems from the historical approach to setting vitamin RDAs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:08">[00:35:08]</a>. These standards are often based on preventing acute, short-term deficiencies like rickets or scurvy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:43">[00:24:43]</a>, rather than preventing long-term diseases of aging or ensuring optimal long-term health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:45">[00:22:45]</a>. This focus on short-term outcomes is a problem in the field of nutrition research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:14">[00:35:14]</a>.

When conducting clinical trials for nutrients, it's crucial to measure nutrient levels before and after supplementation, especially since many people may already have sufficient levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:35">[00:37:35]</a>. Testing a broad population without accounting for baseline nutrient status can dilute results and incorrectly suggest that supplements are ineffective or even harmful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:12">[00:37:12]</a>.

### Triage Theory and Longevity Vitamins

Dr. Bruce Ames proposed the "triage theory," which suggests that when an organism is slightly deficient in a vitamin or mineral, the body prioritizes the limited nutrient towards proteins essential for short-term survival and reproduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:35">[00:11:35]</a>. This comes at the expense of proteins needed for long-term health and disease prevention, such as those involved in DNA repair or preventing arterial calcification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:27">[00:12:27]</a>. These long-term consequences are often "diseases of aging" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:02">[00:15:02]</a>.

This theory implies that current RDAs, based on preventing acute symptoms, may not be high enough to support optimal long-term health and prevent age-related decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:53">[00:22:53]</a>. Dr. Ames suggests that there should be a category of "longevity vitamins" that contribute to a long lifespan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>.
