## Electrode Materials

Spot welding electrodes are primarily made of copper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:15">[00:23:15]</a>. Copper is highly malleable and can be easily bent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:24">[00:23:24]</a>. After bending, copper becomes work-hardened, increasing its durability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:29">[00:23:29]</a>. If copper becomes too hard to bend, it can be annealed (softened) and will re-harden through the bending process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:32">[00:23:32]</a>.

While the electrode body is copper, the tips that contact the workpiece are not typically pure copper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:41">[00:23:41]</a>. Pure copper tips would easily squeeze and wear due to their softness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:48">[00:23:48]</a>. For long-lasting tips, materials like copper-tungsten alloy or copper-chromium alloy are used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:56">[00:24:01]</a>. Copper-chromium alloy is generally the cheapest option and can be purchased in rod form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:05">[00:24:05]</a>. A single rod can yield up to 100 tips <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:11">[00:24:11]</a>.

### Making and Inserting Tips

To insert a tip into a copper electrode:
1.  Machine a slight taper into the electrode, or create a tapered hole <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:18">[00:24:18]</a>.
2.  A simple way to make a tapered hole is by grinding a dowel pin into a tapered shape freehand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:32">[00:24:32]</a>. Grind it flat to half its diameter to create a d-bit (a type of milling cutter) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:40">[00:24:40]</a>. This hard enough to cut copper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:51">[00:24:51]</a>.
3.  Machine a corresponding taper on the insert tip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:57">[00:24:57]</a>.
4.  Press the tip into the electrode. Precision isn't critical, as the copper will flow and conform to the shape when squeezed hard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>.

## Types of Custom Electrodes

Different welding applications require electrodes with specific shapes and features:

*   **Hollow Electrodes for Captive Hardware**
    *   Used for welding standard screws, nuts, studs, standoffs, hinges, and latches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:32">[00:09:32]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:09:50]</a>.
    *   Designed to accept the standard sizes of screws (e.g., M4) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>.
    *   Some standoffs and studs have a small "pip" or point at the end that aligns with a pre-made mark (e.g., from a [[creating_custom_springs_and_minimizing_machining_using_water_jet | water jet cutter]] edge command or 1mm hole) on the workpiece, allowing for accurate positioning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>.
    *   Electrodes for these often feature a ball and spring mechanism to hold the captive hardware securely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.

*   **Conical Hole Electrodes for Balls**
    *   Used for [[welding_techniques_for_rods_and_various_hardware | welding balls]] to flat surfaces or to the ends of rods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:09">[00:16:09]</a>.
    *   The conical shape provides a secure fit for the ball <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:17">[00:16:17]</a>.
    *   For welding balls to rods, a special collet adapter is used to grip the rod <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:08">[00:17:08]</a>. The ball must be smaller than the hole in the adapter to allow removal after welding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:27">[00:17:27]</a>.
    *   Despite melting the ball at the bottom, if there's sufficient contact area between the copper electrode and the ball, there will be no visible marking on the ball <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:00">[00:18:00]</a>.

*   **Electrodes for Invisible Welds**
    *   Achieving an invisible weld requires a special electrode with a very small dimple (a few millimeters in diameter and about 50 microns deep) at the weld point <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:28">[00:19:28]</a>.
    *   This dimple pushes molten metal outwards, compensating for the shrinkage that occurs as the metal cools, resulting in a perfectly flat surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:45">[00:19:45]</a>. This minimizes the need for sanding compared to using a flat electrode <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:53">[00:19:53]</a>.

*   **Deep Access Electrodes**
    *   Electrodes can be bent or extended to reach deep inside boxes or cavities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:21">[00:25:21]</a>.
    *   However, longer electrodes (long necks) can increase inductance, potentially reducing current, especially with ferromagnetic materials <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:45">[00:25:45]</a>. Shorter necks are preferable for minimizing inductance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:58">[00:25:58]</a>.

*   **Thin and Offset Electrodes**
    *   Very thin electrodes are used to access small, confined spaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:14">[00:26:14]</a>.
    *   Offset electrodes are designed to work around folds or other obstructions in the metal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:18">[00:26:18]</a>.

*   **Blade Welding Electrodes**
    *   Similar to those found on a bandsaw welder, these electrodes are used for [[welding_techniques_for_rods_and_various_hardware | welding blades]] together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:28">[00:26:28]</a>.

*   **Angled Welding Electrodes**
    *   Scrap copper can be cut at a precise angle to create an electrode for welding studs or rods at a specific, consistent angle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:45">[00:26:45]</a>. This is useful for producing multiple parts with identical angled features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>.

*   **Large Contact Area Electrodes**
    *   To prevent marking the non-welded side of a part, use an electrode with a much larger contact area than the welding tip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:22">[00:27:22]</a>. If the contact area is significantly larger (e.g., 30mm² for a 3mm² weld), that side will not heat up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:30">[00:27:30]</a>.

*   **Horizontal Welding Electrodes**
    *   For salvaging small pieces of material for a [[creating_custom_springs_and_minimizing_machining_using_water_jet | water jet]] to hold, electrodes can be used to butt-weld two pieces flush horizontally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:08">[00:30:08]</a>. While not as strong as a vertical weld, this creates a smooth, step-free joint for clamping <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:37">[00:30:37]</a>.

Custom electrodes can be made as needed and recycled when no longer required <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>. This flexibility is crucial for [[applications_of_spot_welding_in_prototyping_and_manufacturing | prototyping and manufacturing]] specialized components.
