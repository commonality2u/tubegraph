## Assessing Pain Sensation

In a CNS examination, particularly when assessing general senses, a common method to check for pain sensation involves using specific stimuli <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:00">[01:50:00]</a>:
*   **Pin Prick:** This stimulus is used to activate pain receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:00">[02:08:00]</a>.
*   **Tooth Forceps:** Applying tooth forceps can also stimulate pain receptors, often causing maximum pain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:00">[02:12:00]</a>.
*   **Blunt Object:** Stimulation with a blunt object typically yields no response from nociceptors, as it primarily elicits pressure sensation, which can activate Pacinian corpuscles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:49:00">[02:49:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:06:00">[04:06:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:00">[04:26:00]</a>.

## Differentiating Motor Neuron Lesions

Distinguishing between Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) and Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) lesions is a fundamental aspect of neurological [[clinical_examination_findings | clinical examination findings]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:11:00">[07:11:00]</a>. Key [[clinical_examination_findings | clinical examination findings]] include:

### Muscle Atrophy and Fasciculations
*   **LMN Lesions:** Directly supply skeletal muscle and are classically associated with muscle atrophy and fasciculations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:37:00">[07:37:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:29:00">[08:29:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:00:00">[09:00:00]</a>.
*   **UMN Lesions:** Do not typically show atrophy or fasciculations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:05:00">[09:05:00]</a>.

### Muscle Tone
*   **LMN Lesions:** Characterized by hypotonia or decreased muscle tone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:14:00">[08:14:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:41:00">[08:41:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:10:00">[09:10:00]</a>.
*   **UMN Lesions:** Often present with spasticity (increased tone) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:07:00">[09:07:00]</a>.

### Distribution of Weakness
*   **LMN Lesions:** Weakness tends to be segmental, affecting specific spinal segments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:33:00">[09:33:00]</a>.
*   **UMN Lesions:** Weakness can present as hemiplegia or hemiparesis, affecting broader regions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:17:00">[09:17:00]</a>.

### Reflexes
*   **LMN Lesions:** Lead to hypoactive or absent reflexes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:43:00">[09:43:00]</a>.
*   **UMN Lesions:** Characterized by hyperactive or exaggerated reflexes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:43:00">[09:43:00]</a>.

### Babinski Sign
*   **Babinski Positivity (Dorsiflexion of great toe):** Present in UMN lesions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:06:00">[10:06:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:14:00">[10:14:00]</a>.
*   **Babinski Negativity (Plantar flexion):** Normal finding and seen in LMN lesions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:02:00">[10:02:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:23:00">[10:23:00]</a>.

Understanding these distinctions is critical for diagnosing and localizing neurological conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:55:00">[08:55:00]</a>.
