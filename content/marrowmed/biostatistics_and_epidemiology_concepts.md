## Biostatistics

Biostatistics questions in the NEET PG 2024 exam were generally considered relatively easy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>.

### Sampling Methods
A common question involves identifying the type of sampling method used in a study.
*   **Simple Random Sampling** involves taking random numbers of subjects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:05">[00:04:05]</a>.
*   **Systematic Sampling** involves selecting every nth person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>.
*   **Stratified Sampling** occurs when the population is divided based on certain criteria, and then random numbers of subjects are taken from each stratum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>. For example, selecting randomly from distinct groups like plant, animal, and synthetic medicines is a typical scenario for stratified sampling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>.
*   **Cluster Sampling** involves naturally occurring groups or clusters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:51">[00:04:51]</a>.

### Median Calculation
Calculating the median is a frequently tested skill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a>. The median is the central value after arranging data in ascending or descending order <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:17">[00:12:17]</a>. If the number of data points is odd, there is a single central value; if even, the median is the average of the two central values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:26">[00:12:26]</a>. For instance, with values like 2, 3, 4, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 (8 values), the median would be (4+5)/2 = 4.5 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:39">[00:12:39]</a>.

### Tests of Significance
Choosing the correct [[statistical_tests_and_analysis_in_biostatistics | test of significance]] depends on two factors:
1.  **Type of Data**: Whether it's parametric (quantitative) or non-parametric (qualitative) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:51">[00:13:51]</a>.
2.  **Number of Groups**: Whether there's a single group, two groups, or more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:04">[00:14:04]</a>.
    *   For **quantitative data** in a single group measured before and after an intervention (e.g., cholesterol levels in a drug trial before and after 9 months), a **paired T-test** is typically used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:55">[00:14:55]</a>.
    *   For **qualitative data** comparing two groups (e.g., disease incidence in vegetarians vs. non-vegetarians), a **Chi-Square test** is appropriate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:05">[00:51:05]</a>.

## Epidemiology

Epidemiology concepts are crucial for [[understanding_difficult_medical_concepts | understanding disease patterns]] and public health interventions.

### Iceberg Phenomenon
The iceberg phenomenon illustrates that only a small portion of a disease's burden (the "tip") is clinically apparent, while a larger portion (the "submerged" part) remains undiagnosed or asymptomatic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.
*   Diseases that **show** the iceberg phenomenon include poliomyelitis, mumps, influenza, diphtheria, typhoid, Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, and Rubella <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>. Influenza is a strong example, with 50-70% of infections remaining asymptomatic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.
*   Diseases that **do not show** the iceberg phenomenon include measles, tetanus, and rabies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:37">[00:06:37]</a>.

### Communication Barriers
Effective communication is vital in public health. Barriers can include:
*   **Environmental barriers**: Too much noise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:26">[00:08:26]</a>.
*   **Physiological barriers**: Impaired hearing or expression problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.
*   **Psychological barriers**: Inappropriate intelligence levels or emotional disturbance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:45">[00:08:45]</a>.
*   **Cultural barriers**: Different beliefs, concepts, or language differences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>. For example, interpretation of words being understood differently due to language is a cultural barrier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>.

### Health Indicators
Various indicators are used to assess population health and development.
*   **Human Development Index (HDI)**: A "super duper 20-star MCQ" topic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>. It comprises four indicators across three dimensions:
    1.  Life expectancy in years from birth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:11">[00:11:11]</a>.
    2.  Education (expected school years and mean school years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:16">[00:11:16]</a>.
    3.  Standard of living (Gross National Income per capita) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:24">[00:11:24]</a>.
*   **Physical Quality of Life Index (PQLI)**: Focuses on life expectancy at 1 year, literacy rate, and infant mortality rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:31">[00:56:31]</a>. It does not include income or gross national product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:45">[00:56:45]</a>.

### Mortality Rates

#### Maternal Mortality
*   **Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)**: Calculated as the number of maternal deaths divided by live births, multiplied by one lakh (100,000) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:32">[00:15:32]</a>.
*   **Maternal Mortality Rate (MMRate)**: Defined as the number of maternal deaths divided by the total number of females in the reproductive group, multiplied by one lakh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:47">[00:15:47]</a>.
*   A **maternal death** is any female death due to pregnancy or related causes within 42 days of delivery or termination of pregnancy, irrespective of duration or site of pregnancy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:47">[00:16:47]</a>. It excludes deaths due to accidents, trauma, or electrocution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:13">[01:02:13]</a>.

#### Neonatal Mortality
*   **Early Neonatal Mortality Rate**: Death of children aged less than 7 days, divided by live births, multiplied by 1,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:22">[00:47:22]</a>. For example, if 9 children die within the first 7 days out of 500 live births, the rate is (9/500) * 1000 = 18 per thousand live births <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:44">[00:47:44]</a>.

### Disease Studies and Surveillance

#### Case-Control Study Steps
In a case-control study, the typical [[structure_and_sequence_of_research_studies_in_epidemiology | sequence of steps]] is:
1.  Select cases and controls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:06">[00:20:06]</a>.
2.  Match them based on relevant criteria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:50">[00:20:50]</a>.
3.  Measure exposure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:07">[00:21:07]</a>.
4.  Analyze the data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:08">[00:21:08]</a>.

#### Generation Time
This refers to the period from the entry of an organism into a host to the point of maximum communicability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:33">[00:43:33]</a>.

#### Quarantine
Quarantine is applied to healthy individuals who have been exposed to a disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:04">[00:53:04]</a>. It is maintained for the maximum known incubation period of the disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:08">[00:53:08]</a>. This is considered a primary level of prevention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:16">[00:53:16]</a>.

### Environmental Health & Disaster Management

#### Kata Thermometer
The Kata thermometer is used to measure low air velocities and the cooling power of air <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:01">[00:22:01]</a>. Other instruments for environmental measurements include:
*   Simple thermometer for air temperature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:20">[00:22:20]</a>.
*   Hygrometer or psychrometer for air humidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:26">[00:22:26]</a>.
*   Globe thermometer for radiant heat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:35">[00:22:35]</a>.

#### Air Requirement
The widely quoted standard for fresh air supply, such as the 3,000 cubic feet per person per hour from the Chamon criteria, is calculated based on the carbon dioxide content of the air to ensure stale air is replaced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:40">[01:06:40]</a>.

#### Disaster Management Cycle
The cycle typically follows this order:
1.  Disaster Impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:58">[01:05:58]</a>
2.  Response (including emergency response) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:03">[01:06:03]</a>
3.  Rehabilitation (often the longest phase) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:05">[01:06:05]</a>
4.  Mitigation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:07">[01:06:07]</a>

### Demographic Concepts

#### Demographic Stages
There are five demographic stages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:47">[00:24:47]</a>:
1.  High Stationary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:55">[00:24:55]</a>
2.  Early Expanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:57">[00:24:57]</a>
3.  Late Expanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:57">[00:24:57]</a>
4.  Low Stationary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:59">[00:24:59]</a>
5.  Declining Phase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:59">[00:24:59]</a>

#### Longevity Dividend
The longevity dividend is an economic and health benefit to society resulting from an increased lifespan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:33">[00:59:33]</a>. It is linked to the demographic dividend, where a higher number of economically productive individuals in a population leads to increased income and, subsequently, a longer life expectancy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:50">[00:58:50]</a>. This allows individuals to contribute longer to society through work, new endeavors, and accumulated wisdom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:47">[00:59:47]</a>.
