The sense of taste involves specific receptors that detect different chemical compounds, translating them into distinct taste sensations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:50:02">[06:50:02]</a>.

## Overview of Taste Sensations

There are five primary taste sensations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:19:19">[06:19:19]</a>:
*   Sweet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:24:22">[06:24:22]</a>
*   Salt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:27:14">[06:27:14]</a>
*   Sour <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:30:17">[06:30:17]</a>
*   Bitter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:05">[06:37:05]</a>
*   Umami, a recently discovered taste sensation, often associated with the taste of tomato <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:39:53">[06:39:53]</a>.

## Types of Taste Receptors

Taste receptors generally fall into two main groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:01:46">[07:01:46]</a>:

*   **Metabotropic Channel Receptors**: These are G-protein coupled receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:05:07">[07:05:07]</a>. Sweet, Bitter, and Umami sensations are mediated by metabotropic G-protein coupled receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:37:46">[07:37:46]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:51:00">[07:51:00]</a>.
*   **Ionotropic Receptors**: These receptors lead to an influx of ions upon opening <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:18:04">[07:18:04]</a>. Salt and Sour taste sensations are mediated by ion channel mechanisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:28:10">[07:28:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:46:58">[07:46:58]</a>.
