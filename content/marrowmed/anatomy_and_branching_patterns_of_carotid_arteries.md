## Origin of Carotid Arteries

The carotid arteries originate from the aortic arch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:06">[00:28:06]</a>:
*   **Right Side** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:00">[00:29:00]</a>: The first branch of the aortic arch is the brachiocephalic artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:04">[00:33:04]</a>, which then divides into the right subclavian artery and the right common carotid artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:04">[00:36:04]</a>.
*   **Left Side** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:00">[00:38:00]</a>: The left common carotid artery arises directly from the aortic arch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:04">[00:40:04]</a>.

On an angiogram taken at the level of the origin of the great vessels, both the right common carotid artery and the left common carotid artery are visible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:00">[00:52:00]</a>.

## Division of Common Carotid Arteries

At the level of the mid and upper neck, the common carotid arteries divide into two main branches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:00">[00:58:00]</a>:
1.  **Internal Carotid Artery (ICA)**: This artery continues intracranially <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:00">[01:05:00]</a>. It is the main artery that supplies the brain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>.
2.  **External Carotid Artery (ECA)**: This artery predominantly continues extracranially <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>. The external carotid artery is generally a smaller branch compared to the internal carotid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:00">[01:22:00]</a>.

## Branches of the External Carotid Artery

The external carotid artery gives rise to numerous branches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>. A mnemonic used by anatomists to remember the sequence of origin is:
"**S**ome **A**natomists **L**ike **F**reaking **O**ut **P**otential **M**edical **S**tudents" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:58:00">[01:58:00]</a>.

The branches are <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:00">[02:02:00]</a>:
*   **S**uperior thyroid artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>
*   **A**scending pharyngeal artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:00">[02:14:00]</a>
*   **L**ingual artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:19:00">[02:19:00]</a>
*   **F**acial artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:00">[02:24:00]</a>
*   **O**ccipital artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>
*   **P**osterior auricular artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:32:00">[02:32:00]</a>
*   **M**axillary artery (a very important branch) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:36:00">[02:36:00]</a>
*   **S**uperficial temporal artery (the terminal branch of the external carotid artery) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>.

> [!info] Clinical Relevance: Superficial Temporal Artery
> The superficial temporal artery is an [[muscle_and_nerve_anatomy | extracranial artery]] and the terminal branch of the external carotid artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:17:00">[05:17:00]</a>. It can be identified on an angiogram as an extra cranial vessel close to the skin surface <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a>.

## Segments of the Internal Carotid Artery (Bouthillier Classification)

The internal carotid artery is divided into seven segments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:01:00">[03:01:00]</a>:
1.  Cervical segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>
2.  Petrous segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:00">[03:09:00]</a>
3.  Lacerum segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:00">[03:12:00]</a>
4.  Cavernous segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:17:00">[03:17:00]</a>
5.  Clinoid segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:21:00">[03:21:00]</a>
6.  Ophthalmic segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:00">[03:26:00]</a>
7.  Communicating segment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>

## Intracranial Circulation

The intracranial vessels, often visualized using an MR angiogram, are divided into anterior and posterior circulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

### Anterior Circulation
The anterior circulation is primarily supplied by the internal carotid artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:03:00">[04:03:00]</a>. The internal carotid artery divides on each side into <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:07:00">[04:07:00]</a>:
*   The anterior cerebral artery (with A1 and A2 segments) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:19:00">[04:19:00]</a>
*   The middle cerebral artery (with M1, M2, M3, M4 segments) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:10:00">[04:10:00]</a>

### Posterior Circulation
The posterior circulation is supplied by the vertebral arteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:31:00">[04:31:00]</a>:
*   The vertebral arteries arise from the subclavian arteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:35:00">[04:35:00]</a>.
*   The two vertebral arteries fuse to form the basilar artery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:38:00">[04:38:00]</a>.
*   The basilar artery then divides into the posterior cerebral arteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:43:00">[04:43:00]</a>.
