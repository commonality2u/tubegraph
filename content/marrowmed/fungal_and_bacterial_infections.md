## Introduction
This article summarizes key aspects of various [[Bacterial Infections and Eological Agents | bacterial]] and fungal infections, including their characteristics, diagnostic methods, and treatments, as discussed in a microbiology recall session <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a>. ## Bacterial Infections

### *Haemophilus influenzae*
*   **Satellitism**: *Haemophilus influenzae* exhibits satellitism, a growth property observed on blood agar alongside *Staphylococcus aureus* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. The *Staphylococcus aureus* forms a central thick line, with *Haemophilus influenzae* growing near it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>.

### *Staphylococcus aureus*
*   **Coagulase Test**: *Staphylococcus aureus* yields a positive coagulase test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.
*   **Satellitism**: It aids the growth of *Haemophilus influenzae* through satellitism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>.
*   **Catalase Test**: Catalase is positive for *Staphylococcus* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:35">[00:16:35]</a>.

### *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*
*   **Elek Precipitation Test**: This test is used for *Corynebacterium diphtheriae* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>.

### *Vibrio cholerae*
*   **String Test**: *Vibrio cholerae* gives a positive string test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:06">[00:03:06]</a>. It produces yellow colonies on TCBS agar and gives a cholera reaction positive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>.

### *Streptococcus pneumoniae*
*   **Quellung Test**: The Quellung test is used to detect the capsule of *Streptococcus pneumoniae* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.
*   **Catalase Test**: Catalase is negative for *Streptococcus* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:30">[00:16:30]</a>.

### *Vibrio parahaemolyticus*
*   **Clinical Presentation**: Causes gastroenteritis and wound infection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>.
*   **Culture**: Shows green color colonies on TCBS agar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>.
*   **Kanagawa Phenomenon**: This phenomenon on Wagatsuma agar is seen with *Vibrio parahaemolyticus* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:26">[00:08:26]</a>.

### *Helicobacter pylori*
*   **Prevalence**: Infects about half of the global population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>.
*   **Disease Manifestation**: *H. pylori* infection does not lead to peptic ulcer disease in every infected person because of host factors, environmental factors, and bacterial virulence factors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>. More virulent bacteria, genetically favorable hosts, and specific environmental factors (like socioeconomic status and hygiene) increase the chances of ulcer development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>.
*   **Colonization**: It primarily colonizes the gastric antrum, but can spread throughout the stomach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:45">[00:09:45]</a>.
*   **Asymptomatic Nature**: While infection can be asymptomatic in most people, it does not spontaneously resolve; treatment is required <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:12">[00:10:12]</a>. Concurrent use of NSAIDs can increase the chance of peptic ulcer disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:26">[00:26:26]</a>.

### [[Bacterial Infections and Eological Agents | Agglutination Tests]]
*   **Widal Test**: Used for *Salmonella*, but is positive from the second week of illness, not the first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</a>.
*   **Microscopic Agglutination Test (MAT)**: Done for *Leptospira* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>.
*   **Weil-Felix Agglutination Test**: Used for *Rickettsia*, causing typhus fever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:44">[00:14:44]</a>.
*   **Standard Agglutination Test (SAT)**: Used for *Brucella* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:39">[00:14:39]</a>.
*   **Antibody Detection**: Antibodies are generally detectable from the second week of illness in most organisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:56">[00:15:56]</a>.

### [[diagnosing_and_managing_bone_infections | Osteomyelitis]]
*   **Clinical Presentation**: Presents with pain, swelling, warmth of the affected limb, elevated ESR and CRP, and imaging showing bone fragments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:08">[00:45:08]</a>.
*   **Common Causes**:
    *   Most common overall: *Staphylococcus aureus* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:08">[00:46:08]</a>.
    *   In sickle cell anemia: *Salmonella* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:17">[00:46:17]</a>.
    *   In neonates: Group B *Streptococcus*, *E. coli*, and *Klebsiella* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:04">[00:47:04]</a>.
    *   In India, *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* is a common cause <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:04">[00:48:04]</a>.
*   **Unlikely Cause**: *Klebsiella* is considered the least common cause among Group B *Streptococcus*, *E. coli*, and *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* in a 12-year-old boy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:21">[00:48:21]</a>.

### [[diagnosing_and_managing_bone_infections | Spinal Tuberculosis]]
*   **Diagnosis**:
    *   First step: Acid-fast staining (if available) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:01">[00:36:01]</a>.
    *   Second step (WHO recommended investigation of choice): CBNAAT (Cartridge-Based Nucleic Acid Amplification Test) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:01">[00:36:01]</a>, which detects the gene of the organism and provides results in 2-3 hours <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:42">[00:37:42]</a>.
    *   Culture: Although the "best" for definitive diagnosis, *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* is a slow grower (takes 1.5 to 2 months), making it impractical for timely treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:09">[00:36:09]</a>.
    *   Imaging (X-ray, CT scan): Can show deformity but cannot identify the organism or antibody, thus not a definitive diagnosis for infectious diseases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:03">[00:37:03]</a>.

### *Treponema pallidum* (Syphilis)
*   **Stages and Manifestations**:
    *   **Primary Syphilis**: Transient genital ulcer (chancre) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:16">[00:39:16]</a>.
    *   **Secondary Syphilis**: Rashes on palms and soles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:25">[00:39:25]</a>.
    *   **Latent Syphilis**: Asymptomatic stage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:33">[00:39:33]</a>.
    *   **Tertiary Syphilis**: Gummata, tabes dorsalis, aortic aneurysm <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:42">[00:39:42]</a>.
*   **Diagnosis**: Serological tests are most appropriate, such as VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory) and RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin) tests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:35">[00:40:35]</a>.

### *Neisseria gonorrhoeae* (Gonococcal Urethritis)
*   **Clinical Presentation**: Purulent urethral discharge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:42">[00:43:42]</a>.
*   **Microscopy**: Gram stain shows gram-negative diplococci, often intracellular within neutrophils <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:02">[00:44:02]</a>.
*   **Treatment**: [[drug_of_choice_for_specific_infections_and_conditions | Ceftriaxone]] is the recommended treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:10">[00:44:10]</a>.

### Sterile Pyuria
*   **Definition**: Presence of WBCs (pus) in urine but no bacterial growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:45">[00:41:45]</a>.
*   **Causes**: Can be caused by organisms not readily seen on routine urine culture, such as *Rickettsia*, [[Bacterial Infections and Eological Agents | *Chlamydia*]], or *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:07">[00:42:07]</a>.
*   **Differentiation**: Unlike bacterial prostatitis, where the prostate would be enlarged or tender, in sterile pyuria the prostate can be normal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:59">[00:42:59]</a>.

## Fungal Infections

### [[Dimorphic fungi and associated symptoms | Thermal Dimorphic Fungi]]
*   **Examples**: There are six well-known [[Dimorphic fungi and associated symptoms | dimorphic fungi]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>:
    *   *Histoplasma* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>
    *   *Blastomyces* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>
    *   *Coccidioides* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>
    *   *Paracoccidioides* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>
    *   *Sporothrix schenckii* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>
    *   *Talaromyces marneffei* (formerly *Penicillium marneffei*) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>
*   **Exception**: *Cryptococcus neoformans* is a yeast, not a [[Dimorphic fungi and associated symptoms | dimorphic fungus]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.

### *Aspergillus*
*   **Morphology**: Characterized by narrow hyphae with acute-angle and dichotomous branching (each hypha gives only two branches) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:19">[00:21:19]</a>.
*   **Pigment Production**: *Aspergillus* species produce pigments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:34">[00:24:34]</a>:
    *   *Aspergillus fumigatus*: Smoky green pigment (due to melanin) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:38">[00:24:38]</a>.
    *   *Aspergillus flavus*: Yellow pigment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:41">[00:24:41]</a>.
    *   *Aspergillus niger*: Black pigment (melanin) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:51">[00:24:51]</a>.
*   **Stains**:
    *   Common fungal stains: PAS (Periodic Acid-Schiff) and GMS (Gomori Methenamine Silver) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:09">[00:22:09]</a>.
    *   Fontana-Masson stain: Stains melanin, and thus can be used for *Aspergillus* due to its melanin production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:49">[00:24:49]</a>.
    *   Congo Red: Primarily stains amyloid but can also stain fungal cell walls; however, it is the least preferred stain for *Aspergillus* among the options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:08">[00:23:08]</a>.

### *Candida* (Candidiasis)
*   **Clinical Presentation**: Causes white patches or membranes on skin and mucosa (oral, esophageal, vaginal), which are removable on rubbing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:37">[00:50:37]</a>. Esophageal candidiasis presents as white patches in the esophagus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:22">[00:50:22]</a>.
*   **Morphology**: *Candida* is a yeast-like fungus that shows budding yeasts with pseudohyphae <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:12">[00:51:12]</a>.
*   **Predisposing Factors**: Often seen in immunocompromised patients, such as those with HIV and low CD4 counts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:08">[00:50:08]</a>.
*   **Microscopy**: In esophageal candidiasis, yeast cells and pseudohyphae are seen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:43">[00:52:43]</a>.
