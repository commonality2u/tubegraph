### Understanding Muscle Soreness

Our bodies are designed to adapt to a hunter-gatherer lifestyle, involving activities like chasing animals, throwing spears, and climbing trees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>. In modern times, our muscles aren't typically used in this way <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>. When you first start lifting weights or running on a treadmill, your body experiences a shock, leading to soreness as your muscles recover from this unaccustomed exertion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:09">[00:01:09]</a>. This soreness, while uncomfortable, is a positive sign; it means your body is growing stronger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>. Your muscles are being nudged to "wake up" and adapt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>.

While muscle soreness will always occur to some degree, the intense soreness experienced by beginners will significantly reduce over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>.

### Scientific Methods to Reduce Muscle Soreness

The degree of muscle soreness can be reduced through smart and scientific approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.

#### 1. Cold Water Showers / Ice Baths

Taking a cold water shower immediately after a workout is a highly effective way to reduce muscle soreness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>. When muscles are worked out, they experience micro-tears, which leads to inflammation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>. Similar to applying ice to a bruise to reduce inflammation, cold water helps immediately reduce this inflammation, aiding in long-term healing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. Many professional sports teams, including major football clubs, use ice baths for players post-match, a practice observed by athletes like Cristiano Ronaldo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
