However, there is a growing interest, particularly among younger generations, in exploring [[Controversial perspectives on Indian history | alternate narratives]] and hidden aspects of this pivotal period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:27">[00:01:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>. ## Re-evaluating Revolutionary Figures

A significant point of contention in the official historical narrative concerns the depiction of revolutionary freedom fighters. Delhi University textbooks, for instance, have referred to figures like Bhagat Singh, Batukeshwar Dutt, and Jatindra Nath Das as "revolutionary terrorists" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:08">[00:00:08]</a>. Descendants of Bhagat Singh have protested this terminology, noting that it was a term coined by colonial masters and its continued use is problematic, especially in the context of celebrating independence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:08">[00:01:10]</a>. The podcast host notes that a young child might equate such figures with modern-day terrorists based on this description <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:20">[00:17:20]</a>.

Instead of solely focusing on the non-violent movement led by Mahatma Gandhi and the Congress, which was significant in galvanizing nationalism, alternate narratives highlight the parallel "violent armed resistance" that formed an "unending chain" from the 1857 Uprising through to the 1946 Naval Mutiny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

## The Naval Mutiny of 1946

The Naval Mutiny of 1946, which began in Bombay (now Mumbai), is presented as a crucial event often overlooked in history books <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>. This uprising, initially a protest by sailors over ratings, escalated into demands for liberation, with participants carrying placards of Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose and his Indian National Army (INA) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:51">[00:13:51]</a>. The movement spread to other parts of India, including South India, Karachi, and Jabalpur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:16">[00:14:16]</a>.

Clement Attlee, the British Prime Minister at the time of India's independence, reportedly stated in 1952 or 1953 that the primary reasons for Britain's hasty departure were the heroics of the INA and the inspiration it provided to the naval and army mutinies across India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:38">[00:09:38]</a>. He reportedly considered the impact of the Quit India Movement and Gandhi's non-violent efforts as "minimal" in the final decision to leave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>. This perspective suggests that the armed resistance played a more decisive role than often acknowledged in official narratives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>.

## Reasons for Narrative Control

After independence, the people in power, particularly the government led by Jawaharlal Nehru, are said to have ensured that a specific viewpoint of the freedom struggle was highlighted, primarily focusing on the non-violent movement and the Congress's role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:12">[00:15:12]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:19">[00:16:19]</a>. Anyone outside this "mainstream" framework was not given due recognition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>.

*   **Suppression of Opposing Viewpoints**: Historians like RC Majumdar, who intended to offer a critical view of Gandhi's role and highlighted his "failures," were removed from projects to document official history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:11">[00:18:11]</a>. This created an environment where historians felt compelled to "toe the line" to retain their positions and patronage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:12">[00:19:12]</a>.
*   **Geopolitical Influence**: [[Geopolitical strategies and narratives | Geopolitical powers]], especially the Western Bloc (Britain and later America), are believed to have influenced the narratives surrounding figures like Nehru and Gandhi, leading to their prominent branding as symbols of the Indian Freedom Struggle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:21">[00:20:21]</a>. This created a "win-win situation" where India was depicted as achieving freedom peacefully, and its colonial masters were shown in a "good light" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:46">[00:26:46]</a>.
*   **Collaborators in Power**: Many individuals who held positions of power in the bureaucracy and intellectual circles immediately after independence were part of the "larger collaborat[ing] gang" during British rule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:55">[00:22:55]</a>. An example given is Yashpal, who was reportedly a British informant against revolutionaries and later became a celebrated intellectual, influencing the narrative through his writings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:09">[00:23:09]</a>. These "collaborators" ensured that much of the truth was "rested and kept under the carpet" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>.

## Unsung Heroes and Unveiling Truths

Many figures who contributed significantly to the armed struggle remain "unsung heroes" or are even "demonized" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:00">[00:27:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:19">[01:01:19]</a>. Examples include Sardar Udham Singh, whose story gained prominence only through a recent film <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:01">[00:28:01]</a>. Other revolutionaries from various regions like Maharashtra (Vasudev Balwant Phadke, Chapekar brothers, [[Cultural and historical significance of ancient Indian scriptures | V. D. Savarkar]]), Bengal (Aurobindo Ghosh, Barin Ghosh), and those associated with the Ghadar Movement are often overlooked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:21">[00:28:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:34">[00:28:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:40">[00:28:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:16">[00:29:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:39">[00:29:39]</a>.

[[Cultural and historical significance of ancient Indian scriptures | V. D. Savarkar]] is highlighted as a figure whose contributions are often misunderstood or maligned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:45">[00:33:45]</a>.
*   **Revolutionary Contributions**: Savarkar initiated India's first organized secret society, Abhinav Bharat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:10">[00:34:10]</a>. He organized the first student bonfire of foreign clothes in 1905, years before Gandhi, and was rusticated for it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:20">[00:34:20]</a>. His book on the 1857 Uprising, calling it the "first war of Indian independence," served as a "Bhagavad Gita" for future revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh and Subhas Chandra Bose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:02">[00:35:02]</a>.
*   **Kalapani and Mercy Petitions**: Savarkar endured 14 years of imprisonment, including 12 in Kalapani (Cellular Jail) in Andaman, followed by 13 years under house arrest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:32">[00:35:32]</a>. The brutal conditions in Kalapani, including "oil extraction" torture, unhygienic environments, and mental anguish leading to suicides, are emphasized as horrific aspects of history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:40">[00:44:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:34">[00:45:34]</a>. The narrative clarifies that Savarkar's "Mercy Petitions" were common legal applications by political prisoners, not apologies, and British records confirm his lack of remorse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:47">[00:36:47]</a>. He often filed petitions on behalf of others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:52">[00:37:52]</a>.
*   **"Hindutva"**: The term "Hindutva," as envisioned by Savarkar in his book *Essentials of Hindutva*, is clarified as a cultural and national identity marker, distinct from the theological aspects of Hinduism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:45">[00:41:45]</a>. It emphasized devotion to the nation and opposed the "Khilafat movement," which he believed had dangerous communal ramifications leading to partition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:08">[00:40:08]</a>.
*   **Gandhi Murder Case Exoneration**: Savarkar was implicated in Gandhi's murder due to his past affiliation with Nathuram Godse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:14">[00:57:14]</a>. However, the trial court in 1948 honorably exonerated him due to lack of evidence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:07">[00:58:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:19">[00:58:19]</a>. This exoneration was upheld by the Supreme Court in 2018, dispelling persistent insinuations against him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:24">[00:58:24]</a>.

## The Need for Truth and Reconciliation

The suppression of these narratives has led to an incomplete understanding of India's past. The speaker emphasizes that India's history has been "Delhi-centric," neglecting the contributions of powerful regional empires like the Cholas, Vijayanagara, and Ahom kingdoms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>.

The desire to unveil "hidden history" is driven by a realization that a nation, like an individual, needs to heal from its past wounds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:02">[01:10:02]</a>. Fabricating or whitewashing historical events, such as the anti-Maharashtrian Brahmin carnage in 1948 following Gandhi's murder, prevents closure and can affect future generations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:39">[01:03:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:41">[01:07:41]</a>.

The call is to confront the past with an "honest assessment," to "tell the truth as it is," without succumbing to political correctness or attempts to preserve an "edifice of national unity" built on "fabricated history" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:47">[01:10:47]</a>. This process allows for learning from past mistakes and building a more confident future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:09">[01:10:09]</a>. The role of youth in shaping India and the spread of information through social media are seen as crucial for amplifying these alternate voices and fostering a more complete understanding of history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:18">[00:04:18]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:02">[00:25:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:25">[01:14:25]</a>.
