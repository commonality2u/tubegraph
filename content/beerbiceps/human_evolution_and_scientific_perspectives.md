## Early Human History and Missing Periods

The first Homo Sapien is estimated to have arrived roughly 200,000 years ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:39">[00:16:39]</a>. A Homo Sapien from that era would look indistinguishable from a modern human if dressed in contemporary clothes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:44">[00:16:44]</a>. However, written records only trace back approximately 10,000 years ago, around 10,000 BCE, which is when the Rig Veda is considered to have been composed, based on astrological conjunctions mentioned in some verses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:38">[00:17:38]</a>. This leaves a significant gap of approximately 188,000 years of human history about which very little is known <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:51">[00:17:51]</a>.

Over such a long period, even materials like bones degrade, and modern plastic would degrade within 2,000 to 3,000 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:05">[00:18:05]</a>. The only remnants that might survive geological events like meteor showers and ice ages are rock formations or structures made of stone, such as pyramids <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:27">[00:18:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:30">[00:18:30]</a>. This explains why artifacts from the Stone Age are still prevalent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:37">[00:18:37]</a>.

## Impact of Geological Events

The "missing period" of human history is largely attributed to a major ice age that started around 150,000 years ago and lasted until about 10,000 years ago (10,000 BCE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:21">[00:21:21]</a>. Massive ice glaciers formed globally during this time, drawing water from the oceans and significantly lowering sea levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:04">[00:22:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:22">[00:22:22]</a>. This phenomenon suggests that ancient cities could have been built in areas that are now submerged oceans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:25">[00:22:25]</a>. Researchers like Graham Hancock dedicate their lives to uncovering lost aspects of different world cultures, conducting deep-sea diving off the coast of India to find lost cities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:43">[00:21:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:51">[00:21:51]</a>. The discovery of Dwarka, which was not fully explored, is an example of such remnants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:51">[00:22:51]</a>.

During these ice ages, a major portion of humanity vanished <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:33">[00:33:33]</a>. Humanity survived primarily in tropical regions like parts of the Middle East, Africa, and India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:39">[00:23:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:08">[00:24:08]</a>.

## Early Human Populations and Migrations

Before Homo Sapiens became globally dominant, other species like Neanderthals in Europe and Denisovans in Asia (Russia and Japan) existed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:36">[00:24:36]</a>. In India, subspecies such as Ramapithecus and Sivapithecus, cousins of Homo Sapiens, were discovered in the Shivalik hills of Nepal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>. Evidence of ancient tools has been found across regions like the Soan Valley in Pakistan, India, and Nepal, as well as in Rajasthan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:17">[00:25:17]</a>.

[[human_origin_theories_and_migrations_from_africa_and_india | Human origin theories and migrations from Africa and India]] suggest that the oldest inhabitants of the Indian subcontinent were concentrated in the central region, possibly migrating there via a sea route <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:38">[00:25:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:06">[00:26:06]</a>. Genetic studies indicate a connection between the original Aborigines of Australia and the people living in the Andamans, suggesting early sea routes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:13">[00:24:13]</a>. The theory that the "Vanaras" in the Ramayana could have been a primitive version or subspecies of Homo Sapiens, existing alongside more developed human civilizations, is a distinct possibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:48">[00:31:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:51">[00:34:51]</a>.

Genetic studies have largely indicated that people moved out of India, countering the premise that civilization moved into India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:46">[00:27:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:51">[00:27:51]</a>. [[historical_and_cultural_evolution_according_to_hindu_texts | Hindu texts]], such as the Puranas, describe ancient migrations, like the story of King Yayati's sons who moved in different directions, with some (the Anus and Yadus) moving westward, possibly leading to the Yavanas (Greeks) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:01">[00:28:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:01">[00:29:01]</a>. Early parts of the Rig Veda describe rivers around the Indian subcontinent, while later parts increasingly mention Western rivers, supporting the idea of outward migration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:16">[00:30:16]</a>.

The idea of [[human_migration_and_ancient_civilizations | human migration and ancient civilizations]] suggests that during the ice age, when only a strip of land in the tropical zone was inhabited, culture likely developed there. As the Earth warmed, people moved towards Europe, explaining the [[crosscultural_archaeological_parallels | crosscultural archaeological parallels]] between Greek, Roman, and Syrian cultures with ancient Hindu culture, as well as connections with Southeast Asia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:40">[00:26:40]</a>.

## Future Human Evolution

The tenth and final avatar of Vishnu, Kalki, is predicted to appear at the end of the Kali Yuga when there is a complete decimation of religion and godliness, with a prevalence of atheism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:56">[01:14:56]</a>. Kalki's purpose will be violent, similar to Parashurama, to rid the world of demonic forces and evil kings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>. This aligns with cross-cultural predictions of a Messiah coming for Judgment Day, such as the Fourth Horseman in Christianity or a Hazrat in Islam <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>. Vajrayana Buddhism also holds a strong belief in the Kalachakra teachings, where the last king in a lineage of 32 kings corresponds to Kalki <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:07">[01:07:07]</a>.

Kalki represents a form of man beyond being merely enlightened, possibly an astral being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:50">[01:21:50]</a>. This future evolution may involve a progression of mental faculties and potentially the synthesis of humans and machines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:50">[01:21:50]</a>. Modern scientific concepts like telekinesis, teleportation, or "siddhis" (supernatural powers) could be understood as the harnessing of untapped mental capacities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:50">[01:21:50]</a>. Developments in biotech, including 3D printing, stem cell research, AI, and nanobots, are likely to enhance human capabilities, suggesting Kalki could embody immense powers due to these advanced abilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:50">[01:21:50]</a>.

Ultimately, humanity's next step involves breaking national boundaries and working towards global unity and peace, recognizing that all human beings are fundamentally one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:51">[00:54:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:07">[00:55:07]</a>. The core message is proactive responsibility for society, fostering development, and addressing wrongs, rather than waiting for an external savior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:06">[01:27:06]</a>.
