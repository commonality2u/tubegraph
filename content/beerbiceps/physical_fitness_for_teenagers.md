### The Overcompensation Principle
The body has an inherent ability to adapt and grow stronger in response to physical exertion, known as the "overcompensation principle" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>. For example, if muscles are challenged (e.g., by lifting weights), the body repairs and strengthens them during [[importance_of_sleep_for_teenage_growth | sleep]], preparing them to handle greater demands in the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>.

This principle applies to overall physical development. Sports like basketball, which involve frequent stretching and jumping, can encourage the body to [[height_growth_during_teenage_years | grow taller]] to adapt to the demands of the sport <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a>. This phenomenon has been observed in athletes who surpass their families' genetic [[height_growth_during_teenage_years | limits]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>. Any [[Physical fitness for teenagers | physical activity]] that exhausts muscles, the nervous system, or bones, can lead to the body overcompensating by becoming stronger, faster, and more efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>.
