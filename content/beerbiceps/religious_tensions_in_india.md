## Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) Controversy
The Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) became a major point of contention, with protests occurring against it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:09">[01:11:09]</a>. A key criticism is that many protesters, including public figures, lacked a clear understanding of the legislation they were opposing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:04">[01:12:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:07">[01:12:07]</a>.

The CAA primarily aims to facilitate faster acquisition of Indian citizenship for refugees from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:05">[00:13:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:07">[00:13:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:37">[00:13:37]</a>. It reduces the residency period required to apply for citizenship from 11 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:26">[00:13:26]</a>. The Act is based on the premise that these countries engage in "marked anti-non-Muslim persecution" leading to dwindling demographics of non-Muslim minorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:40">[00:13:40]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:49">[00:13:49]</a>.

A central question raised about the CAA was whether it would affect Indian Muslims <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:59">[00:13:59]</a>. It is argued that the amendment does not deprive any Indian Muslim of their right to citizenship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:19">[00:14:19]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:22">[00:14:22]</a>. The rationale for excluding Muslims from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan is rooted in the "two-nation theory" that led to Partition, asserting that Hindus and Muslims cannot coexist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:37">[00:14:37]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:03">[00:15:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>. Therefore, the same benefit of doubt extended to Indian Muslims is not given to Pakistani or Bangladeshi Muslims <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:26">[00:15:26]</a>.

The Ahmadiyya community (Ahmadi Muslims) were proponents of Pakistan's creation, and despite facing persecution themselves, they continue to "spew venom against Hindus in Pakistan" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:36">[00:15:36]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:47">[00:16:47]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:55">[00:16:55]</a>. Given that India is an 80% Hindu country, inviting groups who sought separation and continue to harbor anti-Hindu sentiments is seen as endangering national interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:01">[00:17:01]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:09">[00:17:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:15">[00:17:15]</a>.

Despite the controversy, the rules for implementing the CAA have not been notified as of 2023, with six extensions sought from Parliament <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:39">[00:17:39]</a>. This lack of implementation despite public discussion raises questions about the government's interest or strength <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:58">[00:17:58]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:06">[00:18:06]</a>.

## Historical Context and Identity
The [[controversial_perspectives_on_indian_history | controversial perspectives on Indian history]] are deeply ingrained in current [[hindu_muslim_relations_in_india | Hindu Muslim relations]]. The concept of a "nation" relies on common heroes and villains, and a largely shared vision of the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:05">[00:25:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>. However, if there isn't a shared vision of the past, a shared future becomes difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:23">[00:25:23]</a>.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's work, *Pakistan or the Partition of India*, is cited for asking fundamental questions about what constitutes a nation and whether India, with its two major communities, could survive as a single nation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:15">[00:24:15]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:20">[00:24:20]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:28">[00:24:28]</a>. He argued that Hindus and Muslims had never existed as a single nation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>.

The idea that conversion to a different faith could alienate individuals from their Indian roots and from "Bharat" (India) poses a "significant threat to the long-term interests of Bharat" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:42">[00:30:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:45">[00:30:45]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:52">[00:30:52]</a>. The existence of a distinct "Indian Islam" is desired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:58">[00:30:58]</a>.

For a harmonious future, three requirements are proposed for Abrahamic faiths in India:
1.  Accept that these faiths are not native to "Bharat" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:30">[00:31:30]</a>.
2.  Recognize that the culture of the land is significantly informed by its native faith systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:34">[00:31:34]</a>.
3.  Be sympathetic to the fact that these are "imposed faiths" as "a lot of them are converts" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:51">[00:31:51]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:55">[00:31:55]</a>.
Conversions back to native faiths should be a matter of "People's Choice" and "free will," not imposition or coercion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:04">[00:32:04]</a>. However, it is critical that the "native Faith system is struggling to find gasps of breath" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:12">[00:32:12]</a>. An example of this tension is the refusal to allow former Prime Minister Vajpayee's ashes to be immersed in rivers in the Northeast due to objections from Christian majority communities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:18">[00:32:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:28">[00:32:28]</a>.
