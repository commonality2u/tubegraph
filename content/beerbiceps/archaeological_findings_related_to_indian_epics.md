## Dating the Epics and Yugas

Based on available evidence, the Mahabharata is dated to more than 7,000 years ago, specifically around 5561 BC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:56:06">[06:56:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:13:13">[07:13:13]</a>. The Ramayana is dated to more than 14,000 years ago, around 12,209 BC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:00:54">[07:00:54]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:17:28">[07:17:28]</a>. The Rig Veda is placed at more than 21,000 years ago, following a "multiplication of seven" pattern for these historical periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:04:15">[07:04:15]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:05:43">[07:05:43]</a>.

The concept of a 'Yuga' in the Indian context is defined as a period of time, but its interpretation varies significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:34:03">[08:34:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:51:30">[08:51:30]</a>. There are over 26 different definitions of a Yuga, with the speaker having added 10 more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:00:15">[09:00:15]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:03:00">[09:03:00]</a>. Some interpretations disconnect Yugas from strict chronological time periods:
*   **Action-based Yuga**: From the Brahmanas connected to the Rig Veda, Kaluga is when one is sleeping, Dwapara when waking up and sitting, Treta when standing, and Satya Yuga when one starts walking and taking action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:11:42">[10:11:42]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:39:18">[10:39:18]</a>.
*   **Emotional State Yuga**: Similar to Einstein's theory of relativity, a Yuga can feel like a minute or an hour depending on the emotional state, as exemplified by Chaitanya Mahaprabhu's shloka where a moment of separation from Govinda feels like a Yuga <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:44:06">[10:44:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:02:12">[12:02:12]</a>.
*   **Mathematical Formulations**: The commonly cited Yuga durations (e.g., 432,000 years for Kali Yuga) are mathematical formulations used in astronomy to precisely calculate orbital periods of planets, not chronological durations of the epics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:12:00">[12:12:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:46:00">[12:46:00]</a>.
*   **Administration/Civic Sense**: The Mahabharata states that the type of Yuga depends on how a king or administrator governs the kingdom (Raja Kalasya Karanam) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:38:00">[13:38:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:57:00">[13:57:00]</a>. This can also extend to the civic sense of the populace (Praja Kalasya Karanam) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:00:00">[14:00:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:04:00">[14:04:00]</a>.

## Reinterpreting Epic Characters and Geography

### Rakshasas and Asuras
The terms "Rakshasas" and "Asuras" are often perceived as villainous in Indian epics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:09:00">[16:09:00]</a>. However, in the Rig Veda, "Asura" is often eulogized and praised, with deities like Indra and Varuna referred to as "Asura" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:37:00">[19:37:00]</a>. The word "Rakshasa" literally means "one who protects" (Raksha+Asa) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:59:00">[19:59:00]</a>. The perception shifted over time, potentially due to the corrupting nature of power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:27:00">[20:27:00]</a>.

The speaker conjectures that Rakshasas like Hidimba, Ghatotkacha, and Bakasura were human beings, perhaps from different geographical locations (e.g., Africa), or people with significant physical prowess <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:16:00">[21:16:00]</a>. Ravana, though a Rakshasa, was a descendant of a Great Sage (Rishi) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:04:00">[21:04:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:10:00">[21:10:00]</a>. His kingdom extended across various parts of the world, including India and possibly South America <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:40:00">[21:40:00]</a>.

### Zoroastrian Connection
Zoroastrianism is considered a sister religion to [[Ancient Hindu culture and its scientific aspects|Sanatan Dharma]] due to many parallels like fire worship, mantras, and linguistic similarities (Avesta and Sanskrit) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:35:00">[15:35:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:53:00">[15:53:00]</a>. In Zoroastrianism, 'Deva' is villainous and 'Ahura' is noble, while in Hindu texts, 'Deva' is divine and 'Asura' is often villainous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:03:00">[16:03:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:13:00">[16:13:00]</a>. This suggests two warring tribes in ancient times, with each epic painting the other side as negative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:36:00">[16:36:00]</a>. Zoroastrians were based in Central Asia (Iran, Uzbekistan), which is referred to as Utara Kuru in Indian texts, existing even during Ramayana times (14,000 years ago) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:03:00">[17:03:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:31:00">[17:31:00]</a>.
