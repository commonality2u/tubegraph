## Ethics: Principles vs. Rules
Landry distinguishes between ethics and morality, akin to principles and rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>.
*   **Principles (Ethics):** General heuristics that apply broadly, often related to communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37">[00:05:37]</a>. For example, a principle is that communication channels are limited if content conditionally affects context (e.g., saying "hang up" on a phone call actually hangs up the phone) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.
*   **Rules (Morality):** Specific applications of principles relevant to a particular situation or context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:52">[00:05:52]</a>. For instance, "don't type in capital letters" on an email forum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>.

As the world changes drastically due to technology, there's a constant need to rethink the relationship between ethical principles and moral rules to ensure choices align with underlying values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>.

## Values, Meaning, and Purpose
Landry distinguishes between three concepts critical for decision-making:
*   **Purpose:** Defined externally (e.g., the purpose of a toaster is to make toast, assigned by the user) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:58">[00:28:58]</a>.
*   **Values:** Innate, coming from within, not imposed externally (e.g., a son's intrinsic desires for his life, independent of his father's expectations) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:07">[00:30:07]</a>. They manifest in choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:26">[00:30:26]</a>.
*   **Meaning:** Occurs in the relationship between the subjective and the objective; it's inherently transpersonal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:06">[00:31:06]</a>. (e.g., the word "dog" gaining meaning through interaction between sound, personal association, and object reference) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:44">[00:30:44]</a>.

Though distinct, these concepts are inseparable; where one occurs, the other two will also be present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:30">[00:32:30]</a>. For profound, impactful choices, especially concerning existential risks or civilization collapse, a rigorous understanding of these distinctions is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:00">[00:33:00]</a>.

## Navigating [[complexity_and_governance | Complexity and Governance]]
When making decisions in complex situations, like using CRISPR, it's essential to recognize that not all outcomes can be predicted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:17">[00:35:17]</a>. Safe-to-fail probes are valuable, but certain experiments (e.g., global nuclear war, self-replicating biological systems) are too consequential to test <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:04">[00:36:04]</a>. The purposes of such probes are connected to the [[the_role_of_narratives_in_understanding_and_decision_making | meaningfulness]] of our values <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:06">[00:37:06]</a>.

Every complicated system is inevitably embedded in at least one complex system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:32">[00:38:32]</a>. For instance, a business (complicated) operates within a marketplace (complex), and a farm (mostly complicated) is embedded in an ecosystem (complex) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:43">[00:38:43]</a>. In these relationships, the complex is stronger and foundational <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:19">[00:39:19]</a>. Therefore, values must be stronger than purposes, guiding choices from a deeper, "transcendent" basis, rather than being debased by mere feedback mechanisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:32">[00:39:32]</a>.

The challenge with industrial farming, for example, is that its complicated processes are causing unsustainable damage to the complex ecosystem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:10">[00:41:10]</a>. Society must move towards sustainable and adaptive practices, allowing systems to evolve and work well with their changing contexts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:52">[00:41:52]</a>. This requires a form of "consciousness" that transcends blind evolutionary processes, which are often inefficient and result in "failed experiments" like species extinction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:48">[00:42:48]</a>.

Humanity's technological power has forced a need for conscious, values-based decision-making beyond unconscious market forces or pure evolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:21">[00:44:21]</a>. With the "power of gods," humanity needs the "wisdom of gods," not capricious ones, to manage its impact on the ecosystem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:57">[00:45:57]</a>.

## The Path Forward: [[sensemaking_and_truthfulness | Sensemaking and Truthfulness]], Choice-making, and Action-taking
To move from a state of "muddle blind deaf and dumb" to effective [[collective_sensemaking_and_decision_making | collective sensemaking and decision making]] and action, three components are needed:
1.  **Sensemaking:** Requires observing the world accurately and sharing information transparently, without personal filtering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:02">[00:52:02]</a>. This involves creating an "information ecology" that counters the disinformation prevalent on platforms like the internet, which currently incentivize individual benefit over community well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:17">[00:51:17]</a>. The process starts with asking the right questions about the state of the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:19">[00:52:19]</a>.
2.  **Choice-making:** Guided by a "compass" of values and criteria for success, enabling holistic and comprehensive solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:11">[00:53:11]</a>.
3.  **Action-taking/Implementation:** The capacity to manifest choices in the world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>.

Landry posits that an effective system requires a map (understanding of the world), a compass (values/guidance), and knowledge of one's current position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:46">[00:52:46]</a>. The failure of neurons to operate on individual benefit (filtering signals) would lead to a lack of computation or consciousness in a brain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:01">[00:55:01]</a>. Similarly, humanity must move beyond individual benefit in communication to a collective responsibility, recognizing that individual and collective well-being are deeply entangled <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:55">[01:01:55]</a>.

The current design of social media, prioritizing corporate and shareholder interests, neglects community and user benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:01">[00:59:01]</a>. A shift is needed where social technology serves community as its primary objective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:22">[00:59:22]</a>. Broadcasters and technology companies must be held responsible for the impact of their platforms, moving towards moral codes that prioritize community well-being over short-term financial gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:42">[00:59:42]</a>.
