Developmental psychology represents one of the longest continuous research projects in the field of psychology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>. Its core construct, [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]], emerged almost a century ago with Jean Piaget's 1923 publication, *The Language and Thought of the Child* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>. While earlier thinkers like James Mark Baldwin and Herbert Spencer touched upon similar ideas, the formal definition of developmental stages solidified with the Neo-Piagetian consensus in the 1980s and 1990s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>.

## Core Concepts

### Hierarchical vs. Horizontal Complexity

[[hierarchical_complexity | Hierarchical complexity]] describes the qualitative leap in a task's difficulty when new, more complex skills integrate lower-order capacities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:50">[00:06:50]</a>. For instance, making a shoe is hierarchically more complex than merely tying a shoelace <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a>. In contrast, horizontal complexity refers to performing more of the same task at the same level of difficulty, like tying a thousand shoes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. [[hierarchical_complexity | Hierarchical complexity]] is a ubiquitous property of mental processes and a fundamental difference in developmental stages from infancy to advanced theoretical physics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.

### Hierarchical Integration and Chunking

The process of [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical integration]] involves many lower-order skills combining to form a single, qualitatively new, more complex skill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. This concept is closely related to "chunking," where a large amount of dense, lower-order information (e.g., sensory-motor material) is captured by a single, higher-order representation or term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:37">[00:16:37]</a>. This allows for a definable scale of chunking, from basic sensory-motor actions to abstract theoretical concepts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:33">[00:17:33]</a>.

### Connection to [[emergentism_and_complexity_science | Emergentism and Complexity Science]]

The notion of [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] aligns with [[emergentism_and_complexity_science | complexity science]]'s understanding of emergence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:55">[00:19:55]</a>. It suggests a continuity of evolutionary process, specifically the emergence of higher-order or [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]], across cosmological and human development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:42">[00:21:42]</a>. This implies that psychological experience reflects universal laws of nature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:11">[00:22:11]</a>. Kurt Fischer, a Neo-Piagetian, pioneered the application of dynamical systems modeling techniques from complexity science to model [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | human development]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:21">[00:25:21]</a>.

### The Role of Working Memory

The small capacity of working memory (e.g., Miller's seven plus or minus two items) is hypothesized to be a significant driver for the emergence of [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:57">[00:27:57]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:28">[00:28:28]</a>. To handle more difficult problems, the brain must make chunks larger, enabling the simultaneous processing of more complex information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:00">[00:29:00]</a>.

### Epistemic Motivation and Understanding

Piaget distinguished between "success" (accomplishing a task) and "understanding" (knowing why it worked) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:59">[00:29:59]</a>. A critical threshold in [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | human development]] is crossed when children shift from merely wanting to succeed to wanting to understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:12">[00:30:12]</a>. This innate "epistemic motivation," fostered socially, drives information processing through the memory bottleneck <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:41">[00:30:41]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:48">[00:30:48]</a>. The lawful, non-random nature of the universe is also a prerequisite; if patterns were truly random, investing in machinery to extract abstract patterns would be useless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:39">[00:31:39]</a>.

## Levels of Hierarchical Complexity (Illustrated via Moral Development)

Within Fischer's framework, there are three tiers: actions, representations, and abstractions, followed by principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:47">[00:41:47]</a>. These roughly correspond to Commons's sensory-motor, operational/primary concrete, and formal/abstract/systemic stages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:56">[00:41:56]</a>.

1.  **Sensory-Motor Tier (Actions)**: Early levels are egocentric and punishment-reward oriented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:06">[01:08:06]</a>. For example, a child's understanding of "fairness" on a playground is concrete: literally seeing if each child has the same amount of M&Ms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:24">[01:08:24]</a>. This involves coordinating basic sensory-motor skills like focusing, reaching, grasping, and drinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:00">[00:43:00]</a>. Exploration of complex sensory-motor environments is crucial for early [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | development]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:43">[00:45:43]</a>.
2.  **Representational Tier**: This tier marks the emergence of the "semiotic function," where sprawling sets of sensory-motor experience are summarized into a single utterance or gesture, like the word "bedtime" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:20">[01:15:20]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:47:32">[01:47:32]</a>. Representations allow for entertaining counterfactuals (e.g., talking about Santa Claus) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:19">[01:48:19]</a>. In moral development, this might involve understanding fairness as conforming to simple, agreed-upon rules of a game <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:43">[01:08:43]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:27">[01:10:27]</a>.
3.  **Abstract Tier**: Abstractions integrate many representations into higher-order concepts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:25">[00:50:25]</a>. Unlike representations, abstractions cannot be simply pointed at (e.g., "quality family time" is an abstraction of bedtime, dinner time, etc.) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:36">[00:50:36]</a>. In moral judgment, this involves understanding fairness in terms of whether abstract rules promote the overall purpose of a game, ensuring it remains fair for all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:46">[01:08:46]</a>.
4.  **Principled Tier**: This highest tier involves meta-systematic integration, allowing for the formation of overarching theories or principles that transcend and include prior notions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:31">[01:09:31]</a>. An example is John Rawls's work on "justice as fairness," where multiple fields of ethics are integrated into a single theory of justice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:41">[01:09:41]</a>. At this level, one can critique the norms of society and make rules about rule-making <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:37">[01:10:37]</a>.

## Applications of Hierarchical Complexity

### [[educational_neuroscience_and_reform | Educational Neuroscience and Reform]]

Lectica, co-founded by Theo Dawson, developed standardized assessments based on [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:01">[01:14:01]</a>. Unlike pass/fail standardized tests (like the SAT), Lectica assessments provide diagnostic insights, indicating what a student understands and the next best thing for them to learn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:46">[01:15:46]</a>. These assessments can rationally reconstruct specific learning sequences, tailoring educational support to an individual's developmental level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:03">[01:16:03]</a>.

### Leadership and Business Development

Lectica's assessments have been applied to leadership development, particularly within intelligence agencies and businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:10">[01:18:10]</a>. A key finding is the "complexity gap" between the demands of leadership roles and the capacities of leaders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:22">[01:19:22]</a>. Struggles often emerge in domains of perspective-taking, perspective-seeking, and perspective-integration, rather than solely in technical problem-solving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:54">[01:19:54]</a>. The assessments provide diagnostic reports for leaders, promoting [[the_role_of_psychotechnologies_in_cognitive_transformation | leadership development]] rather than just hiring/firing decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:06">[01:24:06]</a>.

### [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | Human Development and Societal Evolution]]

The evolution of societies can be viewed through the lens of requisite complexity needed for social coordination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:42">[00:52:42]</a>. Modern [[cultural_and_social_influences_on_thought_processes | civilization]] has become increasingly complex, placing greater educational demands on individuals and requiring higher levels of [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] from citizens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:27">[00:56:27]</a>. This is driven by factors like the demands of legal processes (e.g., reflectively consenting to governance) and the proliferation of technology, especially the internet, which presents a significant influx of signals and complexity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:53">[00:56:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:17">[00:57:17]</a>.

## Important Distinctions and Warnings

*   **Task vs. Person Assessment**: It is crucial not to classify an entire person by a single [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:18">[00:59:18]</a>. Instead, [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] should be assigned to specific tasks or performances accomplished by a person within a domain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:23">[00:59:23]</a>. An individual can operate at different levels of complexity across various domains (e.g., highly complex in physics but sensory-motor in car repair) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:32">[00:34:32]</a>.
*   **Critique of General Intelligence (IQ)**: The concept of "general intelligence" or Spearman's g is seen as a "demi-reality" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:35">[01:00:35]</a>. While IQ tests correlate with [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] in specific domains, they radically oversimplify human intelligence, inviting misuse and problematic categorization of people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:29">[01:00:29]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:53">[01:00:53]</a>.
*   **Misapplication to Cultural Evolution**: Applying [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]] directly to the [[evolving_culture_and_evolutionary_dynamics | evolution of cultures]] (e.g., suggesting indigenous people are "like children" compared to modern people) is a misapplication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:03">[00:55:03]</a>. While historical documents can be analyzed for their [[hierarchical_complexity | hierarchical complexity]], classifying entire cultures or historical periods is problematic and risks reinforcing inaccurate stereotypes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:34">[00:55:34]</a>.
*   **Politics of Quantitative Objectivity**: The use of metrics and psychometrics in fields like leadership development must be carefully managed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:45">[01:25:45]</a>. Measures can be misused as a "bludgeon" or distort social practice if not embedded in appropriate educational practices and used responsibly by trained individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:22">[01:26:22]</a>.
