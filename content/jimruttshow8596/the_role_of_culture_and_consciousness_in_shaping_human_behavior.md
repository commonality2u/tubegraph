## Culture
Culture, as defined by Heather Heying and Bret Weinstein, is the application of solutions to circumstances that, when effective, become refined and driven into a more automatic, less deliberative layer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:54">[00:55:54]</a>. It represents accepted orthodoxy at the population level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:08">[00:57:08]</a>.

Culture functions as an epigenetic regulator, meaning it is "above the gene layer" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:37">[01:17:37]</a>. This means it can modulate gene expression and represents a form of evolution that is faster and more flexible than genetic evolution, with [[cultural_and_social_influences_on_thought_processes | cultural change]] occurring within generations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:07">[01:18:07]</a>. Despite its speed and lability, [[cultural_and_social_influences_on_thought_processes | culture]] inherently serves the genome's interests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:18">[01:18:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:41">[01:18:41]</a>. Anything that has persisted, stood the test of time, and is complex and variable is considered an evolutionary adaptation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:01">[01:19:01]</a>.

Culture, by its nature, is backward-looking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:25">[00:58:25]</a>.
