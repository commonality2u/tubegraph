Ethics, as a field of philosophy, fundamentally explores how to make "good choices" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:57">[00:57:57]</a>. According to philosopher Forrest Landry, ethical thinking is "inherently practical in a very deep way" because it helps individuals make "good effective choices" that yield personal, interpersonal, and global benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:18:20">[09:18:20]</a>. This focus on "effective choice" is central to understanding the role of ethics in shaping [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | societal evolution]] and navigating the challenges brought by [[technological_evolution_and_its_impact | technological advancement]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:06">[01:42:06]</a>.

## Defining Non-Relativistic Ethics
Landry's concept of non-relativistic ethics posits that universal principles exist for making effective choices, independent of specific situations or individual choosers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:05:00">[05:05:00]</a>. This contrasts with relative ethics, where what is right depends on the specific context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:33:00">[04:33:00]</a>.

Traditional ethical philosophy is broadly divided into three domains:
*   **Consequentialist ethics:** Evaluates actions based on their results, with utilitarianism (greatest happiness for the greatest number) as a key example <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>.
*   **Deontological ethics:** Based on reason or universal morality, like Kant's categorical imperative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:00">[02:22:00]</a>.
*   **Virtue ethics:** Focuses on right behavior from an internal perspective, emphasizing character traits like courage, truthfulness, or temperance, famously propagated by Aristotle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:00">[02:44:00]</a>.

Landry's non-relativistic ethics integrates aspects of all three, starting with a values-based orientation (virtue ethics), moving through a foundational perspective (deontological), and considering outcomes in terms of "integrity" (consequentialist) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:27:00">[08:27:00]</a>.

## Ethics vs. Morality
Landry carefully distinguishes between ethics and morality:
*   **Ethics:** Refers to the universal principles of choice that guide a "self" capable of participating in many different "worlds" or contexts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:09:00">[16:09:00]</a>. It's about how to make choices that connect directly to the notion of agency or action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:26:00">[16:26:00]</a>.
*   **Moral Codes:** Are specific rules tied to a particular "game" or "world" (e.g., rules of a sport, workplace conduct, internet communication) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:04:00">[16:04:00]</a>.

While moral codes, norms, rituals, and legal systems can be useful for [[human_development_and_societal_evolution | human development]] by reducing cognitive load and building good habits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:00">[01:08:00]</a>, systems defined in "black and white terms" can become antithetical to life and consciousness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:18">[01:06:18]</a>. These constructs are man-made and should be re-examined and changed if they no longer serve their purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:00">[01:25:00]</a>. The focus should be on the underlying ethical principles rather than rigid rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:00">[01:12:00]</a>.

## Key Concepts for Effective Choice
Forrest Landry identifies several intertwined concepts critical to effective choice and societal progress:

### Integrity
Integrity means "to act as one together," implying coherency, cooperativeness, and diversity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:47">[00:36:47]</a>. An effective choice should simultaneously preserve integrity and increase the potentiality of both life and evolution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:33">[00:34:33]</a>. This applies to the self's integrity, the world's integrity as affected by choices, and the integrity of the communication channel between self and reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:50">[00:37:50]</a>.

### Potentiality, Symmetry, and Continuity
Maximizing potentiality and integrity involves maximizing the combination of symmetry and continuity in the relationship between the subjective self and objective reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:17">[00:39:17]</a>.
*   **Symmetry:** Refers to the correspondence between what is at one end of a communication channel (subjective) and the other (objective) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:07">[00:41:07]</a>. A lack of symmetry degrades integrity, like garbled communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:41">[00:40:41]</a>.
*   **Continuity:** Relates to the smoothness of interaction and the consistent energy flow through the channel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:00">[00:43:00]</a>. Abrupt shifts in energy or context can break the channel's integrity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:10">[00:44:10]</a>.

These concepts are crucial for high-integrity communication, which in turn fosters effective choices and overall progress.

### Value, Meaning, and Purpose
These three concepts are distinct but inseparable and non-interchangeable, meaning contemplating one implicitly involves the other two <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:49">[00:50:49]</a>.
*   **Purpose:** An external ascription of function (e.g., a toaster's purpose is to make toast) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:01">[00:55:01]</a>.
*   **Value:** Intrinsic worth, independent of external use (e.g., a toaster made of solid gold is valuable regardless of its toasting function) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:52">[00:56:52]</a>.
*   **Meaningfulness:** Relates to the dynamic of the relationship between self and reality, often involving narrative or connotative elements beyond mere function or intrinsic worth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:52">[00:57:52]</a>. It serves as the grounding for both value and purpose <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:00">[00:52:00]</a>.

A "meaning crisis" in society may stem from an [[impact_of_technology_on_societal_values_and_behaviors | over-engagement with technology]] and virtualization, which intermediates physical contact and shared context, leading to feelings of being ungrounded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:26">[01:03:26]</a>. This impacts the ability to make good life choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:20">[01:04:20]</a>.

### Responsibility and Communication Rights
Individuals are responsible for the "totality of their choices and expressions," but not for their perceptions or knowing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:46">[01:23:46]</a>. This means thought crime should not be considered unethical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:19">[01:26:19]</a>.

Effective communication, crucial for societal function and [[ethics_practices_and_the_future_trajectory_of_humanity | ethical interaction]], is facilitated when participants grant each other three rights:
1.  The right to speak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:45">[01:26:45]</a>.
2.  The right to be understood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:46">[01:26:46]</a>.
3.  The right to know that one has been understood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:48">[01:26:48]</a>.

These rights form a "handshake" of mutual granting, enabling error correction and preventing miscommunication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:16">[01:27:16]</a>. Assuming others are sending a signal and working in good faith to understand it (mistake theory rather than conflict theory) is vital for high-generative conversation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:55">[01:29:55]</a>.
