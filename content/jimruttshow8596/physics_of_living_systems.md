## Origins of Life

Eric Smith became interested in the origin of life by looking at dynamical phase transitions, which he thought could be a good way to understand how biology works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>. He collaborated with Harold Morowitz, who had spent 40 years in biophysics and physiology and sought laws to explain "signatures of the biosphere" that appeared accidental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>. Morowitz focused on the simple core of biology, with few enough boundary conditions and components to apply [[Complexity and emergence in biological systems | laws of self-organization]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>.

### Metabolism First vs. Control First

The "metabolism first" perspective was a response to the "control first" or "RNA world" hypothesis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:27">[00:09:27]</a>.
*   **Control First**: This view, influenced by Francis Crick's central dogma (DNA to RNA to proteins), attempts to trace this top-down control flow back to the origin of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>. It places a heavy burden on RNA molecules to be both genotype and phenotype, which is chemically challenging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>.
*   **Metabolism First**: This perspective suggests that the laws of small molecule biochemistry were inherited from regularities in geochemistry that existed before the biosphere formed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>. It views the [[Emergence of Life and Complex Systems | emergence of life]] as a hierarchy and cascade of events, where increasingly organized states of geochemistry, centered around paths of least resistance, set a template for biochemistry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:22">[00:11:22]</a>. This template then allowed control structures like genes and the central dogma to emerge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:54">[00:11:54]</a>.

### The Fourth Geosphere

The concept of the "fourth geosphere" comes from Vladimir Vernadsky's idea of geospheres <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:11">[00:12:11]</a>. Vernadsky broke down planetary complexity into three central systems:
1.  **Lithosphere**: The solid core or rocky part of the planet, defined by solid-state chemistry and physics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:54">[00:12:54]</a>.
2.  **Hydrosphere**: The liquid ocean, characterized by the chemistry possible in water <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:59">[00:12:59]</a>.
3.  **Atmosphere**: The gas phase, directly coupled to stellar irradiance, with distinct chemistry and states of matter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>.

Harold Morowitz's insight was to view the **biosphere** as a fourth geosphere, a new and dynamically defined state of matter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:52">[00:13:52]</a>. Its rules are distinct, and it is more integrated by its internal processes than by its exchanges with the other geospheres <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:00">[00:14:00]</a>. The origin of life is understood as the [[Emergence of Life and Complex Systems | emergence of a biosphere]], a transition of systems rather than something contained within individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:18">[00:15:18]</a>.

### Location and Chemistry of Origin

The emergence of the biosphere likely stemmed from chemical organization at rock-water interfaces, driven by electron transfer under voltage differences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</a>. Subsurface water alteration zones are considered excellent places for planetary chemistry and providing electron transfer potentials <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:37">[00:14:37]</a>. The Earth acts as a battery, with electron flow from the deep bulk into the atmosphere and then into space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:33">[00:17:33]</a>. This flow dominantly occurs at spreading centers beneath ocean basins where new crust forms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:31">[00:18:31]</a>. This is a mild, low-energy chemistry, closer to biochemistry than violent UV chemistry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:54">[00:18:54]</a>. Low-energy chemistry necessitates catalysts for reactions to occur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:10">[00:19:10]</a>.

Key chemical organizing centers in biology:
*   **Citric Acid Cycle**: A small system of eleven organic acids (the largest having six carbon atoms) that serves as the foundation for all biochemistry and carbon fixation on Earth today <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:39">[00:19:39]</a>.
*   **Acetic Acid (Vinegar)**: The central molecule for carbon fixation and the deepest part of the biosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:21">[00:20:21]</a>.
*   **Pyruvic Acid**: Looks like the central molecule for the beginning of everything complicated related to amino acids <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:49">[00:20:49]</a>.

These serve as circumstantial evidence to guide the search for small molecule chemistry in the origin of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:04">[00:21:04]</a>.

### Autocatalysis

[[Emergence of Life and Complex Systems | Autocatalysis]] is crucial for the dynamic stability of biological systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:42">[00:21:42]</a>. Since biological patterns constantly degrade, they must continually renew themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:44">[00:21:44]</a>. Autocatalysis takes diverse environmental material and concentrates it into specific outputs, which then facilitate the conversion of more environmental components into the same material, thus rebuilding the system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:31">[00:22:31]</a>. Biochemistry is replete with autocatalytic cycles that maintain its integration and purity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:01">[00:23:01]</a>.

Eric Smith contrasts this with Stu Kauffman's approach, which starts from unstructured, combinatorial media (like Boolean networks) to find feedbacks via laws of large numbers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:13">[00:23:13]</a>. Smith emphasizes that biochemistry itself is a highly structured medium, and the challenge is to understand collective effects and natural feedbacks within this inherent structure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:36">[00:23:36]</a>.

### [[Complex Systems and Thermodynamics | Thermodynamics]] and Energy Flux

Living systems are "far from equilibrium" and require energy flow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:15">[00:24:15]</a>. While energy flow is important, it is one of many flows, and the concept of [[Complex Systems and Thermodynamics | self-organization]] is broader <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:06">[00:25:06]</a>. In [[relationship between thermodynamics and life processes | thermodynamics]], quantities like energy, material composition, and volume define the available state space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:29">[00:25:29]</a>. Moving away from equilibrium means coupling a system to boundary conditions that are not in equilibrium, allowing energy to flow through <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:58">[00:25:58]</a>.

> "It's kind of like Tolstoy, all equilibrium systems are more or less alike, every disequilibrium system is disequilibrium in its own fashion." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:47">[00:26:47]</a>

A driven system has more information in its boundary conditions, which allows it to be more ordered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:30">[00:26:30]</a>. Harold Morowitz proposed that the flow of energy through a system tends to organize it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:15">[00:27:15]</a>. Smith argues that a more radical approach is needed, moving beyond traditional Prigogine-style dissipative systems to consider the "thermodynamics of rule-based systems" and chemical reaction networks as systems in their own right <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:57">[00:27:57]</a>.

### Knowns and Unknowns in Origin of Life Research

Current understanding of the origin of life is characterized by "little islands" of knowledge within vast unknown areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:56">[00:28:56]</a>.
*   **Core Biochemistry**: Extensive information exists about carbon fixation pathways, the citric acid cycle, and reaction usage, suggesting an extension of the periodic table expressed by the biosphere's rigidity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:09">[00:29:09]</a>.
*   **Ribosome and Translation**: The ribosome holds extraordinary historical information about RNA, early peptides, and the [[Emergence of Life and Complex Systems | emergence of translation]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:32">[00:29:32]</a>. We have "the answer to a final exam but don't know the questions" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:50">[00:29:50]</a>.
*   **Genetic Code**: Traditionally viewed as an assignment problem, Smith suggests it's a dynamic process integrated with energetic activation and protein folding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:19">[00:30:19]</a>. The genetic code records how different systems (like RNA and peptides) bootstrapped themselves, hitting walls until innovations in one system opened doors for the others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:57">[00:30:57]</a>.

### The Error Catastrophe and High-Fidelity Information Transfer

A major puzzle is how chemical evolution, prior to high-fidelity information transfer systems like DNA with its error correction machinery, could have "pumped up" to create such complex machinery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:43">[00:32:43]</a>. This is related to the "error catastrophe" in evolutionary computation, where evolution cannot produce much if information fidelity is below a certain threshold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:09">[00:32:09]</a>.

According to Herb Simon's arguments on [[Complexity and emergence in biological systems | complex systems]] architecture:
*   Error correction is local <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:27">[00:33:27]</a>.
*   Complex systems are likely created through mechanisms with integral error correction in subsystems, leading to stable intermediate states of assembly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:59">[00:33:59]</a>. (e.g., assembling watch components in stages rather than all at once) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:21">[00:34:21]</a>.
*   In the origin of life, this means seeking where chemistry tends to regress toward its own central tendencies, reducing the need for constant external control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:03">[00:35:03]</a>.

One example of a pre-DNA error correction mechanism is **substrate-assisted catalysis** in RNA and DNA reactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:12">[00:36:12]</a>. Here, the RNA/DNA molecule itself defines most of the catalytic context, requiring less complex surrounding catalysts to function <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:25">[00:36:25]</a>. This simpler catalysis could have enabled the memory systems to become more complicated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:57">[00:36:57]</a>.

### Frontier Research

The next frontier in origin of life research is learning how to explore chemistry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:12">[00:37:12]</a>. Chemistry is a vast combinatorial system, and current methods are often ad-hoc <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:17">[00:37:17]</a>. New methods are needed to systematically search and reason about this complex space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:51">[00:37:51]</a>. While deep learning shows promise for tasks like pathway discovery and optimization in chemistry, it has yet to be fully explored <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:33">[00:38:33]</a>.

Current work continues at the interface of organized geochemistry and the deep origins of biology, focusing more on the folding of biopolymers and re-examining the genetic code <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:59">[01:23:59]</a>. A key question is understanding how systems capable of carrying large amounts of information become available without being obscured by the heterogeneity of their physical material <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:16">[01:26:16]</a>. This requires understanding "linchpin steps" that are not combinatorial but create narrow bottlenecks, rejecting enough configurational entropy to allow for a large, flat, information-carrying space to emerge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:36">[01:25:36]</a>. This is a productive interface between physics and biology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:29">[01:26:29]</a>.
