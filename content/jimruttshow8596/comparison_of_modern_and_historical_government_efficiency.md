## Historical Perspectives on Governance and Incompetence

When examining history, it's common to view past periods, like the Old Kingdom of Egypt, as intellectually unserious, where beliefs such as a giant dung beetle pushing the sun were prevalent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:00">[01:54:00]</a>. While all historical periods deserve respect, they are not intellectually equal; for instance, the intellectual achievements of the Italian Renaissance are generally respected more than those of Late Antiquity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:00">[02:59:00]</a>.

### Late Roman Empire: A Parallel to Modern Dysfunction?

A compelling parallel is drawn between contemporary society and Late Antiquity, particularly the Late Roman Empire. Scholars of this period failed to recognize their empire's decline, believing everything was "perfectly fine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:34:00">[07:34:00]</a>. This era was characterized by a system built on connections, a pretentious intellectual tradition of "grammarians" who considered their work equal to Virgil's, and a pervasive tone of flattery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:53:00">[07:53:00]</a>. Historical figures like Sidonius, a politically influential writer, focused on mundane flattery in their letters rather than addressing pressing issues like the "fall of the Roman Empire," even when roads became dangerous <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:48:00">[08:48:00]</a>. This detachment from reality ultimately contributed to the society's collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:43:00">[09:43:00]</a>.

### Temporal Chauvinism and Mythos

The concept of "temporal chauvinism," or presentism, suggests an assumption that one's own world is completely real and deserving of immense respect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:16:00">[07:16:00]</a>. This prevents an objective assessment of current societal conditions, as seen in the Victorians, who, despite their perceived flaws, would likely find modern society problematic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:13:00">[06:13:00]</a>.

This relates to the idea of a "mythos," a set of beliefs widely accepted by a society at any given time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:33:00">[10:33:00]</a>. The "political formula," a term from the Italian School of political science (notably Gaetano Mosca), is the element of this mythos that makes people believe the government is good, right, and legitimate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:55:00">[11:55:00]</a>. In modern times, this formula often makes individuals feel powerful and important by supporting the government <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:33:00">[12:33:00]</a>.

### The Unpredictability of Collapse

The fall of the Soviet Union serves as a stark example of how unexpected systemic collapse can be; neither dissidents nor Cold Warriors predicted its demise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:13:00">[15:13:00]</a>. This suggests that even seemingly stable systems can rapidly unravel.

## Modern Government Inefficiency: The Stupidity Quotient

The term "Stupidity Quotient" (SQ) is introduced as a lens to evaluate modern governance by asking how a six-year-old would perceive decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:16:00">[18:16:00]</a>.

### Case Study: COVID-19 Response

*   **Initial Decisions**: A six-year-old would instinctively stop international flights during a pandemic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:24:00">[19:24:00]</a>. Yet, the US government, aware of human-to-human transmission, avoided disrupting travel and trade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:56:00">[19:56:00]</a>.
*   **Incompetence**: The US response to COVID-19 revealed "complete incompetence" compared to other countries, particularly Confucian nations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:50:00">[09:50:00]</a>. The shock of this incompetence may have increased awareness that "the emperor is wearing no clothes" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:56:00">[14:56:00]</a>.
*   **Lab Leak Theory**: The pandemic's origin is plausibly linked to a lab leak from the Wuhan Institute of Virology, where "gain-of-function" experiments were conducted with American funding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:47:00">[25:47:00]</a>. This suggests the pandemic may have originated from a "DC self-licking ice cream cone" of research for research's sake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:47:00">[28:47:00]</a>.
*   **Vaccine Approval**: The slow FDA approval process for COVID-19 vaccines, though ten times faster than usual, is an example of inefficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:30:00">[31:30:00]</a>. A comparison is made to a renegade virologist who self-administered a vaccine in April <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:46:00">[31:46:00]</a>.
    *   The [[Principles of Good Governance | Hippocratic Oath]] is presented as a "mythos" that contributes to risk-averse institutional behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:26:00">[36:26:00]</a>. Institutions prioritize maintaining their prestige by avoiding "positive mistakes" (e.g., vaccine side effects) over preventing large-scale "negative mistakes" (e.g., deaths from delayed vaccines) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:07:00">[39:07:00]</a>. This highlights a fundamental conflict of interest where institutional self-preservation outweighs public interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:07:00">[39:07:00]</a>.
*   **Lockdown Strategy**: The West's adoption of lockdowns differed from China's aggressive "test, trace, and isolate" strategy, which aggressively quarantined positive cases and contacts to halt transmission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:21:00">[45:21:00]</a>. The World Health Organization (WHO) initially advised against quarantines due to influence from the hospitality industry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:04:00">[46:04:00]</a>.
    *   American institutions had an "overstated" state capacity to implement effective measures like test-trace-isolate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:45:00">[47:47:00]</a>. The US government's digital infrastructure for citizenship is described as a "shambles," unable to even accurately count its population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:12:00">[50:12:00]</a>. This contrasts with "seeing like a state" as described by James C. Scott <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:47:00">[51:47:00]</a>, noting that the American system has an inherent "anarchy built into them" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:11:00">[52:11:00]</a>, limiting its ability to control disease or crime.
    *   The "hammer and the dance" strategy, effective for agile governments like Taiwan's, was misapplied to the US, which could not "dance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:47:00">[49:47:00]</a>. This led to a "permanent war mentality" against the virus, accepting not winning as long as losses weren't worse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:25:00">[54:25:00]</a>.

## Shift from Monarchy to Oligarchy

Historically, the US government, exemplified by its efficiency in World War II projects, operated differently.

*   **The Manhattan Project**: This was one of the most successful large engineering projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:31:00">[55:31:00]</a>, run like a startup <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:16:00">[56:16:00]</a> with "two-in-a-box leadership" (technical and management) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:11:00">[59:11:00]</a>. It was a top-down pyramid structure where scientists were "ordered" what to work on, a practice considered demeaning in today's academic environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:52:00">[59:52:00]</a>. If the Manhattan Project were attempted today, it would devolve into a bureaucratic grant-writing exercise, delaying results by decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="57:22:00">[57:22:00]</a>.
*   **FDR's De Facto Monarchy**: The efficacy of the Manhattan Project was rooted in the broader context of 1930s Washington D.C., which functioned as a "de facto monarchy" under Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:51">[01:01:51]</a>. FDR wielded near-absolute power, creating and destroying agencies at will <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:52">[01:01:52]</a>. His 1933 inaugural address showcased this monarchical tone, demanding discipline and temporary extraordinary powers akin to wartime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:42">[01:05:42]</a>.
*   **Modern Oligarchy**: In contrast, modern science and indeed most institutions are run as [[Power dynamics and the structure of governance | oligarchies]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:47">[00:59:47]</a>. This means decisions are made by a few individuals within a process loop, prioritizing internal bureaucratic loyalty and procedural outcomes over actual results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:25">[00:33:25]</a>. This lack of clear accountability, where no one is fired for failures (e.g., CDC tests), marks a fundamental difference from the "monarchical" accountability seen in private companies or during periods like the Manhattan Project <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:00">[01:04:00]</a>.

This comparison highlights a perceived shift in the nature of American governance from a more centralized, results-oriented (though de facto monarchical) structure to a less efficient, self-serving oligarchy, impacting its ability to respond effectively to crises.
