The World Wide Web is considered a critical pillar of human civilization, with John Perry Barlow envisioning it as "cyberspace, the new home of the Mind" where anyone can express their beliefs without fear of coercion or silence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>. However, an existential threat to this openness has emerged due to the consolidation of power in web browsers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>.

## Current Market Landscape

Users access cyberspace primarily through web browsers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. Currently, Chrome, Safari, Edge, and Firefox collectively account for over 90% of the browser market share <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. A significant concern is that all these dominant browsers either share technology or receive funding controlled by Google <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>. This includes even privacy-focused browsers like Brave, which is built on top of Google's Chromium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.

## Risks of Centralized Control

The concentration of control in a single entity like Google poses a hypothetical, but significant, risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>. The saying, "he who controls the browser controls the Internet," highlights this power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. If such a dominant entity were to act maliciously, it could theoretically exert immense control over online content and functionality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>.

The [[importance_and_impact_of_browser_competition | consolidation of power]] on the internet is an ongoing concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>.

## The Browser Wars and New Entrants

Despite this dominance, the "browser wars" are actively raging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:59">[00:00:59]</a>. There are ongoing efforts in the [[development_of_open_source_web_browsers | open-source browser]] space to challenge this status quo. Examples include:
*   **Firefox** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:01">[00:01:01]</a>
*   **Arc browser** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:05">[00:01:05]</a>
*   **Verso**, built with the Servo engine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>
*   **Blitz**, a minimal web renderer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>
*   **Ladybird**, a futuristic open-source browser that emerged from Serenity OS <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>. Ladybird aims to be entirely free and not borrow code from other browsers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>. Its development is a challenging undertaking, given the complexity of adhering to the vast and ever-evolving W3C specifications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>.

::info
Developing a new web browser from scratch is an extremely complex undertaking, often deemed impossible by general consensus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. Browser engines must adhere to the W3C specification, which contains over 4 million words and constantly evolves with new features, raising the barrier to entry significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>. This highlights the [[challenges_in_creating_a_new_web_browser | challenges in creating a new web browser]].
::

Regardless of the market share gained by new browsers, the ongoing efforts to create independent alternatives are beneficial for the overall health and openness of the internet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
