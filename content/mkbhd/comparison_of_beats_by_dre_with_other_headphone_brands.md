## [[profit_margins_and_pricing_strategies_in_the_headphone_industry | Profit Margins and Pricing Strategies]]

All products have margins, which is the difference between the physical manufacturing cost and the retail price <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>. This gap covers additional costs like research, advertising, and profit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>.

### Comparison with Audio Technica M50s

Audio Technica's M50s, for example, primarily allocate their margins to research, testing, and packaging, with minimal advertising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:55">[00:01:55]</a>. This focus on internal development and less on marketing is why many people may not have heard of Audio Technica <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. For consumers prioritizing sound quality, accuracy, and frequency response, the Audio Technica M50s are considered a superior choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:44">[00:05:44]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>.

### Comparison with Samsung Galaxy S5

As a general example of profit margins, the Samsung Galaxy S5 cost approximately $250 in parts to manufacture but retailed for around $600. This difference covered Samsung's costs in research and advertising, in addition to profit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>. This illustrates how companies like Samsung, similar to Beats, factor in significant non-manufacturing costs into their retail pricing.
