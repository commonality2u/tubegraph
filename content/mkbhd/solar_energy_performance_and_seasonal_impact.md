After a year of owning a solar system, including a Tesla solar roof and Tesla Powerwalls, the experience has provided significant insight into solar energy performance across all four seasons. ## Understanding Solar Integration

A typical home is connected to the electrical grid, pulling electricity and spinning a meter forward as power is consumed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>.

When solar panels are added, they can directly power the house during the day when the sun shines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>. If demand exceeds solar supply or if it's cloudy, additional power is pulled from the grid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. Crucially, as soon as the sun sets, solar generation drops to zero, requiring reliance on the grid for evening and nighttime use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:03">[00:02:03]</a>.

### Achieving Zero Electricity Bill with [[net_metering_and_electricity_bill_savings | Net Metering]]

Many power companies offer [[net_metering_and_electricity_bill_savings | net metering]], which allows excess electricity generated during the day (e.g., when no one is home) to be sent back to the grid, spinning the electrical meter backward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. When the sun goes down and household consumption increases, electricity is pulled from the grid, spinning the meter forward. The goal is a net zero usage over a billing cycle, leading to a $0 electricity bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.

### Grid Independence with [[battery_storage_and_grid_independence_with_tesla_powerwall | Battery Storage]]

To achieve complete grid independence, or "off-the-grid" living, [[battery_storage_and_grid_independence_with_tesla_powerwall | batteries]] are essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>. When the sun shines and the house isn't consuming all generated power, the excess fills the batteries, effectively storing solar energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>. When the sun sets, the house can continue to draw power from the stored battery energy until the sun rises again the next morning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:29">[00:03:29]</a>. This system theoretically allows for a $0 power bill and never pulling from the grid, providing self-sustainability and protection against power outages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>.

## [[performance_and_limitations | Seasonal Performance and Limitations]]

The system was activated in July, allowing for observation across all four seasons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.

### Summer (July - October)
Summer days in New Jersey are excellent for solar, with long days and abundant sun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a>. A typical sunny summer day can generate around 200 kWh, with peak production around noon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. The highest single-day generation recorded was almost 300 kWh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:31">[00:12:31]</a>. Even with high usage from AC and daily car charging (often 200+ kWh/day), net grid usage was typically zero to slightly negative, sometimes selling back up to 50 kWh more than consumed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:49">[00:12:49]</a>.

### Fall (October - December)
Days shorten, but temperatures drop, leading to less air conditioning use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:04">[00:13:04]</a>. Daily production drops to the low hundreds of kWh. A typical fall day could generate over 120 kWh, with home needs entirely covered by solar, and the Powerwalls taking over after sunset without touching the grid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:40">[00:13:40]</a>. Fall and spring were the periods of most excess electricity generation, consistently ending with net negative grid usage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:50">[00:13:50]</a>.

### Winter (December - March)
Winter in the Northeast is particularly challenging for solar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:03">[00:14:03]</a>.
*   **Short Days:** Sun sets as early as 4:00 PM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:13">[00:14:13]</a>.
*   **Cloud Cover:** Most days are cloudy, significantly reducing solar generation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:24">[00:14:24]</a>. Typical generation ranges from 20-70 kWh per day, with cloudy days generating only a handful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:30">[00:15:30]</a>.
*   **Heating:** Home heating is natural gas, so solar cannot offset this cost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:43">[00:14:43]</a>.
*   **EV Efficiency:** Electric cars are less efficient in cold weather, consuming more energy to maintain optimal battery temperature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:53">[00:14:53]</a>. A commute taking 15% battery in summer could take 20-30% in winter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:09">[00:15:09]</a>.

During winter, there were positive net grid usage months (November, December, January), meaning more electricity was pulled from the grid than produced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:14">[00:16:14]</a>.

### Spring (March - June)
Performance rapidly improves in spring <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:26">[00:16:26]</a>. May was the month with the most excess solar generation, with almost every day being sunny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:53">[00:16:53]</a>. In May, 6,500 kWh were used, but nearly 8,500 kWh were generated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:44">[00:16:44]</a>. June and July, despite longer days, had more thunderstorms and cloud cover, which reduced total solar output compared to May <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:08">[00:17:08]</a>.

## Miscellaneous Quirk: Snow Melting on Solar Tiles

The surface of the [[comparison_of_solar_tiles_versus_traditional_solar_panels | Tesla solar tiles]] stays slightly above freezing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:07">[00:25:07]</a>. When it snows lightly, the snow melts and does not accumulate, allowing the solar panels to continue working <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:20">[00:25:20]</a>. In heavy snowfall, snow accumulates, but the bottom layer warms up, creating a thin layer of water that causes the entire accumulated snow to slide off the roof in large sheets of ice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:39">[00:25:39]</a>. This can result in large piles of snow around the house, sometimes blocking doors or driveways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:16">[00:26:16]</a>.

## Conclusion

Having a solar system is an investment in bleeding-edge technology that is still rapidly evolving and expensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:15">[00:27:15]</a>. The total cost of the system, including materials, labor, installation, and three batteries, was $122,988.94 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:09">[00:19:09]</a>. However, a federal tax credit of nearly $30,000 brought the final cost down to around $93,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:14">[00:19:14]</a>.

Based on an average electricity cost, the 54 MWh (54,000 kWh) of electricity used in the past year would have cost approximately $9,600 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a>. Dividing the total cost by the annual savings, the estimated payoff period is just under 10 years (9.6 years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:45">[00:20:45]</a>. This falls within the typical 6-10 year payback period for solar setups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:53">[00:20:53]</a>. Given that panels are typically warrantied for 25 years, the system is expected to generate free electricity for 15 years after the payoff period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:03">[00:21:03]</a>.

A "zero power bill" is largely achieved due to [[net_metering_and_electricity_bill_savings | net metering]] credits rolling over monthly, even through positive net grid usage months in winter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:06">[00:23:06]</a>. However, a fixed monthly fee (e.g., $5.75) for simply having an account means the bill is never truly zero <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:55">[00:26:55]</a>.

Power outages become unnoticeable with a battery-backed solar system; the Tesla app notifies the user of grid disconnection while the house seamlessly runs on solar and batteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:02">[00:24:02]</a>. Features like "storm watch" ensure batteries are fully charged before anticipated outages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:29">[00:24:29]</a>.

The ability for electric vehicles to support bidirectional charging, potentially serving as a home's backup battery, could revolutionize future solar setups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:40">[00:28:40]</a>. Car batteries are significantly larger than Powerwalls (e.g., a Tesla Model S battery is equivalent to 7-8 Powerwalls) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:01">[00:29:01]</a>. Vehicles like the F-150 Lightning already support this feature <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:11">[00:29:11]</a>. In the future, a small solar array, an inverter, and a car battery might be all that's needed for home power needs during outages or emergencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:31">[00:29:31]</a>.

Ultimately, investing in sustainable energy technology like solar helps reduce strain on the electrical grid and contributes to clean energy usage, benefiting everyone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:58">[00:29:58]</a>.
