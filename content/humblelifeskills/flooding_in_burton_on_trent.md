Recent observations in Burton on Trent indicate significant flooding, particularly around the River Trent. The river has "broke Banks big time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>, leading to widespread submersion of surrounding areas.

## Affected Areas and Infrastructure
The flooding impacts several key locations and structures:
*   **New Bridge** The area around the New Bridge is noted as being completely flooded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>.
*   **Ferry Bridge** An 18th-century [[burton_on_trent_historic_bridges | bridge]] known as the Ferry Bridge is nearly submerged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>.
*   **Washlands** This area is specifically referred to as "the washlands" because it "just gets flooded all the time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. The water level across the Washlands is notably high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>.
*   **Staple Gardens** Visible in the distance, Staple Gardens is also affected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.
*   **Residential and Public Spaces** A garden is completely flooded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>, and railings that are normally walkable are submerged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>. A bin is also seen underwater <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.

The water is described as "pretty damn flooded" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a> and flowing "pretty fast" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a> with a "big swirl" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>, indicating a strong current. The phrase "keep getting more rain like this" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a> suggests recent [[effects_of_weather_patterns_on_water_levels | weather patterns]] are contributing to the high water levels. A reference to the "1880s 89" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a> is made, possibly alluding to historical flood events or benchmarks for water levels. The consistent flooding of the Washlands highlights the area's natural susceptibility to high water.
