## Geographic and Demographic Overview

Greenland is located between the Atlantic and Arctic oceans, with Canada's Ellesmere Island just 26 km (16 miles) to its west and Iceland 740 km (460 miles) to its east <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. Spanning 2.2 million square km (840,000 square miles), it is the world's largest island <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>. The island's population is approximately 56,000, with most inhabitants residing along coastal regions like the capital, Nuuk, as 80% of the country is covered in ice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>. Nearly 90% of the population is of native Inuit origin, with the remainder being Danish or mixed heritage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.

## [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | History and Political Status of Greenland]]

[[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland's history]] began around 4,500 years ago with the arrival of the first settlers from North America, followed by the ancestors of the modern Inuit population in the 11th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:36">[00:02:36]</a>. Norse settlers also arrived from Iceland around the same time but disappeared by the 15th century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.

Danish presence began in the early 18th century, leading to [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland]] becoming part of the joint state of Denmark-Norway <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. When this union ended in 1814 with the Treaty of Kiel, Denmark retained control over [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>.

During the Second World War, following Denmark's fall to Nazi Germany in 1941, the United States oversaw the territory for several years and established an important airbase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. Although Denmark regained control after the war, the U.S. base remained, serving as a vital intelligence, early warning, and refueling facility throughout the Cold War <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>.

[[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland's colonial status]] was abolished in 1953, and it formally became a Danish province <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>. In 1979, it was granted self-rule, and by 2009, local power expanded significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenlanders]] now control most aspects of their governance, including health, education, natural resources, and the local economy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>. Denmark continues to manage foreign affairs, security, and defense, providing approximately a fifth of [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland's]] income with a yearly grant of about half a billion USD <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>. [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland]] has its own political institutions, including a prime minister, executive, and parliament <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:36">[00:04:36]</a>.

Despite close ties with Denmark, there have been growing calls for independence in recent years, with many viewing it as time for the territory to become a separate sovereign state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>. However, concerns about [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland's]] viability as an independent country remain due to its small population and economic reliance on Denmark, making independence a longer-term goal rather than an immediate priority <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>.

The [[us_interest_in_greenland_under_trump | U.S. interest]] in [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland]] is driven by several factors:
*   **Strategic Location**: As global warming causes Arctic ice to melt, [[greenlands_history_and_political_status | Greenland]] is becoming an increasingly significant point in new shipping routes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.
*   **Resource Accessibility**: Melting ice also makes previously hard-to-reach resources more accessible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:40">[00:05:40]</a>.
*   **Geopolitical Competition**: The territory is seen as vital for reigning in Russian and Chinese activities in the Arctic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:48">[00:07:48]</a>.
