## Psilocybin as a Serotonin Mimic

Psilocybin's primary neurobiological action is its similarity to serotonin. When ingested, psilocybin is converted into psilocin, a molecule that closely resembles serotonin chemically and functionally. This structural resemblance allows psilocin to bind to serotonin receptors in the brain, especially the serotonin 2A (5-HT2A) receptors, leading to its characteristic effects. ## Unique Binding to the 5-HT2A Receptor

Psilocin's selective binding to the 5-HT2A receptor is what distinguishes it from serotonin. This receptor is heavily expressed in the neocortex, especially in areas associated with perception and cognition such as the prefrontal cortex and visual cortex. The activation of these receptors is primarily responsible for the psychedelic effects of psilocybin, including visual and auditory hallucinations and altered thought processes. ## Neuroplastic Changes

Psilocybin's interaction with serotonin receptors, particularly 5-HT2A, induces significant neuroplastic changes. It increases the connectivity between different brain regions, enhancing communication across otherwise separate networks. This leads to a reduction in hierarchical processing and modular networks, potentially explaining the altered states of consciousness and enhanced creativity experienced during a psilocybin session.
