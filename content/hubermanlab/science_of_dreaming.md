## What Happens in the Brain When We Dream?

Dreams primarily occur during Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep, a unique phase of the sleep cycle characterized by intense brain activity and vivid dreams. During REM sleep, the electrical activity of the brain resembles that of an awake state, yet the body experiences muscle paralysis, a natural mechanism preventing the dreamer from acting out their dreams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:05">[01:24:05]</a>.

Dr. Walker explains that one of the hallmark features of REM sleep is the presence of PG waves (Pons-Geniculate-Occipital waves), which originate from the brain stem and broadcast up through the thalamus and out to the visual cortex. These bursts are thought to be linked to rapid eye movements and possibly to the vivid visual hallucinations often experienced in dreams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:30">[00:20:30]</a>.

## Functions of Dreaming

Dreams serve several proposed functions that are vital both from an evolutionary and a psychological perspective:

1. **Creativity and Problem-Solving:** Dreaming may enhance creativity by allowing the brain to make novel associations between disparate thoughts and concepts. This capability often results in new solutions to problems that the waking mind cannot achieve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:00">[00:35:00]</a>. This aligns with the [[role_of_subconscious_in_creativity | role of the subconscious in creativity]].

2. **Emotional Processing:** REM sleep, the stage most associated with dreaming, acts as an overnight therapy session, processing emotional experiences, and facilitating emotional regulation. This function can be particularly important in overcoming stressful or traumatic events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:00">[00:35:00]</a>. Understanding the [[role_of_emotions_in_learning | role of emotions in learning]] might further unravel connections with dreaming.

3. **Memory Integration:** While dreams aren't a straightforward replay of daily experiences, they frequently involve the integration and abstraction of recent memories with older ones, potentially aiding in the internalization and understanding of new information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:00">[00:33:00]</a>. This echoes concepts found in [[memory_and_sleep | memory and sleep studies]].

## The Uniqueness of Human Dreaming

Humans exhibit a larger proportion of REM sleep compared to other mammals. Dr. Walker notes that while most primates have about 9% of their sleep as REM sleep, humans average around 20% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>. This anomaly raises the question of whether humans have unique dreaming capabilities that enhance cognitive and emotional functioning in ways not yet fully understood.

## The Mystery of Nightmares

Nightmares, or distressing dreams that result in daytime dysfunction, present a paradox in our understanding of dreaming. It remains unclear whether nightmares serve a functional purpose, such as focusing our attention on unresolved emotional conflicts, or if they represent a malfunction in the dream and sleep process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:00">[01:02:00]</a>.
