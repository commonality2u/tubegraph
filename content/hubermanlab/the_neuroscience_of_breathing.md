Breathing, a seemingly simple and automatic process, is crucial not only for life but also for optimal organ health, focus, sleep, and stress management. Dr. Jack Feldman, a distinguished professor of neurobiology at UCLA, has pioneered research in understanding the intricate neuroscience behind breathing and its profound impact on human physiology and mental state [[neuroscience_and_brain_function | neuroscience and brain function]]. This article delves into the key aspects of how our brain regulates breathing and how different breathing patterns influence our mental and physical well-being.

## Brain Areas Controlling Breathing

Dr. Feldman identified two critical brain centers in the brainstem that are responsible for controlling various patterns of breathing: the **pre-Bötzinger complex** and the **parafacial respiratory group**.

### Pre-Bötzinger Complex

The pre-Bötzinger complex is crucial for generating rhythmic breaths that sustain life [[breathing_and_cognitive_function | breathing and cognitive function]]. Each breath begins with neurons in this region becoming active, subsequently stimulating motor neurons that trigger inspiratory muscles such as the diaphragm and intercostal muscles to work. These inspiratory efforts lead to the mechanical expansion of the lungs, allowing air to flow in. Once these neurons stop firing, the muscles relax, and a passive exhale occurs, highlighting the intrinsic rhythm of life-dependent breathing (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:12">[00:15:12]</a>).

### Parafacial Respiratory Group

Dr. Feldman's work also uncovered a second oscillator, the parafacial respiratory group, which primarily regulates active expiration, crucial during periods of increased metabolic demand such as exercise [[exercise_physiology_and_health_benefits | exercise physiology and health benefits]]. This group becomes active to boost expiratory efforts beyond passive exhalation by recruiting muscles like the internal intercostals and abdominals (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:24">[00:20:24]</a>).

## Breathing and Its Influence on Brain State

The act of breathing does more than just provide oxygen and remove carbon dioxide; it influences our brain states and emotional responses [[role_of_emotions_and_the_nervous_system | role of emotions and the nervous system]].

### Emotion Regulation

Breathing patterns can modulate brain oscillations and influence states like focus, calmness, and even stress [[breathing_techniques_to_control_stress | breathing techniques to control stress]]. For instance, the practice of deliberate breathing, like sighing, is essential for maintaining lung health and provides acute stress-modulating effects [[physiological_sighs_and_lung_health | physiological sighs and lung health]]. Feldman's research points to how frequent sighing helps re-inflate collapsed alveoli in the lungs, ensuring efficient gas exchange and reducing stress levels (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:41">[00:41:41]</a>).

### Ascending and Descending Pathways

Research by Dr. Feldman and others shows that breathing can affect brain areas like the locus coeruleus, impacting neurochemical pathways associated with arousal and mood regulation. Conversely, emotional states descending from higher brain areas like the amygdala can alter breathing patterns [[role_of_amygdala_in_emotional_processing_and_social_behavior | role of amygdala in emotional processing and social behavior]], linking brain activity with respiratory patterns in a bi-directional manner (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:01">[01:01:01]</a>).
