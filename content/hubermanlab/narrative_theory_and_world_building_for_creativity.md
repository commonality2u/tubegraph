## Introduction to Narrative Theory

Narrative theory offers an alternative to divergent thinking by relying on structured storytelling methods that can reignite the imaginative creativity typically associated with young children. Initially articulated by Aristotle in 335 BCE and later expanded upon by J.P. Guilford in the 1950s, this theory emphasizes the different intellectual capacities beyond standard IQ measurements and proposes a novel view on accessing creativity. 

## Key Concepts in Narrative Theory

### 1. **World Building**

World building is the process of creating detailed imaginary settings in which narrative events occur. It allows individuals to explore creative ideas by altering the perceived rules of the world around them. For example, imagining a world where roles are reversed, such as humans being pets to a more advanced species, can open up creative pathways by setting a new context for stories or innovations [[creative_process_and_creativity_across_domains | creative pathways in storytelling and other domains]] (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:05:04">[02:05:04]</a>).

### 2. **Perspective Shifting**

Perspective shifting involves looking at scenarios from different motivations and viewpoints. Instead of merely visualizing oneself in another's shoes, this method entails adopting their motivational frameworks. If someone is creating from the perspective of an angry person, they should explore the underlying motivation for that anger (e.g., betrayal, jealousy), which frames potential creative narratives in a more authentic manner [[role_of_subconscious_in_creativity | understanding motivational frameworks in perspective shifting]] (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:02">[02:06:02]</a>).

### 3. **Action Generating**

With action generating techniques, the focus shifts towards creating and forcing interactions, known as "creative collisions," between characters or entities with different motivations. This exercise promotes innovation as the narrative naturally unfolds from these interactions, potentially leading to unique and unforeseen narrative developments [[overcoming_creative_blocks | overcoming creative blocks through dynamic interactions]] (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:43">[02:06:43]</a>).
