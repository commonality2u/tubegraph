Breathing is a fundamental life process, supplying oxygen to the body and removing carbon dioxide. However, recent research highlights its significant role beyond mere survival, impacting cognitive function and emotional regulation. This article delves into the insights shared by Dr. Jack Feldman, an expert in the [[the_neuroscience_of_breathing | neuroscience of breathing]], as discussed in a conversation with Dr. Andrew Huberman.

## Breathing and its Broader Impacts

Dr. Feldman's work underscores that breathing affects many aspects of life beyond basic physiological needs. Different patterns of breathing, like the rate and depth, can influence how focused we are, how we regulate emotions, and how we manage stress and anxiety. These patterns can even predict how easily we transition between sleep states and awake states, emphasizing the [[breathing_patterns_during_sleep | brain-breath connection]].

## Cognitive and Emotional Regulation

### Influence on Cognitive States

Breathing has a bidirectional relationship with cognitive and emotional states. Changes in breathing can directly influence brain activity and even the processing of emotions. Research by Noam Sobel's group suggests that inhalation enhances cognitive functions, which explains why taking deep breaths before performing a task can improve focus and performance [[breathing_and_cognitive_function | breathing and cognitive function]] [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:01">[01:22:01]</a>].

### Emotional Control through Breathing

Understanding the emotive control of breathing can also offer avenues for emotional regulation. For example, individuals experiencing stress can benefit from deep breathing techniques, which may help lower anxiety levels. Dr. Feldman points out that employing conscious breath control can disrupt negative emotional circuits, offering a non-invasive approach to mood regulation [[breathing_techniques_for_stress_reduction | breathing techniques for stress reduction]] [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:55:00">[01:55:00]</a>].

### Physiological Sighs

A particularly fascinating aspect of breathing involves the **physiological sigh**, a natural double inhale followed by an extended exhale. This process is unconsciously initiated by the brain every few minutes and plays a critical role in resetting physiological states. It helps reopen collapsed alveoli in the lungs, thus maintaining efficient gas exchange and mitigating stress [[physiological_sighs_and_lung_health | physiological sighs and lung health]] [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:22">[00:38:22]</a>].

## Breathing Practices

Dr. Feldman suggests that even simple breathwork practices can have profound impacts on both cognitive function and emotional regulation. Practices like **box breathing** (a cyclical breathing pattern involving equal phases of inhalation, holding, exhalation, and holding again) can help individuals manage stress responses effectively [[breathwork_practices_and_health_benefits | breathwork practices and health benefits]] [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:01">[02:00:01]</a>].

### The Practical Application

By integrating controlled breathing into daily routines, people can enhance cognitive functions and manage emotional responses more effectively. Whether looking to improve focus or reduce anxiety, understanding and utilizing the relationship between breathing and brain function provides powerful tools for personal and professional development.
