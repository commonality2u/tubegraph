## Perception and its Dynamics

Perception is traditionally viewed as the brain’s interpretation of sensory inputs, converting raw stimuli from the environment into a conscious experience. Dr. Huberman explained the role of different types of perception—**exteroception** and **interoception**—highlighting how these elements choreograph our experience of reality [[interoception_vs_exteroception_in_meditation | perception]]. Exteroception relates to the sensory information coming from outside of oneself, while interoception pertains to internal sensations, including the state of internal organs and skin. Putting these perceptual modalities into play, the discussion navigated how meditation acts as a perceptual exercise, altering our experience and manipulation of these inputs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:59">[00:18:59]</a>.

## The Elastic Nature of Time Perception

A particularly intriguing aspect explored by Huberman and Harris was the concept of time perception, noted to be extremely elastic. Dr. Huberman suggested that the brain can modify its "frame rate"—a term borrowed from visual media to describe the speed of visual or temporal perception in the brain [[the_role_of_neurochemicals_in_time_perception | temporal perception]]. Depending on the state of our consciousness, the brain’s processing of time can either dilate or contract <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a>. This elasticity allows us to experience time differently based on particular mental states and environmental conditions.

Interestingly, the perception of time is not consistent throughout all circumstances. For example, in high-stress or emergency situations, time often seems to slow down, a phenomenon attributed to heightened states of arousal facilitated by neurochemical shifts [[impact_of_dopamine_and_serotonin_on_time_perception | neurochemical shifts]]. On the other hand, during mundane or repetitive tasks, time may seem to pass quicker, reflecting a lower engagement with the moment's specifics.

## The Interplay of Mindfulness and Time Perception

Mindfulness and meditation practices have been empirically shown to affect time perception [[mindfulness_and_meditation_practices | time perception]]. Through intentional focus and heightened awareness, individuals can frequently gain a sense of presence that influences how time is subjectively experienced. Dr. Harris emphasized the profound experiences that mindful practices can provide, challenging traditional perceptions of time and reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:09">[01:35:09]</a>.

In the practice of mindfulness, individuals are often encouraged to observe their thoughts without judgment, an exercise that cultivates a non-reactive awareness of the present moment. This creates a detachment from the usual narratives tied to past and future concerns, fostering an elastic and more immediate sense of time. By paying closer attention to the unfolding of the present moment, one's experience of time can become more expansive or contracted based simply on where and how attention is focused.
