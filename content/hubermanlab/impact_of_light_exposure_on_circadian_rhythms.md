Our understanding of circadian rhythms and their relationship with light exposure has significantly advanced, underscoring the critical role light plays in regulating our internal body clocks. Circadian rhythms refer to the natural, internal processes that follow a roughly 24-hour cycle, responding primarily to light and darkness in our environment. Professor Andrew Huberman, a neurobiologist at Stanford School of Medicine, goes in-depth about these processes in a recent episode of the Huberman Lab Podcast.

## Light as a Zeitgeber

Light acts as a primary zeitgeber, or "time giver," meaning it's a critical environmental cue that helps synchronize internal circadian rhythms with the Earth's 24-hour rotation. These rhythms are evident in every cell of our bodies, influencing everything from hormone release and eating habits to sleep patterns and mood (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>).

### Morning Light Exposure

Professor Huberman emphasizes the profound importance of morning light exposure for aligning circadian rhythms. Morning sunlight exposure, ideally within the first hour after waking, stimulates neurons in the eyes known as [[circadian_rhythms_and_melanopsin_cells | melanopsin ganglion cells]]. These cells trigger biological processes that play into mental alertness, mood regulation, and overall metabolic health (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:25">[00:17:25]</a>).

### Afternoon and Evening Light Exposure

The timing and amount of light exposure in the afternoon and evening hold equal importance. Huberman outlines a practice he refers to as the "Netflix inoculation," recommending exposure to natural light in the late afternoon or early evening to reduce the potential negative effects of artificial light viewed later at night (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:40">[01:34:40]</a>). Such exposure can decrease retinal sensitivity to light at night, which otherwise can disrupt melatonin release and circadian rhythm alignment.

- **Sunset Viewing**: Observing the setting sun or simply spending time outdoors during this part of the day helps adjust the body's internal clock, preparing the body gradually for nighttime sleep cycles.

### Effects of Light at Night

Exposure to bright light from screens or other sources between 10 PM and 4 AM can severely disrupt circadian rhythms and impair cognitive function, immune health, and mood stability. Huberman references studies showing that such late-night exposure negatively impacts learning and memory due to its interference with natural melatonin production (<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:07">[01:34:07]</a>).

## Key Protocols

- **Morning exposure**: Seek sunlight for at least 10-30 minutes shortly after waking to stimulate alertness and reset your internal clock.
- **Afternoon/Evening exposure**: Aim for light exposure as the sun starts setting to assist in melatonin regulation later at night.
- **Reduce late-night light**: Limit exposure to artificial lighting, particularly blue light, from screens before bedtime to ensure quality sleep and proper circadian rhythm maintenance.
