## Pioneering Research in [[artificial_wombs_and_their_development | Artificial Wombs]]

Dr. Jacob Hanna is at the forefront of this field, having developed an [[artificial_wombs_and_their_development | artificial womb]] for mice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>. His team achieved a significant breakthrough by growing mouse embryos outside the uterus beyond the blastocyst stage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>. Previously, embryos could only be grown to the blastocyst stage outside the uterus, a process central to IVF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>. Dr. Hanna's research demonstrated, for the first time, that the critical phase of mammal development where most organs are formed can occur in an artificial environment, challenging the long-held belief that the uterus was "absolutely essential" for proper organ formation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.

While this represents a "major leap" in [[technological_advancements_and_future_potential | technological advancements]], human artificial wombs are "nowhere CLOSE" to reality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>. Dr. Hanna's study covers only a small part of the full gestation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>. Other studies, like one involving a lamb grown for a month outside the uterus after its organs had formed, also demonstrate progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>. The development of [[artificial_wombs_and_their_development | artificial wombs]] is viewed as a "slow movement of science," not a single, sudden breakthrough <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>.

The debate often frames [[artificial_wombs_and_their_development | artificial wombs]] as being "versus pregnancy," but the research holds significant near-term benefits that could *support* pregnancy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>.

The ability to observe mammalian embryos developing outside the uterus, similar to how zebra fish embryos are studied, offers unprecedented insight into the formation of organs and other developmental processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>.
