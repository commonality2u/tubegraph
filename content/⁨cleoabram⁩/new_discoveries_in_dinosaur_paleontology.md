The understanding of dinosaurs has significantly evolved, with many previously held beliefs now considered outdated or incorrect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>. Current paleontological research offers a cutting-edge look into these ancient creatures, often referred to as a "Golden Age of dinosaur discovery" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>.

## The Golden Age of Discovery
Scientists are currently discovering more dinosaurs than at any other time in history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>. On average, a new species is found each week <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. These frequent discoveries are challenging long-held [[misconceptions_about_dinosaurs | misconceptions about dinosaurs]] and refining our knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>. The field is considered to have "just begun" in terms of uncovering the full scope of dinosaur life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>.

## Active Dig Sites and Challenges
One notable active dig site in Alberta, Canada, is an exceptional example of modern paleontological work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:01">[00:01:01]</a>. This particular site contains a herd of *Pachyrhinosaurus* of various ages, from babies to adults <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>.
Led by Dr. Emily Bamforth, researchers believe the herd was killed simultaneously, possibly by a natural disaster like a flood, and subsequently buried in mud that turned to stone over 73 million years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:43">[00:01:43]</a>. The bones at this site are not preserved as individual animals but are "all sort of mixed together," forming what is lovingly referred to as a "Pachyrhinosaurus omelet" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>. This deposit contains hundreds, potentially thousands, of animals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:12">[00:02:12]</a>, making it one of the "densest dinosaur bone beds in North America" with an estimated 6,000 to 10,000 animals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.

The process of reconstructing a dinosaur skeleton is akin to solving a jigsaw puzzle where the picture is unknown, many pieces are missing, pieces from other puzzles are mixed in, and some pieces are damaged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. Finding intact skulls is rare but recently, a large *Pachyrhinosaurus* skull was uncovered at the site <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>.

### Challenges in Species Identification
Nearly half of all known dinosaur species are identified from a single, often incomplete, specimen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>. This incompleteness means scientists must extrapolate what the rest of the creature looked like based on the available pieces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. This makes distinguishing a new species from a part of an already known dinosaur particularly difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>.

## Updated Understanding of [[anatomy_and_appearance_of_dinosaurs | Anatomy and Appearance of Dinosaurs]]

### Size and Scale
The *Pachyrhinosaurus*, despite its large size (similar to an Asian elephant in height but longer), was considered medium-sized compared to other dinosaurs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>. Dinosaurs exhibited a vast range of sizes:
*   **Anchiornis**: Slightly larger than a basketball <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.
*   **Compsognathus**: Size of a small chicken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.
*   **Velociraptor**: Larger than commonly depicted, closer to the size of a German Shepherd <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>.
*   **Nyasasaurus**: Approximately the size of a German Shepherd <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.
*   **Triceratops**: Height of an Asian elephant but longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>.
*   **Stegosaurus** and **T-Rex**: Well-known large dinosaurs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>.
*   **Argentinosaurus**: One of the largest known dinosaurs, as long as and heavier than a commercial airplane <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>.

### Feathers
New research indicates that many dinosaurs, including *Velociraptors*, possessed feathers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. A 1998 discovery in China significantly changed this understanding, and subsequent findings have confirmed feathers on more dinosaur species than previously thought <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>.

### Color and Shading
While debates continue, the colors of some feathered dinosaurs are known due to preserved pigment in their feathers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. Examples include black and white shiny feathers, and rust or deep red colors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. Though skin doesn't preserve color, shading has been found, suggesting some dinosaurs had stripes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>. Modern reconstructions of dinosaur colors are often based on observations of coloring in modern animals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.

### "Shrink-Wrapping" Misconception
Early scientific illustrations often "shrink-wrapped" dinosaur skin tightly around their skeletons, similar to how a zebra or hippo might look if only its skeleton were considered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:59">[00:15:59]</a>. This approach failed to account for muscle, fat, and loose skin. Today, artists and scientists are correcting this, adding more realistic muscle mass and fatty tissues to accurately depict dinosaurs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:22">[00:16:22]</a>.

## Revising Dinosaur Behavior and Physiology

### T-Rex Posture and Body Mass
The iconic *T-Rex* has undergone significant reinterpretation. Originally depicted as a lean, upright predator (the "kangaroo pose" with a tail dragging on the ground), new computer models and comparisons to modern animals show this posture was unstable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>. *T-Rexes* were likely more stable leaning forward, acting like "teeter-totters" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:09:50]</a>.

Furthermore, *T-Rexes* were much chunkier than previously thought <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a>. Discoveries in 2018 of rib-like bones, similar to those in crocodiles, suggested these would have floated in the *T-Rex's* abdomen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:01">[00:10:01]</a>. This increased body mass indicates the *T-Rex* was likely an ambush predator, rather than a pursuit predator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>. Generally, most dinosaurs were chunkier than people typically imagine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a>.

### Evidence of Life and Illness
Fossil discoveries provide insight into the lives of individual dinosaurs. A healed break on a Tyrannosaur tooth provides "fossilized physiology or fossilized behavior," which is rare in the fossil record <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:40">[00:13:40]</a>. Evidence of disease, such as abnormal bone growth suggesting osteoarthritis or bone cancer, indicates that dinosaurs could get sick <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:13">[00:14:13]</a>.

## What Qualifies as a Dinosaur?
Scientifically, the definition of a dinosaur is phylogenetic, tracing back to a common ancestor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:50">[00:11:50]</a>. A "dinosaur" includes everything that descended from a specific branch in the evolutionary tree <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:17">[00:12:17]</a>.

### Not All Ancient Reptiles Are Dinosaurs
Many animals often associated with dinosaurs actually belong to different groups of reptiles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:24">[00:12:24]</a>:
*   **Pterosaurs** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:29">[00:12:29]</a>
*   **Mosasaurs, Plesiosaurs, Ichthyosaurs** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:31">[00:12:31]</a>
*   **Crocodiles**: Branched off earlier in the evolutionary tree <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>

### Birds are Dinosaurs
A significant modern understanding is that birds are not merely "descendants of dinosaurs" but are, by the most scientific definition, actual dinosaurs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:51">[00:12:51]</a>. This means dinosaurs are still alive today and are "all around you" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>.

## Fossilization
What are commonly called "dinosaur bones" are actually rocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:59">[00:16:59]</a>. Dinosaur bones are fossilized, meaning over millions of years, the original bone material has been replaced cell-by-cell with minerals, creating an exact copy of the bone made out of rock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:05">[00:17:05]</a>. Every dinosaur skeleton in a museum is a stone replica of a once-living animal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:29">[00:17:29]</a>.

## Dinosaur Timeline and Extinction
A common [[misconceptions_about_dinosaurs | misconception about dinosaurs]] is that all species lived at the same time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>. Dinosaurs ruled Earth for an immense period of 180 million years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>. To put this in perspective, there is more time separating the *Stegosaurus* from the *T-Rex* than separates the *T-Rex* from modern humans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:32">[00:06:32]</a>. This means the *Stegosaurus* was an ancient relic to the *T-Rex* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:38">[00:06:38]</a>.

The widespread [[dinosaur_extinction_and_survival | dinosaur extinction and survival]] event 66.04 million years ago was caused by an asteroid wider than Mount Everest, traveling at 20 to 30 km per second, hitting Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:35">[00:17:35]</a>. The immediate impact caused vaporization of the space rock, catapulted Earth chunks beyond the atmosphere, and created an apocalypse including a "thermal pulse" (a wave of heat) and mega tsunamis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:56">[00:17:56]</a>. For those that survived the initial impact, the subsequent "global winter" caused by ejecta blocking out the sun led to mass plant death and ecosystem collapse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:22">[00:18:22]</a>. The animals that survived were typically small, the size of a German shepherd or smaller, often capable of going into water or living underground <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:37">[00:18:37]</a>. This event ended the 180-million-year reign of non-avian dinosaurs, but it was "a really great day for mammals," paving the way for their diversification and the eventual rise of humans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:02">[00:19:02]</a>.
