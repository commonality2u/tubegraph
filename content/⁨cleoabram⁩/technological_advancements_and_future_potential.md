## Current Applications and Capabilities

Spot is currently engaged in real work for a range of entities, including fire departments, factories, defunct nuclear power plants, and deep mines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>. More controversially, it's also being used by police departments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. Companies are investing between $75,000 and $300,000 per robot to integrate Spot into their teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>.

Spot's primary role often involves inspection:
*   **Purina dog food factory**: Inspecting packaging for air leaks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.
*   **Oil rig (Gulf of Mexico)**: Inspecting equipment for corrosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>.
*   **Ancient ruins of Pompeii**: Inspecting grave robber tunnels for Italian police <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>.
*   **Fukushima nuclear power plant**: Inspecting areas for radiation levels and safety, navigating areas untouched by humans since 2011 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:24">[00:07:24]</a>.

These jobs often involve environments too dangerous, tedious, or requiring more constant and reliable monitoring than human workers can provide <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>. Spot's four-legged design provides greater stability than two-legged robots and is better suited for places designed for human navigation compared to wheeled robots <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.

## The "Platform" Concept and Ethical Dilemmas

The concept of robot dogs as a "platform" is crucial; it allows individuals and companies to develop new capabilities and uses for the [[Technological advancements enabling AI capabilities | technology]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. This flexibility opens the door to both beneficial and potentially concerning applications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:19">[00:08:19]</a>.

### Controversial Applications
*   **Law Enforcement**: In 2021, the New York Police Department used a remote-controlled Spot with what appeared to be a thermal camera to locate a man hiding after a shooting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>.
*   **Military**: Spot's ancestors, early quadruped robots funded by the US military, were initially intended as robotic pack mules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. As four-legged [[Future of robotics and AI applications | robot technology]] improved, military interest grew. Other companies, like Ghost Robotics, are actively building this [[Technological advancements enabling AI capabilities | technology]] for military use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. For example, the Air Force partnered with Ghost Robotics in 2020 to use robot dogs as inspectors at Tyndall Air Force Base, notably without weapons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>.

Boston Dynamics has signed a pledge advocating against the weaponization of general-purpose robots like Spot, urging other companies to commit to similar stances to ensure robots improve quality of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>. However, not all companies agree on the purpose of robot dogs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:24">[00:09:24]</a>.
