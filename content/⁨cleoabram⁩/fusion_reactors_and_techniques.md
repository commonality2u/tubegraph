## What is Nuclear Fusion?
[[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | Nuclear fusion]] involves smashing tiny atoms, such as hydrogen or helium, together to create a larger atom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>. During this process, a small amount of mass is lost, which is converted into a significant amount of energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.

### Fusion vs. Fission
It's important to distinguish [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] from fission:
*   **Fission** is the process of cracking a large atom, like uranium, into smaller atoms, releasing energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.
    *   **Uncontrolled Fission** results in an atomic bomb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
    *   **Controlled Fission** is used in all currently operational nuclear power plants to generate electricity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>.
*   **Fusion** is the process of smashing small atoms together to form a larger one, releasing even more energy than fission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.
    *   **Uncontrolled Fusion** can create a hydrogen bomb, which has been tested but never used in warfare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>.
    *   **Controlled Fusion** is the objective of current research, aiming for a clean and potentially inexhaustible energy source <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>.

### Energy Potential of Fusion
Compared to fossil fuels, [[energy_potential_of_fusion | fusion]] offers millions of times more energy from the same amount of mass <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>. While fission has a similar energy density, [[energy_potential_of_fusion | fusion]] generally comes with fewer safety risks, particularly regarding nuclear proliferation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. A [[nuclear_fusion_basics | fusion]] bomb, like a hydrogen bomb, still requires uranium and plutonium to initiate, meaning [[nuclear_fusion_basics | fusion]] by itself is not considered a proliferation concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.

## [[challenges_of_creating_commercial_nuclear_fusion_energy | Challenges of Creating Commercial Nuclear Fusion Energy]]
The primary [[challenges_of_nuclear_fusion | challenge]] with [[challenges_of_creating_commercial_nuclear_fusion_energy | nuclear fusion]] is that current methods require more electricity to initiate the reaction than they produce <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>. This net energy deficit is the main hurdle to its commercial viability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:04">[00:12:04]</a>.

Despite the difficulties, the urgency of climate change and the need for clean energy sources are driving increased research into [[fusion_as_a_solution_for_climate_change | fusion]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>.

### Small-Scale Fusion vs. Commercial Reactors
It is "very, very easy" to build a [[comparison_between_smallscale_fusors_and_commercial_fusion_reactors | small fusion system]] that creates a high-energy beam and achieves fusion at a basic level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:22">[00:11:22]</a>. This is what you might hear about when a teenager builds a [[comparison_between_smallscale_fusors_and_commercial_fusion_reactors | fusion reactor in their garage]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>. However, achieving [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] at a commercial scale requires significantly more complex techniques and energy output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>.

### The Quest for Fusion at Scale
To move beyond smashing individual atoms, [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] needs to occur at scale, described as "less smashing and more smooshing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>. This challenge has been the focus of thousands of researchers for decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>.

## Plasma: The Fourth State of Matter
Fusion requires matter to be in the state of plasma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>. Plasma is a state where atoms have so much energy that their individual components (neutrons, electrons, protons) are separated and flying around freely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>. The core challenge is how to confine and pressurize this superheated plasma for long enough to achieve [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.

## Methods of Plasma Confinement
There are three main methods for confining plasma at scale to make [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] happen:

1.  **Gravitational Confinement**: This is how [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] occurs naturally in the sun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>. The immense gravity of the sun pulls plasma inward, compressing and heating the fuel to the point where it fuses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>. On Earth, we lack the ability to create systems large enough for gravitational confinement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>.
2.  **Magnetic Confinement**: This method uses electronics and magnetic fields to contain the plasma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>. A common machine used for this is a tokamak, which heats a circulating fusion plasma until it's hot enough to fuse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>.
3.  **Inertial Confinement**: Instead of steadily holding the fusion fuel, this approach compresses it very quickly, on the order of a billionth of a second <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:24">[00:09:24]</a>. This is often achieved by shooting lasers at the plasma to push it together rapidly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:29">[00:09:29]</a>. This creates "plasma blinks" rather than a continuous plasma soup <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.

### Hybrid Approach: Magneto-inertial Confinement
Some companies, like Helion, are pursuing a mixture of magnetic and inertial confinement called magneto-inertial confinement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:01">[00:10:01]</a>. This method aims to combine the continuous confinement benefits of magnetic fields with the rapid compression of inertial methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:04">[00:10:04]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>. The process involves ejecting plasma, compressing it to increase pressure and temperature, causing it to fuse, and then allowing it to expand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:33">[00:10:33]</a>.

## Fusion Fuels
The materials needed for [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] are not ordinary hydrogen and helium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>. The most commonly used types of hydrogen in [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] are deuterium and tritium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:33">[00:11:33]</a>. Tritium is a rare isotope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>. If tritium is unavailable, helium-3, another rare isotope, can be used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>. There is ongoing debate within the [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] community regarding the best materials for scaling [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion | fusion]] to serve millions of people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:47">[00:11:47]</a>.
