## Unveiling Exoplanets

The James Webb Space Telescope offers the best view of worlds beyond our own solar system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>. So far, approximately 5,000 "exoplanets" have been identified outside our solar system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. This number is expected to be much higher, considering that the Kepler telescope was pointed at just one column of space for four years to find many of these <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. The mapping of these exoplanets represents decades of scientific work and significant technological advancements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>.

### How the James Webb Telescope Detects Exoplanets

It is challenging to determine if a planet exists around a distant star, let alone what its composition might be <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. The James Webb Space Telescope addresses this by analyzing the light from distant stars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:43">[00:01:43]</a>. When a planet passes between us and its star, it causes a tiny [[exoplanet_discovery_through_starlight_dips | dip in the starlight]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. While this method helped locate many planets, the planet does not block all the light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>. A small amount of light passes through the planet's atmosphere on its way to us <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>. Different gases in that atmosphere block different kinds of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>. The James Webb Space Telescope is powerful enough to detect what light is "missing," indicating the atmospheric composition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>. This capability allows the telescope to analyze incredibly tiny specks of darkness against tiny specks of light in the vastness of space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>.

### The Hunt for Biosignatures

By analyzing a planet's atmosphere, scientists can look for "biosignatures" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>. For example, if observing Earth from afar, one wouldn't see people or cities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:46">[00:01:46]</a>. However, if the atmosphere showed 78% nitrogen and 21% oxygen, it would indicate a good chance of finding life (though not necessarily intelligent life) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. The James Webb Space Telescope has the ability to find these indicators of life on other planets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>.

## The Discovery of Water Vapor on [[wasp_96b_and_detection_of_water_vapor | WASP 96b]]

One of the first deep-space targets for the James Webb Space Telescope was [[wasp_96b_and_detection_of_water_vapor | WASP 96b]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>. Light from this planet began its journey 1,150 years ago and only recently reached the telescope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>. By analyzing the kinds of light blocked by its atmosphere, scientists learned that [[wasp_96b_and_detection_of_water_vapor | WASP 96b]] contains water <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.

While this does not confirm the discovery of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>, [[wasp_96b_and_detection_of_water_vapor | WASP 96b]] is a hot gas giant with water vapor in a steamy atmosphere, not liquid water <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>. Although water vapor has been found on other planets closer to Earth (a couple of hundred light-years away) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>, obtaining such detailed information about a planet over 1,000 light-years away is an astounding feat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>. This initial data from Webb serves as a "proof of concept" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>, demonstrating that this method of searching works and signaling just the beginning of what's to come <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
