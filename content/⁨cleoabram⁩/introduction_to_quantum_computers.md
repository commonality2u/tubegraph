Quantum computers represent a fundamentally different approach to computation compared to traditional computers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a>. They are not simply bigger or faster versions of existing technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:45">[00:00:45]</a>. ## What is a Quantum Computer?

A quantum computer is a device designed to perform computations based on the principles of quantum mechanics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>.

The physical component of a quantum computer is typically a chip, which is housed within a large structure designed to maintain extremely cold temperatures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a>. For instance, IBM's quantum computer is kept at 15 millikelvin, making it one of the coldest places in the universe, even colder than outer space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>. This requires a specialized "dilution refrigerator" system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</a>. Cables run signals into the processor at these cold temperatures, and then the results are translated by room-temperature control electronics into human-understandable information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>.

## [[difference_between_classical_and_quantum_computers | Bits vs. Qubits]]

The core [[difference_between_classical_and_quantum_computers | difference between classical and quantum computers]] lies in their fundamental units of information:

*   **Classical Computers** operate using **bits** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>. These bits, made of silicon, exist in one of two states: zero (ground state) or one (excited state) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>. All information processed by a classical computer is translated into this binary language <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:57">[00:08:57]</a>.
*   **Quantum Computers** operate using **qubits** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:03">[00:09:03]</a>. Unlike bits, qubits are more complex <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. They can exist in a superposition, meaning they have a probability of being zero and a probability of being one, similar to a wave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>. When a quantum computer is working, the probabilities of multiple qubits interact, constructively or destructively, much like waves in a pond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. The computer then finds the most likely answer by watching these interactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

It's a common misconception that quantum computers "try all the options" simultaneously; instead, they manipulate these probabilities to find solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:53">[00:09:53]</a>.

## What Quantum Computers Excel At

Quantum computers are not designed to be universally faster than classical computers; for instance, they are not better at simple addition than a calculator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>. Their strength lies in "finding structure in tons of data" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>.

### Analogy: Cars vs. Boats
A useful analogy describes computation as traveling across a mathematical map <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>. Classical computers (like cars) have allowed us to explore vast areas of this map by getting faster and more efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:50">[00:04:50]</a>. However, quantum computers are like "boats" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:12">[00:05:12]</a>. They are not necessarily better than cars, but they are "built for totally different terrain" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>—new mathematical "waters" that classical computers cannot navigate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. This analogy has been further refined to a "submarine" to reflect the different levels and depth of exploration possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:54">[00:15:54]</a>.

### [[potential_applications_of_quantum_computing | Potential Applications of Quantum Computing]]

1.  **[[potential_applications_of_quantum_computing | Simulating Nature]]**: Nature fundamentally obeys quantum physics, especially at the molecular and atomic levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>. Classical computers struggle to calculate how materials or atoms will behave beyond a certain size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>. Quantum computers can help in developing:
    *   Battery technology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:24">[00:11:24]</a>
    *   New materials with long chains of molecules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>
    *   New medicines and molecules by better predicting molecular behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:36">[00:11:36]</a>

2.  **[[quantum_computing_and_encryption_challenges | Encryption Challenges]]**: A significant concern and area of research is the ability of quantum computers to impact current encryption methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:12">[00:12:12]</a>.
    *   **Shor's Algorithm**: Developed by Peter Shor, this algorithm can efficiently find prime factors of very large numbers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:38">[00:12:38]</a>.
    *   **RSA Encryption**: The security of RSA encryption, which underpins most internet transactions, relies on the classical impossibility of factoring large numbers within a reasonable timeframe (billions of years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:56">[00:12:56]</a>.
    *   **Quantum Threat**: A quantum computer could perform this task in hours to days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:12">[00:13:12]</a>, posing a major threat to current online security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:23">[00:12:23]</a>. This capability is a driving force behind the [[quantum_supremacy_and_global_competition | global competition]] in quantum computing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:19">[00:13:19]</a>.
    *   **Quantum-Safe Algorithms**: While a practical quantum computer with code-breaking capabilities isn't yet available (it would likely require a million or more qubits, whereas the current record is 433 qubits by IBM) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:31">[00:13:31]</a>, experts anticipate a shift to quantum-safe algorithms in the next five years or less <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:50">[00:13:50]</a>.

## Current State and Future Outlook

The field of quantum computing is advancing at an incredibly fast rate, solving problems that were incomprehensible just a few years ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:06">[00:14:06]</a>. There are no known mathematical or physical barriers to continued progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:17">[00:14:17]</a>. This ongoing improvement is expected to lead to the discovery of new, currently unconsidered applications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:43">[00:14:43]</a>, exploring a "bottomless ocean" of possibilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:52">[00:14:52]</a>. The ultimate dream is to use this technology to enable fundamentally different and better things, and to better understand the world, which is inherently quantum mechanical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:08">[00:16:08]</a>.
