## Fission vs. Fusion

To understand [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|nuclear fusion]], it's helpful to distinguish it from fission:

*   **Fission**: This process involves cracking a large atom, such as uranium, into smaller atoms, releasing energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.
    *   **Uncontrolled Fission**: An atomic bomb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
    *   **Controlled Fission**: All currently operational [[history_of_nuclear_power_and_waste_management|nuclear power]] plants that generate electricity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>.
*   **Fusion**: This process involves smashing tiny atoms, like hydrogen or helium, together to create a larger atom, releasing even more energy than fission <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.
    *   **Uncontrolled Fusion**: A hydrogen bomb, which has been tested but never used in war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>. It's important to note that a fusion bomb still contains fissionable materials like uranium and plutonium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.
    *   **Controlled Fusion**: The goal for future energy generation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>.

[[energy_potential_of_fusion|Fusion has enormous potential]] as a clean energy source. While fission and fusion have similar energy densities, [[energy_potential_of_fusion|fusion can yield millions of times more energy]] from the same amount of mass compared to fossil fuels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>. [[energy_potential_of_fusion|Fusion comes with fewer safety risks]], particularly regarding nuclear proliferation, as fusion itself is not a proliferation concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.

The urgent need to address [[fusion_as_a_solution_for_climate_change|climate change]] and find more clean energy sources is driving the push for [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>.

## Confinement Methods for Fusion

To achieve [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] at scale, plasma must be confined and pressurized for a sufficient duration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>. Plasma is a state of matter where atoms have broken into bits, with neutrons, electrons, and protons flying around due to immense energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a>.

There are three primary methods to confine plasma for fusion at scale:

1.  **Gravitational Confinement**: This is how the Sun and other stars power the universe. Their immense gravity pulls plasma inward, compressing and heating the fuel until it fuses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>. This method is not feasible on Earth due to the inability to build systems large enough <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:16">[00:08:16]</a>.
2.  **Magnetic Confinement**: This method uses electronics and magnetic fields to pull plasma in and keep it contained <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>. A well-known machine for this is the tokamak, which heats fusion plasma as it circles around until it gets hot enough to fuse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>.
3.  **Inertial Confinement**: Instead of steadily holding fusion fuel, this approach compresses it extremely quickly, typically in a billionth of a second, by shooting lasers at the plasma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>. This creates "plasma blinks" rather than a continuous plasma soup <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.
4.  **Magnetoinertial Confinement**: Helion, the company interviewed, uses a hybrid approach combining magnetic confinement to hold the fuel and inertial confinement for rapid compression <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>. This process produces a pink light as the plasma is ejected, compresses, and then expands, creating a "manmade star" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.

## Remaining [[challenges_of_nuclear_fusion|Challenges of Nuclear Fusion]]

Despite the promise, there are still significant [[challenges_of_creating_commercial_nuclear_fusion_energy|challenges to creating commercial nuclear fusion energy]]:

*   **Fuel Availability**: The specific hydrogen isotopes used in [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]], deuterium and tritium, are needed. Tritium is rare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:33">[00:11:33]</a>. Helium-3 is an alternative but is also rare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>.
*   **Net Energy Gain**: Currently, all human-made [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] methods require more electricity input than they produce output <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:56">[00:11:56]</a>.

However, the development of [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] should be seen in a broader historical context. For example, the concept of electricity carrying energy was understood in the late 1600s, but it took until the 1800s to figure out how to use it, a longer period than has been spent on [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:21">[00:12:21]</a>. The potential societal impact of widely available, cheap energy from [[basics_of_nuclear_fusion|fusion]] is profound, offering the possibility of reducing human suffering and allowing for greater experimentation to improve lives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:50">[00:12:50]</a>.
