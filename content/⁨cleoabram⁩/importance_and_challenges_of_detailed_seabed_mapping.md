The ocean, which covers 71% of Earth's surface, remains largely unmapped and unexplored <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. We possess significantly less knowledge about the ocean floor than we do about the surface of Mars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:14">[00:00:14]</a>.

## The Current State of Ocean Mapping

The [[current_state_of_ocean_mapping_and_its_limitations | current state of ocean mapping]] shows stark contrasts in resolution:
*   Mars has been mapped to a resolution of 5 meters per pixel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:19">[00:00:19]</a>.
*   Land on Earth is mapped to approximately 30 centimeters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>.
*   Most of our oceans are mapped only to 1.5 kilometers per pixel, which is 300 times less detail than land maps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>.

At the start of the [[seabed_2030_and_future_goals_for_ocean_mapping | Seabed 2030]] project, only 6% of the ocean floor had detailed maps; as of the video's creation, this has increased to 25% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:24">[00:11:24]</a>.

## Why Detailed Maps Matter

Mapping the ocean floor in detail is crucial for several reasons:
*   **Infrastructure** Laying [[challenges_of_existing_internet_infrastructure | internet cables]] across the ocean to carry global internet traffic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>.
*   **Economic Activity** Supporting global trade and sourcing food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>.
*   **Disaster Preparedness** Better predicting tsunamis, storms, and sea level rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>.
*   **Energy** Utilizing the ocean for new kinds of renewable energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>.
*   **Search and Rescue** In a crisis, a good map is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>. For example, during the search for MH370, entire unmapped mountain ranges and valleys were discovered, demonstrating how easily a Boeing 777 could be hidden in the complex topography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>.
*   **Management** Steve Hall, a marine scientist and ocean mapping expert, emphasizes that "you can't manage what you haven't measured," and maps create a level playing field for understanding and utilizing resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:47">[00:11:47]</a>.

## Challenges of Mapping the Ocean Floor

Mapping the ocean is "shockingly hard" due to several factors:
*   **Water Absorption of Light** Light does not penetrate to the ocean floor, unlike for mapping Mars or Earth's land surface where pictures can be taken <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>. Beyond 1,000 meters, no surface light can reach, leaving the depths in complete darkness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:09">[00:02:09]</a>.
*   **Extreme Depth** The ocean is far deeper than most people realize <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:43">[00:01:43]</a>:
    *   At 10 meters (33 feet), an additional atmosphere of pressure is experienced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.
    *   The deepest human diver record (without equipment) is 214 meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>.
    *   828 meters is the depth of the Burj Khalifa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>.
    *   Deepest known military submarines operate around 1220 meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:20">[00:02:20]</a>.
    *   The Titanic wreck is at 3800 meters, where pressure exceeds 350 atmospheres <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>.
    *   The deepest natural point, Challenger Deep, is 10,935 meters (6.8 miles) deep, deeper than Mount Everest is tall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>.
*   **Vastness** Over 70% of Earth's surface is water, making it the "last unknown place on earth" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>.

## Historical and Modern Mapping Techniques

### Historical Evolution of Ocean Floor Mapping Techniques

*   **Satellite Gravity Data** Early approximations of ocean floor topography seen on Google Maps are derived from satellite gravity data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>. The ocean surface is not perfectly flat; it subtly mirrors the shape of the seabed below due to gravitational variations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>. Satellites send radar pulses to measure the water's height variations, estimating the seafloor's shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</a>. This method, however, provides only a "good quality but educated guesswork" and lacks precision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>.
*   **Sonar and Marie Tharp** In the 1920s, a device was created to send acoustic waves into the water and record their bounce-back time, known as sonar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>. However, it wasn't until the 1940s that Marie Tharp transformed these sonar measurements into the first ocean floor maps, revealing mountains and valleys previously unseen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37">[00:05:37]</a>. Tharp manually connected raw sonar data lines with numbers indicating depth, drawing contours with colored pencils to create 2D profiles that eventually became maps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>. Her earliest published map of the ocean floor dates to 1957 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>. While beautiful, these maps involved significant "filling in the blanks" due to reliance on narrow lines of sonar data from old shipping routes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:12">[00:08:12]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.

### Technological Advancements in Mapping the Ocean Floor

Today, [[technological_advancements_in_mapping_the_ocean_floor | technological advancements in mapping the ocean floor]] have improved significantly:
*   **Multibeam Sonar** Modern sonar devices send thousands of beams in a wide fan that can span several kilometers, allowing much larger areas to be mapped at once <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>. This provides significantly more detail compared to older methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>.
*   **Robots and AI** The "rise of the robots," specifically unmanned or autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) (referred to as underwater drones), are transforming mapping efforts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:45">[00:10:45]</a>. These robots can perform repetitive "mowing the lawn" tasks, collecting data by going back and forth, which is tedious for human crews <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>. [[challenges_and_considerations_in_deploying_selfdriving_vehicles | Self-driving vehicles]] combined with AI can also be smart enough to identify and investigate interesting features with higher accuracy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>.

## Seabed 2030 and Future Goals

[[seabed_2030_and_future_goals_for_ocean_mapping | Seabed 2030]] is an international initiative aiming to map the entire ocean floor in detail by 2030 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:45">[00:09:45]</a>.
*   **Resolution Goals** The project seeks to map even the most unknown areas to a resolution of 800 meters, and higher traffic areas down to a few meters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:54">[00:09:54]</a>.
*   **Data Sharing** A key aspect of Seabed 2030 is gathering data from diverse sources, including oil and gas companies, environmental groups, and militaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:05">[00:10:05]</a>. While some high-resolution military data may remain proprietary, even shared data at 100-meter accuracy would be a significant improvement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>.

Despite concerns that better maps might lead to resource exploitation like deep-sea mining, the argument is that the lack of maps does not prevent such activities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:35">[00:11:35]</a>. Instead, comprehensive maps facilitate better management and understanding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:47">[00:11:47]</a>. Humanity's ability to map its environment is the first step toward understanding and hopefully using it for the betterment of all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>. Exploring the last unknown places on our planet, like the deep ocean, is an inspiring endeavor that can be achieved through collaborative efforts, similar to the International Space Station <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>.
