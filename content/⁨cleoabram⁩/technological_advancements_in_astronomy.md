## The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

NASA has released the first full-color images from the James Webb Space Telescope, which is described as the "most powerful telescope ever built" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>.
Its initial images include:
*   The deepest view into space ever recorded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:12">[00:00:12]</a>.
*   An exceptionally clear view of stars being born <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>.

The development and launch of the JWST represent "huge leaps forward in technology" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:16">[00:01:16]</a>, enabling scientists to observe "worlds beyond our own solar system" with unprecedented clarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>.

## Exoplanet Discovery and Characterization

Before the James Webb Space Telescope, the Kepler telescope observed a specific column of space for four years, contributing to the discovery of approximately 5,000 "exoplanets" (planets outside our solar system) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. The map of these exoplanets is the result of "decades of scientific work" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>.

### Detecting Biosignatures
A significant advancement offered by the JWST is its ability to find "biosignatures" on other planets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. Biosignatures are indicators of life, such as specific atmospheric compositions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a>. For example, an atmosphere with 78% nitrogen and 21% oxygen would suggest a high probability of finding life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>.

The method involves observing tiny dips in a star's light as an exoplanet passes between the telescope and the star <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. Crucially, a small amount of starlight passes *through* the exoplanet's atmosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>. Different gases in an atmosphere block different kinds of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>. The James Webb Telescope is powerful enough to detect what specific light is missing, thereby identifying the gases present in the exoplanet's atmosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>. This capability allows for the detection of "tiny, tiny specks of darkness against tiny, tiny specks of light in the depths of space" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:56">[00:02:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.

### WASP 96b
The scientists behind the JWST first pointed it at WASP 96b, a gas giant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>. Light from WASP 96b took 1,150 years to reach the telescope <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>. By analyzing the blocked light, the telescope confirmed the presence of water vapor in WASP 96b's hot, steamy atmosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>. While water vapor has been found on other planets, those observations were much closer to Earth, only a few hundred lightyears away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>.

The ability to obtain such detailed information about a planet over 1,000 lightyears away is considered "insane" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a> and represents a "proof of concept" that this method of searching for atmospheric compositions works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>. This marks just the beginning of what the JWST can achieve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
