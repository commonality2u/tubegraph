## The Exoplanet Landscape

Currently, approximately 5,000 "exoplanets"—planets outside our solar system—are known <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. Many of these discoveries resulted from the Kepler telescope, which focused on a single column of space for four years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. This extensive map of exoplanets represents decades of scientific work and significant [[technological_advancements_in_astronomy | technological advancements]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. Despite the vast number of discoveries, there's a sense that we are currently alone in our cosmic neighborhood <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>.

## Detecting Life on Distant Worlds

Identifying life on exoplanets is a formidable challenge, as it's difficult enough to even confirm a planet's existence from tiny specks of light <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. While individuals or cities wouldn't be visible from far away, detecting the composition of a planet's atmosphere could indicate the presence of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. For instance, an atmosphere with 78% nitrogen and 21% oxygen would suggest a high probability of finding life, though not necessarily intelligent life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a>. These indicators of life are known as [[biosignatures_and_atmospheric_analysis | biosignatures]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>.

### The [[James Webb Space Telescope | James Webb Space Telescope]]'s Method

The [[James Webb Space Telescope | James Webb Space Telescope]] is capable of identifying [[biosignatures_and_atmospheric_analysis | biosignatures]] on exoplanets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. Its method involves observing slight dips in starlight when a planet passes between us and its star <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. Crucially, some starlight passes *through* the exoplanet's atmosphere before reaching us <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>. Different gases in the atmosphere absorb different types of light, and the [[James Webb Space Telescope | James Webb Space Telescope]] is powerful enough to detect what light is "missing," thereby revealing the atmospheric composition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>. This incredible feat involves observing tiny specks of darkness against tiny specks of light in the vastness of space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>.

## Discovery of Water Vapor on WASP 96b

One of the first targets for the [[James Webb Space Telescope | James Webb Space Telescope]] was WASP 96b <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>. This exoplanet is located 1,150 light-years away, meaning the light observed from it left the planet over a millennium ago <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>. By analyzing the blocked light patterns, scientists confirmed the presence of water in WASP 96b's atmosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.

While this doesn't signify the discovery of life, as WASP 96b is a hot gas giant with water vapor in a steamy atmosphere, not liquid water <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>, the detail achieved for a planet over 1,000 light-years away is groundbreaking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>. Previously, water vapor could only be detected on planets a few hundred light-years from Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>. This successful detection serves as a "proof of concept" for the telescope's capability in the [[search_for_extraterrestrial_life | search for extraterrestrial life]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>.
