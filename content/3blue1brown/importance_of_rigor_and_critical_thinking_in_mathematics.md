Mathematics, while often intuitive, requires rigorous proofs and critical thinking to avoid false conclusions. Exploring "fake proofs" can highlight subtle pitfalls and underscore the necessity of precision in mathematical reasoning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:04">[00:00:04]</a>.

## Case Study 1: The Surface Area of a Sphere

One illustrative "proof" attempts to derive the surface area of a sphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>.

### The Proposed Method
The method involves subdividing a sphere into vertical slices, similar to an orange <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:14">[00:00:14]</a>. These slices from both the northern and southern hemispheres are then "unraveled" and interlaced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>. The base of this new shape is the sphere's circumference (2πR), and its height is described as a quarter-walk around the sphere (πR/2) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>. As the slices become finer, the shape is thought to approach a perfect rectangle, with an area of (πR/2) * (2πR) = π²R² <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>.

### The Flaw
Despite its elegance and intuitive appeal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>, this proof is fundamentally incorrect; the true surface area of a sphere is 4πR² <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. The error lies in the assumption that the wedges can be flattened without distortion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>. When accurately flattened to preserve area, the orange wedges would bulge outward, not appear as triangles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:01">[00:11:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>. The width of a wedge varies according to a sine curve as one moves from top to bottom, not linearly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:03">[00:12:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:06">[00:12:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:09">[00:12:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:14">[00:12:14]</a>. This non-linear growth leads to a persistent overlap between the interlaced wedges, which accounts for the difference between the false π²R² and the true 4πR² <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:23">[00:12:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:31">[00:12:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:43">[00:12:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:47">[00:12:47]</sup>.

The issue stems from the fundamental difference between the geometry of a curved surface and flat space, described by [[Gaussian curvature]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:24">[00:14:24]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:29">[00:14:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:33">[00:14:33]</a>. Flattening a sphere inherently loses geometric information <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:36">[00:14:36]</a>. For such limiting arguments to work, the pieces must get smaller in *both* directions to appear locally flat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:48">[00:14:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:52">[00:14:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:54">[00:14:54]</sup>. The orange wedges only got thin in one direction, maintaining their curvature in the other <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:59">[00:14:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:02">[00:15:02]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:06">[00:15:06]</sup>.

## Case Study 2: The "Proof" That Pi Equals 4

Another classic "fake proof" attempts to show that π = 4 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

### The Proposed Method
Starting with a circle of radius 1, its circumference (2πR or 2π) is to be determined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</sup>. The method begins by drawing a square whose sides are tangent to the circle; this square has a perimeter of 8 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</sup>. A sequence of curves is then generated by folding in the corners of the previous shape, always maintaining a perimeter of 8, while increasingly approximating the circle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</sup>. The key observation is that each fold preserves the perimeter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:31">[00:02:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:46">[00:02:46]</a>.

The argument claims that the limit of these approximations *is* the circle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>. To understand this mathematically, each shape can be described as a parametric function C_n(t) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</sup>. For any specific input `t`, the sequence of points C_n(t) converges to a point on the circle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:03">[00:04:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</sup>. Thus, a new function C_infinity(t) can be defined as this limiting value for any `t`, which indeed traces the genuine smooth circular curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</sup>. Since each curve has a perimeter of 8, the limit of their lengths is also 8 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</sup>.

### The Flaw
The fundamental issue here, a crucial concept in [[the_importance_of_calculations_in_mathematics | calculus]] and [[mathematical_problemsolving_and_flexibility | limiting arguments]], is that the limit of the lengths of the curves is not necessarily the same as the length of the limit of the curves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:29">[00:15:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:33">[00:15:33]</sup>. While the sequence of curves visually approaches the circle, they remain jagged at any finite step, taking "inefficient steps" along the curve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</sup>. This example serves as a counter-example to demonstrate why this interchangeability is not guaranteed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:38">[00:15:38]</sup>. This highlights that [[problemsolving_strategies_in_mathematics | applying limiting arguments]] in mathematics requires careful rigor to be explicit about the error in approximations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:45">[00:15:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:50">[00:15:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:20">[00:16:20]</sup>.

## Case Study 3: The "Proof" That All Triangles Are Isosceles

This proof, presented in a [[challenges_of_formalizing_mathematical_insights | Euclid-style]] fashion, demonstrates how subtle errors can occur even with seemingly rigorous steps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:23">[00:05:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</sup>.

### The Proposed Method
The proof attempts to show that for any triangle ABC, side AB must equal side AC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</sup>. It involves:
1.  Drawing the perpendicular bisector of side BC, intersecting at D <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</sup>.
2.  Drawing the angle bisector of angle A, intersecting the perpendicular bisector at P <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</sup>.
3.  Drawing perpendicular lines from P to AC (point E) and from P to AB (point F) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:48">[00:06:48]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:53">[00:06:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</sup>.

The proof then uses congruence relations:
*   Triangle AFP is congruent to Triangle AEP (Side-Angle-Angle, due to shared side AP, angle alpha, and 90-degree angle) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:16">[00:07:16]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</sup>. This implies AF = AE <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</sup>.
*   Triangle PDB is congruent to Triangle PDC (Side-Angle-Side, due to shared base PD, 90-degree angle, and equal heights from perpendicular bisector) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:46">[00:07:46]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:48">[00:07:48]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</sup>. This implies PB = PC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</sup>.
*   Triangle BFP is congruent to Triangle CEP (Side-Side-Angle, due to PB=PC, FP=EP from first congruence, and 90-degree angles) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:25">[00:08:25]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:28]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</sup>. This implies FB = EC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</sup>.

Finally, adding the corresponding lengths (AF + FB = AB) and (AE + EC = AC) leads to the conclusion that AB = AC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:17">[00:09:17]</a>. Since the triangle was arbitrary, this would imply all triangles are isosceles, or even equilateral <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:27">[00:09:27]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</sup>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</sup>.

### The Flaw
The result is obviously false <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:59">[00:05:59]</a>. The error, however, is highly subtle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:48">[00:17:48]</a>. All the congruence relations used are valid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:34">[00:08:34]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:19">[00:17:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:22">[00:17:22]</sup>. The hidden assumption that breaks the proof is the spatial relationship of the points <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:32">[00:17:32]</a>.

If the angle bisector and perpendicular bisector are constructed accurately, their intersection point (P) may actually sit *outside* the triangle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:00">[00:17:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:04">[00:17:04]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:08">[00:17:08]</a>. Consequently, the point E (perpendicular from P to AC) might not lie *between* A and C <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:37">[00:17:37]</a>. The final step, AF + FB = AB and AE + EC = AC, only holds true if E and F are interior points on the sides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:32">[00:17:32]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:43">[00:17:43]</sup>. For many triangles, point E (and F) will sit *outside* the segment, meaning the addition relationship used (AE + EC = AC) is incorrect; it might be AC = AE - EC, or similar, depending on the arrangement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:43">[00:17:43]</sup>.
