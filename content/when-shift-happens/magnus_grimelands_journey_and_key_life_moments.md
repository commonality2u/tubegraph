## Early Life and Childhood Inspirations

Grimeland grew up in a small community of about 100 people in the countryside of Norway, approximately 10 kilometers from the nearest store <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. His father was a professional husky sledger, meaning much of his childhood was spent in the woods with 30 huskies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>.

A significant turning point occurred around the age of seven or eight, when his great-grandmother passed away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>. This early encounter with loss prompted him to ponder the meaning of life and humanity's purpose on Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>. This introspection led to a realization that one should strive to "do something impactful" with their life, slowly building an internal drive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>.

Another pivotal moment came at age 12 or 13, when he read an article about United World Colleges (UWC), specifically the school in the UK located at an old castle, "Sundance Castle," reminiscent of Harry Potter's Hogwarts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:57">[00:08:57]</a>. The article, featuring the Norwegian Queen, described students from across the globe living and studying together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>. Despite being told he was too young and facing immense competition (hundreds applying for 20 scholarships), he set it as a goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>. This aspiration became a driving force, leading him to excel in sports, academics, and community service (like working with the Red Cross) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:01">[00:10:01]</a>. At 16, he earned a scholarship to UWC, opening up the world to him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:21">[00:10:21]</a>.

## Military Service and Educational Pursuits

After high school, Grimeland joined the Norwegian military, eventually becoming a Navy SEAL <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a>. This experience, particularly the Seal training, was described as the "hardest period" from a mental and physical perspective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:18">[01:10:18]</a>. He learned to operate with the mindset that "there's no other alternative" than success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:05">[01:42:05]</a>. This training forged a strong sense of [[personal_growth_through_adversity | grit]] and resilience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:29">[01:04:29]</a>.

He later attended Harvard University, studying alongside figures like Mark Zuckerberg and Eduardo Saverin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:05">[01:53:05]</a>. He emphasizes that the most valuable aspect of Harvard was not the curriculum, which can be found elsewhere, but the "incredible people" and the discussions with them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:04">[01:16:04]</a>. He believes one can replicate this environment anywhere by intentionally surrounding themselves with inspiring, driven individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:01">[01:17:01]</a>.

After Harvard, he worked at McKinsey & Company for five and a half years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:16">[01:19:16]</a>. Reflecting on his journey, Grimeland suggests he might have done some things "quicker," such as spending less time at Harvard and McKinsey, and starting businesses or having children earlier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:50">[01:19:50]</a>.

## Entrepreneurial Ventures and Lessons Learned

Grimeland has an extensive entrepreneurial background. He started his first business as a child, selling eggs from chickens he raised, making "two dollars worth of eggs every day" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:16">[01:27:16]</a>. In college, he started a real estate company and an internet platform <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:54">[01:27:54]</a>.

He later founded Solora, an e-commerce company, which was eventually sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:22">[01:54:22]</a>. During the Solora days, he faced significant challenges, such as launching in Indonesia where credit card penetration was only 2-3% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:33">[01:05:33]</a>. This necessitated implementing a cash-on-delivery system, which presented its own complex logistical and trust issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:55">[01:06:55]</a>. He describes these as moments where "everything is at risk" and the problem seems "unsolvable" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:48">[01:07:48]</a>.

These experiences solidified his entrepreneurial philosophy:

*   **Front-loading**: The first year or two of a business are critical, requiring 100% effective dedication from founders to build momentum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:06">[01:31:06]</a>. He advises eliminating "wastes" like excessive Netflix, idling, drinking, and partying to focus energy on the business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:35:14">[01:35:14]</a>.
*   **Delusional Self-Belief (Ambition)**: True ambition isn't delusional; it's setting incredibly high targets, understanding you might not reach them perfectly but continually striving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:04">[01:29:04]</a>.
*   **Overcoming Fear of Failure**: Once the decision to start a business is made, eliminate the mindset that failure is an option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:47">[01:40:47]</a>. Resilient teams, not necessarily the "best" teams, are those that succeed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:16">[01:41:16]</a>. One strategy to foster this is to publicly share goals, creating social pressure to avoid abandonment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:07">[01:42:07]</a>.
*   **Hustle and Embrace Rejection**: Entrepreneurs must learn to hustle, convince people, and get comfortable with constant rejection. He cites examples of successful founders like Nike's founder and Bill Gates who "hustled" their way to initial deals without established products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:50">[01:45:50]</a>. Rejection can be transformed into "positive energy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:51">[01:49:51]</a>.

## Personal Life and Growth

Grimeland became a father at 22 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>. He notes that having children young meant he "grew up much more mature, much faster" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:48">[01:37:48]</a>. He cherishes the experience of living a "young life together" with his now 19-year-old son, sharing interests and learning from each other <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:40">[01:38:40]</a>. His parenting philosophy centers on exposing children to diverse experiences to foster inspiration rather than forcing accomplishments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:50">[01:14:50]</a>.

Grimeland has a unique perspective on loneliness, suggesting that on occasion, he "on purpose" seeks to be "a little bit more lonely" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:01">[01:21:01]</a>. He actively schedules time for self-reflection, such as taking a week-long vacation alone in the Norwegian mountains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:15">[01:22:15]</a>.

He maintains a wide circle of friends from different periods of his life – Norway, boarding school, military – spread across the globe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:32">[01:23:32]</a>. These "meaningful relationships" are cherished through intentional efforts to meet regularly, fostering "completely different conversations" free from business discussions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:02">[01:25:02]</a>.

On [[personal_hardships_and_mental_health | mental health]], Grimeland states he hasn't had major struggles but recognizes periods of fluctuating energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:28">[01:12:28]</a>. He ties his mental sharpness closely to regular physical activity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:34">[01:13:34]</a>. He emphasizes the importance of open communication within a team to identify and address warning signs of burnout, which he believes is uncorrelated to hours worked but rather to the workplace culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:10">[01:16:10]</a>.

## Defining Success and Purpose

For Grimeland, happiness and success are deeply intertwined with **purposefulness** – feeling that what he does has a "real impact" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:31">[01:30:31]</a>. He maintains a detailed personal planning system: a one-month, one-year, three-year, five-year, and ten-year plan, covering business, family, friends, and health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:31">[01:31:31]</a>. This structured approach ensures that daily actions align with long-term aspirations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:03">[01:32:03]</a>. He believes this practice leads to greater happiness and a better life trajectory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:56">[01:32:56]</a>.

His personal purpose has evolved, moving from excelling in military special forces and academics to building businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:03">[01:37:03]</a>. With Antler, he feels he has finally found his true calling: "supporting incredible people in solving important problems" and "moving the world forward" through innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:41">[01:37:41]</a>. This aligns with Antler's mission to "make progress inevitable" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:00">[01:38:00]</a>.

### Advice for Discovering Purpose

Grimeland offers three key pieces of advice for young people seeking their purpose:
1.  **Seek opportunities for inspiration**: "Get out of the box," travel, go to museums, read, listen, and meet new people to gain diverse experiences and exposure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:16">[01:39:16]</a>.
2.  **Be prepared**: Develop your strengths, build mental resilience, and cultivate drive and the ability to execute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:52">[01:39:52]</a>.
3.  **Make real decisions**: Don't passively follow a predetermined path. Actively think about where you want to go and make conscious choices to shape your life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:16">[01:40:16]</a>.

He emphasizes that the world faces "pretty big challenges" in climate change, education, and healthcare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:40">[01:42:40]</a>. His strongest conviction is that only "great people innovating" can solve these problems and ensure continued global improvement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:52">[01:43:52]</a>. He believes that AI, for example, can revolutionize education by providing more personalized learning experiences and greater access globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:43">[01:45:43]</a>.

One of the best pieces of advice he received was during his Navy SEAL training, from a former SAS teacher: choose whether you want "immediate impact on a smaller amount of people" (like a surgeon saving a life) or a "more indirect impact on a large amount of people" (like a business leader whose product affects millions) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:16">[01:49:16]</a>. This choice guided his path toward scaling impact through business.
