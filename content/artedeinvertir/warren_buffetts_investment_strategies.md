## Key Investment Techniques

### 1. Invest in Good Businesses

The first and most important principle for [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] is to invest in good businesses that can generate high returns for many years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>. This involves researching a stock and benefiting from its value generation over 10, 15, or 20 years, doing the work once <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:00">[01:15:00]</a>. Buying a poor-quality stock just because it's cheap, only to sell it quickly, does not allow for the benefit of compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:00">[01:20:00]</a>.

A "good business" is defined by its "moat," or competitive advantage—something special that allows it to prosper against competition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:00">[01:42:00]</a>. This is an intangible or qualitative aspect, requiring human thought rather than a machine-like approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a>.

*   **Examples of Good Businesses:**
    *   **American Express:** This company operates in a credit card business that is essentially a monopoly of three companies (Visa, Mastercard, American Express), unlike the thousands of steel producers globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:08:00">[03:08:00]</a>. They benefit from the "network effect"—more businesses accepting a card leads to more customers wanting that card <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:20:00">[03:20:00]</a>. American Express can raise prices because their product is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:00">[03:36:00]</a>. Historically, American Express has yielded a 1200% return, multiplying its value by 13 since 2010 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:00">[02:56:00]</a>.
    *   **Google:** [[warren_buffetts_market_insights_and_strategies | Charlie Munger]], [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]]'s partner, considers Google to have the greatest competitive advantage globally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:16:00">[04:16:00]</a>. Since going public, it has multiplied its value by almost 50 times in 15 years, continuing to grow at significant rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:26:00">[04:26:00]</a>.
    *   **Coca-Cola and Nike:** These companies possess strong brand recognition, which acts as a competitive advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:07:00">[05:07:00]</a>. They can control prices and are less affected by price increases because their products are desired by consumers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:17:00">[05:17:00]</a>.

*   **Examples of Businesses Lacking Competitive Advantage:**
    *   **U.S. Steel Corporation:** This is a typical steel manufacturing business with no unique technology or competitive advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:12:00">[02:12:00]</a>. Steel is a commodity, meaning the company doesn't control its sale price, which is dictated by supply and demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>. Demand for steel also fluctuates with economic cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:00">[02:50:00]</a>. Over ten years, US Steel yielded only 13% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:00">[02:50:00]</a>.
    *   **American Airlines, Toll Brothers, General Motors:** These companies operate in highly competitive sectors (airlines, home building, auto manufacturing) where brand loyalty is low, and they cannot freely raise prices without losing customers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37:00">[05:37:00]</a>.

To identify a good business, analyze its fundamentals, read annual reports, and consider the factors that indicate a competitive advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:43:00">[07:43:00]</a>. Companies that have performed well on the stock market for 10-20 years are typically good businesses that generate value, while those that have traded sideways for extended periods warrant caution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:49:00">[08:49:00]</a>.

### 2. Invest Only in Businesses You Understand

[[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] strongly advises investing only in businesses you understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:45:00">[09:45:00]</a>. This goes beyond a superficial understanding, requiring certainty that a company's profits will be the same or higher in the next five to ten years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:13:00">[12:13:00]</a>.

*   **Example of Understood Business: Disney:** Disney's revenue streams from movies, merchandising, and amusement parks are clear and easy to understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:10:00">[10:10:00]</a>. Its iconic franchises, like Mickey Mouse, provide a unique competitive advantage that is hard to replicate, allowing Disney to raise prices without significant impact on consumer demand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00">[10:31:00]</a>.
*   **Example of Not Understood Business: Moderna:** For many, the future of vaccine production and Moderna's long-term profitability is uncertain, making it difficult to confidently predict future earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:59:00">[12:59:00]</a>.

While it's important to expand your "circle of competence" by learning about new sectors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:17:00">[14:17:00]</a>, honesty with oneself is crucial to avoid being tempted by greed into investments one doesn't understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:25:00">[14:25:00]</a>.

### 3. When You See an Opportunity, Take Advantage of It

[[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] emphasizes seizing opportunities without hesitation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:47:00">[16:47:00]</a>. He famously states, "The stock market is a mechanism to transfer wealth from the impatient to the patient" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>. Market panics, like the one in March 2020, present opportunities for patient investors to buy high-quality stocks at undervalued prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>.

This also applies to individual stocks facing temporary problems. If an investor can determine that a problem is temporary, it can be an excellent buying opportunity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:23:00">[02:23:00]</a>.

*   **Examples of Opportunities Seized:**
    *   **Bank of America (2009-2011 financial crisis):** [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] invested heavily in Bank of America, confident that central banks would rescue banks and that the sector was understandable and essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:37:00">[18:37:00]</a>. Despite an eight-year wait for significant profits, his investment multiplied by six times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:32:00">[19:32:00]</a>.
    *   **Coca-Cola (New Coke crisis):** When Coca-Cola released "New Coke" and the stock plummeted due to public dislike, [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] recognized the temporary nature of the problem, knowing the company could revert to the original formula. He bought shares at an attractive valuation, multiplying his investment by 20 times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:21:00">[21:21:00]</a>.
    *   **Apple (2016 sales stagnation):** In 2016, [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] invested $15 billion in Apple when sales stagnated and the market reacted negatively to larger screen terminals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:55:00">[21:55:00]</a>. He recognized strong customer loyalty and the company's undervaluation. This investment has since multiplied by six times, becoming one of his main positions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:50:00">[22:50:00]</a>.
    *   **American Express (1960s scandal):** A client defrauded American Express, causing the stock to fall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:14:00">[23:14:00]</a>. [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] analyzed the situation, concluded it was an isolated incident, and invested, seeing past the negative headlines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:33:00">[23:33:00]</a>.

It's crucial not to be influenced by past price movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:36:00">[27:36:00]</a> and to distinguish between temporary and permanent problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:02:00">[28:02:00]</a>. Don't regret missing opportunities you don't understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:42:00">[28:42:00]</a>; focus on long-term discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:10:00">[14:10:00]</a>.

### 4. Do Not Be in a Hurry to Sell

[[warren_buffetts_market_insights_and_strategies | Charlie Munger]], [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]]'s partner, advises that "real money... is not found in the constant buying and selling but in waiting" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:05:00">[32:05:00]</a>. True wealth is built by finding investments that multiply many times over, patiently holding companies that generate significant value long-term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:22:00">[32:22:00]</a>.

*   **Example: Wells Fargo:** [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] invested in Wells Fargo in 1990-91 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:42:00">[32:42:00]</a>. Although it took 20 years for the stock to multiply by 10 times, the long-term patience was rewarded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:55:00">[32:55:00]</a>. This highlights that even good stocks can experience periods of sideways movement or corrections, but their underlying business fundamentals eventually drive stock performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:02:00">[34:02:00]</a>.

> "The stock market is not there to instruct you, it is there to serve you."<br>
> — [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="38:51:00">[38:51:00]</a>

This means that a stock price drop doesn't automatically mean the business is bad; it might be an opportunity to buy more if the fundamentals are strong <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:06:00">[39:06:00]</a>. Good stocks are often volatile, and their growth is never a straight line <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:24:00">[39:24:00]</a>.

### 5. Enjoy Investing, Avoid Envy and Greed

Successful investors often enjoy the mental challenge of analyzing businesses and learning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:58:00">[39:58:00]</a>. Envy and greed are considered the worst traits in the stock market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:45:00">[40:45:00]</a>.

*   **Example: Dot-com Bubble (2000):** During the dot-com bubble, many criticized [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]] because his company's shares were declining while tech stocks on the Nasdaq soared <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:40:00">[41:40:00]</a>. However, after the bubble burst, [[warren_buffetts_investment strategies and insights | Warren Buffett]]'s traditional investments proved more valuable, demonstrating the folly of chasing fads driven by envy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:02:00">[42:02:00]</a>.
*   **Example: Moody's (2015-2017):** While the broader market was rising and new trends were popular, Moody's, a fundamentally strong business, experienced sideways price movement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:14:00">[44:14:00]</a>. Despite the lack of short-term excitement, its strong fundamentals eventually led to a significant price increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:35:00">[44:35:00]</a>.

### 6. Be Sensitive to Asset Valuations

[[warren_buffetts_investment principles | Warren Buffett]] often discusses "intrinsic value," defining it as the discounted cash flows over a business's remaining life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:16:00">[46:16:00]</a>. While this is theoretical, it's a subjective value that can be estimated using various methods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:34:00">[46:34:00]</a>.

The stock price tends to replicate the intrinsic value of a business over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:21:00">[47:21:00]</a>. The goal is to buy when there is a significant difference between the intrinsic value and the market price <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:32:00">[47:32:00]</a>. Investors should focus on companies whose intrinsic value is growing consistently, indicating predictable and stable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="47:43:00">[47:43:00]</a>.

### 7. Invest in Companies with Good Management

[[warren_buffetts_investment principles | Warren Buffett]] believes that "if someone is sitting in the shade, it is because someone planted a tree a long time ago" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:54:00">[49:54:00]</a>. Good management, focused on the long term, tends to produce good benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:02:00">[50:02:00]</a>.

Indicators of good management include:
*   Managers who have been in charge for a long time (10-15 years) with a positive stock market performance during their tenure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:47:00">[50:47:00]</a>.
*   Managers who own a significant amount of shares in the business, aligning their interests with shareholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:52:00">[50:52:00]</a>. Family-run companies, for example, tend to outperform non-family companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:36:00">[50:36:00]</a>.
*   Evaluating management's actions, not just their words. Consistent achievement of growth targets is a good indicator <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:25:00">[51:25:00]</a>.
