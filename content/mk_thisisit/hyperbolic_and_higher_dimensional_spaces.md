Hyperbolic spaces are a focus of study in mathematics, particularly in the context of higher-dimensional situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:10">[01:13:10]</a>. These spaces possess a unique property that differentiates them from more commonly imagined geometries.

## Characteristics of Hyperbolic Space
A key characteristic of a hyperbolic space is its "thickness" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:01">[01:20:01]</a>. If two photons are shot into a hyperbolic space, they will spread out very quickly, diverging rapidly in all directions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:01">[01:22:01]</a>. This means that a hyperbolic space, in some sense, has "much more" directions than a Euclidean space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:01">[01:38:01]</a>. When exploring a small portion of it, the space appears to open up to a much wider world <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:01">[01:43:01]</a>. This concept is difficult to imagine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:01">[01:46:01]</a>.

## Dimensions in Physics and Mathematics
The concept of [[dimensionality_in_physics | dimensionality]] is central to understanding different spaces:
*   Our world is commonly considered three-dimensional <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:01">[01:40:01]</a>.
*   If time is included, it is considered four-dimensional <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:01">[01:42:01]</a>.
*   Some theories, like String Theory, propose an 11-dimensional universe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:01">[01:44:01]</a>, or even up to 27 dimensions if considering micro strings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:01">[02:22:01]</a>.
*   A "holographic model" in physics suggests that our perceived four-dimensional spacetime might be an illusion, and we might actually exist in one dimension less, with quantum phenomena in a three-dimensional spacetime creating the four-dimensional appearance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:01">[01:49:01]</a>.

Mathematicians often study two-dimensional objects because they are interesting yet easier to understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:01">[01:51:01]</a>. While we have a strong intuition for three-dimensional space due to living within it, four-dimensional spaces are generally difficult to imagine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:01">[02:55:01]</a>. However, one can imagine a three-dimensional space evolving in time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:00:01">[03:00:01]</a>.

### Examples of Multi-Dimensional Spaces
A simple example of a four-dimensional space is [[geometry_and_spacetime | spacetime]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:01">[01:38:01]</a>. In mathematics, multi-dimensional spaces can be constructed through "products" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:01">[01:46:01]</a>. Just as two sections can form a square (a 2D object) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:50:01">[01:50:01]</a>, two two-dimensional objects (like a donut with three holes and a donut with eleven holes) can be "folded" together to create a four-dimensional object <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:01">[02:00:01]</a>. The neighborhood of any point in such an object would resemble the product of a fragment of one donut and a fragment of the second <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:01">[02:09:01]</a>.

There are also spaces with infinitely many dimensions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:24:01">[02:24:01]</a>, though these differ slightly from finite-dimensional spaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:30:01">[02:30:01]</a>. Symmetries of all symmetries appear in these infinite-dimensional Euclidean spaces where photons fly in a straight line <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:37:01">[02:37:01]</a>.

## Current Research and Theorems
Current research focuses on higher-dimensional situations, specifically spaces that have more dimensions but retain the hyperbolic property <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:01">[01:10:01]</a>. The focus is often on odd-numbered dimensions, such as 130-dimensional spaces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:01">[01:52:01]</a>. The goal is to demonstrate that such a space always originates from a 136-dimensional space that changes over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:01">[02:06:01]</a>.

### [[mathematical_theorem_on_fibration | Fiberization Theorem]]
A significant area of research involves "fiberization" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:42:01">[04:42:01]</a>, a phenomenon where a complex object is understood as a smaller object changing over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:01">[04:57:01]</a>. For example, spacetime can be viewed as a two-dimensional surface (like the Earth's surface) evolving through a time dimension, forming a three-dimensional space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:01">[04:57:01]</a>. Each point on the surface, as it moves through time, leaves a "fiber," and the entire space is composed of these fibers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:01">[04:57:01]</a>. This concept allows mathematicians to reduce the dimension of a problem by one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:01">[04:57:01]</a>.

This research extends a theorem by Jan Ig concerning three-dimensional varieties with hyperbolic geometry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:57:01">[04:57:01]</a>. The broader approach shows that if a space possesses a specific algebraic property, it will fiberize, regardless of its initial dimension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:32:01">[05:32:01]</a>.
