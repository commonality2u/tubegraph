## [[Average grocery spending by household size in Canada | Average Grocery Spending by Household Size in Canada]] in 2025

According to the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, average annual grocery spending varies significantly by age and household size, excluding restaurant or takeout expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>.

Here are the projected average annual and monthly grocery costs for 2025:
*   **Single Adult**: Approximately $330 per month or $4,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple**: $667 per month or $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four (two adults, two kids)**: $1,403 per month or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

These figures are averages, and the actual range can be wide due to variations in consumption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>. While costs appear to double as a household grows, the jump from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. This is because feeding a family involves additional factors: kids snack, waste food, require variety, and grow quickly, leading to larger and more frequent grocery runs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>. Parents also prioritize nutrition and convenience, further increasing the bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>.

These averages serve as a useful benchmark for Canadians to compare their grocery spending against their life stage and adjust their budget realistically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

## Food Inflation Trends

Food costs have continued to worsen due to inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. The annual cost for a typical Canadian family of four has risen significantly over recent years:
*   **2022**: Saw a substantial 10.3% inflation, bringing the average cost for a family of four to $15,200, an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **2023**: Experienced a 5.9% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>.
*   **2024**: Cooled down to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
*   **2025**: The Canada Food Price Report estimates a 3% to 5% inflation forecast, projecting the annual amount for a family of four to be $16,833 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>.

Despite inflation slowing from its 2022 peak, costs are still accumulating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. A typical family is expected to spend up to $800 more on the same groceries in 2025 than in 2024, merely to maintain their standard of living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>. This is described as "inflation on autopilot, draining your budget a little more every year" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.

Driving factors behind these rising costs include climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain and logistic issues, and tariffs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>. Food inflation, even seemingly small on paper, profoundly impacts choices, quality of life, and health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>.

## Grocery Costs by Age Group

Food spending varies across a lifetime:
*   **Ages 19-30**: Spend approximately $3,861 per year or $322 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. This group often lives with roommates, splits bills, opts for cheaper options like pasta, and might skip meals to save money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **Ages 30-50**: Experience the peak in grocery spending, around $4,152 a year or $346 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. This increase is attributed to having children, less time for cooking, a preference for quality or convenience, bulk buying, and a greater emphasis on healthy eating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.
*   **Ages 50-60**: Spending remains high at $4,110 a year, nearly the same as peak years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. Habits tend to stick, and individuals may still be cooking for adult children or visiting family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.
*   **Over 70**: A clear dip in spending occurs, averaging about $3,788 per year or $316 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>. This is due to smaller portions, fewer mouths to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.

## Broader [[cost_of_living_crisis_in_canada | Cost of Living Crisis]]

The rising [[cost_of_living_crisis_in_canada | cost of living crisis]] in Canada means that nearly half of Canadians find it hard to cover basic expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. The 2025 food price report indicates that 45% of Canadians struggle with day-to-day costs, with food being a major component <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>.
*   **Impact on Consumption**: 28% of Canadians report eating less food, not just switching brands, a serious red flag <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>.
*   **Budgeting for Survival**: People are budgeting not to get ahead, but to survive, as expenses outpace even smart financial plans and income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.
*   **Income Gap**: Incomes are generally not rising by 3% to 5% in Canada, meaning the gap between earnings and expenses continues to widen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>.

## Growing [[Food insecurity and shopping habits in Canada | Food Insecurity and Shopping Habits in Canada]]

Food insecurity is a growing crisis in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>:
*   **Food Bank Usage**: Over 2 million food bank visits were recorded in March 2024, a 90% jump since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.
*   **Gen Z Impact**: Nearly half of Gen Z have had to use savings or borrow money just to afford food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>.
*   **Shifting Habits**: 48% of Canadians are actively hunting for discounts out of necessity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:32">[00:07:32]</a>. More Canadians are skipping meals, cutting non-essentials, or opting for lower quality food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>.
*   **Brand Loyalty Fading**: Brand loyalty is decreasing as affordability becomes the primary concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. Consumers are cooking more creatively and reducing waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>. Even middle-income households are feeling the strain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.

## Beef as a Luxury Item

Beef prices have been climbing, with a 9.2% increase in 2024 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. This is largely due to Canada having its smallest cattle herd since 1987, leading to less supply and higher prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>. This shift is causing Canadians to swap steak for more affordable options like chicken, pork, or lentils <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>, impacting cultural traditions around meat consumption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.

In 2024, Canadians boycotted Loblaws due to frustration over rising prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>.
