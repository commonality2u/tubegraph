## The Problem: Unused TFSA Room and Excess RRSP Balances

A significant issue contributing to higher tax bills for retirees is wasted [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA room]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>. While 17.77% of Canadians hold a [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]], only 1.54 million (8.66%) manage to max out their contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>. This indicates that the vast majority are missing out on the potential for incredible tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>. Many retirees, specifically, enter their golden years with unused [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA contribution room]], often tens of thousands of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:53">[00:00:53]</a>.

For instance, among Canadians aged 60 and older, only a small percentage fully maximize their [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA contributions]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. While 1.56 million [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] holders are between 60 and 64, only 9.48% maximize contributions in that age range <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:08">[00:01:08]</a>. Interestingly, the percentage of maximizers increases with age, peaking at 18.71% for those aged 75 to 79, before slightly dropping to 14% for those 80 and older <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>.

Another challenge arises from excess [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSP balances]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>. While [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSPs]] are beneficial for saving during working years, every dollar withdrawn is taxed as income, potentially pushing retirees into a higher tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>. Furthermore, at age 71, the CRA mandates the [[converting_rrsp_to_rrif_for_tax_efficiency | conversion of RRSPs into RRIFs]] and requires mandatory withdrawals, regardless of immediate need <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

## The Strategy: RRSP/RRIF to TFSA Transfer

The core strategy involves taking any excess funds withdrawn from an [[rrsp_withdrawal_strategies | RRSP]] (or [[converting_rrsp_to_rrif_for_tax_efficiency | RRIF]]) that are not immediately needed for living expenses and contributing them to a [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>. This approach can save thousands in the long run <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>.

### Example: Eddie's Tax-Free Growth Plan

Consider Eddie, who is 71 and has converted his [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSP]] into a [[converting_rrsp_to_rrif_for_tax_efficiency | RRIF]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>. The CRA requires him to withdraw $30,000 per year, which is taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:32">[00:02:32]</a>. In a 30% tax bracket, Eddie owes $9,000 in tax, leaving him with $21,000 after tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.

However, Eddie only needs $11,000 of that for his living expenses, as his CPP, OAS benefits, and work pensions cover most of his costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>. This leaves him with $10,000 of excess RRIF income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>.

Instead of putting this excess income into a normal savings account or a taxable non-registered investment account, Eddie contributes the entire $10,000 annually into his [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] and invests it in a portfolio of stocks and ETFs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.

Assuming an average annual return of 6%:
*   **Year 1**: Eddie contributes $10,000, growing to approximately $10,600 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>.
*   **Subsequent Years**: Eddie adds another $10,000 annually, and compounding accelerates the growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:39">[00:03:39]</a>.
*   **After 10 Years**: Eddie has contributed $100,000 to his [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]], which has grown to approximately $132,000â€”all tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>.

### Comparison: TFSA vs. Non-Registered Account

If Eddie had not used his [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] and instead invested the same $10,000 annually in a non-registered account:
*   His investments would still grow at 6% annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>.
*   However, since 30% of the gains would be lost to taxes each year, his effective growth rate would drop to around 4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.
*   After 10 years, his non-registered account would grow to approximately $120,000 after taxes, which is significantly less than the $132,000 in his [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>.
*   If he had put it in a taxable savings account earning only 1%, the amount would be much lower, closer to $55,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>.

## Key Takeaways and Pro Tips

By contributing all excess income into his [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]], Eddie saves approximately $12,000 in taxes by avoiding annual tax drag <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:39">[00:04:39]</a>. This approach maximizes retirement savings and offers flexibility to use funds for future needs or leave them untouched for continued tax-free compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:56">[00:04:56]</a>.

*   **Prioritize TFSA**: Always prioritize your [[role_of_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA]] over a taxable non-registered account if you have [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | TFSA room]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:15">[00:05:15]</a>. Let your money grow tax-free instead of losing a chunk of your gains to taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.
*   **Strategic Withdrawals**: Avoid withdrawing large [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSP amounts]] in years where you already have high income, such as from the sale of a property or other assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>.

## Does This Strategy Eliminate RRSP Taxes?

This strategy does not eliminate the tax owed on your [[rrsp_withdrawal_strategies | RRSP withdrawals]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>. [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSP withdrawals]] will still be considered taxable income and are subject to your marginal tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>.

However, by strategically combining [[rrsp_withdrawal_strategies | RRSP withdrawals]] with [[managing_taxefficient_withdrawals_from_a_tfsa | TFSA withdrawals]], you can reduce your taxable income and potentially avoid additional penalties, such as OAS clawbacks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. The key is to minimize the tax impact while maximizing overall retirement income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>.
