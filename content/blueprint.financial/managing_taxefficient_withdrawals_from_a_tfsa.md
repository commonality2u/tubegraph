## When to Withdraw: TFSA vs. RRSP First?

A common question in retirement planning is whether to withdraw from your TFSA or Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>. The answer is not "one-size-fits-all" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.

### Why Withdraw from RRSP First?

*   **Taxable Income**: RRSP withdrawals are considered taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
*   **Reduce Future Tax Burden**: Drawing down your RRSP earlier in retirement may help reduce your overall tax burden later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>.
*   **Avoid OAS Clawbacks**: This strategy can be particularly beneficial if it helps prevent Old Age Security (OAS) clawbacks, as OAS is subject to income level restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:26">[00:09:26]</a>.

### Why Prioritize TFSA Growth?

*   **Tax-Free Withdrawals**: Withdrawals from a TFSA are not taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>.
*   **Continued Tax-Free Growth**: This flexibility allows your TFSA investments to continue growing tax-free for longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>.
*   **No Impact on OAS**: TFSA withdrawals provide a tax-efficient source of funds that does not affect your OAS entitlement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:40">[00:09:40]</a>.

## Strategies for a Million-Dollar TFSA in Retirement

With a substantial TFSA, like $1 million, you want to ensure it lasts and continues to grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>.

### The Bucket Strategy

Consider implementing a "bucket strategy" for [[using_tfsa_in_retirement_planning | using TFSA in retirement planning]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:09:50]</a>. This involves dividing your TFSA into portions based on the time horizon of your needs:

*   **Short-Term Funds**: Keep these in safer investments, such as bonds or Guaranteed Investment Certificates (GICs), to cover immediate expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>.
*   **Medium-Term Funds**: Allocate these to balanced funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.
*   **Long-Term Funds**: Keep these in equities to ensure growth that combats inflation over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

This approach ensures funds are available when needed while still working for you through investment growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>.

## Coordinating Your TFSA with CPP

A strategic approach is key to managing your TFSA alongside your Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:19">[00:10:19]</a>.

*   **Supplementing Income**: Since CPP provides regular income, you can use your TFSA to supplement these amounts, particularly during years when other income sources are lower <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>.
*   **Delaying CPP**: Consider delaying CPP withdrawals while drawing from your TFSA, especially if you retire early and need income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. This allows your government benefits to grow, thereby maximizing your income later in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>.

A smart strategy for your TFSA, combined with proper risk and return management, is essential for reaching your financial goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>. The more challenging aspect often lies in figuring out the optimal [[utilizing_tfsa_and_rrsp_for_tax_efficiency | tax strategy for your TFSA]] once it has grown <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>.
