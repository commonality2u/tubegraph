The Canada Pension Plan (CPP) has undergone significant changes since 2019, including the introduction of the [[cpp_2025_enhancements_and_contribution_updates | CPP2 enhancement]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>. While some view these changes as a "scam" or "ripoff," others consider them a necessity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:08">[00:00:08]</a>. These [[cpp_2025_enhancements_and_contribution_updates | CPP enhancements]] aim to provide more secure retirement benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>.

## Key Changes and Contribution Structure

The CPP now features two tiers of contributions with updated thresholds and rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>.

### Regular CPP Contributions

[[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | Regular CPP contributions]] are calculated on earnings up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>. In 2025, the YMPE is set at $71,400 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>. Employers match employee contributions, and self-employed individuals pay the combined rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:46">[00:00:46]</a>.

### CPP2 Contributions

[[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | CPP2]] applies to earnings between the YMPE and the Year's Additional Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YAMPE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>. In 2025, the YAMPE is capped at $81,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. Earnings above the YAMPE do not incur additional CPP contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>.

### Contribution Examples for 2025

**Example 1: Mark (Earning $71,500)**
Mark's pensionable earnings are calculated by taking the YMPE ($71,400) minus a $3,500 exemption, resulting in $67,800 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>. At a 5.95% contribution rate, Mark contributes approximately $4,033, which his employer matches, leading to a total contribution of over $8,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>.

**Example 2: James (Earning $81,300)**
James's contributions include both [[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | regular CPP and CPP2]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>.
*   **Tier 1 (Regular CPP)**: Same as Mark, approximately $4,030 for earnings up to $71,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>.
*   **Tier 2 (CPP2)**: For earnings between the YMPE and YAMPE ($9,900), he contributes an additional 4%, totaling $396 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:03">[00:02:03]</a>.
James's total contribution is $4,426, matched by his employer, bringing the total to nearly $9,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:14">[00:02:14]</a>.

For those earning $81,300 or more, total contributions for both [[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | regular CPP and CPP2]] reach their caps, totaling $4,440 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>. There are no further contributions for incomes above the YAMPE of $81,300 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>.

> [!NOTE]
> [[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | CPP2]] contributions can be thought of as an add-on to a streaming subscription, providing extra future benefits for an added cost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>.

## Increased Income Replacement Rate

A key component of the [[cpp_2025_enhancements_and_contribution_updates | CPP enhancement]] is an increased income replacement rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>. Previously, CPP replaced 25% of a worker's average lifetime earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>. With the enhanced rate, this is set to rise to 33.33% (or one-third) of average work earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:26">[00:03:26]</a>. This change is being phased in over 40 years, starting in 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>.

### Impact of Gradual Implementation

Individuals retiring sooner will see a more modest increase compared to those retiring later, who will benefit from the full impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>.
*   **Retiring in 2026**: Someone earning $60,000 annually and contributing for only 7 years under the enhanced system might see an income replacement rate closer to 26% or 27%, equating to $15,600 to $16,200 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>.
*   **Retiring in 2059**: Individuals contributing for the full 40 years under the enhanced system will benefit from the maximum 33.33% replacement rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>. For someone earning $60,000 annually, this would equate to $20,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>.

## Rationale for CPP Enhancements

The primary reason for enhancing CPP is to adapt to shifts in the retirement landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a>.

### Three Pillars of Retirement Income

Canada's retirement income system traditionally relies on three pillars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>:
1.  **Government Benefits**: Including CPP, Old Age Security (OAS), and Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.
2.  **Employer-Sponsored Pension Plans**: Such as defined benefit or defined contribution plans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.
3.  **Personal Savings**: Like Registered Retirement Savings Plans (RRSPs), Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSAs), and other assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.

### Adapting to a Changing Landscape

Traditional defined benefit pension plans, which once provided significant retirement security, are now rare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. They have largely been replaced by defined contribution plans, which place investment risk on employees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>. To address this growing uncertainty and ensure more predictable income in retirement, the government is strengthening the first pillar through [[cpp_2025_enhancements_and_contribution_updates | CPP enhancements]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. These changes aim to provide a stable, [[cpp_and_inflation_considerations | inflation-adjusted]] foundation of guaranteed income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>. While personal savings and workplace pensions remain important, their volatility makes the enhanced CPP a crucial safety net, reducing dependence on uncertain sources and creating a more balanced approach to [[cpp_sustainability_and_financial_planning | retirement planning]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. The government seeks to help Canadians navigate rising costs, longer life expectancies, and evolving financial risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.

## [[criticisms_and_economic_impacts_of_cpp_enhancements | Criticisms]] of CPP Enhancements

Despite the goal of increased financial security, the [[cpp_2025_enhancements_and_contribution_updates | CPP enhancements]] face several criticisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>.

### Financial Strain on Workers and Employers

The primary concern is the added costs for workers, employers, and self-employed individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>.
*   **Workers**: Many complain about the contribution increase from 4.95% to 5.95%, further impacted by [[details_of_regular_cpp_and_cpp2_contributions | CPP2]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>. This means less take-home pay, especially when [[cpp_and_inflation_considerations | living costs and inflation]] are already high <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</a>.
*   **Employers**: Employers must match increased contributions, which can be substantial for smaller businesses, potentially leading to tough choices regarding hiring or cutting back in other areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>. There's also a hidden cost if employers reduce employee pay to offset these increases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>.
*   **Self-Employed**: Self-employed individuals are responsible for both the employee and employer portions, making cash flow management more challenging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>.

### Broader Economic Concerns

There are worries about the [[criticisms_and_economic_impacts_of_cpp_enhancements | broader economic impacts]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:14">[00:08:14]</a>. If employees have less disposable income, they might spend less, potentially slowing economic growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:20">[00:08:20]</a>. Higher payroll costs for businesses might deter growth plans or investment in new opportunities, affecting jobs and innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:28]</a>.

### Debates About Mandatory Contributions

A significant point of contention is whether mandatory contributions are the best approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>. Many argue that personal savings options, such as RRSPs and TFSAs, offer more flexibility and control, allowing individuals to decide how much to save and where to invest based on their own goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>.
