## Choosing Tax-Efficient Investments

Selecting the right investment types within taxable accounts is a straightforward way to retain more earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>.

### ETFs vs. Mutual Funds

Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs) are generally more tax-efficient than mutual funds in taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>.

*   **ETFs** generally trade assets less frequently, leading to fewer taxable events <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>.
*   **Mutual Funds**, especially actively managed ones, constantly buy and sell assets, generating tax bills for investors even if they haven't sold their holdings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>.

For investments in a taxable account, ETFs are typically the smarter choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>.

### Non-Dividend Paying Stocks and ETFs

Stocks and ETFs that do not pay annual dividends are advantageous for taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>.

*   Companies like Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway avoid paying annual dividends to prevent yearly taxable income for shareholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>.
*   Gains from these investments are only taxed upon the sale of the stock <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>.
*   Only 50% of capital gains are taxable up to $250,000 under current rules, making them significantly more tax-efficient than dividend-paying or interest-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:17">[00:01:17]</a>. For example, a $20,000 gain is taxed on only $10,000 when sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

### Permanent Life Insurance

Permanent life insurance can serve as an investment vehicle that grows funds within the policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. The payout to beneficiaries is tax-free, making it an advanced [[financial_planning_with_taxfree_accounts_for_canadians | strategy]] for estate planning to reduce taxes passed to loved ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>.

## Impact of Registered Accounts

Choosing the wrong investment accounts can cost significant amounts over time, potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>.

For instance, a [[Taxfree savings account withdrawal strategy | TFSA]] greatly outperforms a non-registered taxable account. An initial $10,000 investment growing at 7% annually over 30 years results in $76,122 in a TFSA (tax-free), versus only $42,000 in a non-registered account due to ongoing taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:15">[00:02:15]</a>. This demonstrates the power of tax-free compounding in registered accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.

## Asset Location for Taxable Accounts

[[Strategies for taxefficient investment in Canada | Tax efficiency]] is not just about the investment type but also where it's held, as different income types are taxed differently in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>.

*   **Interest Income**: This is the least tax-efficient, taxed at the full marginal tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.
*   **Canadian Dividends**: These benefit from the dividend tax credit, reducing the effective tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.
*   **Foreign Dividends and Interest**: Subject to withholding taxes that cannot be recovered in a [[utilizing_tfsa_and_rrsp_for_tax_efficiency | TFSA]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>.
*   **Capital Gains**: Only 50% of gains are taxable up to $250,000 (after which it's 2/3), generally making them more tax-efficient than interest or dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

### Strategies for Taxable Accounts

If registered accounts are maxed out, certain investments are better suited for taxable (non-registered) accounts:

*   **Canadian Dividend Stocks**: These are suitable for taxable accounts because the dividend tax credit significantly reduces the tax payable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. For example, $1,000 in Canadian dividends for someone in a 30% tax bracket might only result in $70 in taxes, compared to $300 on the same amount of interest income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>.

## Advanced Strategies for Taxable Accounts

### Splitting Capital Losses

Investment losses can be converted into [[Tax saving strategies for investments in Canada | tax savings]] by transferring capital losses between spouses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>. For instance, one spouse's $4,000 capital loss can reduce the other spouse's $15,000 capital gain, saving the couple $800 in taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. This strategy only applies to non-registered accounts, and the buyer must hold the shares for at least 30 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:31">[00:09:31]</a>.

### Tax Loss Harvesting

Tax loss harvesting involves selling investments at a loss to offset taxes on gains from other investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>.

*   If losses exceed gains, the remaining losses can be carried forward to future years or back to previous years to obtain a tax refund <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.
*   For example, a $6,000 loss can offset a $10,000 gain, meaning tax is only paid on $4,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:15">[00:10:15]</a>.
*   **Superficial Loss Rule**: Be aware that if the same stock is bought back by the individual or their spouse within 30 days of selling it, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) will disallow the loss. Instead, the loss is added to the cost of the new shares <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even with effective [[tax_saving_strategies_for_investments_in_canada | tax strategies]], simple mistakes can be costly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>:

*   **Over-contributing to Registered Accounts**: Exceeding contribution limits for TFSAs or [[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | RRSPs]] can result in penalties of 1% of the excess amount per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:59">[00:10:59]</a>. Always track limits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:08">[00:11:08]</a>.
*   **Forgetting Deadlines**: Some strategies, such as RRSP contributions, have strict deadlines (e.g., 60 days after year-end), and missing them can mean losing out on tax deductions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:13">[00:11:13]</a>.
*   **Not Planning for Withdrawals**: Withdrawals from registered accounts like RRSPs can trigger unexpected taxes. Having a plan for tax-efficient withdrawals, particularly in retirement, is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:25">[00:11:25]</a>.
