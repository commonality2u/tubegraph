Saving for a home in Canada's high-priced housing market can be challenging, but combining the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and the Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RSP) Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) offers a powerful, tax-free strategy to reach a down payment goal, potentially up to $200,000 or more for a couple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. ## [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | First Home Savings Account (FHSA)]]

The [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] is a new tool designed specifically for first-time home buyers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>.

*   **Contribution Limits**: You can contribute up to $8,000 per year, with a lifetime maximum contribution of $40,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>.
*   **Tax Deductibility**: Contributions are tax deductible, similar to an RSP, offering a tax break each year you contribute <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>.
*   **Spousal/Common-Law Partners**: If buying with a spouse or common-law partner, you can double your combined [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] savings to $80,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>.
*   **Tax-Free Withdrawals**: A key benefit is that qualifying withdrawals for a home purchase are entirely tax-free, meaning every dollar saved goes directly towards your home without repayment headaches like the RSP's HBP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

> ### Pro Tip: Spousal Contributions
> If someone else, like a spouse, contributes to your [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]], the contribution is treated as if *you* made it. This means the tax benefits, such as deductions and tax-free growth, apply to you, the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] holder, not the person who contributed. The person contributing has no tax obligation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

## Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RSP) and Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)

The RSP is a long-standing tool for [[common_retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | retirement savings]] in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:54">[00:01:54]</a>. The Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) allows you to use your RSP funds for a first-time home purchase.

*   **Contribution Limits**: You can contribute up to 18% of your income annually, or a maximum of $31,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>.
*   **HBP Withdrawal Limit**: Through the HBP, you can withdraw up to $60,000 from your RSP tax-free for a first home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.
*   **Spousal/Common-Law Partners**: If buying with a spouse, this doubles to $120,000 combined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>.
*   **Repayment Obligation**: Unlike the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]], funds withdrawn via HBP must be repaid over 15 years, starting 5 years after the withdrawal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>. Missing a repayment means that amount is added to your taxable income for the year, increasing your tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:45">[00:02:45]</a>.

## Combining FHSA and HBP for a Down Payment

By strategically combining the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and the RSP's HBP, first-time homebuyers can significantly boost their down payment savings.

### Example: Ross and Rachel

Ross and Rachel aim to buy their first home:
*   They each max out their [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSAs]], contributing $40,000 each, totaling $80,000 in tax-free savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.
*   They also tap into their RSPs through the HBP, withdrawing $60,000 each, for a combined $120,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>.
*   **Total Down Payment**: By combining their [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and HBP funds, Ross and Rachel secure $200,000 for their down payment, all tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.

Even if you don't have a partner, this strategy can help an individual save up to $100,000 for a home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>.

## Growth Potential of the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]]

The [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] also offers potential for serious wealth growth for your down payment.

*   If you contribute the maximum $8,000 per year for 5 years, reaching the $40,000 lifetime limit, and assume a 6% annual return, your account could grow to approximately $45,000 by the end of those 5 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:12">[00:04:12]</a>.
*   If that $45,000 remains in your [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] for another 10 years at the same 6% return, it could grow to around $80,000, effectively doubling your original contribution, completely tax-free when used for a home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.

*   With a 6% return over the next 10 years, their combined [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] balance could grow to over $160,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>.

## Canadian Housing Market Reality Check

The Canadian housing market, especially in major cities like Toronto and Vancouver, presents a significant challenge for first-time home buyers due to skyrocketing prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:55">[00:05:55]</a>. Even in more affordable areas, rapid price increases necessitate larger down payments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:17]</a>.

For instance, a $200,000 down payment:
*   In cities like Montreal and Calgary, this amount can cover about 32% of the total home cost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.
*   In higher-priced markets like Toronto and Vancouver, the same $200,000 might only cover around 16-19%, requiring buyers to take on much larger mortgages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>.

This highlights the critical importance of using [[retirement_tax_strategies_in_canada | tax-free savings tools]] like the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and HBP. These savings help offset larger mortgage burdens in expensive cities or make it easier to enter the market with more favorable mortgage terms in affordable cities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>. Real estate agents are optimistic about the new [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and increased HBP limit, as combining these accounts makes home ownership more achievable for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>.

## Tax Advantages of Using These Accounts

The [[retirement_tax_strategies_in_canada | tax advantages]] of the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and RSP are substantial when saving for a large down payment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.

*   **Without tax benefits**: If you needed to save $200,000 without tax-free accounts and had a 30% tax rate, you would need to earn over $285,000 to have $200,000 left after taxes. This means saving an additional $85,000 just to reach your down payment goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:07">[00:08:07]</a>.
*   **With tax benefits**: By using the [[first_home_savings_account_in_canada | FHSA]] and RSP, your contributions are tax deductible, meaning you don't pay taxes on that income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</a>. This allows you to save the full $200,000 without paying a penny in taxes on those saved funds, drastically cutting down on the overall amount you need to earn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>.
