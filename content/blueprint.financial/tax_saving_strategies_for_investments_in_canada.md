## Saving Money on Investments: Registered Accounts

Utilizing registered accounts is fundamental for long-term wealth building due to their significant tax advantages over non-registered accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.

### The Impact of Registered Accounts: An Example

Consider Mark, a 30-year-old with $30,000 to invest, splitting it evenly ($10,000 each) into a Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA), a Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RSP), and a non-registered account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>. With a 7% annual return over 30 years, and assuming a 40% working tax rate and 20% retirement tax rate, the outcomes vary significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:59">[00:00:59]</a>:

*   **TFSA**: Mark pays 40% tax upfront, leaving $6,000 to invest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:14">[00:01:14]</a>. After 30 years of tax-free growth, his TFSA is worth $45,733, with no tax on withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>.
*   **RSP**: Mark invests the full $10,000 tax-free upfront <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>. After 30 years, the account grows to $76,123 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>. With a 20% tax rate in retirement, the net value after withdrawal taxes is $60,900 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>.
*   **Non-Registered Account**: Mark invests the full $10,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>. However, annual taxes on returns reduce growth, leaving him with $31,385 after 30 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a>.

The TFSA provides 45% more, while the RSP nearly doubles the value compared to a non-registered account, thanks to tax-free or tax-deferred growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>.

For first-time home buyers, the new [[financial_planning_with_taxfree_accounts_for_canadians | First Home Savings Account (FHSA)]] combines the tax benefits of both a TFSA and an RSP, offering powerful wealth-building potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:15">[00:02:15]</a>.

## Tax-Efficient Investment Choices for Each Account

Knowing what to invest in each account is as important as contributing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.

*   **TFSA**: This account is a powerhouse for tax-free compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.
    *   **Avoid US dividend stocks and ETFs**: A 15% withholding tax cuts into returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>.
    *   **Stick with Canadian or non-dividend paying international stocks or ETFs**: These avoid the withholding tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.
*   **RSP**: Fantastic for deferring taxes on income-heavy assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>.
    *   **Best place for US dividend stocks and ETFs**: There is no withholding tax in an RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.
    *   **Plan strategically for withdrawals**: Withdrawals are taxed as regular income, especially as [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | retirement]] approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:04">[00:03:04]</a>.
*   **Non-Registered Accounts**: Flexible but the least tax-efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:11">[00:03:11]</a>.
    *   **Best for investments with favorable tax treatment**:
        *   **Canadian dividend stocks**: Benefit from the dividend tax credit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.
        *   **Non-dividend paying stocks (e.g., Berkshire Hathaway)**: Growth comes from capital gains, deferring taxes until the sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>.
    *   **Avoid interest-heavy investments (e.g., bonds or GICs)**: These are fully taxed at your marginal rate, making them less suitable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>.

There are many other tax-advantaged accounts beyond the TFSA and RSP, such as the Registered Pension Plan (RP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>. For more detailed information on the ideal investment order in Canada, consult additional resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>.

## [[Income Splitting Strategies for Canadian Taxpayers | Income Splitting]] Related to Investments

[[Income splitting strategies for Canadian taxpayers | Income splitting]] can balance taxable income between partners, leveraging lower tax brackets to save thousands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>.

*   **Using Your FHSA for Income Splitting**: The high-income partner can gift money to the low-income partner, who contributes to their FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:32">[00:04:32]</a>. This avoids taxable investments and provides a tax deduction, resulting in immediate tax savings and tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:36">[00:04:36]</a>. This is most effective when the higher-income partner has already maxed out their registered accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.
*   **Spousal RSP Contributions**: The higher-income partner can contribute to the lower-income spouse's RSP, deferring taxes and balancing [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | retirement income]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>. A key tip is to avoid early withdrawals within three years to prevent attribution back to the contributing spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>.
*   **Splitting Capital Losses**: Capital losses from one spouse can offset the other's capital gains using superficial loss rules, specifically applicable to non-registered accounts and requiring compliance with CRA rules for timing and holding periods <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>.

## [[Retirement Tax Strategies in Canada | Saving Taxes in Retirement]]

How funds are withdrawn and managed in retirement significantly impacts overall taxes paid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.

*   **RRIF Withdrawal Strategies**: Using a younger spouse's age to calculate RRIF minimums allows for smaller annual withdrawals, keeping taxable income lower for longer and potentially avoiding [[financial_strategies_for_canadian_retirees | OAS clawbacks]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:00">[00:09:00]</a>.
*   **TFSA and RSP Optimization in Retirement**: Strategic use of these accounts can manage taxable income and preserve government benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:20">[00:10:20]</a>.
    *   **Use TFSA withdrawals to avoid higher tax brackets**: TFSA withdrawals are tax-free and can supplement RSP withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>.
    *   **Shift excess RSP withdrawals to TFSA**: If a TFSA is not maxed out, transferring extra RSP funds into it reduces future RRIF withdrawals, avoids higher taxes, and provides tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:51">[00:10:51]</a>.
*   **Using Life Insurance as a Tax Saving Tool**: Permanent life insurance policies (e.g., whole or universal life) offer tax-efficient ways to transfer wealth upon death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:15">[00:11:15]</a>.
    *   **Tax-free growth and death benefit**: Policyholders can grow investments within the policy tax-free and leave a tax-free death benefit to beneficiaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:27">[00:11:27]</a>.
    *   This is especially effective for large RSPs, which are taxed at the estate level upon death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>. Shifting some RSP savings into a life insurance policy can transfer that value to beneficiaries completely tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>. The best time to obtain life insurance is while still healthy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:10">[00:12:10]</a>.

By understanding and implementing these [[strategies_for_taxefficient_investment_in_canada | tax-saving strategies for investments]], Canadians can significantly protect and grow their net worth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:11">[0:14:11]</a>.
