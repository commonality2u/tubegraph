## 1. Choose Tax-Efficient Investments in Taxable Accounts

Selecting the right investments for your taxable accounts is a straightforward way to keep more of your money growing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>.

### ETFs vs. Mutual Funds

Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs) are generally more tax-efficient than mutual funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>. ETFs typically trade assets less frequently, resulting in fewer taxable events for you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>. Actively managed mutual funds, however, frequently buy and sell assets, passing the resulting tax bills to you, even if you haven't sold your shares <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>. For taxable accounts, ETFs are usually the smarter choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>.

### Non-Dividend Paying Stocks and ETFs

Consider investments that do not pay annual dividends, such as Berkshire Hathaway <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. This approach avoids yearly taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. Gains from these investments are only taxed when you sell the stock, and only 50% of capital gains are taxable up to $250,000 under new rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>. This makes them significantly more tax-efficient than dividend-paying or interest-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:25">[00:01:25]</a>. For instance, a $20,000 gain is taxed on only $10,000, and only upon sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

### Permanent Life Insurance

Permanent life insurance offers more than just protection; it can also grow investments within the policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. The payout to beneficiaries is tax-free, making it an advanced strategy for estate planning to reduce taxes passed to loved ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>.

## 2. Prioritize the Right Registered Accounts

Choosing the correct investment accounts to prioritize can result in significant savings, potentially tens or even hundreds of thousands of dollars over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>. This decision makes a substantial difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

### TFSA vs. Taxable Account Example

Consider Jason, 30 years old, who invests $10,000 over 30 years at a 7% annual return, with a 30% tax rate on his non-registered account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:15">[00:02:15]</a>.

*   **TFSA:** His $10,000 investment grows tax-free to $76,122 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:31">[00:02:31]</a>.
*   **Non-Registered Account:** His $10,000 investment grows to only $42,000 due to ongoing taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:45">[00:02:45]</a>.

The [[taxfree_savings_account_tfsa_strategies | TFSA]] outperforms the non-registered account by $34,122, nearly doubling the value due to the power of tax-free compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>.

### Order of Priority for Investing

To maximize savings and minimize taxes, follow this order of investment priority:

1.  **RSP with Employer Matching:** Always maximize employer matching first, as it's essentially free money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>. For example, a 50% match up to $1,500 means an easy $750 added to your savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>.
2.  **RESP (Registered Education Savings Plan):** If saving for a child's education, the RESP offers a 20% government match up to $500 per year per child, with a lifetime maximum of $7,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>. Lower-income families may also receive the Canada Learning Bond, adding up to $2,000 without requiring contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>.
3.  **FHSA (First Home Savings Account):** First-time home buyers should prioritize the FHSA, which offers tax-deductible contributions and tax-free withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:56">[00:03:56]</a>. Funds can grow tax-free or be transferred to an RSP later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.
4.  **TFSA or RSP:** These are the most common choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:12">[00:04:12]</a>.
    *   The [[investment_strategies_for_tfsa | TFSA]] is ideal for flexibility, emergencies, or lower-income years, allowing tax-free withdrawals at any time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>. When in doubt, contribute to your [[tfsa_investment_strategies | TFSA]] due to its versatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>.
    *   The RSP is best for higher incomes due to tax-deductible contributions and tax-deferred growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>. Both RSP and FHSA come with withdrawal restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>.

The final priority order is: RSP with employer matching, RESP, FHSA, and then TFSA or RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>. For more detail, refer to resources on [[optimizing_investment_accounts_in_canada | optimizing investment accounts in Canada]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>.

## 3. Optimize Your Asset Location

Tax efficiency also depends on where you hold your investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>. Different income types (interest, dividends, capital gains) are taxed differently in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.

### Tax Rules for Income Types

*   **Interest Income:** Taxed at your full marginal tax rate, making it the least tax-efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>.
*   **Canadian Dividends:** Benefit from the dividend tax credit, reducing your effective tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.
*   **Foreign Dividends and Interest:** Subject to withholding taxes, which cannot be recovered in a TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>.
*   **Capital Gains:** Only 50% of gains are taxable up to $250,000 (after that, 2/3), making them more tax-efficient than interest or dividends in most cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

### Key Asset Location Strategies

*   **RSP for US Dividend-Paying Stocks:** Hold US dividend stocks in your RSP to avoid the 15% withholding tax on US dividends, thanks to the Canada-US tax treaty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>. This ensures you receive the full dividend amount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.
*   **TFSA for High-Growth Investments:** Use your TFSA to shelter capital gains and allow high-growth investments to compound tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>. Avoid foreign dividend stocks in a TFSA if possible, as withholding taxes apply and cannot be recovered, leading to a loss of up to 15% of your dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>. For example, Apple stock in a TFSA would yield less dividend than in an RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>.
*   **Taxable Accounts for Canadian Dividend Stocks:** If registered accounts are maxed out, Canadian dividend-paying stocks are a good option for taxable non-registered accounts due to the dividend tax credit, which significantly reduces taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. For example, $1,000 in Canadian dividends for someone in a 30% tax bracket might result in only about $70 in taxes, compared to $300 on the same amount of interest income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>.
*   **Registered Accounts for Interest-Generating Investments:** Place investments that generate income or interest, such as GICs or bonds, in RSPs or TFSAs to avoid high taxes on interest income in taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>.

## 4. Income Splitting

[[tax_planning_strategies_for_canadian_families_and_business_owners | Income splitting]] is a more advanced strategy to reduce a household's overall tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:49">[00:07:49]</a>.

### FHSA for Income Splitting

While primarily for home savings, the FHSA can help couples save on taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:54">[00:07:54]</a>. This applies to couples where one partner has maxed out their registered accounts (RSP, TFSA, FHSA) and the other has unused FHSA room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>. The higher-income partner can gift money to the lower-income partner, who then contributes it to their FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. This avoids investing in a taxable account, allows for a tax deduction, and enjoys tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>. For example, if Jim gifts $8,000 to Pam's unused FHSA, Pam gets a $2,400 tax deduction and avoids $132 in taxes on growth, saving the couple $2,532 total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.

### Spousal RSP Contributions

Spousal RSPs help balance retirement incomes and reduce taxes for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>. The higher-income spouse contributes to the lower-income spouse's RSP, receiving an immediate tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>. In retirement, the lower-income spouse withdraws the funds at their lower tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>. For example, if Jim contributes $12,000 to Pam's RSP, Jim's taxes decrease by $4,200. When Pam withdraws, she pays only $2,400 in taxes, saving the couple $1,800 overall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>.

### Splitting Capital Losses

You can turn investment losses into tax savings by transferring capital losses between spouses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. For example, Jim's $4,000 capital loss can reduce Pam's $15,000 in capital gains, saving the couple $800 in taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>. This strategy only works in non-registered accounts, and the buyer must hold the shares for at least 30 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.

## 5. Tax Loss Harvesting

[[tax_strategies_for_investments_in_canada | Tax loss harvesting]] helps save on taxes by using losing investments to offset gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>. If you sell an investment at a loss, you can use that loss to reduce taxes on gains from other investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>. If losses exceed gains, you can carry those losses forward to future years or back to previous years for a tax refund <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:05">[00:10:05]</a>. For instance, if you had a $10,000 stock gain, selling another stock with a $6,000 loss would offset part of the gain, meaning you're only taxed on $4,000 instead of $10,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.

### Superficial Loss Rule

Beware of the Superficial Loss Rule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. If you or your spouse buy back the same stock within 30 days of selling it, the CRA will not allow you to claim the loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:33">[00:10:33]</a>. Instead, the loss is rolled into the cost of your new shares <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:41">[00:10:41]</a>.

## Bonus Tips to Avoid Costly Mistakes

Even the best [[retirement_tax_strategies_in_canada | tax strategies]] can fail if simple mistakes are made <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>.

*   **Over-contributing to Registered Accounts:** Exceeding contribution limits for your TFSA or RSP can lead to hefty penalties of 1% of the excess amount each month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:58">[00:10:58]</a>. Always track your limits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:07">[00:11:07]</a>.
*   **Forgetting About Deadlines:** Some strategies, like RSP contributions, have strict deadlines (e.g., 60 days after year-end) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:12">[00:11:12]</a>. Missing these can mean losing tax deductions for the year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:19">[00:11:19]</a>.
*   **Not Planning for Withdrawals:** Withdrawals from registered accounts like RSPs can trigger unexpected taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:25">[00:11:25]</a>. Have a plan to manage these withdrawals [[tax_efficient_strategies_for_withdrawing_from_a_tfsa | tax-efficiently]], especially in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:30">[00:11:30]</a>.

Implementing these strategies can save you thousands in taxes and significantly boost your wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>.
