## Average Grocery Spending by Household Type

According to the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, average annual grocery spending (excluding restaurants or takeout) varies by household type <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>:
*   **Single Adult:** Approximately $330 per month or $4,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple:** Around $667 per month or $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four (two adults, two kids):** About $1,403 per month or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

While costs appear to double as a household grows, the jump from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. This is attributed to children's snacking, food waste, need for variety, and rapid growth, as well as parents prioritizing nutrition and convenience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>. These figures serve as a useful benchmark for personal budgeting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

## Impact of Food Inflation

Food costs have worsened significantly in recent years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>:
*   **2022:** A 10.3% [[impact_of_inflation_on_food_prices_in_canada | inflation]] rate brought the average cost for a family of four to $15,200, an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.
*   **2023:** Continued high [[impact_of_inflation_on_food_prices_in_canada | inflation]] with a 5.9% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.
*   **2024:** Cooled down to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
*   **2025 (Projected):** An estimated 3% to 5% [[impact_of_inflation_on_food_prices_in_canada | inflation]] forecast, projecting an annual cost of $16,833 for a family of four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>.

Despite a slowdown in [[impact_of_inflation_on_food_prices_in_canada | inflation]] since its 2022 peak, costs continue to rise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. A typical family is expected to spend up to $800 more in 2025 than in 2024 just to maintain the same standard of living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>. This "inflation on autopilot" is driven by climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain issues, and tariffs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

## Grocery Spending by Age Group

Grocery costs also vary across different age groups, reflecting evolving lifestyles and priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:46">[00:03:46]</a>:

*   **Ages 19-30:** This youngest group spends approximately $322 per month or $3,861 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. Spending is typically lower due to living with roommates, splitting bills, and opting for cheaper alternatives like pasta or no-name brands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **Ages 30s-50s (Peak Spending):** Canadians in this age bracket spend the most on groceries, at about $346 per month or $4,152 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. This increase is often due to having kids, less time to cook, a preference for quality or convenience, bulk buying, and increased awareness of nutrition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:32">[00:04:32]</a>.
*   **Ages 50s-60s:** Spending remains nearly the same as the peak years, at $4,110 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. Habits tend to stick, and individuals may still be cooking for adult children, visiting family, or simply unwilling to scale back <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.
*   **Ages 70+:** A clear dip in spending is observed, averaging about $316 per month or $3,788 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:00">[00:05:00]</a>. This is attributed to smaller portions, fewer mouths to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>. However, even in this age range, costs are not significantly low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:11">[00:05:11]</a>.

## Broader Economic Challenges

The rising cost of living, especially food, is a significant challenge for many Canadians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.
*   **Struggling to Cover Expenses:** 45% of Canadians report struggling with day-to-day costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.
*   **Food Insecurity:** 28% of Canadians are eating less food, not just switching brands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>. In March 2024, there were over 2 million food bank visits, a 90% increase since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>.
*   **Financial Strain:** Nearly half of Gen Z have dipped into savings or borrowed money just to eat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>. 48% of Canadians actively hunt for discounts to get by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.
*   **Income vs. Expenses:** Incomes are generally not rising at the same rate as food prices (3-5% increase projected for 2025), causing the gap between earnings and expenses to widen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>. More information on this can be found in discussions on [[average_income_in_canada_by_age | Canadian incomes by age]].

## Geographic Variation in Food Prices

Where one lives significantly affects food costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</a>. For example, Quebec is projected to see the smallest increase in food prices at 1.8%, while Newfoundland could see the highest at 3.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>. Other provinces hover around 3% increases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>.

## Shifting Consumer Habits and Responses

Consumers are adapting to rising costs:
*   **Brand Loyalty Fades:** Affordability is now emphasized over brand loyalty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>.
*   **Cooking Habits:** Many are cooking more creatively and reducing waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>.
*   **Dietary Changes:** With beef prices rising by 9.2% in 2024 due to the smallest cattle herd since 1987, many Canadians are swapping steak for chicken, pork, or lentils <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. This shift impacts not only meals but also cultural traditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.
*   **Shopping Behavior:** Shoppers are visiting smaller stores, comparing prices, and returning to farmers' markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
*   **Loblaws Boycott:** In 2024, a boycott of Loblaws by Canadians frustrated with rising prices symbolized widespread dissatisfaction and a desire for change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>.
