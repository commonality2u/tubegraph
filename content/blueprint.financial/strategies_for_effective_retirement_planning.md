## Current Trends in Canadian Retirement

Canadians are increasingly working past the traditional retirement age of 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>. In 1997, the average retirement age was 60.9 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>, but by 2024, it has risen to 65.3 years, the highest in nearly half a century <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. More than half of retirees are now leaving the workforce after age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>. In 2022, 21% of Canadians aged 65 to 74 were still employed, with 12% working by choice and 9% due to financial necessity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>.

The average retirement ages vary by sector:
*   Public sector employees: around 63 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>
*   Private sector workers: around 65.5 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>
*   Self-employed Canadians: nearly 69 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a>

## Reasons for Later Retirement and Planning Implications

Several factors contribute to Canadians working longer:

### 1. Increased Life Expectancy
Life expectancy is not fixed; it increases the older you get <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. For example, a 65-year-old in good health can expect two or more additional decades of life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>. This means [[financial_planning_for_retirement | retirement planning]] must account for a 20- to 30-year lifestyle rather than a 10-year plan, requiring more funding and management of health and time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.

### 2. Changes in Pension Coverage
Defined benefit pensions, which offered predictable lifetime income, are less common, particularly in the private sector <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>. Defined contribution plans, where retirement income depends on investment performance, are more prevalent, shifting more risk and responsibility to the individual <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>. Only 37.5% of paid workers in Canada are covered by a workplace pension, meaning the majority rely on personal savings vehicles like RRSPs and TFSAs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.

### 3. Rising Cost of Living
Retirees face increasing costs without a matching boost in income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>:
*   **Shelter costs**: Up 6.2% year-over-year as of February 2024, with mortgage interest rates up over 26% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</a>.
*   **Groceries**: Prices have jumped over 20% since 2020 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:55">[00:04:55]</a>.
*   **Healthcare**: While public healthcare exists, out-of-pocket costs for dental, glasses, physio, and prescriptions rise with age <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:05">[00:05:05]</a>.
*   **Taxes**: Can significantly reduce retirement income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

### 4. Less Physical Nature of Work
Many jobs have transitioned from physically demanding labor to knowledge-based or service roles, often involving desk work or computers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. If work is not physically taxing and remains mentally engaging, individuals may choose to continue working longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>.

### 5. Changing Views on Retirement
For many, retirement is seen less as a complete stop and more as a flexible transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>. It can involve part-time consulting, freelance work, or starting a dream business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>. Work also provides structure, identity, and social connection that many retirees miss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:01">[00:07:01]</a>.

### 6. Divorce and Late-Life Breakups
"Gray divorce" among Canadians over 50 has been rising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>. These late-life separations often necessitate a major financial reset, including asset division and supporting multiple households, leading many to delay retirement or re-enter the workforce <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:30">[00:07:30]</a>.

## Addressing Uncertainty in Retirement Planning

> [!NOTE] Uncertainty is a significant concern for retirees, even with sufficient savings on paper <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>. Market volatility, unpredictable housing costs, persistent inflation, rising healthcare expenses, and geopolitical tensions contribute to anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>. Many Canadians delay retirement not out of necessity, but to achieve "peace of mind" and build a larger financial buffer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.

However, delaying retirement indefinitely can come at a cost, as time becomes more valuable in later years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>. The goal of [[strategies_for_financial_planning_in_retirement | financial planning for retirement]] should be to enable individuals to retire as early as safely possible to enjoy healthy years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>.

## Effective Retirement Planning Strategies Illustrated

A concrete scenario using financial planning software demonstrates how various strategies can impact retirement outcomes:

### Scenario: Keanu's Retirement
Keanu is a 65-year-old single individual aiming for a modest lifestyle with $50,000 in annual spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>. He earned $90,000 annually and has $400,000 in his RRSP and $100,000 in his TFSA. His home is paid off, but he lacks a corporate pension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:39">[00:09:39]</a>. CPP (Canada Pension Plan) and OAS (Old Age Security) are assumed to start at 65.

1.  **Initial Plan: Retiring Cold Turkey at 65**
    If Keanu retires at 65 and immediately begins collecting CPP and OAS, he would run out of money by approximately age 83 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:55">[00:09:55]</a>. His RRSP would deplete quickly, and his TFSA would not cover the remaining years, leading to a negative net worth by his mid-80s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:07">[00:10:07]</a>.
    > Lesson: Even substantial savings ($500,000) may not be enough for 25+ years of retirement without a solid plan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>.

2.  **Strategy 1: Working a Few More Years**
    If Keanu works three more years (until age 68), earning $90,000 annually and continuing to save, while still taking CPP and OAS at 65, his money would easily last through age 90, with a net estate exceeding $400,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>.
    > Lesson: Just three additional years of work can provide over a decade of financial security in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43">[00:10:43]</a>.

3.  **Strategy 2: Delaying CPP (Without a Smart Withdrawal Strategy)**
    If Keanu works only two more years (until age 67) but delays starting CPP until age 70 (locking in a higher monthly benefit), he still faces a shortfall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:52">[00:10:52]</a>. Aggressively drawing from his RRSP between ages 67 and 69 to cover the gap drains it too quickly, leaving less for his later years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.
    > Lesson: Delaying CPP is powerful, but without a [[rrsp_withdrawal_strategies_for_retirement | smart withdrawal strategy]] for interim expenses, it can backfire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:15">[00:11:15]</a>.

4.  **Strategy 3: Combined Approach (Working Longer + Delayed CPP + Strategic RRSP Withdrawal)**
    Keanu retires at 67 and delays CPP to 70 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:23">[00:11:23]</a>. This time, he draws gradually from his RRSP between ages 67 and 69, just enough to cover his needs while keeping taxes low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:29">[00:11:29]</a>. This combined approach results in a steady income throughout retirement, a lower lifetime tax bill, and a sizable estate of over $500,000 remaining by age 90 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:36">[00:11:36]</a>.
    > Lesson: The right sequencing of payments and withdrawals is powerful. By tweaking factors like CPP timing, smart withdrawals, and working slightly longer, a viable plan can be built without doubling savings or working excessively longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:11">[00:12:11]</a>.

Effective [[comprehensive_financial_planning_for_retirement | retirement planning]] involves considering many moving parts, including taxes, OAS clawback, draw-down strategies, inflation, health costs, and estate planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>. Utilizing financial planning software and expert advice can help individuals understand their options and achieve greater financial freedom <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:28">[00:12:28]</a>.
