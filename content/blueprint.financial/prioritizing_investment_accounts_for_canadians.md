## The Big Three: FHSA, RRSP, and TFSA

Choosing between the First Home Savings Account (FHSA), Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP), and Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) is a common challenge for investors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>.

### First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

For those saving for a first home, the FHSA is often the top priority due to its unique combination of benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>:
*   **Dual Tax Benefits**: It offers tax-deductible contributions, similar to an RRSP, and tax-free withdrawals, like a TFSA, provided the funds are used for a home purchase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>.
*   **Flexibility**: Even if a home purchase isn't immediate, the FHSA allows for tax-free investment growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. Funds can be easily transferred to an RRSP if financial goals change later, adapting to future needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>.

> [!TIP] Income Splitting
> A higher-income spouse can gift up to $8,000 annually to a lower-income spouse, who can then contribute it to their FHSA. The growth remains tax-free, and the lower-income spouse receives a tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>.

> [!WARNING] Loss of Contribution Room
> Withdrawing from an FHSA for a non-qualifying purpose (e.g., buying a car) results in the permanent loss of that contribution room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>. For emergencies, a TFSA is generally preferred over an FHSA due to this inflexibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:59">[00:01:59]</a>.

### TFSA vs. RRSP: Choosing Between Them

The choice between a TFSA and an RRSP depends on individual income goals and the need for flexibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>.

#### Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)

The TFSA is renowned for its flexibility and simplicity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>.
*   **Flexible Withdrawals**: Funds can be withdrawn tax-free at any time without penalties, making it ideal for both short-term goals (e.g., vacations, big purchases, emergency funds) and long-term investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>.
*   **Simplicity**: It has no complex withdrawal rules or tax implications, provided contribution limits are respected <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. Contributions can be made, withdrawn, and recontributed the following year within the contribution room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>.

The TFSA should be prioritized when:
*   Easy access to funds without penalty is desired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:12">[00:03:12]</a>.
*   Saving for short-term goals like vacations, large purchases, or emergencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>.
*   In a lower-income year, where RRSP tax deductions would yield less benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.
*   Seeking a simple way to grow investments tax-free without locking in funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:28">[00:03:28]</a>.

#### Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)

The RRSP is designed for [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | long-term retirement savings]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>.
*   **Tax Deductions**: Contributions offer immediate tax deductions, reducing taxable income in the present, while investments grow tax-free until retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.
*   **High-Income Earners**: It is a must for higher-income earners who benefit most from the tax deductions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:49">[00:03:49]</a>.
*   **Government Benefits**: RRSPs can be used to control income in key years, potentially helping qualify for government benefits like the Canada Child Benefit or GST/HST credits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.

The RRSP should be prioritized when:
*   Having a higher income and needing tax deductions to reduce taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:58">[00:03:58]</a>.
*   The employer offers RRSP matching contributions, as this is "free money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>.

In summary, for general [[financial_planning_with_taxfree_accounts_for_canadians | financial planning with tax-free accounts]], choose the TFSA first if you need flexibility, are in a lower income year, or want a simple tax-free way to save for both short and long-term goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>. Choose the RRSP first if you have a higher income and want immediate tax savings or if your employer offers RRSP matching <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.
Overall, the FHSA is often recommended first, followed by either the TFSA or RRSP, depending on individual circumstances. When in doubt, the TFSA is generally a safe choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>.

## Important Exceptions: Accounts with "Free Money"

Beyond the "Big Three," several other accounts offer significant benefits, often in the form of government grants or employer contributions, which should be prioritized even before the FHSA or TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.

### Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP)

The RDSP offers substantial support for Canadians with disabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a>:
*   **Canada Disability Savings Grant (CDSG)**: Matches 300% on the first $500 contributed and 200% on the next $1,000, allowing for up to $3,500 per year in grants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.
*   **Canada Disability Savings Bond (CDSB)**: Provides up to $1,000 annually without requiring contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>.
    *   *Example*: Contributing $1,500 to an RDSP could yield $3,500 from the CDSG and $1,000 from the CDSB, totaling $4,500 in free government support annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.
This makes the RDSP a powerful tool for [[financial_planning_with_taxfree_accounts_for_canadians | financial planning with tax-free accounts]] and building security for loved ones with disabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:51">[00:05:51]</a>.

### Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP)

RESPs help families save for post-secondary education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>:
*   **Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG)**: Matches 20% of contributions up to $500 per child annually, with a lifetime maximum of $7,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>.
    *   *Example*: Contributing $2,500 annually would result in a $500 grant from the CESG, an instant 20% return on investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:12">[00:06:12]</a>.
*   **Canada Learning Bond (CLB)**: Lower-income families can receive up to $2,000 without requiring contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>.
Starting early with an RESP maximizes growth, especially with compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>.

### RRSP Employer Matching

If your employer offers RRSP matching, maximizing these contributions is crucial as it's essentially "free money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40">[00:06:40]</a>:
*   *Example*: If an employer matches 50% of contributions up to $1,500, contributing $3,000 yields an instant 50% return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>.
Employer matching accelerates retirement savings and fosters saving discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>. Always maximize RRSP matching if available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>.

These three accounts (RDSP, RRSP employer matching, RESP) should be prioritized before the TFSA or FHSA due to the "free money" advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.

## Non-Registered Accounts

Non-registered accounts are the final destination after maximizing contributions to FHSA, TFSA, and RRSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>. While they lack the tax advantages of registered accounts, careful asset selection can help minimize [[hidden_investment_taxes_in_canada | hidden investment taxes in Canada]] and allow for wealth growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>.

To optimize [[tax_saving_strategies_for_investments_in_canada | tax saving strategies for investments in Canada]] within non-registered accounts:
*   **Avoid Interest Income**: Interest income is taxed at the highest possible rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>.
*   **Focus on Growth Stocks**: Growth stocks with no dividends (e.g., Shopify, Nvidia, Amazon) are ideal because capital gains are taxed at a lower rate and only when the assets are sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:56">[00:07:56]</a>. Always conduct due diligence before investing in higher-risk growth stocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a>.
*   **Consider Canadian Dividend Stocks**: Take advantage of the Canadian dividend tax credit to lower taxes on eligible dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:14">[00:08:14]</a>.
    *   *Example*: A $1,000 dividend is grossed up to $1,380, resulting in a 15% federal tax credit that reduces taxes by $207. Provincial credits provide further relief <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>.
*   **Avoid US Dividend Paying Stocks**: These face withholding taxes and do not qualify for the Canadian dividend tax credit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>.

## Final Order of Priority for Investments

When [[prioritizing_contributions_to_taxadvantaged_accounts | prioritizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts]] and making the most of savings while minimizing taxes, here is a general order of priority for [[selecting_the_right_investment_accounts_in_canada | selecting the right investment accounts in Canada]], assuming eligibility for each <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>:

1.  **RDSP (if qualified)**: Offers the most generous government grants for disability savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:57">[00:08:57]</a>.
2.  **RRSP with Employer Matching**: Prioritize for free employer contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.
3.  **RESP**: Provides a 20% government match (up to $500/year) for education savings and can act as a form of income splitting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>.
4.  **FHSA**: Best for first-time home buyers with tax-deductible contributions and tax-free withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:15">[00:09:15]</a>.
5.  **RRSP or TFSA**:
    *   **RRSP**: For higher income earners seeking tax deductions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:23">[00:09:23]</a>.
    *   **TFSA**: For flexibility and tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:26">[00:09:26]</a>.
6.  **Non-Registered Accounts**: The last option, focusing on growth stocks or Canadian dividend stocks for [[strategies_for_taxefficient_investment_in_canada | tax-efficient investment in Canada]], while avoiding US dividend stocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:33">[00:09:33]</a>.

Remember, the optimal account choice depends on your personal financial goals, income, and savings needs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>.
