## RRIF Withdrawal Strategies

To minimize tax income, mandatory [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | RRIF]] withdrawals can be managed to avoid pushing you into a higher tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:58">[00:08:58]</a>.
Using the younger spouse's age to calculate [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | RRIF]] minimums allows for less annual withdrawal, keeping taxable income lower for a longer period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:03">[00:09:03]</a>.
For example, if Sarah is 70 and her spouse is 65, using the spouse's age for [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | RRIF]] calculations reduces the minimum withdrawal amount, helping Sarah maintain a lower tax bracket and potentially avoid OAS clawback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>.

## Tax Credits for Retirees

[[Basic Tax Credits for Retirees in Canada | Retirees]] can significantly lower their tax bills by claiming specific credits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:23">[00:09:23]</a>.

### Age Amount Credit
Canadians age 65 or older may claim an additional $9,28 as a non-refundable tax credit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>. This credit begins to phase out for incomes exceeding $44,355 and phases out entirely at higher income levels, requiring strategic tax planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>.

### Pension Income Credit
Up to $2,000 of eligible pension income can be claimed, offering a federal tax credit of 15% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:49">[00:09:49]</a>. This credit applies to income sources such as [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | RRIF]] withdrawals or employer pensions, but it does not cover CPP or OAS benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:56">[00:09:56]</a>.
For instance, if Mary, age 67, has a taxable income of $40,000, claiming the age amount credit ($9,28) and the pension income credit ($2,000) reduces her taxable income by $1,028 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.

## TFSA and RSP Optimization in Retirement

Strategic use of your [[Role of TFSA in retirement planning | TFSAs]] and RSP in [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | retirement]] can help manage taxable income and preserve government benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:20">[00:10:20]</a>.

### TFSA Withdrawals to Avoid Higher Tax Brackets
[[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]] withdrawals are tax-free, making them an ideal supplement to RSP withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>. For example, John might withdraw $10,000 from his RSP and $20,000 from his [[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]] to meet his $30,000 income needs, thereby avoiding a higher tax bracket because the $20,000 from his [[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]] is not taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:36">[00:10:36]</a>.

### Shifting Excess RSP Withdrawals to TFSA
Many individuals retire without having maximized their [[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]] contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:51">[00:10:51]</a>. You can move any extra RSP funds into a [[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]], which reduces future [[Retirement planning strategies in Canada | RRIF]] withdrawals, avoids higher taxes, and provides tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:58">[00:10:58]</a>. For example, Sarah could withdraw $60,000 from her RSP and contribute $20,000 to her [[Role of TFSA in Retirement Planning | TFSA]] to reduce future taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:06">[00:11:06]</a>.

## Income Splitting in Retirement

Income splitting allows couples to balance the tax load by shifting income from a higher-income partner to a lower-income partner, taking advantage of lower tax brackets and saving thousands of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:59">[00:03:59]</a>.

### Pension Income Splitting
[[Pension income splitting in Canada | Shifting pension income]] to a lower-income spouse helps balance taxable income and reduces household taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>.
*   **Eligibility Rules**: Before age 65, only defined benefit pension plans (DBPPs) qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>. After age 65, defined contribution pension plans, RRIFs, and LIRAs also qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a>.
*   **Example**: If Jerry earns $40,000 from his pension, taxed at 30%, allocating $20,000 to Elaine (who is taxed at 15%) reduces household taxes from $12,000 to $9,000, saving $3,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>.

### Spousal RSP Contributions
Spousal RSPs allow the higher-income partner to contribute to the lower-income spouse's account, deferring taxes and balancing [[financial_planning_for_canadian_retirees | retirement income]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.
*   **Example**: Jerry contributes $12,000 to Elaine's RSP, reducing his taxable income by $4,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>. When Elaine withdraws the funds at a 20% tax rate, they save $1,800 in total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>.
*   **Pro Tip**: Avoid early withdrawals within three years of contribution to prevent attribution back to the contributing spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>.

### CPP Income Splitting
Couples receiving CPP can split up to 50% of their benefits, lowering their overall tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>.
*   **Example**: Jerry shifts $3,000 of his CPP income to Elaine, saving $450 in taxes by taking advantage of her lower tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>.

## Using Life Insurance as a Tax-Saving Tool

Permanent life insurance policies, such as whole or universal life, offer tax-efficient ways to transfer wealth upon death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:15">[00:11:15]</a>. Policyholders can grow investments within the policy tax-free and leave a tax-free death benefit to beneficiaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:25">[00:11:25]</a>.
*   **Example**: Sarah uses a whole life policy to grow $100,000 tax-free, which will transfer to her heirs completely tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:32">[00:11:32]</a>.
*   This is particularly effective because upon death, an entire estate is deemed disposed of and will be taxed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:42">[00:11:42]</a>. Shifting some RSP savings into a life insurance policy can transfer that value to beneficiaries completely tax-free, especially considering a large RSP might otherwise be taxed at a 40% or 50% rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a>.
*   **Pro Tip**: This strategy's effectiveness depends on health, and the best time to acquire life insurance is while still healthy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:09">[00:12:09]</a>.
