## [[income_splitting_strategies | Income Splitting Strategies]]

[[income_splitting_strategies | Income splitting]] involves distributing income among family members, particularly spouses, to take advantage of lower tax brackets and reduce the overall household tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:49:15"></a>.

### [[fhsa_for_income_splitting | FHSA for Income Splitting]]

The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) can be used by couples as an [[income_splitting_strategies | income splitting strategy]] beyond just saving for a home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:54:33"></a>.

*   **Who it's for:** Couples where one partner has maximized their tax-advantaged accounts (like RRSP, TFSA, FHSA) and the other has unused FHSA contribution room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:00:06"></a>.
*   **How it works:** The higher-income partner can gift money to the lower-income partner, who then contributes it to their FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:11:00"></a>.
*   **Benefits:** This strategy allows the couple to avoid investing in a taxable account, claim a tax deduction, and enjoy tax-free growth within the FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:16:00"></a>.
*   **Example:** If Jim gives $8,000 to Pam, who has unused FHSA room, Pam can receive a $2,400 tax deduction and avoid $132 in taxes on growth, with all future growth being tax-free, leading to total savings of $2,532 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:24:00"></a>.

### Spousal RRSP Contributions

[[rrsp_income_splitting_for_tax_efficiency | Spousal RRSPs]] help balance retirement incomes for couples and reduce taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:00"></a>.

*   **How it works:** The higher-income spouse contributes to the lower-income spouse's RRSP, immediately receiving a tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:47:00"></a>.
*   **Benefit in retirement:** When the lower-income spouse withdraws the funds in retirement, they do so at their lower tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:53:00"></a>.
*   **Example:** Jim contributes $12,000 to Pam's RRSP, which lowers Jim's taxes by $4,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:59:00"></a>. When Pam withdraws the funds in retirement, she pays only $2,400 in taxes, resulting in a total couple saving of $1,800 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:05:00"></a>.

### Splitting Capital Losses

Investment losses can be converted into tax savings by transferring capital losses between spouses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:16:00"></a>.

*   **Example:** Jim's $4,000 capital loss can reduce Pam's $15,000 in capital gains, saving the couple $800 in taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:22:00"></a>.
*   **Pro Tip:** This strategy only works in non-registered accounts, and the buyer must hold the shares for at least 30 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:31:00"></a>.

## Avoiding Costly Mistakes

Even effective tax strategies can fail if common mistakes are made <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:47:39"></a>.

### Superficial Loss Rule

This rule often causes issues with tax loss harvesting and splitting capital losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00"></a>.

*   **Impact:** If you or your spouse buy back the same stock within 30 days of selling it, the CRA will not allow you to claim the loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:33:00"></a>.
*   **Result:** The loss is rolled into the cost of the new shares, delaying the benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:41:00"></a>.
