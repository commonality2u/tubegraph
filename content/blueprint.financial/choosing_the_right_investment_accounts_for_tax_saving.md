## 1. Choosing Tax-Efficient Investments in Taxable Accounts

A simple and effective way to retain more of your money for growth over time is by [[Taxefficient investment strategies in Canada | choosing tax-efficient investments]] within your taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>.

### ETFs Versus Mutual Funds
Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) generally trade assets less frequently than mutual funds, leading to fewer taxable events for you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>. Actively managed mutual funds, on the other hand, constantly buy and sell, passing on the tax bill for these trades to you, even if you haven't sold anything yourself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:47">[00:00:47]</a>. For investments in a taxable account, ETFs are typically the smarter choice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>.

### Non-Dividend Paying Stocks and ETFs
Stocks like Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway are well-suited for taxable accounts because they deliberately avoid paying annual dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. This strategy helps avoid yearly taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. Gains are only taxed when you sell the stock, and only 50% of capital gains under $250,000 are taxable under new rules, making them significantly more tax-efficient than dividend-paying or interest-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>. For instance, a $20,000 gain is taxed on only $10,000, and only upon sale <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

### Permanent Life Insurance
Beyond protection, permanent life insurance can also grow investments within the policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. The payout to your beneficiaries is tax-free, making it an advanced strategy worth considering for estate planning to reduce [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] passed to loved ones after death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>.

## 2. Prioritizing the Right Registered Accounts

[[Choosing the right investment accounts in Canada | Choosing the wrong investment accounts]] to prioritize can result in substantial financial losses, potentially tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.

### TFSA Versus a Taxable Account Example
Consider Jason, a 30-year-old who invested $10,000 in both a [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] and a [[NonRegistered Accounts Investment Strategies | non-registered taxable account]] over a 30-year period with a 7% annual return and a 30% tax rate on the taxable account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>.

*   In his [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]], the $10,000 grew tax-free to $76,122 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:31">[00:02:31]</a>.
*   In his [[NonRegistered Accounts Investment Strategies | non-registered taxable account]], the $10,000 grew to only $42,001 due to ongoing annual taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>.

The [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] outperformed the [[NonRegistered Accounts Investment Strategies | non-registered account]] by $34,122, nearly double the value, showcasing the power of tax-free compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>.

### Order of Priority for Investing
To [[maximizing_contributions_to_taxadvantaged_accounts | maximize savings and minimize taxes]], consider this order of priority for your accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>:

1.  **RSP with Employer Matching**: Always maximize employer matching first, as it's essentially free money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:13">[00:03:13]</a>. For example, a 50% match up to $1,500 means an easy $750 added to your savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.
2.  **RESP (Registered Education Savings Plan)**: If saving for a child's education, the RESP offers a 20% government match up to $500 per year per child, with a lifetime maximum of $7,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>. Lower-income families may also receive the Canada Learning Bond, adding up to $2,000 without requiring contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>.
3.  **FHSA (First Home Savings Account)**: First-time home buyers should prioritize the FHSA, which offers tax-deductible contributions and tax-free withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:56">[00:03:56]</a>. Funds can grow tax-free or be transferred to an RSP later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.
4.  **TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account) or RSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan)**: These are the most common choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:12">[00:04:12]</a>.
    *   **[[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]]**: Best for flexibility, emergencies, or lower-income years, allowing tax-free withdrawals at any time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>. It's considered the most versatile and flexible account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:28">[00:04:28]</a>.
    *   **RSP**: Ideal for higher incomes due to tax-deductible contributions and tax-deferred growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>. However, be mindful of withdrawal restrictions associated with RSPs and FHSAs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>.

The final priority order is: RSP with employer matching, RESP, FHSA, and then TFSA or RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>.

## 3. Optimizing Asset Location

Tax efficiency involves not just what you invest in, but also where you hold those investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:09">[00:05:09]</a>. Different income types are taxed differently in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.

### Breakdown of Tax Rules
*   **Interest Income**: Taxed at your full marginal tax rate, making it the least tax-efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:25">[00:05:25]</a>.
*   **Canadian Dividends**: Benefit from the dividend tax credit, which reduces your effective tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.
*   **Foreign Dividends and Interest**: Subject to withholding taxes, which typically cannot be recovered in a [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>.
*   **Capital Gains**: Only 50% of gains are taxable up to $250,000 (after that, 2/3), making them more tax-efficient than interest or dividends in most cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

### Key Strategies for Asset Location
1.  **RSP for US Dividend-Paying Stocks**: Hold US dividend-paying stocks in your RSP to avoid the 15% withholding tax on US dividends, thanks to the Canada-US tax treaty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>.
2.  **[[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] for High-Growth Investments**: Use your [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] to shelter capital gains and allow high-growth investments to compound tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>. Avoid foreign dividend stocks in a [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] if possible, as withholding taxes apply and cannot be recovered, leading to a loss of up to 15% of dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>.
3.  **Taxable Accounts for Canadian Dividend Stocks**: If you've maxed out registered accounts, Canadian dividend-paying stocks are relatively tax-efficient in [[NonRegistered Accounts Investment Strategies | taxable non-registered accounts]] due to the dividend tax credit, which significantly reduces [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. For example, a $1,000 Canadian dividend income in a taxable account might only incur about $70 in [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] for someone in a 30% tax bracket, compared to $300 on the same amount of interest income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>.
4.  **Registered Accounts for Interest-Generating Investments**: Place investments that generate interest income, such as GICs or bonds, in RSPs or TFSAs to avoid paying high [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] on interest income in taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>.

## 4. Income Splitting

Income splitting is a more advanced strategy that can help couples save on [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]].

### FHSA for Income Splitting
While primarily for saving for a home, the FHSA can also help couples save on [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:54">[00:07:54]</a>.
*   **Who it's for**: Couples where one partner has maxed out their tax-advantaged accounts (RSP, [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]], FHSA) and the other has unused FHSA room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.
*   **How it works**: The higher-income partner gifts money to the lower-income partner, who then contributes it to their FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. This avoids investing in a taxable account, allows for a tax deduction, and enables tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>. For example, Jim gives $8,000 to Pam's unused FHSA. Pam receives a $2,400 tax deduction, avoids $132 in [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] on growth, and all future growth is tax-free, saving $2,532 in total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.

### Spousal RSP Contributions
Spousal RSPs help balance retirement incomes and reduce [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>.
*   **How it works**: The higher-income spouse contributes to the lower-income spouse's RSP, getting an immediate tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>. In retirement, the lower-income spouse withdraws funds at their lower tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>. For example, Jim contributes $12,000 to Pam's RSP, lowering Jim's [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] by $4,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>. When Pam withdraws in retirement, she pays only $2,400 in [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]], saving the couple $1,800 overall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>.

### Splitting Capital Losses
Investment losses can be turned into tax savings by transferring capital losses between spouses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. For example, Jim's $4,000 capital loss can reduce Pam's $15,000 in capital gains, saving the couple $800 in [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>.

> [!PRO TIP]
> This strategy only works in [[NonRegistered Accounts Investment Strategies | non-registered accounts]], and the buyer (spouse receiving the shares) must hold them for at least 30 days <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.

## 5. Tax Loss Harvesting

[[tax_loss_harvesting_to_minimize_investment_taxes | Tax loss harvesting]] helps save on [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] by using losing investments to offset gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>.

### How it Works
If you sell an investment at a loss, you can use that loss to reduce the [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] on gains from other investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>. If losses exceed gains, you can carry those losses forward to future years or back to previous years to obtain a tax refund <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

For instance, if you sold a stock for a $10,000 gain earlier in the year, you could sell another stock that's down $6,000 to offset part of your gain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>. You would then only be taxed on $4,000 instead of $10,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.

> [!CAUTION] Superficial Loss Rule
> If you or your spouse buy back the same stock within 30 days of selling it, the CRA will not allow you to claim the loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. Instead, the loss is added to the cost of your new shares, delaying the benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:41">[00:10:41]</a>.

## Bonus Tips to Avoid Costly Mistakes

Even the best [[planning_taxsaving_strategies | tax strategies]] can be undermined by simple mistakes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>.

1.  **Over-contributing to Registered Accounts**: Exceeding contribution limits for your [[TaxFree Savings Account Strategies | TFSA]] or RSP can result in penalties of 1% of the excess amount each month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>. Always track your limits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:05">[00:11:05]</a>.
2.  **Forgetting About Deadlines**: Strategies like RSP contributions have strict deadlines (e.g., 60 days after year-end) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:10">[00:11:10]</a>. Missing these can mean losing out on tax deductions for the year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.
3.  **Not Planning for Withdrawals**: Withdrawals from registered accounts like RSPs can trigger unexpected [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:24">[00:11:24]</a>. Have a plan to manage these withdrawals tax-efficiently, especially in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:29">[00:11:29]</a>.

These strategies can save you thousands in [[Tax saving strategies on investments in Canada | taxes]] and boost your wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:38">[00:11:38]</a>.
