## [[income_splitting_strategies | Income Splitting Strategies]]

### 1. Using Your FHSA for Couples

This lesser-known [[income_splitting_strategies | income splitting strategy]] is particularly useful for couples where one partner has unused First Home Savings Account (FHSA) contribution room, and the other has maximized their tax-advantaged accounts like an RRSP or TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. The FHSA offers tax-deductible contributions similar to an RRSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.

*   **How it Works:** A partner who has maxed out their tax-advantaged accounts can gift money to their partner who has unused FHSA room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. This prevents the gifted money from being invested in a taxable non-registered account and allows the receiving partner to get a tax deduction and benefit from tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>.
*   **Example (Ted & Robin):** Ted, who has maxed out his registered accounts, gifts $8,000 to Robin, who hasn't contributed to her FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>.
    *   **Without FHSA:** Ted would invest $8,000 in a taxable account, paying $132 annually on $400 growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>.
    *   **With FHSA:** Robin contributes $8,000 to her FHSA, gains an immediate $2,400 tax deduction, and avoids taxes on $400 in investment growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>.
    *   **Total Savings:** This results in a combined saving of $2,532, plus all future investment growth is tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>. The FHSA can also be rolled over into an RRSP if not used for a home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>.

*   **Pros:**
    *   **Double Benefit:** The gifting partner avoids taxable investment growth, and the receiving partner gains a tax deduction and tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>.
    *   **Tax-Free Withdrawals:** Withdrawals for a home purchase are tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:19">[00:02:19]</a>.
    *   **Flexibility:** If not used for a home, the FHSA can be rolled over into an RRSP after 15 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:24">[00:02:24]</a>.
*   **Cons:**
    *   **Eligibility:** The receiving partner must not have owned a home in the past four years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:31">[00:02:31]</a>.
    *   **Ownership Risk:** Gifted funds legally belong to the receiving partner, making them difficult to reclaim if the relationship ends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>.

### 2. Splitting Employer-Sponsored Pension Income

[[income_splitting_strategies_for_canadian_taxpayers | Pension income splitting]] allows a higher-income spouse to allocate up to 50% of their eligible pension income to a lower-income spouse, reducing the overall household tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>.

*   **Eligibility:**
    *   **Before Age 65:** Income from a Defined Benefit Pension Plan (DBPP) qualifies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>. Withdrawals from a Defined Contribution Pension Plan (DCPP) generally do not qualify unless the spouse passes away <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>.
    *   **After Age 65:** Both DBPP and DCPP income are eligible for [[income_splitting_strategies | income splitting]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:52">[00:03:52]</a>. Withdrawals from RIFs (Registered Income Funds) and LIFs (Life Income Funds) also become eligible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.
*   **Example (Ted & Robin):** Ted, 60s, has a $40,000 annual pension (30% tax bracket), while Robin has little income (15% tax bracket) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>.
    *   **Without Splitting:** Ted pays $12,000 in taxes himself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.
    *   **With Splitting:** By splitting 20% of the income with Robin, she is taxed at her lower 15% rate, saving $3,000 in taxes overall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:53">[00:04:53]</a>.

### 3. Spousal RRSP Contributions

This strategy allows a higher-income spouse to contribute to an RRSP in the lower-income spouse's name, gaining an immediate tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. In retirement, the lower-income spouse withdraws funds, taxed at their lower rate, balancing retirement incomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.

*   **Example (Ted & Robin):** Ted earns $120,000 (35% tax rate), and Robin earns $40,000 (20% tax rate) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>. Ted has a work pension, Robin does not.
    *   **Strategy:** Ted contributes $12,000 to a spousal RRSP in Robin's name <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.
    *   **Immediate Deduction:** Ted's contribution lowers his taxable income, saving $4,200 in taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a>.
    *   **Future Tax Efficiency:** When Robin withdraws the funds, she pays $2,400 in taxes at her 20% rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.
    *   **Net Savings:** This results in a net saving of $1,800 in household taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:26">[00:06:26]</a>. The goal is to predict who will have the lower income in retirement and capitalize on that fact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>.
*   **Pro Tips:**
    *   **Avoid Early Withdrawals:** If the spousal RRSP funds are withdrawn within three years of Ted's contribution, they will be attributed back to Ted and taxed at his higher rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>.
    *   **Plan for Equal Balances:** Aim for equal RRSP balances to prevent uneven retirement incomes and potential tax inefficiencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>.
    *   **Consider Age Differences:** If one spouse is significantly younger, plan contributions to ensure balanced withdrawals during retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:03">[00:07:03]</a>.

### 4. Splitting CPP Income

For couples receiving Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits, [[income_splitting_strategies | income splitting]] offers an easy way to reduce taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>.

*   **How it Works:** Up to 50% of CPP benefits can be shifted from the higher-income spouse to the lower-income spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>. This reduces the household tax bill and may help avoid the OAS (Old Age Security) clawback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>.
*   **Example (Ted & Robin):** Ted receives $12,000 annually in CPP benefits (30% tax rate), and Robin receives $6,000 (15% tax rate) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:40">[00:07:40]</a>.
    *   **Without Splitting:** Ted pays more taxes due to his higher tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:59">[00:07:59]</a>.
    *   **With Splitting:** By shifting $3,000 of Ted's CPP income to Robin, they lower their overall tax bill by $450 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>.

### 5. Splitting Capital Losses (Superficial Loss Rule)

Capital losses can be transferred to a spouse to offset their capital gains using the Superficial Loss Rule, which helps reduce the household tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:13">[00:08:13]</a>.

*   **How it Works:**
    1.  Sell shares at a loss in a non-registered account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.
    2.  Your spouse buys the same shares within 30 days (before or after your sale) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:28]</a>.
    3.  The loss is denied to you but added to your spouse's Adjusted Cost Basis (ACB) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.
    4.  Your spouse must hold the shares for at least 30 days after the purchase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>.
*   **Example (Ted & Robin):** Ted has a $4,000 capital loss, and Robin has $15,000 in capital gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:45">[00:08:45]</a>.
    *   **Process:** Ted sells his shares, incurring the $4,000 loss. Robin purchases the same shares within 30 days, triggering the Superficial Loss Rule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>. Ted's loss is denied but added to Robin's cost base <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. When Robin eventually sells those shares, the $4,000 loss effectively offsets her taxable gains, reducing their overall household tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>.
*   **Pro Tip:** This strategy only works for non-registered accounts. Maintaining accurate records of your Adjusted Cost Bases (ACB) is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>.

### 6. Investment Loans for Your Spouse or Child (Prescribed Rate Loan)

A prescribed rate loan can be a smart way to reduce a family's overall tax bills, though it comes with challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>.

*   **How it Works:** A high-income spouse or family member lends money to a lower-income spouse or child at the CRA's prescribed interest rate (e.g., 5% at the time of recording) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>. The borrower invests the money, and any investment income is taxed at their lower rate instead of the lender's higher one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58">[00:10:02]</a>.
*   **Considerations:** This strategy is worth exploring for families with significant income differences and solid investment returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:04">[00:10:04]</a>.
*   **Risks:**
    *   Potential for poor investment returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.
    *   Possibility of increased prescribed rates in the future, which would reduce the strategy's effectiveness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>.
*   **Recommendation:** Proceed carefully with professional guidance before attempting this strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>.

### 7. [[family_trusts_for_wealth_management_and_tax_efficiency | Family Trusts]]

A [[family_trusts_for_wealth_management_and_tax_efficiency | Family Trust]] in Canada is a legal arrangement where a trustee holds assets for beneficiaries, typically family members <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:29">[00:10:29]</a>. It offers several tax-saving opportunities:

*   **Income Splitting Potential:** Income can be distributed to beneficiaries in lower tax brackets, reducing the overall family tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>.
*   **Multiply Capital Gains Exemptions:** Allocating capital gains to multiple beneficiaries allows each to utilize their lifetime capital gains exemption, potentially leading to significant tax savings when selling qualified small business shares <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>.
*   **Estate Freezing:** Transferring the future growth of assets to beneficiaries can minimize taxes upon death by capping the value subject to capital gains taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.
*   **Complexity:** [[family_trusts_for_wealth_management_and_tax_efficiency | Family Trusts]] are complicated and typically require legal and accounting professionals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:13">[00:11:13]</a>.

Think of these as tools that, when used together, build a strong financial foundation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>.

*   **Example of Combined [[planning_and_implementing_taxsaving_strategies | Planning and Implementing Tax-Saving Strategies]]:** A couple could contribute to an FHSA for one partner to claim a tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>. In retirement, they could use pension splitting to balance income and add spousal RRSP contributions to create more tax-deferred savings, further reducing household taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:45">[00:11:45]</a>. These combined [[advanced_strategies_like_income_splitting_and_tax_loss_harvesting | advanced strategies like income splitting and tax loss harvesting]] can seamlessly lower taxes, balance income streams, and help achieve financial goals like saving for a home or planning for retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:55">[00:11:55]</a>.
