Retirement is a period where individuals shift from decades of saving and investing to spending their accumulated wealth, a transition that can feel profoundly uncomfortable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>. This difficulty stems from a necessary [[save_or_spend_dilemma_in_retirement | mindset shift]] from a scarcity mindset, focused on accumulation, to an abundance mindset, where one feels confident using what has been saved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>. This [[save_or_spend_dilemma_in_retirement | save or spend dilemma]] is a common challenge for most retirees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>.

## The Dilemma: To Spend or Not to Spend

Retirees often face the [[save_or_spend_dilemma_in_retirement | dilemma]] of how much to spend versus how much to retain in savings each year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:20">[00:00:20]</a>.

### Why Not Spending Enough Can Be Detrimental

Hesitating to spend can lead to several negative outcomes:
-   **Missed Experiences and Opportunities** Retirees may forego meaningful experiences such as travel, hobbies, or even healthcare improvements that could enhance their quality of life during their "Golden Years" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:01">[00:01:01]</a>.
-   **Healthspan vs. Lifespan** Dr. Peter Attia's distinction between lifespan (total years lived) and healthspan (years lived in good, active health) is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>. Delaying spending can mean missing out on activities while one is physically capable, as abilities may decline later in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>.
-   **Inflation's Impact** Inflation erodes the value of money over time, diminishing purchasing power if not properly accounted for in a well-structured [[financial_planning_for_retirement | retirement plan]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>.

### Why Cautious Spending Can Be Beneficial

Conversely, not spending all available funds can offer advantages:
-   **Encourages Cautious Spending** A hesitation to spend promotes thoughtful, deliberate financial decisions, preventing impulse purchases and overspending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>.
-   **Longevity Risk Reduction** A careful approach helps money last longer, reducing the risk of running out of funds prematurely, a common concern for retirees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.
-   **Portfolio Risk Reduction** Cautious spending helps protect against "sequence of returns risk," where significant market losses early in retirement can deplete a portfolio faster than later losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>.
-   **Alignment with Legacy Goals** For many, leaving a substantial inheritance or charitable contributions is a priority. Cautious spending helps achieve these [[balancing_spending_and_legacy_goals_in_retirement | legacy goals]] while still enjoying retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:59">[00:02:59]</a>.

## Finding the Right Balance

The key to navigating the [[save_or_spend_dilemma_in_retirement | save versus spend dilemma]] is finding a healthy balance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>. A major problem is that many retirees are unsure how much they can safely spend, leading them to err on the side of caution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:32">[00:03:32]</a>. While understandable, this caution can prevent them from enjoying the wealth they've worked to build <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>.

A well-structured [[strategies_for_financial_planning_in_retirement | financial plan]] is essential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>. It helps outline affordable spending limits and includes stress testing of investment portfolios, moving retirees from fear to confidence and enjoyment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.

### Case Study: Michael and Karen

Michael (66) and Karen (64) exemplify the challenge of spending more freely in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
-   **Profile:** Combined income of $180,000 (pensions, investments), expenses of $70,000, and $4 million in diversified savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:32">[00:04:32]</a>.
-   **Goal:** Increase spending on luxury travel, home renovations, and charitable giving, but unsure how much <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:53">[00:04:53]</a>.
-   **Process:** Despite a $110,000 annual surplus, they were hesitant to spend more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:14">[00:05:14]</a>. Analysis showed their $4 million portfolio with a 3.5% withdrawal rate could provide an additional $140,000 annually, well beyond their desired increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>. Their guaranteed income already covered basic expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.
-   **Solution:** Their portfolio was stress-tested against a 30% decline in early years, and longevity projections were run assuming they lived to age 95. Both scenarios confirmed long-term sustainability even with higher spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>.
-   **Outcome:** Michael and Karen gained confidence to book luxury vacations, begin home renovations, and increase charitable contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>.

### Case Study: Robert and Susan

Robert (70) and Susan (68) sought to balance spending with [[balancing_spending_and_legacy_goals_in_retirement | legacy goals]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>.
-   **Profile:** Combined income of $100,000, expenses of $75,000, and $1.2 million in savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>.
-   **Goal:** Leave a meaningful inheritance while still enjoying retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:23">[00:07:23]</a>.
-   **Process:** They were cautious about increasing spending despite a healthy income and savings, unsure if they could afford more leisure while ensuring an inheritance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>. Their $1.2 million portfolio with a 4% withdrawal rate could provide $48,000 annually, which, combined with their income, was more than enough to cover expenses and allow for increased spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>.
-   **Solution:** Recommended increasing spending from $75,000 to $85,000 per year, allocating more to travel and experiences while maintaining their inheritance goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. Stress testing confirmed their ability to maintain spending even in adverse market conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:23">[00:08:23]</a>.
-   **Outcome:** Robert and Susan felt confident enjoying a higher quality of life now while still planning for their [[balancing_spending_and_legacy_goals_in_retirement | legacy goals]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>.

> [!TIP] Pro Tip: Easing into Spending
> For those with larger portfolios who developed strict savings habits, it can be particularly hard to start spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:34">[00:06:34]</a>. Try easing into spending by setting small, meaningful goals like short trips or new hobbies to gradually shift your mindset and better enjoy retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>.

Don't let fear prevent you from enjoying retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>. A custom [[strategies_for_financial_planning_in_retirement | financial plan]] can help determine how much you can comfortably spend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>.
