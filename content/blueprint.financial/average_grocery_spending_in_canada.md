## Average Annual Grocery Spending by Household Type

Based on the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, average annual grocery spending (excluding restaurants or takeout) varies significantly by household type <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>:

*   **Single Adult**: Approximately $330 per month or $4,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple**: Approximately $667 per month or $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four (two adults, two kids)**: Approximately $1,403 per month or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

While costs generally double as a household grows, the jump from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>. This is because [[cost_of_raising_a_child_in_canada | feeding a family is a different game]] due to factors like kids' snacking, food waste, need for variety, rapid growth, and parents prioritizing nutrition and convenience, all of which drive up the bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>.

These figures serve as a useful benchmark. For example, spending $800 a month as a single person is likely high, while $800 for a family of four would be too low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. Comparing your bill to these averages can help determine if you're overspending and aid in realistic budgeting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>.

## Impact of [[inflation_and_food_prices_in_canada | Inflation]] on Food Costs

[[inflation_and_food_prices_in_canada | Food costs]] have significantly worsened in recent years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>:

*   **2022**: Saw a 10.3% inflation, bringing the average cost for a family of four to $15,200, an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **2023**: Continued with a high 5.9% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.
*   **2024**: Cooled down to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>.
*   **2025 (Projected)**: Forecasted at 3% to 5% inflation, with a projected annual cost of $16,833 for a family of four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>. This means a typical family is projected to spend up to $800 more on the same groceries in 2025 than in 2024, simply to maintain their standard of living due to "inflation on autopilot" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.

Underlying these increases are factors like climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain and logistic issues, and tariffs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>. The impact of food inflation is significant, affecting choices, quality of life, and health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>.

## [[grocery_costs_by_age_group_in_canada | Average Grocery Cost by Age Group]]

Grocery spending varies across age groups:

*   **Canadians in their 30s to 50s**: Spend the most, around $4,152 a year ($346 a month) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. This peak reflects factors such as raising families, having less time to cook, and a preference for quality or convenience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.
*   **Ages 19 to 30**: Spend about $3,861 per year ($322 a month), roughly $300 less annually than the peak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. This group often lives with roommates, splits bills, and prioritizes cheaper options like pasta or no-name brands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **Ages 50s and 60s**: Spending remains nearly the same as peak years, at $4,110 a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. Habits tend to stick, and individuals might still be supporting adult children or cooking for visiting family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.
*   **Over 70s**: See a clear dip to about $3,788 per year ($316 a month) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. This is attributed to smaller portions, fewer mouths to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.

## Broader Impact of Rising Food Prices: The [[cost_of_living_crisis_in_canada | Cost of Living Crisis]]

The [[cost_of_living_crisis_in_canada | cost of living is eating us for lunch]] in Canada, with nearly half of Canadians struggling to cover basic expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. Food is a major part of this, with 28% of Canadians reporting that they are [[impact_of_food_costs_on_canadians | eating less food]], not just switching brands <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>. People are no longer budgeting to get ahead, but to survive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.

Common coping strategies include bulk buying, comparing flyers, and cutting impulse spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. However, expenses are outpacing even the smartest plans and incomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>. Incomes are generally not rising at the same rate as food price increases, widening the gap between earnings and expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a>. (For more details on income, refer to the article on [[average_income_in_canada_by_age | Canadian incomes by age]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:17]</a>).

Where one lives also significantly impacts food prices. Quebec is projected to see the smallest increase (1.8%), while Newfoundland could see the highest (up to 3.8%), with other provinces hovering around 3% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.

### Food Insecurity and Changing Habits

Food insecurity is a growing crisis. March 2024 saw over 2 million food bank visits, a 90% jump since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>. Nearly half of Gen Z have had to dip into savings or borrow money to eat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>. Forty-eight percent of Canadians are actively hunting for discounts just to get by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.

This situation goes beyond budgeting, affecting dignity and the ability to feed oneself without fear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>. Many Canadians are skipping meals, cutting non-essentials, or settling for lower-quality food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>. Shopping habits have shifted, with brand loyalty fading as affordability becomes paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>. Some are cooking more creatively or reducing waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. Even middle-income households are feeling the pinch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>.

### Specific Food Price Increases: Beef

Beef is becoming a luxury item in Canada, with prices rising by 9.2% in 2024 and continuing to climb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>. This is largely due to Canada having its smallest cattle herd since 1987, leading to less supply and higher prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>. This shift is changing how Canadians eat, with more opting for chicken, pork, or lentils instead of steak <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>.

## Consumer Response

In 2024, Canadians boycotted Loblaws out of frustration over rising prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. While the boycott didn't make a significant dent in the system, it symbolized widespread fatigue with feeling powerless and low trust in large grocers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. Shoppers are shifting to smaller stores, comparing prices, and returning to farmers markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
