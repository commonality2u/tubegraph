## Average Grocery Spending in Canada (2025)

The actual cost of groceries in Canada varies significantly based on age and household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>. According to the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, the following are average annual grocery spending figures by household type, excluding restaurants or takeout <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>:

*   **Single Adult**: Approximately $330 per month or $4,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple**: Roughly $667 per month or $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four (two adults, two kids)**: About $1,403 per month or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

These figures are averages and the actual range can be extremely wide due to variances in consumption habits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>. While costs appear to double as a household grows from a single adult to a couple, the jump from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. This is because feeding a family involves additional factors like kids snacking, food waste, need for variety, and rapid growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>. Parents also prioritize nutrition and convenience, further driving up the bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>.

This data can serve as a useful benchmark to assess personal grocery spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. For example, $800 a month for a single person is likely high, while for a family of four, it might be too low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. Comparing your grocery bill to others in a similar life stage can help determine if you are overspending and where to adjust your budget <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>.

## Historical Food Inflation and Costs

Food costs have significantly worsened in recent years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>:

*   **2022**: Food inflation was 10.3%, bringing the average cost for a family of four to $15,200 annually, an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **2023**: Inflation remained high at 5.9% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.
*   **2024**: Inflation cooled considerably to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:42">[00:02:42]</a>.
*   **2025 (Projected)**: The 2025 report estimates a 3% to 5% inflation forecast, with a projected annual cost of $16,833 for a family of four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>.

Even with inflation slowing since its peak in 2022, costs continue to stack up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. A typical family is projected to spend up to $800 more in 2025 than in 2024 to maintain the same standard of living, solely due to inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.

Behind these numbers are various challenges, including climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain and logistic issues, and tariffs from the US, all affecting food availability and shelf prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

## Average Grocery Cost by Age Group

Grocery spending also varies across age groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>:

*   **Canadians in their 30s to 50s**: Spend the most, around $4,152 a year or $346 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. This peak reflects factors such as raising children, less time for cooking, and a preference for quality or convenience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.
*   **Ages 19 to 30**: Spend about $3,861 per year or $322 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. This group often lives with roommates, splits bills, and prioritizes cheaper options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **Ages 50s and 60s**: Spending remains nearly the same as peak years, at $4,110 a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. This might be due to assisting adult children, cooking for visiting family, or an unwillingness to scale back <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:55">[00:04:55]</a>.
*   **Over 70**: See a clear dip to about $3,788 per year, or $316 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. This is attributed to smaller portions, fewer mouths to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.

## Broader Impact of Rising Costs

The [[rising_food_and_housing_costs_in_canada | rising food and housing costs in Canada]] are contributing to a severe [[cost_of_living_crisis_in_canada | cost of living crisis in Canada]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.

*   **Financial Struggles**: Nearly half (45%) of Canadians report struggling with day-to-day costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>.
*   **Reduced Food Intake**: A significant 28% of Canadians are eating less food, not just switching brands, to make ends meet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>. This is a serious concern for a wealthy country like Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.
*   **Budgeting for Survival**: Beyond groceries, rising costs for rent, gas, utilities, and debt mean many Canadians are budgeting to survive rather than get ahead <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:43">[00:05:43]</a>.
*   **Coping Strategies**: People are resorting to strategies like bulk buying, comparing flyers, and cutting impulse spending, but many still fall behind as expenses outpace incomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. Incomes are generally not rising at the same rate as food prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>.

## Growing Food Insecurity

Food insecurity is a growing crisis in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>:

*   **Food Bank Visits**: Over 2 million food bank visits occurred in March 2024, a 90% jump since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.
*   **Generational Impact**: Nearly half of Gen Z has had to dip into savings or borrow money just to afford food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>.
*   **Widespread Discount Hunting**: Across all demographics, 48% of Canadians actively hunt for discounts not just to save money, but to get by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.
*   **Coping Mechanisms**: More Canadians are skipping meals, cutting non-essentials, or settling for lower quality food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>. The fact that almost a third of the population is cutting down on eating to get by in an advanced country like Canada is striking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>.

Shopping habits are shifting, with brand loyalty fading as affordability becomes the primary focus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. Many are cooking more creatively or reducing waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. Even middle-income households are feeling the pinch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.

## Specific Food Item Impacts: Steak

The price of beef has seen a significant increase, with beef prices rising by 9.2% in 2024 and continuing to climb <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. This is largely due to Canada having its smallest cattle herd since 1987, leading to less supply and higher prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>. As a result, steak is increasingly becoming a luxury item <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</a>. This shift is causing Canadians to swap steak for alternatives like chicken, pork, or lentils, which impacts traditional meals and cultural practices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>.

## Consumer Response: The Loblaws Boycott

In 2024, Canadians boycotted Loblaws out of frustration with rising prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. While the boycott made noise, it did not significantly impact the grocery giant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>. However, it symbolized broader discontent, showing that people are tired of feeling powerless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>. Trust in large grocers is low, leading shoppers to shift towards smaller stores, price comparisons, and even farmers' markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>. Despite these efforts, major chains retain significant power, frustrating consumers who desire change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:27">[00:09:27]</a>.
