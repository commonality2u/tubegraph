The Canada Pension Plan (CPP) has undergone significant changes since 2019, including the recent CPP2 enhancement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:02">[00:00:02]</a>. While some view it critically, others consider it a necessity for [[retirement_income_planning_and_cpp | retirement income planning]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:12">[00:00:12]</a>. These enhancements were introduced to replace a larger portion of Canadians' income in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:08">[00:01:08]</a>.

## CPP Enhancement Details

The CPP now incorporates two levels of contributions with updated thresholds and rates designed to enhance future retirement benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>.

### Contribution Tiers for 2025

*   **Regular CPP Contributions (Tier 1):** These are calculated on earnings up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>. In 2025, the YMPE is $71,400 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>. Employers match employee contributions, and self-employed individuals pay the combined rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>. The contribution rate is 5.95% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>.
*   **CPP2 Contributions (Tier 2):** This applies to earnings between the YMPE ($71,400 in 2025) and the Year's Additional Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YAMPE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a>. In 2025, the YAMPE is capped at $81,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. Earnings above the YAMPE do not incur additional CPP contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>. The contribution rate for CPP2 is 4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

For both tiers, a $3,500 exemption is applied before calculating pensionable earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>.

> For example, in 2025:
> *   **Mark**, earning $71,500, contributes approximately $4,033 to regular CPP (based on $71,400 YMPE - $3,500 exemption) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>. With his employer's match, the total contribution is over $8,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>.
> *   **James**, earning $81,300, contributes to both regular CPP and CPP2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:47">[00:01:47]</a>. His first tier contribution is about $4,030 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. For the $9,900 earned between the YMPE and YAMPE ($71,400 to $81,300), he contributes an additional 4%, which is $396 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>. His total personal contribution is $4,420, matched by his employer, bringing the total close to $9,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>.
>
> For incomes above $81,300, total contributions remain capped at approximately $4,443 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:53">[00:02:53]</a>.

### Increased Income Replacement Rate

A key component of the CPP enhancement is the increased income replacement rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.
*   **Previous Rate:** The CPP replaced 25% of a worker's average lifetime earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:24">[00:03:24]</a>.
*   **Enhanced Rate:** The replacement rate is set to rise to 33.33% (or one-third) of average work earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. This change is being phased in over 40 years, starting in 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.

For instance, someone earning $60,000 annually who previously received about $15,000 in CPP benefits per year, is now projected to receive approximately $20,000 with the enhancements once fully rolled out <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:58">[00:03:58]</a>.

### Gradual Implementation

The enhancements are being phased in gradually since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a>.
*   **Early Retirees (e.g., 2026):** Individuals retiring in 2026, having contributed for only 7 years under the enhanced system, will see a modest increase in their CPP benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>. For someone earning $60,000 annually, the income replacement rate might be closer to 26% or 27%, equating to $15,600 to $16,200 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>.
*   **Later Retirees (e.g., 2059):** Those retiring in 2059, having contributed for the full 40 years under the enhanced system, will benefit from the maximum 33.33% replacement rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. For the same $60,000 annual earning, this equates to $20,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:03">[00:05:03]</a>.

## Why the CPP was Enhanced

The enhancements aim to strengthen the first pillar of Canada's retirement income system in response to shifts in the retirement landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:14">[00:06:14]</a>.

### Three Pillars of Retirement Income in Canada

Canada's [[common_retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | retirement income system]] is built on three key pillars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>:
1.  **Government Benefits:** Includes the CPP, Old Age Security (OAS), and Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>.
2.  **Employer-Sponsored Pension Plans:** Such as defined benefit (DB) or defined contribution (DC) plans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>.
3.  **Personal Savings:** Includes Registered Retirement Savings Plans (RRSPs), Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSAs), and other assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:43">[00:05:43]</a>.

These pillars are designed to provide a secure and comfortable retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>.

### Shifting Retirement Landscape

Traditionally, defined benefit pension plans were a cornerstone of retirement security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. However, these are now quite rare, replaced by defined contribution plans that shift investment risk to employees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:03">[00:06:03]</a>. This has left many Canadians with less predictable and reliable income in their later years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.

The enhanced CPP aims to provide a stable, inflation-adjusted foundation of guaranteed income that retirees can rely on, regardless of market fluctuations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</a>. While personal savings and workplace pensions remain important, their increasing subject to volatility and individual decision-making makes the enhanced CPP a crucial safety net <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. This helps Canadians navigate the [[challenges_of_retirement_in_canada | challenges of retirement]] such as rising costs, longer life expectancies, and evolving financial risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.

## Criticisms of the CPP Enhancement

Despite the goal of increasing financial security in retirement, the CPP enhancements have faced criticisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:59">[00:06:59]</a>.

### Financial Strain on Workers and Employers

*   **Workers:** Many workers have complained about previous contribution increases (from 4.95% to 5.95%) and now face additional payments due to CPP2 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>. Higher contribution rates mean less take-home pay, which is challenging amid rising living costs and inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>.
*   **Employers:** Employers must match increased contributions, which can be significant for smaller businesses and may lead to difficult decisions about hiring or cutting back in other areas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:39">[00:07:39]</a>. There's also a concern that employers might offset these costs by paying employees less <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>.
*   **Self-Employed Individuals:** Self-employed individuals are responsible for both the employee and employer portions of contributions, making cash flow management harder <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:54">[00:07:54]</a>.

### Broader Economic Concerns

There are worries that reduced take-home pay for employees could lead to less spending, potentially slowing economic growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:25">[00:08:25]</a>. For businesses, higher payroll costs might deter growth or investment in new opportunities, impacting jobs and innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:34">[00:08:34]</a>.

### Debates About Mandatory Contributions

The mandatory nature of CPP contributions is also a point of contention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>. Many argue that personal savings options like RRSPs and TFSAs offer more flexibility and control, allowing individuals to decide how much to save and where to invest based on their personal goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:58">[00:08:58]</a>.
