Retirement, envisioned as a time for relaxation, is increasingly becoming a significant financial concern for many Canadians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This is due to falling retiree incomes, rising inflation, and a growing uncertainty about financial security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:06">[00:00:06]</a>. This article explores the realities of retiree incomes in Canada and whether a comfortable retirement is achievable on an average income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:12">[00:00:12]</a>.

## The Reality of Retirement Income in Canada

The average retirement income in Canada may be lower than many expect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>. As of 2022, for Canadians aged 65 and older:
*   **Couples**: The median after-tax income was $74,200 per year, or $6,183 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>.
*   **Single Seniors**: The median after-tax income was $30,820 per year, which equates to only $2,568 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.

For context, the median income across all age groups in Canada is approximately $95,000 for couples and $33,000 for single individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. This indicates that many retirees are living on significantly less than the working population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>. The rising costs of housing, food, and healthcare further challenge how far this income can stretch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>.

## Impact of Surging Inflation

Retirement income is not just stagnating; it is actively losing purchasing power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>.
*   Between 2020 and 2022, senior couples saw their median after-tax income drop from $76,860 to $74,200, representing a 1.4% decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.
*   Single retirees experienced a decrease from $32,720 to $30,820, a 1.2% decline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.
*   Concurrently, inflation surged by approximately 10%, significantly increasing the cost of essential goods like groceries, gas, and housing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a>.

Even if incomes appear somewhat stable, inflation acts like a "silent tax," eroding financial security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. It's compared to a "leaky bucket," where no matter how much you pour in, it keeps leaking out, eroding purchasing power over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>.

## Why Average Retirement Incomes Are Dropping

Several factors contribute to the decline in average retirement incomes:
*   **Weaker Pensions**: Fewer employers offer guaranteed pensions, transferring more financial risk to retirees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.
*   **Savings Shortfalls**: Wages have not kept pace with inflation, making it harder for individuals to accumulate sufficient savings for retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>.
*   **Years of Low Interest Rates**: Before recent increases, bonds and savings accounts offered minimal returns, forcing retirees to take on more risk or withdraw more from their savings than initially planned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
*   **Longer Retirements**: Canadians are living longer, meaning their savings must last for more decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>.

[[financial_planning_for_retirement_in_canada | Smart planning]] is crucial for protecting retirement savings and navigating these challenges to minimize the risk of outliving one's assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>.

## Comparing Retiree Incomes: Where Do You Fall?

Not all retirees share the same financial situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>.
*   **Senior Couples**: Earning above $74,200 per year places you in the top 50% of retiree households; less than that means you're in the lower half <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.
*   **Single Seniors**: Earning above $30,820 per year puts you in the upper half, while earning below that places you in the lower 50% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.

A significant challenge arises for single retirees, who face a tougher financial situation than couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>. While their overall needs may be less, their costs do not scale down proportionally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. Single individuals often spend 60% to 80% of what a household spends but may only have 50% or less of the income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.
*   **Housing Costs**: Rent or home maintenance costs are often similar for single individuals and couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>.
*   **Food and Utilities**: Couples can share expenses, but a single retiree covers everything alone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a>.
*   **Healthcare Expenses**: Certain medical and insurance costs are fixed regardless of household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.

For single retirees, [[financial_planning_for_retirement_in_canada | financial planning]] is even more crucial to stretch income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.

> [!bulb] Pro Tip: Budget in Phases
> Most retirees spend more in their first 10 to 15 years on activities like travel, hobbies, and home renovations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. It's advisable to structure your [[budgeting_for_retirement_in_canada | retirement plan]] to account for this more active phase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>.

## Why Many Seniors Keep Working

Retirement does not always mean a complete cessation of work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. In 2022, over 1.9 million Canadians aged 65 and older reported employment income, totaling $58.8 billion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. Reasons seniors continue working include:
*   **Financial Necessity**: Insufficient savings to cover all expenses is a primary reason <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>.
*   **Inflation Pressure**: Rising costs make it difficult to rely solely on pensions and savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.
*   **Personal Fulfillment**: Many retirees enjoy staying active through part-time jobs, consulting, or starting small businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.

While working can supplement income, it is not the only strategy. [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | Smart planning]] can help retirees achieve a sustainable financial future without prolonged employment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.

## A [[financial_planning_for_retirement_in_canada | Financial Planning]] Scenario

A typical [[financial_planning_for_retirement_in_canada | financial planning]] process involves multiple meetings and in-depth analysis, but a condensed scenario can illustrate its importance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>.

### Client Profile (John Doe)
*   **Age**: 65, planning to retire soon <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>.
*   **Expected Retirement Income**: Approximately $30,000 (around the Canadian average for single seniors) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:50">[00:06:50]</a>.
*   **Desired Annual Spending**: $65,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:55">[00:06:55]</a>.
*   **Assets**:
    *   RRSP: $400,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>
    *   TFSA: $75,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:03">[00:07:03]</a>
    *   Non-registered account: $50,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>
    *   Principal residence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>
*   **Investment Mix**:
    *   RRSP and TFSA: 60% equity, 40% fixed income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:33">[00:07:33]</a>.
    *   Non-registered account: 20% equity, 80% cash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>.
*   **Concerns/Goals**: Not running out of money, and leaving some money for his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

### Initial Assessment by Financial Planner (Simon Wong)
Using conservative estimates (5.4% for equity, 3.4% for fixed income, 2.4% for cash, and 2.1% inflation rate for spending increases) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:47">[00:07:47]</a>, the initial assessment revealed significant issues:
*   **Unsustainable Spending**: The desired $65,000 spending would increase to $88,000 by age 80 due to inflation, requiring higher withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:26">[00:08:26]</a>.
*   **Running Out of Money**: John would run out of investment money around age 78, with nothing left by age 79, except his principal residence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.
*   **Negative Estate**: To sustain the lifestyle until age 95, John would need to borrow $1.58 million, exceeding the house's growth and resulting in a negative estate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.
*   **Goals Not Met**: Both goals—not running out of money and leaving an inheritance—would not be met under the current situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>.

### Preliminary Recommendations
To address the issues, the financial planner suggested several [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | strategies]]:
1.  **Downgrade Housing**: Sell the $800,000 house and purchase a $600,000 house <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>.
2.  **Delay Retirement**: Delay retirement to age 70, which also allows for delaying Old Age Security (OAS) and Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits until age 70 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>.
3.  **Increase Savings**: Save an additional $1,000 per year into TFSA and $2,000 per year into RRSP over the next five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.
4.  **Adjust Investments**: Be more aggressive with investments due to a longer time horizon, increasing equity allocation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:34">[00:10:34]</a>.
5.  **Decrease Spending**: Reduce annual spending to $60,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:46">[00:10:46]</a>.

### Outcome of Recommendations
With these adjustments, the plan becomes sustainable. While John would still incur some debt by age 95, it would be lower than the house's value, and he would achieve his goals of having enough money to live until 95 and leaving a good amount of assets to his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:53">[00:10:53]</a>.
