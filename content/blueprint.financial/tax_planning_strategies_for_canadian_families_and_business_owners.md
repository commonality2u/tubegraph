## 1. Prescribed Rate Loan: Income Splitting

The prescribed rate loan is one of the remaining legal [[tax_efficient_investment_strategies_in_canada | income splitting]] loopholes in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>. [[common_retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | Income splitting]] is an effective way for families to lower their overall tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:27">[00:00:27]</a>. The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) has strict attribution rules that prevent high-income earners from simply gifting money to a lower-income spouse for investment purposes, as any income generated would typically still be taxed at the higher earner's rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>.

With a prescribed rate loan, money can be legally lent at the CRA's prescribed rate, which is currently 4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>. This shifts investment income into the lower tax bracket of the receiving spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>. Originally designed for corporate planning, this strategy remains viable for families when rules are followed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.

### How it Works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>

1.  **Lending the Money**: The high-income spouse lends money to the low-income spouse at the CRA's prescribed interest rate (e.g., 4%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.
2.  **Investing the Funds**: The low-income spouse invests the borrowed money in stocks, ETFs, or other income-generating assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>.
3.  **Taxation of Income**: Investment income is taxed at the lower-income spouse's tax rate, reducing the overall family tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:17">[00:01:17]</a>.
4.  **Interest Payment**: The low-income spouse *must* pay interest on the loan annually by January 30th for the strategy to remain valid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>.

### Example Scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>

Jim is in a 50% tax bracket, and Pam is in a 20% tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>. Jim lends Pam $100,000 at the 4% prescribed rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>. Pam invests and earns a 30% return ($30,000 in capital gains) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:42">[00:01:42]</a>.

*   **Pam's Taxes**: Pam pays Jim $4,000 in interest, leaving $26,000 in gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:54">[00:01:54]</a>. Since only 50% of capital gains are taxable, Pam is taxed on $15,000 at 20%, resulting in $3,000 in taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.
*   **Jim's Taxes**: Jim pays $2,000 tax on the $4,000 of interest he receives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>.
*   **Total Family Tax**: $3,000 (Pam) + $2,000 (Jim) = $5,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.
*   **Comparison**: If Jim had invested alone, his $30,000 gain ($15,000 taxable) would be taxed at 50%, costing $7,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>.
*   **Tax Savings**: The strategy saves $2,500 ($7,500 - $5,000) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>.
    *   This saving is achieved by shifting investment gains to Pam's lower tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:32">[00:02:32]</a>.
    *   If the income generated is interest or dividend income, the tax savings could be even greater <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>.
    *   For instance, in 2024, the Vanguard VFV S&P 500 ETF delivered a 35% return, showing that such a return in a single year is possible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:59">[00:02:59]</a>.

### Risks and Downsides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:12">[00:03:12]</a>

*   **Investment Risk**: If investments underperform or lose money, the tax savings may not offset the losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>. The low-income spouse still owes interest to the high-income spouse, potentially requiring them to use savings or sell investments at a loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>. The high-income spouse still reports the interest as taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.
*   **Prescribed Rate Changes**: If the CRA raises the prescribed rate (e.g., from 1% in 2020 to 4% presently), the strategy becomes less effective as the loan money needs to earn more to be worthwhile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>.
*   **Strict Payment Rules**: Interest must be paid on time every year. Missing even one payment will cause the CRA to cancel the entire strategy, and all investment income will be taxed back to the high-income spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.

This strategy is most effective in a low-interest rate environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>. As rates in Canada begin to drop, it could become more attractive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>. Further information on [[tax_efficient_investment_strategies_in_canada | income splitting strategies]] can be found in specialized guides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>.

## 2. Capital Dividend Account (CDA): Tax-Free Corporate Withdrawals

The Capital Dividend Account (CDA) is a powerful loophole for [[advanced_tax_strategies_for_canadian_small_businesses | business owners]] to extract tax-free money from a corporation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>. This account tracks the non-taxable portion of capital gains, allowing business owners to withdraw that amount as a tax-free dividend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. Normally, withdrawing money from a corporation incurs personal tax on dividends or salary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>.

### How it Works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>

1.  **Asset Sale**: Your corporation sells an asset, which could be a business, stocks, real estate, or digital assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>.
2.  **Capital Gain Calculation**: In Canada, only 50% of capital gains are taxable, while the other 50% remains non-taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>.
3.  **CDA Credit**: The non-taxable 50% portion of the capital gain is credited to the CDA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>.
4.  **Tax-Free Dividend**: The business owner can then withdraw the full CDA balance as a tax-free dividend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>.

### Example Scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>

Alex's corporation sells a digital asset and makes a $200,000 gain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>. If Alex were to take this money out as a regular dividend, it would be fully taxed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>. However, because 50% of capital gains are non-taxable, $100,000 goes into the CDA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>. Alex can then pay himself a $100,000 tax-free dividend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>. Without the CDA, Alex would have incurred personal tax on those funds, significantly reducing the amount kept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>.

### Key Considerations and Risks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>

*   **Proper Tracking**: The CDA balance must be accurately recorded and reported to the CRA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>. Incorrect reporting can lead to withdrawals being taxed as regular dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>.
*   **Applicable Income**: The CDA applies only to the non-taxable portion of capital gains, life insurance proceeds, and certain capital transactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>. Regular business income, for instance, does not qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
*   **Professional Tax Planning**: [[tax_tips_for_selfemployed_canadians | Careful corporate tax planning]] with a professional is crucial when selling a business or asset within a corporation to maximize tax-free withdrawals and correctly set up the CDA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>.

## 3. Converting Mortgage to Tax-Deductible Loan (Smith Manoeuvre)

In Canada, mortgage interest is generally not tax-deductible, unlike in the US <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>. However, a strategy exists to convert it into a deductible investment loan over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>. By reborrowing against home equity and investing in income-generating assets, homeowners can legally deduct the interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>. This works because the CRA allows interest deductions on loans used for investments but not on mortgages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:49">[00:07:49]</a>.

### How it Works <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>

1.  **Regular Mortgage Payment**: Each month, a portion of your mortgage payment reduces the principal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>.
2.  **Reborrowing via HELOC**: The amount paid towards the principal becomes available in a Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC), especially with a readvanceable mortgage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.
3.  **Invest Borrowed Money**: Funds from the HELOC are used to purchase income-generating investments like dividend stocks or ETFs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:23">[00:08:23]</a>.
4.  **Claim Tax Deduction**: Since the borrowed money is used for investments, the interest on the HELOC is tax-deductible, reducing taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>.
5.  **Repeat**: This process is repeated monthly, gradually converting the entire mortgage into a tax-deductible investment loan while the investment portfolio grows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:40">[00:08:40]</a>.

### Example Scenario <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>

David has a $500,000 mortgage and makes a $2,000 monthly payment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>. Out of this, $800 goes towards the principal and becomes available in his HELOC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>. He borrows and invests that $800 in dividend stocks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>. Over time, David continues to reborrow and convert his mortgage into an investment loan, making the HELOC interest tax-deductible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>.

### Risks and Downsides <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>

*   **Interest Rate Risk**: HELOCs have variable interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. If rates rise, borrowing costs increase, making the strategy less attractive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:26">[00:09:26]</a>.
*   **Market Volatility**: If investments underperform or lose money, interest on the HELOC still must be paid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:33">[00:09:33]</a>. Even if the portfolio drops, the borrower is still responsible for loan payments, potentially leading to a dangerous financial position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>.
*   **Discipline Required**: The HELOC money *must* be reinvested consistently and used solely for investment purposes, not personal expenses, to maintain the tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:56">[00:09:56]</a>. Misuse can lead to serious financial and tax issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:02">[00:10:02]</a>.
*   **CRA Scrutiny**: The CRA requires that borrowed money be used for income-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>. If investments do not generate income (e.g., a growth stock with no dividends), the tax deduction could be denied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:16">[00:10:16]</a>.

This strategy is most effective in a low-interest rate environment where borrowing costs are low compared to potential investment returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>. While rates are starting to come down, making it more attractive, it is not for everyone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:29">[00:10:29]</a>. It requires significant comfort with leverage and investment risk, as well as strict discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:37">[00:10:37]</a>.
