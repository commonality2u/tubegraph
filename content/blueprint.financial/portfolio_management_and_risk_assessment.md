## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Two significant mistakes can hinder your journey to a $1 million TFSA:

### Mistake 1: Investing Only in a Savings Account
Relying solely on a savings account for TFSA investments is a slow and costly approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>. For example, with an initial $95,000, annual contributions of $7,000, and a 2% interest rate, it would take 56 years to reach $1 million, costing $487,000 in contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>. Starting at age 25, this strategy would mean reaching the goal at age 81 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:01">[00:01:01]</a>. A survey indicates that 47% of Canadian TFSA holders have only cash and savings in their accounts, making it unlikely for many to reach a $1 million goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:09">[00:01:09]</a>.

### Mistake 2: Taking On Too Much Risk
On the opposite end, extreme high-risk investments, such as highly speculative crypto coins, are also a poor strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>. While an investor might hypothetically double their money yearly to reach $1 million in 3-4 years with $120,000 invested <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>, the vast majority trying this "high-risk, high-reward" approach will not achieve such returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. The chances of losing all capital are extremely high, rendering the strategy worthless if the investment goes to zero <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>. As Warren Buffett famously stated, the first rule of investing is "never lose money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>.

## [[balancing_risk_and_return_to_optimize_investment_strategies | Balancing Risk and Return to Optimize Investment Strategies]]

Investing is akin to choosing a driving road; a faster highway comes with higher risks, while a slower back road is safer but takes longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>. Similarly, higher investment returns often involve higher risks, while safer investments offer steadier but lower returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>. The key is to find a middle ground between the too-slow, low-risk approach and the high-risk method with a high chance of losing everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.

To begin, you need to [[understanding_risk_and_investment_choices | understand your risk tolerance]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:29">[00:03:29]</a>. This can be done by completing a free online questionnaire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. Once you've assessed your risk tolerance, you can select appropriate investments to reach your goal within a reasonable timeframe, combined with regular TFSA contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.

### Realistic Investment Examples

Here are two realistic strategies based on different risk tolerances:

*   **100% Equities (High Risk Tolerance)**: Investing entirely in stocks (equities) with an average return of 7% (based on the Canadian stock market's 20-year return of 7.8%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. Starting with $95,000 and contributing $7,000 annually, it would take 25 years to reach $1 million, with total contributions of $270,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. This allows someone starting at age 40 to reach the goal by age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.
*   **50% Equities / 50% Bonds (Medium Risk Tolerance)**: For a medium risk tolerance, a portfolio split evenly between equities and bonds (with bonds averaging 2-3% per year over 20 years) could yield a 4.5% annual return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>. With an initial $95,000 and $7,000 annual contributions, it would take about 35 years to reach $1 million, with total contributions of $340,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. This strategy allows someone starting at age 30 to reach the goal by age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>.

The time it takes to reach your goal depends on your risk and return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. The "sweet spot" involves taking enough risk for a sufficient return without risking a substantial loss of capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:24">[00:05:24]</a>.

## [[Choosing Investment Types and Accounts | Choosing Investment Types and Accounts]]

Here are five investment options for your TFSA:

1.  **Avoiding Mutual Funds at Big Banks**: While convenient, mutual funds offered by big banks often have very high fees, sometimes exceeding 3% annually, which significantly erodes investment gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>. This makes them less recommended for long-term wealth building <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>.
2.  **ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds)**: [[Choosing the right allinone ETF based on risk tolerance | ETFs]] are a cost-effective option that mirror market index performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>. Providers like Vanguard and iShares offer ETFs with very low fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>. However, they expose you to market volatility and require basic trading knowledge as they are bought and sold on stock exchanges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>.
3.  **Individual Stocks**: Investing in individual stocks offers ultimate control and the lowest fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. A dividend stock strategy can provide both capital appreciation and regular income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. This approach requires significant market knowledge, time for portfolio management, and diversification to mitigate increased risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>. [[Developing an investment philosophy | Developing an investment philosophy]] is crucial here.
4.  **Robo-Advisors**: These automated platforms manage investments using algorithms, aligning with your financial goals and [[understanding_risk_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>. Popular Canadian options include Wealthsimple and Questwealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>. They offer convenience, simplicity, and a hands-off approach, accessible even with small starting amounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>. However, they lack the personal touch of a human advisor and may not fully capture unique investment opportunities or tailor strategies for changing financial circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.
5.  **Portfolio Managers**: For those who find self-management overwhelming or prefer a personal touch over algorithms, hiring a professional portfolio manager is an excellent option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>. Traditionally for the wealthy, this service is now more accessible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:30">[00:08:30]</a>. A professional manager can tailor your portfolio to specific needs and [[understanding_risk_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]], optimizing chances for success without daily hassle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>.

## Retirement Withdrawal Strategies

Once you reach your $1 million TFSA goal, strategic withdrawal planning is essential to [[investment_account_prioritization_and_tax_efficiency | maximize tax savings in retirement]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>.

### TFSA vs. RSP Withdrawal Prioritization
The decision to withdraw from your TFSA or RSP first is not one-size-fits-all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>.

*   **RSP First**: Withdrawing from your RSP earlier in retirement can reduce your overall tax burden, especially before Old Age Security (OAS) clawbacks might apply <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
*   **TFSA First**: TFSA withdrawals are not taxable, allowing your investments to continue tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>. This provides a tax-efficient fund source that doesn't affect your OAS <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:38">[00:09:38]</a>.

### Bucket Strategy for a $1 Million TFSA
For a substantial TFSA, consider a bucket strategy to ensure longevity and continued growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>:

*   **Short-Term Funds**: Keep in safer investments like bonds or GICs for immediate expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>.
*   **Medium-Term Funds**: Invest in balanced funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.
*   **Long-Term Funds**: Maintain in equities for growth against inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

This approach ensures funds are available when needed while still [[tracking_and_managing_investment_performance | working for you]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>.

### Managing TFSA with CPP
A strategic approach integrates your TFSA with your Canada Pension Plan (CPP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>. You can use your TFSA to supplement CPP income during years of lower income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>. Additionally, consider delaying CPP withdrawals while drawing from your TFSA if you retire early and need funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[0:10:31]</a>. This allows your government benefits to grow, maximizing later retirement income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>.
