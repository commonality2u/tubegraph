This article compares the features of Japan's Nissa account and the USA's Roth IRA, as part of a global tournament-style evaluation of [[Comparison of taxfree accounts in different countries | tax-free accounts]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>.

## Contribution Limits

*   **Japan's Nissa** The revamped Nissa offers two contribution pathways:
    *   Growth Nissa: Up to 2.4 million yen per year (approximately $23,000 CAD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>.
    *   Sumitate Nissa: 1.2 million yen per year (approximately $11,000 CAD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>.
    Even the lower Sumitate amount surpasses the Roth IRA's limit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:04">[00:03:04]</a>. The Growth Nissa is a powerhouse for those looking to invest more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:11">[00:03:11]</a>.
*   **USA's Roth IRA** Allows contributions of $7,000 USD (approximately $9,500 CAD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:05">[00:03:05]</a>.
    > The goal for annual contribution limits goes to Japan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.

## Flexibility

*   **Japan's Nissa** Allows tax-free withdrawals at any time, with no age restrictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:19">[00:03:19]</a>. However, withdrawn amounts cannot be recontributed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>.
*   **USA's Roth IRA** Allows penalty-free and tax-free withdrawal of *contributions* at any time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>. However, withdrawing *earnings* before age 59.5 may incur taxes and penalties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>.
    > Japan wins in flexibility due to the freedom to access funds without penalty on earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:26">[00:03:26]</a>.

## Lifetime Limit

*   **Japan's Nissa** The new Nissa has raised its lifetime cap to approximately 18 million yen (about $174,000 CAD) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a>. While this is a significant improvement for Japan, it remains capped, which limits its long-term growth potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>.
*   **USA's Roth IRA** Features a growing lifetime contribution room that is essentially unlimited <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
    > The Roth IRA's unlimited lifetime contribution space is a decisive advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:52">[00:03:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.

## Simplicity

*   **Japan's Nissa** Can be challenging for new investors to navigate due to its two account types, lifetime tracking, and overlapping rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>.
*   **USA's Roth IRA** While not perfect, it is generally easier to understand than the Japan's Nissa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.
    > Simplicity favors the USA's Roth IRA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>.

## Retirement Focus

*   **USA's Roth IRA** Excels in retirement-focused savings due to its tax-free withdrawals in retirement and strong incentives for qualified use <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:07">[00:06:07]</a>. It offers more options for estate planning, allowing assets to be passed to beneficiaries in a tax-free manner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>.
    > The Roth IRA is considered the best for long-term retirement savings and estate planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>.

## Conclusion

The competition between Japan's Nissa and the USA's Roth IRA is very close, ending in a tie at the end of regulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>. However, in overtime, the Roth IRA's unlimited lifetime contribution space proves decisive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>. Despite Japan's Nissa offering strong features like higher annual limits and greater flexibility, its lower lifetime cap ultimately limits its long-term advantage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>. Therefore, the USA's Roth IRA wins the match, advancing to the finals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:36">[00:04:36]</a>.
