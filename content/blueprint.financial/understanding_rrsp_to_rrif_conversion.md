Converting a Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RSP) into a Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF) is a critical step in [[estate_planning_with_rrif | retirement planning]] that can significantly impact your financial future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>. Think of your RSP as a highway leading to retirement, and the RRIF as the exit roundabout where you manage withdrawals to avoid "speeding tickets," which are taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>.

## Mandatory Conversion

You must convert your RSP to a RRIF by December 31st of the year you turn 71 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>. Missing this deadline will result in the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) considering your entire RSP balance as taxable income for that year, potentially pushing you into the highest marginal tax bracket (50% or more) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>.

For example, if Thea turns 71 on June 15th, 2025, and has a $500,000 RSP, she must convert it to a RRIF by December 31st, 2025 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>. If she misses this deadline, her $500,000 RSP balance would be treated as taxable income for 2025, potentially leading to a $250,000 tax bill at a 50% marginal tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

## Mandatory RRIF Withdrawals

Once your RRIF is set up, withdrawals are mandatory starting in the year you turn 72 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>. The government requires a minimum withdrawal percentage based on your age <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>. For instance, at age 72, the minimum withdrawal is 5.4% of the RRIF's value, gradually increasing each year to 20% by age 95 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:15">[00:01:15]</a>.

Continuing Thea's example, if she converts her RSP on time and her RRIF is valued at $500,000 on December 31st, 2025, then in 2026 (the year she turns 72), she must withdraw the government-mandated minimum of 5.4%, which is $27,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. This percentage will gradually increase as she ages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>.

## [[tax_minimization_strategies_for_rrif | RRIF Tax Minimization Strategies]] and Essentials

Several basic strategies can help reduce your tax burden from RRIF withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>.

### Pension Income Tax Credit

One significant benefit of RRIFs is access to the pension income tax credit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:30">[00:02:30]</a>. Canadians age 65 and older can claim a deduction on up to $2,000 of eligible pension income annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>. This credit reduces the amount of income tax owed on that portion of your income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>. It's important to note that RSP withdrawals do not qualify for this credit, but RRIF withdrawals do <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.

For example, Leila, 65, has a $200,000 RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>. To take advantage of the credit, she converts $40,000 from her RSP into a RRIF and plans to withdraw $2,000 annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. By claiming the $2,000 pension income tax credit, and assuming a 30% marginal tax rate, she saves $600 annually ($2,000 x 30%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>. Over five years, she withdraws a total of $10,000 tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

### Avoiding OAS Clawbacks

Large RRIF withdrawals can push your income above the Old Age Security (OAS) clawback threshold, which is approximately $90,000 as of 2024, potentially reducing your benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:52">[00:03:52]</a>. Coordinating withdrawals with tax-free income sources like a Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) can help mitigate this <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>.

For instance, David, 72, withdraws $20,000 annually from his $200,000 RRIF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>. By supplementing his income with TFSA withdrawals, he keeps his taxable income below the clawback threshold, preserving his OAS benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14">[00:04:14]</a>. This aligns with the principle of [[using_tfsa_wisely_with_rrsp | using TFSA wisely with RRSP]].

### Minimize Fees

Unlike direct RSP withdrawals, which often incur additional fees (typically $50-$150) from financial institutions, moving funds into a RRIF for withdrawals eliminates these costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.

### Using a Younger Spouse's Age

A lesser-known strategy is to leverage a younger spouse's age to calculate the mandatory minimum RRIF withdrawal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>. This lowers the percentage you're required to withdraw each year, preserving more funds in your RRIF for tax-deferred growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:59">[00:04:59]</a>.

For example, Emma, 72, has a $1 million RRIF, normally requiring a 5.4% ($54,000) withdrawal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>. If her spouse, Alex, is 65, Emma can use Alex's age to calculate her mandatory withdrawal at 4%, reducing her required withdrawal to $40,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. This adjustment lowers her taxable income by $14,000 and leaves more of her RRIF to continue growing tax-deferred <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>.

### Understanding Withholding Taxes

Withholding taxes on RRIF withdrawals act as a prepayment towards your annual income tax bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:42">[00:05:42]</a>. While you cannot directly minimize them, they are reconciled when you file your tax return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:53">[00:05:53]</a>. If the withholding tax exceeds your actual tax liability, you'll receive a refund; otherwise, you'll owe the difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>.

Key points regarding withholding taxes:
*   Mandatory minimum RRIF withdrawals are not subject to withholding tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.
*   Withdrawals above the minimum are subject to withholding tax based on the amount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>:
    *   Up to $5,000: 10% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>
    *   $5,000.01 to $15,000: 20% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>
    *   Above $15,000: 30% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>

For example, Jon, 72, has a $400,000 RRIF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:37">[00:06:37]</a>. His mandatory minimum withdrawal in 2026 is 5.4%, or $21,600, which he receives in full without withholding tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>. If Jon withdraws an additional $10,000 (totaling $31,500), a 20% withholding tax applies to the additional $10,000, meaning $2,000 is withheld, and he receives $8,000 of the additional amount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:56">[00:06:56]</a>.

## Advanced Tax Strategies for Managing Your RRIF

Managing your RRIF effectively can reduce taxes, maintain a steady retirement income, and preserve wealth for beneficiaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>.

### Using RRIF as a Bridge Fund to Delay CPP and OAS

A smart strategy involves using RRIF withdrawals to fund early retirement while delaying Canada Pension Plan (CPP) and OAS for higher future benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>. Delaying CPP to age 70 can increase it by 42% compared to age 65, and OAS can increase by 36% if delayed to age 70 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:54">[00:07:54]</a>.

For instance, Michael retires at 60 with a $400,000 RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>. He converts $200,000 to a RRIF and withdraws $20,000 annually until 70 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. At 70, his CPP increases from $1,200 to $1,704 per month, and his OAS rises from $700 to $952 per month (compared to a 65-start date) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.

Advantages include:
*   **Higher Lifetime Benefits**: Delayed CPP and OAS offer inflation-adjusted income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>.
*   **Potential Tax Efficiency**: RRIF withdrawals spread income over lower tax years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>.
*   **Preserved Savings**: The remaining RSP continues to grow tax-deferred <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:42">[00:08:42]</a>.

### RRIF Meltdown Strategy

This strategy involves gradually reducing your RRIF balance while reinvesting withdrawals into tax-efficient accounts like a TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>. The goal is to spread taxable income over many years, avoiding significant tax hits in high-income years or at death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>.

For example, David, 70, has a $500,000 RRIF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>. He withdraws $40,000 annually, with $20,000 going into his TFSA and the other $20,000 covering living expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>. This approach steadily decreases his RRIF balance, reducing the taxable amount left to his estate while maintaining a diversified investment portfolio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:21">[00:09:21]</a>.

### RRIF Income Splitting for Couples

RRIF withdrawals can be split with a spouse to reduce household taxes, especially if one spouse has a much lower income tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>. Both partners can claim the $2,000 pension income tax credit, effectively doubling the benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:41">[00:09:41]</a>.

For instance, Mark withdraws $10,000 annually from his RRIF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:44">[00:09:44]</a>. By allocating $5,000 to his wife Susan, who earns less, their combined taxable income decreases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>. Both claim the $2,000 pension income tax credit, saving $1,200 in taxes annually, potentially accumulating to $12,000 over a decade <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:53">[00:09:53]</a>.

### Partial RSP to RRIF Conversions

Converting smaller amounts of your RSP into a RRIF before age 71 spreads taxable income over multiple years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>. This also allows you to start benefiting from features like the pension income tax credit as early as age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>.

For example, Sophia, 65, has a $300,000 RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>. She partially converts $50,000 annually to a RRIF for six years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>. By withdrawing $2,000 each year, she maximizes the pension income tax credit, saving up to $600 annually, while the remaining RSP funds continue to grow tax-deferred <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:29">[00:10:29]</a>. More advanced strategies related to [[taxefficient_rrsp_withdrawal_strategies | tax-efficient RRSP withdrawal strategies]] are available in other resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:41">[00:10:41]</a>.

## [[estate_planning_with_rrif | Estate Planning for RRIFs]]

Planning your RRIF as part of your estate can reduce taxes and ensure beneficiaries receive the maximum benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:49">[00:10:49]</a>.

### Spousal Rollovers

If your spouse or common-law partner is the designated beneficiary, the RRIF balance can roll over tax-deferred to their RSP or RRIF <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:58">[00:10:58]</a>. This avoids immediate taxation and preserves the funds for their retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:04">[00:11:04]</a>.

For instance, if John's RRIF is worth $500,000 when he passes away and his wife Sarah is the designated beneficiary, the entire balance transfers to her RRIF without triggering taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:09">[00:11:09]</a>. Sarah can then withdraw funds based on her own schedule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:20">[00:11:20]</a>.

### Non-Spouse Beneficiaries

When the beneficiary is not a spouse, the RRIF balance is fully taxed as income in the year of your death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:25">[00:11:25]</a>. Proper planning can help mitigate this impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>. For example, if Tom has a $400,000 RRIF and his adult child is the beneficiary, his estate may face taxes exceeding $200,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a>. By starting withdrawals earlier and spreading them out, Tom could reduce the RRIF balance and thus the tax bill upon his death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:45">[00:11:45]</a>.

### Using Life Insurance

A highly effective strategy is to use life insurance to cover the cost of these taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:53">[00:11:53]</a>. You can withdraw from your RRIF throughout the years to pay for this life insurance policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:00">[00:12:00]</a>.

### Charitable Giving

Naming a charity as a beneficiary of your RRIF is a tax-smart way to leave a legacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:13">[00:12:13]</a>. The donation generates a tax credit that can offset the taxes owed on the RRIF balance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>. Key benefits include reduced estate taxes, bypassing probate fees, and directly supporting a meaningful cause <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:25">[00:12:25]</a>.

## Pro Tips for Managing RRIF Accounts

*   **Consider starting RSP to RRIF conversions early**: Converting partial small amounts before age 71 allows you to claim the $2,000 pension income tax credit starting at 65, managing taxable income and avoiding large tax hits later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:34">[00:12:34]</a>.
*   **Consolidate RRIF accounts**: Combining multiple RRIFs simplifies administration, reduces fees, and ensures mandatory withdrawals are met without confusion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:50">[00:12:50]</a>.
*   **Use RRIF calculators**: Online calculators can project withdrawals, estimate taxes, and show how different rates impact savings, giving you better control of your finances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03">[00:13:03]</a>.
*   **Use in-kind transfers**: Rather than selling investments, you can transfer them from your RSP to your RRIF in kind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:22">[00:13:22]</a>. This preserves the growth potential of assets, avoids transaction fees, and maintains your investment strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:27">[00:13:27]</a>.
*   **Coordinate RRIF withdrawals with other income**: Balance RRIF withdrawals with CPP, OAS, TFSAs, and other accounts to manage taxes and prevent OAS clawbacks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:34">[00:13:34]</a>. A mix of taxable and tax-free income maximizes after-tax retirement income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:43">[00:13:43]</a>. This is part of [[using_tfsa_and_rrsp_together_for_tax_savings | using TFSA and RRSP together for tax savings]].

Smart RRIF management is key to keeping more of your money in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:48">[00:13:48]</a>. With the right strategies, you can reduce taxes, extend the life of your savings, and enjoy financial peace of mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:53">[00:13:53]</a>.
