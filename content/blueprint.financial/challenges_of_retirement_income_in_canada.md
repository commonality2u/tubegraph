## The Reality of Retirement Income in Canada

The average retirement income in Canada is often lower than anticipated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>. As of 2022, for Canadians aged 65 and older, the median after-tax income was <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>:
*   **For couples**: $74,200 per year, or $6,183 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>.
*   **For single seniors**: $30,820 per year, or $2,568 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>.

For context, the median income across all age groups in Canada is approximately $95,000 for couples and $33,000 for single individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. This highlights that many retirees are living on significantly less than the working population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>. The question then becomes whether a comfortable retirement is possible with these income levels, especially with rising costs for housing, food, and healthcare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:13">[00:01:13]</a>.

## Impact of Surging Inflation

Retirement income isn't just stagnating; its purchasing power is actively eroding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:25">[00:01:25]</a>.
*   Between 2020 and 2022, senior couples saw their median after-tax income decline by 1.4%, from $76,860 to $74,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>.
*   Single retirees experienced a 1.2% decrease in income, from $32,720 to $30,820 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

During this same period, inflation surged by approximately 10%, increasing the cost of essential goods like groceries, gas, and housing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a>. Even if incomes appear somewhat stable, inflation acts as a "silent tax," steadily reducing [[economic_challenges_and_their_effect_on_retirement_savings | financial security]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:02">[00:02:02]</a>. Inflation erodes purchasing power over time, similar to a leaky bucket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:07">[00:02:07]</a>.

## Factors Contributing to Declining Retirement Incomes

Several key [[factors_affecting_retiree_financial_security | factors]] contribute to the drop in average retirement incomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>:

*   **Weaker Pensions**: Fewer employers offer guaranteed pensions, transferring more financial risk to retirees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>.
*   **Savings Shortfalls**: Wages have not kept pace with inflation, making it challenging for individuals to accumulate sufficient savings for retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:35">[00:02:35]</a>.
*   **Years of Low Interest Rates**: Before recent increases, bonds and savings accounts, traditionally reliable income sources, offered minimal returns, forcing retirees to take on more risk or withdraw more than planned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
*   **Longer Retirements**: Canadians are living longer, meaning their savings must last for decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>.

This highlights why smart [[retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | planning]] is crucial, not just for saving, but for protecting accumulated wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>. Proper planning helps retirees navigate these challenges and minimize the risk of outliving their savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>.

## Comparing Retiree Incomes

Not all retirees are in the same financial situation; some are comfortable, while others struggle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>.
*   A senior couple earning above $74,200 per year is in the top 50% of retiree households <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.
*   A single senior earning above $30,820 per year is in the upper half <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>.

Single retirees face a significantly tougher [[financial_planning_for_canadian_retirees | financial situation]] than couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:47">[00:03:47]</a>. While their overall needs are less, their costs do not scale down proportionally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. Single individuals often spend 60% to 80% of what a household spends, but may have 50% or less of the income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.

This disparity is due to various costs:
*   **Housing**: Rent or home maintenance costs are often the same for single individuals as for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:10">[00:04:10]</a>.
*   **Food and Utilities**: A single retiree covers all expenses on their own, unlike a couple who can share grocery and utility bills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:15">[00:04:15]</a>.
*   **Healthcare**: Certain medical and insurance costs are fixed regardless of household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.

For single retirees, [[financial_planning_for_canadian_retirees | financial planning]] is even more critical, requiring greater strategic effort to stretch income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>.

> [!TIP] Budget in Phases
> Most retirees spend more in their first 10 to 15 years on activities like travel, hobbies, and home renovations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. It is advisable to structure a retirement plan to account for this active phase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>.

## Why Many Seniors Keep Working

Retirement does not always mean a complete cessation of work. In 2022, over 1.9 million Canadians aged 65 and older reported employment income, contributing a combined $58.8 billion in earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>.

Reasons why seniors continue working include:
*   **Financial Necessity**: Some retirees lack sufficient savings to cover their expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>.
*   **Inflation Pressure**: Rising costs make it difficult to rely solely on pensions and savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>.
*   **Personal Fulfillment**: Many retirees enjoy staying active in the workforce through part-time jobs, consulting, or starting small businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:33">[00:05:33]</a>.

While working can supplement income, it is not the only strategy. [[retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | Smart planning]] can help create a sustainable financial future without relying on extended employment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>.

## Importance of Financial Planning

A comprehensive financial plan helps reduce uncertainty, provides options, and enhances retirees' sense of security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. It can also highlight unrealistic goals early on, which is better than discovering a shortfall later in life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:55">[00:11:55]</a>.

### Sample Retirement Planning Scenario

To illustrate the value of planning, a condensed scenario was presented involving a client named John Doe, a 65-year-old planning to retire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>.

**John's Initial Situation**:
*   **Age**: 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>
*   **Planned Retirement Income**: $30,000 (around Canadian average) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:50">[00:06:50]</a>
*   **Desired Spending**: $65,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:56">[00:06:56]</a>
*   **Assets**:
    *   RRSP: $400,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>
    *   TFSA: $75,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>
    *   Non-registered account: $50,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:04">[00:07:04]</a>
*   **Investment Mix**:
    *   RRSP & TFSA: 60% equity, 40% fixed income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:33">[00:07:33]</a>
    *   Non-registered: 20% equity, 80% cash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>
*   **Concerns**: Running out of money, wanting to leave money for his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

**Initial Assessment (Conservative Estimates)**:
*   **Rates of Return**: Equity 5.4%, Fixed Income 3.4%, Cash 2.4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>.
*   **Inflation (spending increase)**: 2.1% annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:03">[00:08:03]</a>.

The initial assessment revealed that John's $65,000 spending would increase to about $88,000 by age 80 due to inflation, requiring higher withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:26">[00:08:26]</a>. Unfortunately, with his current plan, John would run out of money around age 78, with nothing left by age 79 except his principal residence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>. If he were to live to 95, he would need to borrow $1.58 million to sustain his lifestyle, which would exceed his home's growth, leading to a negative estate and failure to meet both his goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.

**Recommendations for John**:
To address these issues, the financial planner proposed several adjustments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>:
1.  **Downgrade Housing**: Move from an $800,000 house to a $600,000 house <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>.
2.  **Delay Retirement**: Delay retirement to age 70, which would allow for delayed Old Age Security (OAS) and Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits to age 70 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>.
3.  **Increase Savings**: Save an additional $1,000 annually into his TFSA and $2,000 annually into his RRSP over the next five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:25">[00:10:25]</a>.
4.  **Adjust Investment Aggressiveness**: Increase asset allocation into equity due to a longer investment time frame <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:34">[00:10:34]</a>.
5.  **Decrease Spending**: Reduce annual spending to $60,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:46">[00:10:46]</a>.

**Revised Outcome**:
With these changes, the plan became sustainable. John would still have some debt by age 95, but it would be lower than his house value, allowing him to live to age 95 and leave a significant amount of assets to his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:57">[00:10:57]</a>.

If someone is relying solely on average income without assets or home ownership, [[retirement_planning_strategies_in_canada | financial planning]] becomes even more vital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:03">[00:12:03]</a>. The earlier one starts, the more control they will have over their [[financial_stability_and_retirement_income | financial future]] and the easier it will be to build security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:13">[00:12:13]</a>. Retirement is too important to leave to chance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:19">[00:12:19]</a>.
