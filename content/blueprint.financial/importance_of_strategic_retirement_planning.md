## The Reality of Retiree Income in Canada

The average retiree income in Canada is lower than many might anticipate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:33">[00:00:33]</a>. As of 2022, for Canadians aged 65 and older, the median after-tax income was:
*   **Couples:** $74,200 per year ($6,183 per month) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>. This is significantly less than the median income for all age groups in Canada, which is approximately $95,000 for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>.
*   **Single Seniors:** $30,820 per year ($2,568 per month) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>. This is also considerably lower than the median income of about $33,000 for single individuals across all age groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.

This disparity means many retirees are living on substantially less than the working population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:00">[01:06:00]</a>. With rising costs for housing, food, and healthcare, the purchasing power of these incomes is severely challenged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>.

### The Impact of Surging Inflation

Retirement income isn't just stagnating; it's actively losing purchasing power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:00">[01:22:00]</a>. Between 2020 and 2022:
*   Senior couples saw their median after-tax income decrease by 1.4% (from $76,860 to $74,200) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:00">[01:30:00]</a>.
*   Single retirees experienced a 1.2% decrease (from $32,720 to $30,820) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:40:00">[01:40:00]</a>.

Simultaneously, inflation surged by approximately 10%, driving up the cost of essential goods like groceries, gas, and housing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:00">[01:49:00]</a>. Even minor income drops, when combined with significant inflation, act like a "silent tax," eroding financial security and purchasing power over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:00">[02:02:00]</a>.

### Why Average Retirement Incomes Are Dropping

Several factors contribute to the decline in average retirement incomes:
*   **Weaker Pensions** Fewer employers offer guaranteed pensions, shifting financial risk onto retirees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:26:00">[02:26:00]</a>.
*   **Savings Shortfalls** Wages have not kept pace with inflation, making it challenging for individuals to accumulate sufficient savings for retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:35:00">[02:35:00]</a>.
*   **Years of Low Interest Rates** Before recent increases, bonds and savings accounts offered minimal returns, forcing retirees to take on more risk or withdraw more than planned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:00">[02:41:00]</a>.
*   **Longer Retirements** Canadians are living longer, meaning their savings need to sustain them for more decades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:00">[02:55:00]</a>.

## The Crucial Role of Strategic Planning

Given these challenges, [[importance_of_retirement_planning | smart planning really matters]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:02:00">[03:02:00]</a>. [[comprehensive_financial_planning_for_retirement | Proper planning]] is not just about saving; it's about protecting accumulated wealth and helping retirees navigate financial challenges to minimize the risk of outliving their savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:04:00">[03:04:00]</a>.

### Planning for Single Retirees

Single retirees face a tougher financial situation compared to couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:47:00">[03:47:00]</a>. While their overall spending needs might be lower, their costs do not scale down proportionally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. For instance, single individuals might spend 60-80% of what a household spends, but often have 50% or less of the income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

This is due to:
*   **Housing Costs** Rent or home maintenance costs are often the same whether single or part of a couple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:10:00">[04:10:00]</a>.
*   **Food and Utilities** Single retirees cover all expenses independently, unlike couples who can share bills and grocery costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:15:00">[04:15:00]</a>.
*   **Healthcare Expenses** Certain medical and insurance costs remain fixed regardless of household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:21:00">[04:21:00]</a>.

For single retirees, [[strategies_for_retirement_planning_and_income_management as a single retiree | financial planning is even more crucial]] and requires more strategic approaches to stretch income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>. A key tip is to budget in phases, as most retirees spend more on travel, hobbies, and home renovations in their first 10-15 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:35:00">[04:35:00]</a>.

### Why Seniors Keep Working (and how planning helps)

A significant number of seniors continue working in their later years due to:
*   **Financial Necessity** Insufficient savings to cover expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:20:00">[05:20:00]</a>.
*   **Inflation Pressure** Rising costs make it difficult to rely solely on pensions and savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:27:00">[05:27:00]</a>.
*   **Personal Fulfillment** Many enjoy staying active through part-time jobs, consulting, or starting small businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:33:00">[05:33:00]</a>.

While working can supplement income, it's not the only strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:42:00">[05:42:00]</a>. With smart planning, retirees can build a sustainable financial future without extending their employment, or they can choose to work for fulfillment rather than necessity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:46:00">[05:46:00]</a>.

## A Glimpse into Strategic Financial Planning

A financial plan typically involves multiple meetings and extensive analysis to set goals, assess finances, and build a strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:03:00">[06:03:00]</a>. Simon Wong, Head Financial Planner at Blueprint Financial, illustrates this with a condensed example <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:22:00">[06:22:00]</a>.

**Scenario: "John Doe" (age 65, planning to retire soon)**
*   **Income:** $30,000 in retirement (around Canadian average) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:48:00">[06:48:00]</a>.
*   **Desired Spending:** $65,000 in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:56:00">[06:56:00]</a>.
*   **Assets:** $400,000 in RRSP, $75,000 in TFSA, $50,000 in non-registered account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:59:00">[06:59:00]</a>.
*   **Concerns:** Running out of money and wishing to leave money for his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:17:00">[07:17:00]</a>.
*   **Current Investments:** 60% equity/40% fixed income in RRSP/TFSA; 20% equity/80% cash in non-registered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:33:00">[07:33:00]</a>.
*   **Conservative Estimates Used:** 5.4% for equity, 3.4% for fixed income, 2.4% for cash, and 2.1% inflation on spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:47:00">[07:47:00]</a>.

### Initial Assessment (Without a Plan)

The initial assessment reveals significant concerns for John:
*   His $65,000 spending will increase to about $88,000 by age 80 due to inflation, requiring higher withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:26:00">[08:26:00]</a>.
*   He would likely run out of money around age 78, with nothing left by age 79 except his principal residence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:48:00">[08:48:00]</a>.
*   To sustain his lifestyle to age 95, he would need to borrow $1.58 million, exceeding his house value and resulting in a negative estate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:12:00">[09:12:00]</a>.
*   Both goals (not running out of money, leaving an inheritance) are not met in his current situation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:32:00">[09:32:00]</a>.

### Recommendations (With Strategic Planning)

To address these issues, the financial planner proposes several [[financial_strategies for retirement | strategies for securing financial future in retirement]]:
*   **Downgrade Housing:** Sell an $800,000 house for a $600,000 one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:09:00">[10:09:00]</a>.
*   **Delay Retirement:** Delay retirement to age 70, allowing for delayed OAS and CPP benefits (which increase over time) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:18:00">[10:18:00]</a>.
*   **Increase Savings:** Save an additional $1,000 annually into TFSA and $2,000 into RRSP over the next five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:25:00">[10:25:00]</a>. This highlights the [[importance_of_regular_contributions_in_retirement_planning | importance of regular contributions in retirement planning]].
*   **Adjust Investment Aggressiveness:** Increase asset allocation to equity due to the longer investment timeframe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:34:00">[10:34:00]</a>.
*   **Decrease Spending:** Reduce overall spending to $60,000 (from $65,000) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:46:00">[10:46:00]</a>.

With these adjustments, the plan becomes sustainable. John would have enough money to live comfortably up to age 95, with manageable debt that is lower than his house value, and he would be able to leave a substantial amount to his daughter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:07:00">[11:07:00]</a>.

## Conclusion: Securing Your Future

This example demonstrates how a well-structured plan reduces uncertainty, provides options, and helps retirees feel more secure about their future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:46:00">[11:46:00]</a>. It also helps to identify if financial goals are unrealistic before it's too late <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:55:00">[11:55:00]</a>.

While this example assumes existing assets, financial planning is even more critical for those relying solely on average income without assets or homeownership <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:03:00">[12:03:00]</a>. The earlier one starts planning, the more control they will have over their financial future, making it easier to build long-term security <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:13:00">[12:13:00]</a>. Retirement is too important to leave to chance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:19:00">[12:19:00]</a>.
