## CPP Basics: When to Claim?

While over 95% of Canadians start CPP at or before age 65, and over 70% claim benefits at age 60 or 65, there are options to start payments between age 60 and 70 [00:00:01].

*   **Starting Early (Age 60)**: Choosing to start CPP at age 60 reduces your payments by 0.6% per month before age 65, leading to a 36% lifetime reduction compared to starting at 65 [00:01:19]. For example, if you qualify for $1,000 per month at 65, you'd receive $640 per month at 60 [00:01:42].
*   **Delaying (Age 70)**: Waiting until age 70 boosts your payments by 0.7% per month after age 65, resulting in a 42% increase in monthly income [00:01:31]. The same $1,000 per month at 65 would become $1,420 per month at 70 [00:01:51].

## Actuarial Report Insights: Optimizing Your CPP Start Age

A detailed 2020 Actuarial study on CPP take-up decisions provides a "cheat sheet" to guide your decision [00:02:19].

### Starting CPP at 60
This option is generally best for those with immediate income needs or health concerns suggesting a shorter [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancy]] [00:02:44].

*   **Permanent Drawbacks**: Comes with reduced lifetime payments (36% reduction compared to age 65), significantly impacting long-term income, especially for those living beyond age 75 [00:02:54].
*   **High Investment Returns**: If your investment funds can achieve 6% or more in annual returns, taking CPP at 60 allows you to invest the payments and potentially come out ahead, though at higher risk [00:03:07].
*   **GIS and OAS Considerations**: Can help avoid clawbacks for low-income retirees, smoothing income across retirement [00:03:21].
*   **Example Scenario**: John, a 60-year-old with health issues and little savings, decides to take CPP early to secure income and avoid clawbacks on his Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) benefits [00:03:31].

### Starting CPP at 65
This "default option" strikes a balance, providing steady payments and avoiding the need for savings to bridge an income gap [00:03:46]. It's ideal for retirees with below-average to average [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancy]] and moderate estimated investment returns [00:03:58].

*   **Predictable Income**: Offers reliable, inflation-adjusted income without requiring additional planning [00:04:05].
*   **Tradeoffs**: Leaves potential income on the table for those who live past age 80 [00:04:12].
*   **Break-Even Analysis**: Payments equalize with the age 70 option around age 80, making 65 a reasonable middle ground for those unsure about longevity or returns [00:04:18].
*   **Example Scenario**: Maria, age 65, retires with modest RSP savings and average health. She decides to take CPP now to maintain predictable income without depleting her savings [00:04:30].

### Delaying CPP to 70
This option provides the highest guaranteed lifetime income, increasing payments by the maximum amount, and is inflation-adjusted [00:04:46]. It's ideal for those with longer [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancies]] and sufficient savings to bridge the income gap [00:04:56].

*   **Longevity Protection**: Payments continue for life, ensuring secure income into advanced ages [00:05:03].
*   **High Effective Return**: Waiting until 70 offers an effective return equivalent to 6-8% annually, making it a strong choice compared to many other investment options [00:05:10].
*   **Break-Even Age**: For most, delaying CPP becomes advantageous after around age 80, when cumulative payments start to exceed earlier options [00:05:22].
*   **Example Scenario**: Susan, age 70, expects to live well into her late 80s or 90s. With sufficient RSP funds, she decides to delay CPP, maximizing her lifetime inflation-protected income [00:05:32].

## Estimating Your Life Expectancy

Your [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancy]] is a crucial factor in determining your optimal CPP start age [00:05:50].

*   **Gender Differences**: Women tend to live longer than men; 82% of women are likely to live past age 80, compared to 79% of men [00:06:23]. Delaying CPP is especially beneficial for women to secure inflation-protected income in later years [00:06:30].
*   **Healthy Lifestyle/Family History**: A healthy lifestyle or a family history of longevity increases your odds of living longer (85% for women, 79% for men to live past 80), making delayed CPP a smarter choice [00:06:35].
*   **General Trend**: Even with low longevity expectations, the majority of Canadians are expected to live past 80, suggesting most retirees would benefit from waiting [00:06:50].
*   **Stats Canada Data**: The older you are, the longer your life expectancy becomes. For instance, a 50-year-old man can expect to live until about 82, while a woman of the same age can expect to live nearly to 86 [00:07:14]. This data suggests [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancy]] is longer than commonly perceived [00:07:30].
    *   Women benefit more from delaying CPP due to their longer lifespans [00:07:44].
    *   Men should balance longevity with their specific needs and financial situation [00:07:58].
    *   Delaying CPP acts as longevity insurance, providing a safety net, especially for women [00:08:13].
    *   Strategic planning, integrating CPP timing with other [[retirement_planning_strategies_to_navigate_pension_changes | strategies]] like RSP withdrawals, is key to optimized [[strategies to supplement CPP and OAS income | retirement income]] [00:08:23].

## Using a CPP Present Value Calculator

While a CPP Break Even table is a good starting point, it falls short by not considering the investment rate of return or the present value of your money [00:10:53]. A CPP present value calculator incorporates these factors for a more accurate estimate [00:11:04].

For example, using an online calculator (like the one found on TaxTips.ca):
*   **Inputs**:
    *   Estimated monthly benefit at age 65 (e.g., $1,000) [00:11:35].
    *   Estimated annual indexation of CPP (inflation rate, e.g., 2.5%) [00:11:41].
    *   Desired start ages for evaluation (e.g., 60, 65, 70) [00:11:48].
    *   Life expectancy (e.g., 86 for a woman in her 50s) [00:12:00].
    *   Estimated rate of return on investments (e.g., 2% to 6%) [00:12:20].
*   **Interpreting Results (Present Value)**:
    *   With a 2% rate of return and living to age 86, the present value of choosing CPP at age 70 (approx. $320,000) is significantly higher than at age 65 (approx. $286,000) or age 60 (approx. $222,000) [00:12:42].
    *   Increasing the rate of return (e.g., to 4% or 6%) can significantly close the gap between the present values of different start ages, making age 65 or even age 60 more competitive if you can achieve high investment returns [00:13:08].
    *   **Impact of Longevity**: If you live to 100, starting at age 70 is overwhelmingly the best choice, providing over $130,000 more in present value compared to starting at 60 [00:14:01]. Conversely, if you pass away early (e.g., at 72), starting CPP at age 60 would have been more beneficial [00:14:31].
    *   **Inflation Rate**: A higher inflation rate (e.g., 4%) generally favors delaying CPP, while a lower rate (e.g., 1%) makes the present values for different start ages much closer [00:15:24].

It's important to remember that while the cumulative difference in payments over a lifetime can be large (e.g., $500,000), the *present value* difference, which accounts for the time value of money, is often much smaller (e.g., $130,000) [00:14:18].

*   **Scenario Analysis**: Running different scenarios for your [[life_expectancy_and_longevity_planning_for_cpp | life expectancy]] and break-even points helps determine when delaying CPP starts to pay off [00:16:30].

*   **OAS Clawback Mitigation**: Delaying CPP could help avoid the Old Age Security (OAS) clawback, or in some cases, starting earlier might be a better option [00:16:48].
*   **Income Coordination**: Integrating CPP with your RSP withdrawals, TFSA savings, and other [[strategies to supplement CPP and OAS income | retirement income]] sources creates the most efficient [[retirement_planning_strategies_for_canadians | retirement plan]] [00:17:00].
