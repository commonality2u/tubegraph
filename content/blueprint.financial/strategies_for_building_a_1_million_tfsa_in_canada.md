## Mistakes to Avoid

To reach a $1 million [[The TFSA as a strategic financial tool|TFSA]] goal, it's crucial to avoid two significant errors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>.

### Investing Only in a Savings Account
Relying solely on a savings account for your TFSA is a common mistake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>. For example, if you have $95,000 in your TFSA today and contribute $7,000 annually, earning a low 2% interest, it would take 56 years to reach $1 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>. By then, you would have contributed $487,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>. Starting this strategy at age 25 means you'd be 81 by the time you reach $1 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:01">[00:01:01]</a>.

Despite this, a recent survey indicates that 47% of Canadian TFSA holders only have cash and savings in their accounts, which means many may never reach a $1 million goal even if they maximize their TFSAs annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>.

### Taking on Excessive Risk
On the opposite end of the spectrum, some investors take on too much risk <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>. While a high-risk approach, like aggressively investing in volatile crypto, could theoretically reach $1 million in 3 to 4 years by doubling money annually, this is an extremely poor strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. The vast majority of people attempting such high-risk, high-reward approaches will not achieve similar returns, and the chances of losing all capital are extremely high, rendering the strategy worthless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>.

> [!quote] Warren Buffett's famous quote:
> "Rule number one: Never lose money. Rule number two: Never forget rule number one." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>
This quote emphasizes avoiding losses over spectacular gains in [[Investment strategies for TFSA growth|investments]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>.

## Realistic Strategies for Building a $1 Million TFSA

The key to reaching a $1 million [[The TFSA as a strategic financial tool|TFSA]] is finding a middle ground that balances risk and return in a sustainable manner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>. This involves understanding your risk tolerance and choosing appropriate [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investments]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>.

### Determine Your Risk Tolerance
You can assess your risk tolerance by completing a free online questionnaire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>.

*   It would take 25 years to reach $1 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>.
*   Total contributed: $270,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
*   Starting at age 40, you could reach this goal by retirement age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>.

### Example: 50% Equities / 50% Bonds (Medium Risk Tolerance)

Given historical bond returns of 2-3% per year, combining bonds with equities could yield an average return of 4.5% annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>.
*   Starting with $95,000 and contributing $7,000 annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>.

This medium-risk strategy is attainable for most people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>.

## Investment Options for Your TFSA

Here are five [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investment options]] for your TFSA:

### 1. Avoiding Mutual Funds at Big Banks
While convenient, mutual funds offered by big banks often come with very high fees, sometimes topping 3% annually, which significantly reduces [[investment_strategies_for_tfsa_growth|investment gains]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>.

### 2. Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs)
ETFs are an excellent choice for keeping costs down, as they mirror market index performance. Vanguard and iShares are popular Canadian providers known for their low fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:19">[00:06:19]</a>. However, ETFs expose you to market volatility and require basic trading knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>.

### 3. Individual Stocks
Investing in individual stocks offers ultimate control over your [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investments]] with the lowest fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>. A dividend stock strategy can provide capital appreciation potential and regular income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. This option requires significant market knowledge and time for portfolio management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>. Greater control also comes with increased risk, especially if your choices aren't diversified <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</a>.

### 4. Robo-Advisors
Robo-advisors are automated platforms that manage [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investments]] using algorithms, aligning with your financial goals and risk tolerance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>. Wellsimple and Questwealth are popular Canadian examples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>. They offer convenience and simplicity, ideal for new investors or those preferring a hands-off approach, and allow investing with relatively small amounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>.

However, robo-advisors lack the personal touch and deeper understanding of a human advisor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>. They operate based on algorithms, which might not capture unique opportunities or tailor strategies for changing financial circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>.

### 5. Portfolio Manager
For those overwhelmed by self-management or desiring a personal touch beyond algorithms, hiring a portfolio manager is an option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>. While traditionally for the wealthy, it's now accessible to many <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>. A professional manager can tailor your portfolio to specific needs and risk tolerance, optimizing success without daily hassle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>. This is an excellent option for individuals who lack the time or expertise to manage their own [[investment_strategies_for_tfsa_growth|investments]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>.

## Withdrawing Your $1 Million TFSA in Retirement

A crucial aspect of a $1 million [[The TFSA as a strategic financial tool|TFSA]] is how to manage withdrawals in retirement to [[maximizing_the_benefits_of_your_TFSA|maximize tax savings]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>.

### TFSA vs. RRSP Withdrawals
Deciding whether to withdraw from your TFSA or RRSP first is a common question <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>.
*   **RRSP withdrawals**: These are taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>. Drawing down your RRSP earlier in retirement can reduce your overall tax burden later, especially if you anticipate receiving Old Age Security (OAS), which can be subject to clawbacks based on income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:20">[00:09:20]</a>.
*   **TFSA withdrawals**: These are not taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>. This allows your [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investments]] to continue growing tax-free, providing a tax-efficient source of funds that does not affect your OAS <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>.

### Strategy for a $1 Million TFSA: The Bucket Strategy
With a substantial TFSA, it's wise to ensure the funds last and continue to grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:47">[00:09:47]</a>. Consider a "bucket strategy":
*   **Short-term funds**: Keep these in safer [[investment_options_for_reaching_tfsa_goals|investments]] like bonds or GICs to cover immediate expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:57">[00:09:57]</a>.
*   **Medium-term funds**: Place these in balanced funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.
*   **Long-term funds**: Maintain these in equities to ensure growth against inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

This strategy ensures funds are available when needed while still working for you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>.

### Managing TFSA with CPP
A strategic approach is also essential for coordinating your TFSA with your Canada Pension Plan (CPP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>. Since CPP provides regular income, your TFSA can supplement these amounts during years with lower other income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:22">[00:10:22]</a>.

Consider delaying CPP withdrawals while drawing from your TFSA if you retire early and need funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. This allows your government benefits to grow, [[maximizing_the_benefits_of_your_TFSA|maximizing your income]] later in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>.

Building a $1 million [[The TFSA as a strategic financial tool|TFSA]] requires a simple yet smart strategy and proper management of risk and return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:44">[00:10:44]</a>. The more challenging part can be figuring out the optimal tax strategy for your TFSA once it's built up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:54">[00:10:54]</a>.
