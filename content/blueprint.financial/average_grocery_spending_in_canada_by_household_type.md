## Average Grocery Costs by Household Type in 2025

According to the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, average annual grocery spending varies significantly based on household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>. These figures do not include restaurant or takeout meals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:50">[00:00:50]</a>.

*   **Single Adult:** Approximately $330 per month, or $4,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple:** Doubles to around $667 per month, or $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four (two adults, two kids):** Jumps to $1,403 per month, or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.

While costs appear to double as a household grows, the increase from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. This is attributed to factors like kids snacking, food waste, the need for variety, rapid growth, and parents prioritizing nutrition and convenience, all of which drive up the bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>.

These figures serve as a useful benchmark <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. For example, a single person spending $800 a month on food is likely spending high, while a family of four spending $800 might be spending too little <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. Comparing one's grocery bill to these averages can help in realistic budgeting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>.

## Food Inflation in Canada

Food costs have significantly worsened over recent years due to inflation:
*   **2022:** Saw a substantial 10.3% inflation rate, bringing the average cost for a family of four to $15,200, an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **2023:** Continued with a high 5.9% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>.
*   **2024:** Cooled down considerably to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>.
*   **2025 Forecast:** Projected inflation of 3% to 5%, leading to an estimated annual cost of $16,833 for a typical family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>.

Despite a slowdown since the 2022 peak, costs continue to accumulate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. A typical family is expected to spend up to $800 more in 2025 just to maintain the same standard of living, without buying more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>. This "inflation on autopilot" is caused by climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain issues, and tariffs from the U.S. <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>.

## [[grocery_spending_trends_by_age_group_in_canada | Average Grocery Cost by Age Group]]

Food spending patterns shift across different age groups in Canada:
*   **19-30 years old:** Spend approximately $3,861 per year, or $322 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. This group often lives with roommates, splits bills, eats cheaper foods like pasta, or skips meals to stretch their income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **30s to 50s:** Spend the most on groceries, peaking at about $4,152 a year, or $346 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. This increase is often due to having children, less time for cooking, and a preference for quality or convenience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>.
*   **50s and 60s:** Spending remains nearly the same as the peak years, at $4,110 a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. Habits tend to stick, and individuals might be supporting adult children or cooking for extended family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.
*   **Over 70:** A clear dip in spending to about $3,788 per year, or $316 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. This is typically due to smaller portions, fewer people to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.

## Cost of Living and Food Insecurity

Rising prices are making it difficult for nearly half of Canadians to cover basic expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. The 2025 Food Price Report indicates that 45% of Canadians struggle with day-to-day costs, with food being a major component <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a>. Alarmingly, 28% of Canadians report eating less food, not just switching brands, to make ends meet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>.

This struggle extends beyond groceries to rent, gas, utilities, and debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>. Many Canadians are no longer budgeting to get ahead, but simply to survive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>. Strategies include bulk buying, comparing flyers, and cutting impulse spending, but many still fall behind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. Expenses are outpacing even the smartest financial plans and income, as [[income_growth_versus_cost_of_living_in_canada | incomes are not rising by the same 3-5% as food prices]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>. Information on [[Average Income in Canada by Age]] can provide further context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:17]</a>.

Geographic location also affects food prices. Quebec is projected to see the smallest increase at 1.8%, while Newfoundland could experience the highest at 3.8%. Other provinces hover around 3% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:30">[00:06:30]</a>.

Food insecurity is a growing crisis in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>. In March 2024, there were over 2 million food bank visits, a 90% increase since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:16">[00:07:16]</a>. Nearly half of Gen Z have had to use savings or borrow money for food <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>. About 48% of Canadians actively seek discounts not for savings, but for survival <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:31">[00:07:31]</a>.

### Shifting Consumption Habits
Brand loyalty is fading as affordability becomes paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. Consumers are cooking more creatively, reducing waste, and choosing cheaper protein alternatives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. Beef prices, for example, rose by 9.2% in 2024 and continue to climb, partly due to Canada's smallest cattle herd since 1987 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>. Steak is increasingly becoming a luxury item, leading more Canadians to opt for chicken, pork, or lentils <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:50">[00:08:50]</a>.

### Consumer Action
In 2024, Canadians boycotted Loblaws in protest of rising prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. While the boycott did not significantly impact the company, it symbolized widespread frustration with powerful grocers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:14">[00:09:14]</a>. Shoppers are increasingly visiting smaller stores, comparing prices, and returning to farmers' markets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
