## How Medical Expense Claims Work in Canada

[[how_to_claim_medical_expenses_on_taxes_in_canada | Claiming medical expenses]] on a tax return can be intricate, but understanding the process is key <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:25">[00:00:25]</a>. The Medical Expense Tax Credit (METC), often referred to as the MEC, enables Canadians to [[eligible_medical_expenses_for_tax_deductions | claim eligible out-of-pocket medical expenses]] on their tax returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:34">[00:00:34]</a>.

To claim these expenses, they must be entered on specific lines of your tax return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:41">[00:00:41]</a>:
*   **Line 33099:** For expenses incurred for yourself, your spouse or common-law partner, or children under 18 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>.
*   **Line 33199:** For expenses for other dependents, such as adult children, parents, grandparents, or siblings who rely on you for support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>.

A crucial aspect is that you can only claim the portion of expenses not reimbursed by insurance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>. Additionally, you must subtract the lower of 3% of your net income or $2,759 (for 2024 expenses) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>. This deduction results in a non-refundable credit that reduces your taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Jessica's Medical Expense Claim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:20">[00:01:20]</a>
> Jessica earns $50,000 annually and had $2,500 in medical expenses in 2024 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>.
> *   3% of her net income: $50,000 * 0.03 = $1,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>
> *   Eligible claim: $2,500 (total expenses) - $1,500 (3% of income) = $1,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:32">[00:01:32]</a>
> Jessica can claim $1,000 as a non-refundable credit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>.

## Common Medical Expenses You Can Claim

Several types of medical expenses are [[eligible_medical_expenses_for_tax_deductions | eligible for tax deductions]]:

### Prescribed Drugs and Medications
Prescribed drugs and medications can be a significant expense, and some costs may be recouped at tax time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>.
*   **Eligibility:** Must be prescribed by a licensed medical professional and dispensed by a pharmacist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:04">[00:02:04]</a>.
*   **Included:** This includes [[eligible_medical_expenses_for_tax_deductions | eligible prescription drugs]] and, with conditions, medical marijuana (requiring a valid medical document and purchase from a licensed producer) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>.
*   **Excluded:** Over-the-counter medications, vitamins, and supplements typically do not qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Sarah's Medication Claim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>
> Sarah earns $60,000 annually and spent $3,000 on prescribed medications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>.
> *   3% of her income: $60,000 * 0.03 = $1,800 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:28">[00:02:28]</a>
> *   Eligible claim: $3,000 - $1,800 = $1,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>
> Sarah can claim $1,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Pro Tip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>
> Keep track of pharmacy receipts and prescriptions to [[maximizing_tax_deductions_and_credits | maximize your claim]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:43">[00:02:43]</a>.

### Dental and Vision Care
Many dental and vision expenses are tax deductible in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:50">[00:02:50]</a>.
*   **Eligibility:** Services provided by licensed professionals like dentists, orthodontists, optometrists, and ophthalmologists <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>.
*   **Included:** Routine checkups, fillings, root canals, gum surgery, braces, and prescription eyewear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>.
*   **Excluded:** Purely cosmetic procedures (e.g., teeth whitening) and non-prescription sunglasses do not qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Alex's Dental and Vision Claim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:15">[00:03:15]</a>
> Alex earns $150,000 annually and spent $3,100 on dental work ($2,500) and new prescription glasses ($600) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:17">[00:03:17]</a>.
> *   3% of his income: $150,000 * 0.03 = $4,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>
> *   Threshold for 2024: $2,759 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>
> *   Lesser of the two amounts: $2,759 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>
> *   Eligible claim: $3,100 (total expenses) - $2,759 (threshold) = $341 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>
> Alex can claim $341 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:56">[00:03:56]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Pro Tip for High Earners <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:58">[00:03:58]</a>
> High-income earners often require larger medical expenses to qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. Consider timing major procedures within the same 12-month period to [[maximizing_tax_deductions_and_credits | maximize deductions]] (e.g., laser eye surgery and a root canal in the same year) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:05">[00:04:05]</a>.

### Medical Travel Expenses
If you need to travel for medical care, you may be able to claim travel costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>.
*   **Eligibility:**
    *   Travel at least 40 km one way to access medical services not available near home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.
    *   Travel over 80 km allows claiming meals and accommodations in addition to transportation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41">[00:04:41]</a>.
*   **Included:** Public transportation (bus, train, taxi, flights), vehicle expenses (fuel, parking, maintenance), lodging (hotel, Airbnb), and meals for extended stays <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>.

> *   3% of her income: $55,000 * 0.03 = $1,650 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:22">[00:05:22]</a>
> *   Eligible claim: $2,300 (total expenses) - $1,650 (3% of income) = $650 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:27">[00:05:27]</a>
> Jennifer can claim $650 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:32">[00:05:32]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Pro Tip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:35">[00:05:35]</a>

### Private Health Insurance Premiums
Premiums paid out-of-pocket for private health insurance may be deductible from your taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.
*   **Included:** Plans covering medical, dental, vision, prescription drugs, and hospitalization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:55">[00:05:55]</a>.
*   **Excluded:** Employer-paid premiums do not qualify unless included as taxable income on your T4 slip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.
*   **Self-employed:** Premiums for self-employed individuals can be claimed if they cover [[eligible_medical_expenses_for_tax_deductions | eligible expenses]] and are not reimbursed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.
*   **Family Plans:** Family plans may allow claiming premiums for your spouse and children to [[maximizing_tax_deductions_and_credits | maximize deductions]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:15">[00:06:15]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Mark's Private Health Insurance Claim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:20">[00:06:20]</a>
> Mark earns $90,000 and pays $3,500 annually for private health insurance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:22">[00:06:22]</a>.
> *   3% of his income: $90,000 * 0.03 = $2,700 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27">[00:06:27]</a>
> *   Eligible claim: $3,500 - $2,700 = $800 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>
> Mark can deduct $800 from his taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:34">[00:06:34]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Pro Tip <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:37">[00:06:37]</a>

### Medical Devices and Equipment
Many medical devices and equipment, though costly, qualify as tax deductible medical expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>.
*   **Included:** Wheelchairs, hearing aids, insulin pumps, CPAP machines, prosthetics, orthopedic braces, and in some cases, home modifications like wheelchair ramps or stairlifts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>.
*   **Requirements:** Some devices require a doctor's prescription to qualify; keep records of both the prescription and receipt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>.
*   **Maintenance:** Ongoing maintenance costs (e.g., batteries for hearing aids, CPAP filters) may also be deductible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>.

> Emily can claim $550 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>. While this amount might seem small, combining it with other medical expenses could increase her total deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>.

## [[strategies_for_family_medical_expense_deductions | Maximizing Family Medical Expense Write-offs]]
For families, correctly claiming medical expenses can significantly impact your tax return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>. The CRA allows combining medical costs for your spouse and dependents, and knowing who should claim them can maximize savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Richard and Pauline's Family Claim <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>
> Richard and Pauline are a Canadian couple with two children: Jen (16) and Rob (19) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. Their medical expenses were:
> *   Richard: $2,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>
> *   Pauline: $2,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>
> *   Jen (16): $1,800 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:32">[00:08:32]</a>
> *   Rob (19): $1,300 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:34">[00:08:34]</a>
> *   Total: $7,600 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>

Since Jen is under 18, her expenses combine with Richard and Pauline's, making $6,300 eligible for line 33099 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>. Rob's expenses must be claimed separately on line 33199 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>.

**Calculation for Pauline:**
*   Pauline earns $55,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:52">[00:08:52]</a>.
*   3% of her income: $55,000 * 0.03 = $1,650 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:54">[00:08:54]</a>
*   Eligible claim: $6,300 - $1,650 = $4,650 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:59">[00:08:59]</a>

**Calculation for Richard:**
*   Richard earns $42,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:05">[00:09:05]</a>.
*   3% of his income: $42,000 * 0.03 = $1,260 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>
*   Eligible claim: $6,300 - $1,260 = $5,040 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>

Since Richard can get the higher claim, he should claim the full amount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>.
