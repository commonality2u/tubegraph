## What Are Options?

Options are financial contracts that grant the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying stock at a specified price in the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:00">[02:55:00]</a>. Because they don't involve purchasing the actual stock, options are generally cheaper than buying the stock itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:02:00">[03:02:00]</a>. However, they are extremely sensitive to price movements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>. A small price jump in the underlying stock can lead to a significant return on the option, but if the stock drops or fails to move quickly enough, the option can become worthless instantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:10:00">[03:10:00]</a>. This makes options trading akin to "investing on steroids," where small wins can become huge, but a single mistake can lead to a crash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:00">[03:19:00]</a>.

*   Starting with $10,000 in a TFSA in 2015 and buying call options on a volatile stock like Tesla, anticipating a jump <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:00">[01:37:00]</a>. If these options 10x, the $10,000 becomes $100,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:00">[02:01:00]</a>.
*   Reinvesting the $100,000 into Nvidia call options, betting on the rise of AI when the stock was under $50, which could turn $100,000 into $500,000 in a few months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:00">[02:10:00]</a>.
*   Making another successful bet on Shopify before the e-commerce boom during COVID, potentially growing the TFSA to $2.5 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:00">[02:22:00]</a>.
*   Two more "huge lucky bets," perhaps on GameStop or another Nvidia run, could then escalate the TFSA to over $35 million, all tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:00">[02:33:00]</a>. This demonstrates the immense power of timing and luck in options trading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:00">[02:48:00]</a>.

## Significant Risks

Despite the theoretical potential for massive gains, options trading in a TFSA carries substantial [[risks_and_challenges_of_unconventional_tfsa_investments | risks and challenges]]:

### CRA Scrutiny
The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) has rules against "business-like activity" within a TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:00">[03:26:00]</a>. If an account shows signs such as frequent trades, short holding periods, or high volume, the CRA can audit it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:00">[03:29:00]</a>. If the CRA determines that the account holder is running a business, all the "tax-free" gains become fully taxable income, potentially incurring a 50% tax bill plus penalties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:38:00">[03:38:00]</a>. The guidelines for what constitutes "too much" activity are deliberately vague <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:56:00">[03:56:00]</a>.

> [!warning] CRA Risk
> If your trades look too frequent or sophisticated, the CRA can classify your activity as business income, resulting in 100% taxation of your gains, plus penalties and interest <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:34:00">[10:34:00]</a>.

### Extreme Financial Risk
Options and other speculative plays can wipe out an entire TFSA balance in an instant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:43:00">[10:43:00]</a>. One wrong move can turn a $10,000 investment into zero <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:43:00">[04:43:00]</a>. In some cases, it could even lead to owing money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:49:00">[04:49:00]</a>. This strategy is described as the "financial equivalent of trying to jump across a canyon" â€“ incredible if successful, but with no second chance if missed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:51:00">[04:51:00]</a>. This highlights the importance of [[investment_risk_management_in_tfsa_accounts | investment risk management in TFSA accounts]] and [[balancing_risk_and_return_in_tfsa_investments | balancing risk and return]].

### Psychological Pressure
Beyond knowledge and skill, succeeding with options trading requires surviving "gut-wrenching volatility," such as watching a position drop by 60% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:24:00">[04:24:00]</a>. There is also the challenge of illiquid trades that cannot be exited cleanly during panic, and immense psychological pressure to cash out early <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:29:00">[04:29:00]</a>. The temptation to make "just one more trade" can lead to losing everything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:00">[04:37:00]</a>. Most people would likely capitulate long before reaching significant figures due to these emotional pressures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:54:00">[10:54:00]</a>.

## Feasibility

While technically possible with expert-level options knowledge, perfect market timing on multiple occasions, staying under the CRA's radar, and extraordinary luck <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:08:00">[04:08:00]</a>, options trading is not a viable strategy for most people to build [[optimal_uses_and_misuse_of_a_tfsa | TFSA wealth]]. It is considered the fastest way for most to "blow up their TFSA" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:40:00">[04:40:00]</a>. Trying to time the market with short-term options and meme stocks is described as so unlikely that it "borders on fantasy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:28:00">[10:28:00]</a>.
