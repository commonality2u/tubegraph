One lesser-known [[income_splitting_strategies_in_canada | income splitting strategy]] involves using the First Home Savings Account (FHSA) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>.

## FHSA for Income Splitting

The FHSA is a relatively new account, and its use for [[income_splitting_to_reduce_tax_burden | income splitting]] is not widely discussed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:43">[00:00:43]</a>. While commonly associated with saving for a home <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:49">[00:00:49]</a>, it can be strategically employed by couples to manage their tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>.

### How it Works

This strategy is particularly effective for couples where one partner has available [[fhsa_contributions_and_growth_potential | FHSA contribution room]] and the other has already maximized their tax-advantaged accounts, such as an RRSP or TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>.

The mechanism is as follows:
1.  The FHSA offers tax-deductible contributions, similar to an RRSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>.
2.  If one partner has maxed out their tax-advantaged accounts, they can gift money to their partner who has unused FHSA room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:12">[00:01:12]</a>.
3.  This prevents the gifted funds from being invested in a taxable non-registered account by the higher-income partner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>.
4.  The receiving partner can then contribute to their FHSA, gaining an immediate tax deduction and benefiting from tax-free investment growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>.

### Example: Ted and Robin

Consider Ted and Robin:
*   Ted has maxed out his registered accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.
*   Robin has not yet contributed to her FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>.
*   Ted gifts $8,000 to Robin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:32">[00:01:32]</a>.

**Without FHSA:** Ted would invest $8,000 in a taxable account, paying $132 annually in taxes on an assumed $400 growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>.

**With FHSA:** Robin contributes $8,000 to her FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>.
*   She gains an immediate tax deduction of $2,400 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.
*   She avoids taxes on the $400 in investment growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:55">[00:01:55]</a>.
*   This results in a total household savings of $2,532, plus all future investment growth remains tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:58">[00:01:58]</a>.

### Pros of this Strategy

*   **Double Benefit:** The gifting partner avoids taxable investment growth, while the receiving partner gains a tax deduction and tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:12">[00:02:12]</a>.
*   **Tax-Free Withdrawals:** If the FHSA is used for a home purchase, the withdrawals are tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:19">[00:02:19]</a>.
*   **Flexibility:** If the FHSA is not used for a home, the funds can be rolled over into an RRSP after 15 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a>. This highlights the [[tax_advantages_of_using_fhsa_and_rrsp | tax advantages of using FHSA and RRSP]] in combination.

### Cons and Considerations

*   **Eligibility:** The receiving partner must not have owned a home in the past four years to qualify for the FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:32">[00:02:32]</a>.
*   **Ownership Risk:** Gifted funds legally belong to the receiving partner. If the partnership ends, the gifting partner may have difficulty reclaiming the contribution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>.

## Combining Strategies for Maximum Benefit

The "magic" of [[income_splitting_strategies_in_canada | income splitting]] often occurs when multiple strategies are combined <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:27">[00:11:27]</a>. For instance, a couple could start by using an FHSA for one partner to claim a tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>, then incorporate [[pension_income_splitting | pension income splitting]] in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>, and further utilize spousal RRSP contributions for additional tax-deferred savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:46">[00:11:46]</a>. These strategies can work together to lower taxes, balance income streams, and help achieve financial goals like home saving or retirement planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:54">[00:11:54]</a>.
