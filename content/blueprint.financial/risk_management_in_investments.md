## Mistakes to Avoid

Two common mistakes can derail your investment goals:

### Investing Only in a Savings Account
Relying solely on a savings account with low interest rates, such as 2%, means it could take an incredibly long time to reach significant goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>. For example, starting with $95,000 and contributing $7,000 annually at a 2% interest rate, it would take 56 years to reach $1 million, with total contributions of $487,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:45">[00:00:45]</a>. This means someone starting at age 25 would be 81 by the time they reach their goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:03">[00:01:03]</a>. Sadly, a recent survey indicates that 47% of Canadian TFSA holders only have cash and savings in their accounts, making it unlikely for many to reach a million-dollar goal even if they max out their TFSAs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:11">[00:01:11]</a>.

### Taking on Excessive Risk
On the opposite end of the spectrum, taking on too much risk, such as betting on very [[risks_of_highrisk_investments_in_a_tfsa | high-risk crypto coins]], is also a terrible strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. While theoretical returns might seem amazing (e.g., doubling money annually could reach $1 million in 3 to 4 years from $95,000 with $120,000 invested) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>, the vast majority of people attempting such strategies will not achieve these returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. The chances of losing all your money (going to zero) are extremely high, rendering the strategy worthless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>. As Warren Buffett famously stated, Rule number one is "never lose money," and Rule number two is "never forget rule number one," emphasizing the importance of avoiding losses over spectacular gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>.

## Finding the Middle Ground

Think of investing like choosing a driving road <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>. A highway might get you there faster, but driving over the speed limit carries higher risks like accidents or tickets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:53">[00:02:53]</a>. A slower back road is safer but takes longer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:02">[00:03:02]</a>. Similarly, higher returns in investing often come with higher [[understanding_investment_risks_and_returns_in_a_tfsa | risks]], while safer investments offer steadier but lower returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:07">[00:03:07]</a>.

The key to success is finding a balanced approach <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>. This begins by determining your [[understanding_risk_tolerance_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]], which can be assessed through online questionnaires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:29">[00:03:29]</a>. Once you understand your risk comfort, you can select appropriate investments that will help you reach your goal in a reasonable timeframe with regular contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>.

### Examples of Sustainable Strategies

*   **High Risk Tolerance (100% Equities):** If you have a high [[understanding_risk_tolerance_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]], investing 100% in stocks could be suitable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. With an assumed average annual return of 7% (based on the Canadian stock market's historical 20-year return of 7.8%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:56">[00:03:56]</a>, starting with $95,000 and contributing $7,000 annually, it would take 25 years to reach $1 million, with total contributions of $270,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. This strategy allows someone starting at age 40 to reach their goal by retirement age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.

*   **Medium Risk Tolerance (50% Equities, 50% Bonds):** For those with a medium [[understanding_risk_tolerance_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]], a balanced portfolio of 50% equities and 50% bonds might be appropriate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:31">[00:04:31]</a>. Assuming an average annual return of 4.5% (considering bonds' historical 2-3% returns over 20 years) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:37">[00:04:37]</a>, starting with $95,000 and contributing $7,000 annually, it would take about 35 years to reach $1 million, with total contributions of $340,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>. This allows someone starting at age 30 to reach their goal by age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. This medium-risk strategy is attainable for many Canadians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:11">[00:05:11]</a>.

The timeline to reach your goal directly correlates with your chosen risk and return profile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>. The optimal strategy avoids being overly risky (to prevent losing money) while still providing sufficient returns to achieve your goal efficiently <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:24">[00:05:24]</a>.

## Investment Options and Associated Risks

### 1. Avoiding Mutual Funds from Big Banks
While convenient, mutual funds offered by big banks often come with very high fees, sometimes exceeding 3% per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>. These high fees can significantly erode your investment gains, making them generally not recommended <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>. This falls under [[risks_and_stress_linked_to_mutual_fund_investments | risks and stress linked to mutual fund investments]].

### 2. Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)
ETFs are a cost-effective choice, mirroring market index performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:25">[00:06:25]</a>. Providers like Vanguard and iShares offer ETFs with very low fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>. However, ETFs still expose you to market volatility, and some basic trading knowledge is needed as they are bought and sold on stock exchanges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>.

### 3. Individual Stocks
Investing in individual stocks offers ultimate control and the lowest fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>. A dividend stock strategy can provide both capital appreciation and regular income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. However, this approach demands significant market knowledge and time dedicated to managing your portfolio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>. Greater control often comes with increased risk, especially if your choices are not diversified <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</a>.

### 4. Robo-Advisors
Robo-advisors are automated platforms that manage investments using algorithms, aligning with your financial goals and [[understanding_risk_tolerance_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>. They offer convenience and simplicity, ideal for new investors or those preferring a hands-off approach, and allow investing with relatively small amounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>. A drawback is their lack of personal touch and deeper understanding compared to a human advisor, as they operate solely on algorithms and might not capture unique opportunities or tailor strategies for changing circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.

### 5. Portfolio Managers
For those who find managing investments overwhelming or prefer a personal touch over algorithms, hiring a portfolio manager is an excellent option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>. Traditionally for the wealthy, this option is now accessible to almost anyone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>. A professional manager can tailor your portfolio to specific needs and [[understanding_risk_tolerance_and_investment_choices | risk tolerance]], optimizing chances for success without daily hassle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:36">[00:08:36]</a>. This is particularly beneficial for those who lack the time or expertise to manage their own investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>.

## Risk Management in Retirement

Once you reach your TFSA goal, managing withdrawals effectively is key to maximizing tax savings in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>. This is a form of [[reviewing_and_optimizing_investments | reviewing and optimizing investments]] for the future.

### TFSA vs. RSP Withdrawals
A common question is whether to withdraw from your TFSA or RSP first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>.
*   **RSP withdrawals** are taxable income and can be beneficial earlier in retirement to reduce your overall tax burden, especially to avoid Old Age Security (OAS) clawbacks later, which are based on income levels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.
*   **TFSA withdrawals** are not taxable, providing a tax-efficient source of funds that does not affect your OAS eligibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>. This flexibility allows your investments to continue growing tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>.

### Bucket Strategy for a Million-Dollar TFSA
To ensure a substantial TFSA lasts and continues to grow, consider using a **bucket strategy** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>. This involves dividing your TFSA into portions for:
*   **Short-term needs:** Kept in safer investments like bonds or GICs to cover immediate expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:59">[00:09:59]</a>.
*   **Medium-term needs:** Placed in balanced funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:03">[00:10:03]</a>.
*   **Long-term needs:** Maintained in equities to ensure growth and combat inflation over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>.

This approach ensures funds are available when needed while still working for you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10">[00:10:10]</a>.

### Managing TFSA with CPP
A strategic approach is key to manage your TFSA in conjunction with your Canada Pension Plan (CPP) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>. Since CPP provides regular income, your TFSA can supplement these amounts during years with lower other income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:24">[00:10:24]</a>. Also, consider delaying CPP withdrawals while drawing from your TFSA if you retire early and need funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. This allows your government benefits to grow, maximizing your income later in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:38">[00:10:38]</a>. This is part of [[monitoring_and_managing_investment_performance | monitoring and managing investment performance]] for long-term financial health.

Achieving a million-dollar TFSA goal requires a smart strategy and proper risk-return management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:48">[00:10:48]</a>. The more challenging aspect often lies in optimizing tax strategies once the TFSA is built up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>.
