## The Impact of Account Prioritization

Choosing the correct investment accounts to prioritize can result in savings of tens or even hundreds of thousands of dollars over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:02">[02:00:02]</a>.

### TFSA vs. Taxable Account Example

Consider Jason, who invests $10,000 at age 30 into both a Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) and a non-registered taxable account, assuming a 7% annual return over 30 years and a 30% tax rate on the taxable account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:48">[02:14:48]</a>:
*   **TFSA:** The $10,000 grows tax-free to $76,122 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:33:43">[02:33:43]</a>.
*   **Taxable Account:** The $10,000 grows to only $42,001 due to ongoing taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:44:42">[02:44:42]</a>.
This demonstrates that the TFSA outperforms the non-registered account by $34,122, nearly doubling the value due to tax-free compounding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:41">[02:50:41]</a>.

## Ideal Order of Investment Accounts

To maximize savings and minimize taxes, individuals should prioritize contributions to specific registered accounts in a particular order <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:09:12">[03:09:12]</a>:

1.  **RSP with Employer Matching** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a>:
    *   Employer matching is essentially free money and should always be maximized first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:17:28">[03:17:28]</a>.
    *   For example, a 50% match on contributions up to $1,500 means an easy $750 added to savings automatically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:01">[03:25:01]</a>.

2.  **Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:36:04">[03:36:04]</a>:
    *   For saving for a child's education, the RESP offers a 20% government match up to $500 per child annually, with a lifetime maximum of $7,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:39:10">[03:39:10]</a>.
    *   Lower-income families may also receive the Canada Learning Bond, adding up to $2,000 without requiring additional contributions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:47:33">[03:47:33]</a>.

3.  **First Home Savings Account (FHSA)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:54">[03:55:54]</a>:
    *   First-time homebuyers should prioritize the FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:04">[03:59:04]</a>.
    *   It provides tax-deductible contributions and tax-free withdrawals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:02:18">[04:02:18]</a>.
    *   Funds can grow tax-free or be transferred to an RSP later, even if home ownership isn't immediate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:05:54">[04:05:54]</a>.

4.  **TFSA or Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RSP)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:11:39">[04:11:39]</a>:
    *   **TFSA:** Best for flexibility, emergencies, or lower-income years, allowing tax-free withdrawals at any time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:16:32">[04:16:32]</a>. If in doubt, contributing to a TFSA is generally a safe choice due to its versatility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:25:04">[04:25:04]</a>.
    *   **RSP:** Ideal for higher incomes due to tax-deductible contributions and tax-deferred growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:25">[04:37:25]</a>.
    *   Both the RSP and FHSA come with some withdrawal restrictions that require careful consideration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:43:08">[04:43:08]</a>.

The final recommended order of priority is: RSP with employer matching, RESP, FHSA, and then TFSA or RSP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:53:15">[04:53:15]</a>. More detailed information on this priority order is available in other resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:00:54">[05:00:54]</a>.

## Optimizing Asset Location for Registered Accounts

[[optimizing_tfsa_rrsp_and_fhsa_accounts_for_tax_benefits | Tax efficiency]] is not just about what you invest in, but where those investments are held <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:13:30">[05:13:30]</a>. Different types of income (interest, dividends, capital gains) are taxed differently in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:17:34">[05:17:34]</a>.

### Tax Rules Overview
*   **Interest Income:** Taxed at your full marginal tax rate, making it the least tax-efficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:25:12">[05:25:12]</a>.
*   **Canadian Dividends:** Benefit from the dividend tax credit, reducing the effective tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:33:43">[05:33:43]</a>.
*   **Foreign Dividends and Interest:** Subject to withholding taxes that generally cannot be recovered in a TFSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:42:07">[05:42:07]</a>.
*   **Capital Gains:** Only 50% of gains are taxable up to $250,000, and two-thirds after that, making them more tax-efficient than interest or dividends in most cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:49:10">[05:49:10]</a>.

### Key Strategies for Asset Location
*   **RSP for US Dividend-Paying Stocks:** Holding US dividend-paying stocks in an RSP avoids the 15% withholding tax on US dividends due to the Canada-US tax treaty, ensuring the full dividend amount is received <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:04:02">[06:04:02]</a>.
*   **TFSA for High-Growth Investments:** Use a TFSA to shelter capital gains and allow high-growth investments to compound tax-free <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:21:05">[06:21:05]</a>.
    *   Avoid foreign dividend stocks in a TFSA if possible, as withholding taxes apply and cannot be recovered, leading to a loss of up to 15% of dividends annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:28:44">[06:28:44]</a>.
*   **Taxable Accounts for Canadian Dividend Stocks:** Once registered accounts are maxed out, Canadian dividend-paying stocks can be held in taxable non-registered accounts due to the dividend tax credit, which significantly reduces taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:51:00">[06:51:00]</a>. This makes them more tax-efficient than other income types in a taxable account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:08:04">[07:08:04]</a>.
*   **Registered Accounts for Interest-Generating Investments:** Place investments that generate income or interest, such as GICs or bonds, in RSPs or TFSAs to avoid high taxes on interest income in taxable accounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:34:49">[07:34:49]</a>.

## Income Splitting Strategies Utilizing Registered Accounts

[[family_tax_planning | Income splitting]] can reduce overall household tax burdens by distributing income among family members in lower tax brackets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:51:09">[07:51:09]</a>.

*   **Using FHSA for Income Splitting:**
    *   Primarily for couples where one partner has maxed out their tax-advantaged accounts (RSP, TFSA, FHSA) and the other has unused FHSA room <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:54:19">[07:54:19]</a>.
    *   The higher-income partner gifts money to the lower-income partner, who contributes it to their FHSA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:11:00">[08:11:00]</a>.
    *   This avoids investing in a taxable account, allows for a tax deduction, and enables tax-free growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:18:21">[08:18:21]</a>.

*   **Spousal RSP Contributions:**
    *   Spousal RSPs help balance retirement incomes and reduce taxes for couples <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:42:01">[08:42:01]</a>.
    *   The higher-income spouse contributes to the lower-income spouse's RSP, receiving an immediate tax deduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:46:17">[08:46:17]</a>.
    *   In retirement, the lower-income spouse withdraws funds at their lower tax rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:53:57">[08:53:57]</a>.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even the best [[planning_and_implementing_taxsaving_strategies | tax strategies]] can backfire if simple mistakes are made <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:49:03">[10:49:03]</a>.
*   **Over-contributing to Registered Accounts:** Exceeding contribution limits for TFSAs or RSPs can result in hefty penalties (1% of the excess amount each month) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:58:39">[10:58:39]</a>. Always track limits to avoid unnecessary costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:07:07">[11:07:07]</a>.
*   **Forgetting About Deadlines:** Some strategies, like RSP contributions, have strict deadlines (e.g., 60 days after year-end), and missing them can mean losing out on tax deductions for the year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:12:44">[11:12:44]</a>.
*   **Not Planning for Withdrawals:** Withdrawals from registered accounts like RSPs can trigger unexpected taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:25:06">[11:25:06]</a>. It's crucial to have a plan to manage these withdrawals tax-efficiently, especially in retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:29:05">[11:29:05]</a>.

These [[prioritizing_contributions_to_taxadvantaged_accounts | strategies for prioritizing contributions to tax-advantaged accounts]] can save thousands in taxes and significantly boost wealth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:38:15">[11:38:15]</a>.
