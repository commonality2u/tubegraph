Canadians have increasingly felt the impact of rising costs at the checkout counter, with grocery bills often $50 higher for the same items <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This phenomenon is not imagination, and understanding average spending can help in personal budgeting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a>. This article breaks down what Canadians spend on groceries in 2025, according to the 2025 Canada Food Price Report, excluding restaurant or takeout expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:42">[00:00:42]</a>.

## Average Spending by Household Type

The actual cost of groceries in Canada significantly depends on factors like age and household size <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>.

*   **Single Adult**
    A single adult is estimated to spend approximately $330 per month, or about $4,000 per year, on groceries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:52">[00:00:52]</a>.
*   **Couple**
    For a couple, the cost roughly doubles to $667 per month, totaling $8,000 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>.
*   **Family of Four**
    A family of four, consisting of two adults and two children, faces a much higher grocery bill, estimated at $1,403 per month or $16,833 per year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>.

While it might seem that costs merely double as a household grows, the jump from a couple to a family of four is more than double <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>. This increase is not solely due to inflation but reflects the different dynamics of feeding a family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:30">[00:01:30]</a>. Children tend to snack more, waste food, require variety, and grow rapidly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>. Grocery runs become larger and more frequent, and parents often prioritize nutrition and convenience, which further drives up the bill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:44">[00:01:44]</a>. This aligns with the [[cost_of_raising_a_child_in_canada | cost of raising a child in Canada]] being a significant factor in household expenses.

## Using the Data as a Benchmark

These average figures serve as a useful benchmark for personal budgeting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>. For instance, a single person spending $800 a month on food is likely on the high side and might consider adjustments if it significantly impacts their budget <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. Conversely, $800 a month would be too little for a family of four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:01">[00:02:01]</a>. Comparing one's grocery bill to others in a similar stage of life can help determine if there is overspending, where the money is going, and how to budget realistically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>.

## Impact of Inflation on Grocery Costs

Food costs have significantly worsened due to inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. For a typical Canadian family of four, annual food costs have seen substantial increases:
*   **2022:** 10.3% inflation, bringing the average cost to $15,200 (an increase of over $1,000 from the previous year) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:27">[00:02:27]</a>.
*   **2023:** 5.9% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.
*   **2024:** Cooled down to 2.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:41">[00:02:41]</a>.
*   **2025 (Projected):** An estimated 3% to 5% inflation forecast, leading to a projected annual cost of $16,833 for a family of four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:52">[00:02:52]</a>. This represents an increase of up to $800 in 2025 compared to 2024, merely to maintain the same standard of living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:09">[00:03:09]</a>. These [[changes_in_grocery_bill_for_canadians_in_2025 | changes in grocery bill for Canadians in 2025]] are significant, draining budgets through "inflation on autopilot" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:18">[00:03:18]</a>.

Behind these numbers are challenges such as climate events, volatile commodity prices, supply chain issues, and tariffs from the US <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:23">[00:03:23]</a>. Food inflation, though seemingly a small percentage on paper, has a huge [[impact_of_grocery_budgeting_on_canadians | impact of grocery budgeting on Canadians]], affecting choices, quality of life, and even health <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>.

## Average Grocery Cost by Age Group

Food spending also varies across different age groups, reflecting changing life stages and priorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>.
*   **Canadians in their 30s to 50s:** Spend the most on groceries, averaging about $4,152 a year or $346 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>.
*   **Ages 19 to 30:** Spend less, approximately $3,861 per year or $322 a month, about $300 less annually than the peak years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>. This group might live with roommates, split bills, and prioritize cheaper options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:23">[00:04:23]</a>.
*   **Ages 50s and 60s:** Spending remains nearly the same as the peak years, at $4,110 a year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:47">[00:04:47]</a>. Habits tend to stick, and individuals might still be supporting adult children or cooking for extended family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.
*   **Over 70:** A clear dip in spending is observed, to about $3,788 per year or $316 a month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:01">[00:05:01]</a>. This is likely due to smaller portions, fewer mouths to feed, and simpler routines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.

## Broader Impact on Canadians

The rising cost of living, particularly food, is a significant challenge for many Canadians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>.
*   **Struggling with Expenses:** Nearly half (45%) of Canadians report struggling with day-to-day costs, with food being a major component <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:20">[00:05:20]</a>.
*   **Eating Less:** Alarmingly, 28% of Canadians are eating less food, not just switching brands, a serious concern in a wealthy country like Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:34">[00:05:34]</a>. This points to growing [[food_insecurity_and_shopping_habits_in_canada | food insecurity and shopping habits in Canada]].
*   **Budgeting for Survival:** Many are no longer budgeting to get ahead but merely to survive, resorting to bulk buying, comparing flyers, and cutting impulse spending <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>. Expenses are outpacing even the smartest financial plans and income growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>, as incomes are generally not rising at the same rate as food prices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>.
*   **Geographic Variation:** Food price increases also vary by province. Quebec is projected to see the smallest increase at 1.8%, while Newfoundland could see up to 3.8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:35">[00:06:35]</a>.

The **Food Insecurity** crisis is growing, evidenced by over 2 million food bank visits in March 2024, a 90% jump since 2019 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>. Many, particularly nearly half of Gen Z, have had to dip into savings or borrow money just to eat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>. This highlights a shift in shopping habits, where brand loyalty fades, and affordability is emphasized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. Many are cooking more creatively or reducing waste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.
