## Prescribed Rate Loan (Income Splitting)

The prescribed rate loan, one of the last legal income splitting loopholes in Canada, allows a high-income earner to lend money to a lower-income spouse to invest, thereby shifting investment income to a lower tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:00">[00:21:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:00">[00:54:00]</a>. However, it carries several risks:

*   **Investment Risk** If the invested money underperforms or loses value, the tax savings may not offset the losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:14">[03:14:14]</a>. The low-income spouse is still obligated to pay the prescribed interest on the loan to the high-income spouse, even if the investments yield little or no profit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:25:01">[03:25:01]</a>.
*   **Changing Prescribed Rate** The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) can change the prescribed interest rate (e.g., it was 1% in 2020 and is now 4%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:44:07">[03:44:07]</a>. If the rate increases, the strategy becomes less effective as the loan money needs to generate a higher return to be worthwhile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
*   **Strict Payment Rules** The low-income spouse *must* pay interest on the loan annually by January 30th <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:58">[03:57:58]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:01:40">[04:01:40]</a>. Missing even a single payment causes the entire strategy to fall apart, and all investment income will be taxed back to the high-income spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:03:00">[04:03:00]</a>.
*   **Optimal Environment** This strategy is most effective in a low-interest-rate environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>.

## Capital Dividend Account (CDA)

The Capital Dividend Account (CDA) allows business owners to extract the non-taxable portion of capital gains from a corporation as a tax-free dividend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:58:00">[04:58:00]</a>. This is a powerful mechanism, but it requires careful management:

*   **Proper Tracking and Reporting** The CDA balance must be accurately recorded and reported to the CRA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:47:00">[06:47:00]</a>. If done incorrectly, withdrawals from the CDA could be taxed as regular dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:52:00">[06:52:00]</a>.
*   **Limited Applicability** The CDA applies *only* to specific types of income, such as the non-taxable portion of capital gains, life insurance proceeds, and certain capital transactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:57:00">[06:57:00]</a>. Regular business income, for example, does not qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:05:00">[07:05:00]</a>.
*   **Expert Tax Planning Required** Utilizing the CDA effectively, especially when selling a business or a major asset, necessitates careful [[planning_taxsaving_strategies | corporate tax planning]] and often the assistance of a tax professional to maximize tax-free withdrawals and ensure correct setup <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:09:00">[07:09:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:15:00">[07:15:00]</a>.

## Converting Your Mortgage into a Tax-Deductible Loan

This strategy, often referred to as the Smith Manoeuvre, involves reborrowing against home equity and investing the funds in income-generating assets to make mortgage interest tax deductible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:38:00">[07:38:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:42:00">[07:42:00]</a>. While powerful, it comes with significant risks:

*   **Interest Rate Risk** Home Equity Lines of Credit (HELOCs) typically have variable interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:23:00">[09:23:00]</a>. If interest rates rise, borrowing costs increase, making the strategy less attractive or even detrimental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:28:00">[09:28:00]</a>.
*   **Market Volatility** If the investments purchased with the HELOC funds underperform or lose money, the borrower is still responsible for paying interest on the HELOC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:34:00">[09:34:00]</a>. This can put an individual in a dangerous financial position if the portfolio drops significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:49:00">[09:49:00]</a>.
*   **Discipline Required** Strict discipline is essential. The HELOC money must be consistently reinvested into income-generating assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:57:00">[09:57:00]</a>. Using the funds for personal expenses rather than investments will result in the loss of the tax deduction and could lead to serious financial and tax issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:02:00">[10:02:00]</a>.
*   **CRA Scrutiny** The CRA mandates that borrowed money must be used for income-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:11:00">[10:11:00]</a>. If the investments do not generate income (e.g., a growth stock with no dividends), the tax deduction for the HELOC interest could be denied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:16:00">[10:16:00]</a>.
*   **High Risk** This strategy is not suitable for everyone and requires comfort with leverage, investment risk, and strict financial discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:34:00">[10:34:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:38:00">[10:38:00]</a>.
