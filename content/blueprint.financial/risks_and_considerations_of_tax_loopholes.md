## Prescribed Rate Loan for Income Splitting

The prescribed rate loan is one of the last legal income splitting loopholes in Canada designed to shift investment income to a lower tax bracket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:56">[00:00:56]</a>. However, it comes with several risks and strict conditions:

*   **Investment Risk**: If the investments underperform or lose money, the tax savings may not offset the losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>. The lower-income spouse still owes interest on the loan annually, potentially requiring them to use savings or sell assets at a loss to make payments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:28">[00:03:28]</a>. The higher-income spouse still reports the received interest as taxable income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:39">[00:03:39]</a>.
*   **Prescribed Rate Changes**: The CRA's prescribed rate can change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:44">[00:03:44]</a>. If the rate increases, the strategy becomes less effective as the loan money needs to earn more than that rate to be worthwhile <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>.
*   **Strict Payment Rules**: Interest on the loan must be paid annually by January 30th <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:25">[00:02:25]</a>. Missing even one payment cancels the entire strategy, and all investment income is taxed back to the higher-income spouse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>.
*   **Optimal Environment**: This strategy works best in a low interest rate environment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:09">[00:04:09]</a>.

## Capital Dividend Account (CDA) for Corporate Tax-Free Withdrawals

The Capital Dividend Account (CDA) allows business owners to withdraw the non-taxable portion of capital gains as a tax-free dividend from their corporation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:58">[00:04:58]</a>. Key considerations include:

*   **Proper Tracking and Reporting**: The CDA balance must be accurately recorded and reported to the CRA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:47">[00:06:47]</a>. Incorrect reporting can lead to withdrawals being taxed as regular dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>.
*   **Applicability to Specific Income**: The CDA only applies to the non-taxable portion of capital gains, life insurance proceeds, and certain capital transactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>. Regular business income does not qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:05">[00:07:05]</a>. There was a proposed two-thirds inclusion rate for capital gains, but it is unlikely to be implemented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:41">[00:05:41]</a>.
*   **Requirement for [[strategies_for_investors_and_business_owners_amidst_tax_changes | Careful Corporate Tax Planning]]**: Selling a business or asset inside a corporation requires careful [[strategies_for_investors_and_business_owners_amidst_tax_changes | corporate tax planning]] with a [[importance_of_professional_tax_planning | tax professional]] to maximize tax-free withdrawals and correctly set up the CDA <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:09">[00:07:09]</a>.

## Turning Your Mortgage into a Tax-Deductible Loan

This strategy involves re-borrowing against home equity through a Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) and investing in income-generating assets, allowing homeowners to deduct the interest on the HELOC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>. This powerful, yet unintended, loophole carries significant risks:

*   **Interest Rate Risk**: HELOCs have variable interest rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:23">[00:09:23]</a>. If rates rise, borrowing costs increase, making the strategy less attractive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>.
*   **Market Volatility**: If investments underperform or lose money, the borrower still has to pay interest on the HELOC <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>. A drop in the investment portfolio means the borrower is still liable for loan payments, potentially leading to a dangerous financial position <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:49">[00:09:49]</a>.
*   **Strict Discipline Required**: To maintain the tax deduction, the HELOC money must be consistently reinvested in income-generating assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:57:00">[09:57:00]</a>. Using the funds for personal expenses will result in losing the tax deduction and could lead to serious financial and tax issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:02">[00:10:02]</a>.
*   **CRA Scrutiny**: The CRA requires borrowed money to be used for income-generating investments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:11">[00:10:11]</a>. If investments, like growth stocks with no dividends, do not generate income, the tax deduction could be denied <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:18">[00:10:18]</a>.
*   **Not for Everyone**: This is not a strategy for everyone due to the high comfort level required with leverage, investment risk, and strict discipline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:34">[00:10:34]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:42">[00:10:42]</a>.

## General Cautions

*   **Evolving Tax Laws**: Tax laws can change over time, rendering current strategies ineffective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:46">[00:10:46]</a>. It is crucial to stay updated on the latest CRA rules before making any significant financial moves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:53">[00:10:53]</a>.
*   **Importance of Professional Guidance**: It is highly advisable to consult with a [[importance_of_professional_tax_planning | tax professional]] or financial advisor for guidance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>. They can help navigate complex tax rules, ensure compliance, and maximize tax savings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:59">[00:10:59]</a>.
