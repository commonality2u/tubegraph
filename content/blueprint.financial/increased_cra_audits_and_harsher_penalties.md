## Targeting Tax Fraud with New Technologies

The CRA is enhancing its capabilities by using AI and machine learning to track down tax fraud and evasion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. These advanced tools enable the CRA to analyze vast amounts of data, quickly identifying suspicious patterns and inconsistencies in tax filings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>. An AI system is likely to flag a return if it does not add up, even before a human auditor reviews it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>.

A significant shift is the adoption of AI-driven audit selection. The CRA now employs advanced analytics to flag tax returns that contain unusual deductions, excessive claims, or inconsistent income reporting, rather than relying solely on random audits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:57">[00:00:57]</a>. Automated fraud detection utilizes machine learning models to recognize patterns from past fraudulent filings and apply this knowledge to detect new cases in real-time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>.

The CRA is also increasing its efforts against GST and HST return fraud. Businesses or individuals seeking unwarranted tax refunds, rebates, or input tax credits are now automatically cross-checked against financial and corporate records <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>. Discrepancies in numbers can lead to an audit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>. Furthermore, new data sharing agreements with banks and foreign financial institutions allow the CRA to compare tax filings with banking data and offshore disclosures, making it more challenging to conceal unreported income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>.

## Proposed CRA's Expanded Audit Powers

The CRA is poised to acquire [[new_cra_audit_powers_and_their_implications | sweeping new audit powers]], making tax enforcement more aggressive and potentially more invasive for taxpayers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>. These changes, introduced in Budget 2024, aim to address unpaid taxes but also raise concerns regarding fairness, due process, and the increasing burden on businesses and individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. If enacted, routine audits could involve court orders, mandatory sworn statements, and financial penalties even before the audit concludes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:12">[00:03:12]</a>.

The government cites a tax gap of over $20 billion from unpaid taxes as a reason for needing stronger enforcement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:27">[00:03:27]</a>. Budget 2024 proposes specific measures to tighten compliance:

*   **Fines for Non-Compliance:** Failure to fully cooperate with an audit request can result in a Notice of Non-Compliance (NNC), leading to a fine of $50 per day, up to $25,000, until the issue is resolved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>. The CRA can also pause your reassessment period, extending the duration of the audit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:48">[00:03:48]</a>.
*   **Questioning Under Oath:** The CRA will have the authority to compel taxpayers to provide oral statements under oath or affirmation, a significant departure from current procedures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>. Unlike court testimony, this process may not include standard legal protections or formal transcripts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:02">[00:04:02]</a>.
*   **Severe Compliance Penalties:** Non-compliance with a court-issued order can result in a 10% penalty on your total tax bill for the years in question <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a>. Even withholding a single document can trigger this fine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:19">[00:04:19]</a>.
*   **Extended Audit Deadlines:** Challenging an NNC or fighting a CRA demand in court will indefinitely extend your reassessment period until the case is resolved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.

These expanded powers suggest that audits could become more aggressive, time-consuming, and costly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>. Even routine audits could evolve into legal and financial burdens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>.

## Increased Audit Activities in Real Estate and Cash-Based Businesses

The CRA is intensifying its audit activities, particularly targeting [[real_estate_transactions_and_cashbased_business_audits | real estate and cash-based businesses]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:02">[00:05:02]</a>. These industries have historically been scrutinized due to concerns about unreported income, undeclared capital gains, and tax evasion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:12">[00:05:12]</a>. With improved data tracking and increased funding for enforcement, the CRA is scrutinizing these sectors more than ever <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>.

### Who's at Risk?

*   **Real Estate Investors, House Flippers, and Landlords:** These groups face increased scrutiny, especially those misusing the principal residence exemption to avoid capital gains taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:28">[00:05:28]</a>. Selling multiple properties in recent years may lead the CRA to question whether you are a genuine homeowner or merely flipping houses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:38">[00:05:38]</a>. Landlords, particularly those using platforms like Airbnb or VRBO, are also at risk if they fail to report rental income correctly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>.
*   **Cash-Based Businesses:** Restaurants, bars, construction companies, and auto repair shops are prime targets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:54">[00:05:54]</a>. The CRA recognizes that businesses primarily dealing in cash have a higher risk of underreporting sales or inflating expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>. Inconsistent books may trigger an audit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>.

Real estate transactions are receiving extra attention, focusing on undeclared capital gains and rental income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. The Underused Housing Tax (UHT) is also a key area, as the CRA identifies homeowners with vacant or underused properties who have not properly reported or paid what they owe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:17">[00:06:17]</a>. For business owners, the CRA is taking a harder stance on cash transactions; reported earnings that appear suspiciously low compared to industry standards may result in an audit notice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:32">[00:06:32]</a>.

## Crackdown on Aggressive Tax Planning and Evasion

The CRA is adopting a much tougher stance on aggressive tax planning and tax evasion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>. Individuals and businesses utilizing complex tax strategies, especially those involving offshore accounts or sophisticated corporate structures, can expect increased scrutiny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:52">[00:06:52]</a>. The CRA is targeting those who attempt to sidestep tax obligations using new data sharing agreements and enforcement tools <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>.

One significant focus is offshore tax evasion. Canada collaborates with over 100 countries through the Common Reporting Standard, enabling financial institutions worldwide to share data with tax authorities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:09">[00:07:09]</a>. This makes it more likely for the CRA to discover undeclared foreign assets or income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</a>.

Another key area is corporate tax planning. Businesses employing strategies like income shifting, artificial losses, or questionable tax credits are facing tougher audits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>. The CRA is also investigating tax shelters and arrangements designed to conceal income through trusts, numbered companies, and shell corporations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:34">[00:07:34]</a>. These methods, often aimed at creating paper losses or manipulating taxable revenue, are under intense scrutiny <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:41">[00:07:41]</a>.

For those caught, the penalties are severe. The CRA has increased the number of tax evasion cases referred for prosecution, putting individuals and businesses using aggressive tax strategies at risk of not just hefty fines and interest charges, but also [[tax_fraud_consequences_in_Canada | criminal investigations and even jail time]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:50">[00:07:50]</a>.

### Who's at Risk?

*   **High Net Worth Individuals:** Those using offshore tax strategies are a major target; foreign accounts or assets not properly disclosed can lead to an audit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>.
*   **Businesses in Complex Tax Planning Schemes:** Especially those that artificially shift profits or exploit tax loopholes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>. The CRA closely examines transactions that appear to exist solely for tax benefits rather than genuine business purposes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>.

If a tax strategy seems too good to be true, it is likely on the CRA's radar <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:37">[00:08:37]</a>.
