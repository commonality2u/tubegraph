Canadian business owners and investors face significant tax updates in 2025, which are reshaping financial planning and investment strategies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. These changes aim to minimize taxes, protect cash flow, and allow more capital for business investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:07">[00:00:07]</a>.

## Key Tax Changes and Strategies for Response

### Capital Gains Tax for Corporations

Starting in 2025, Canada will fully implement a new capital gains tax structure for corporations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>. The inclusion rate for capital gains will increase from 50% to 66.67% (or 2/3) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:28">[00:00:28]</a>. This means a larger portion of a corporation's capital gains will be subject to taxation, affecting after-tax profits, investment decisions, and financial planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:36">[00:00:36]</a>.

For example, if ABC Corp. sells an investment property with a $200,000 capital gain in 2025, approximately $133,000 would be included in taxable income under the new rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:24">[00:01:24]</a>. At a 10% corporate tax rate, the tax owed would increase to $13,334, resulting in an extra tax liability of $3,334 compared to the old rules <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>.

This change particularly impacts businesses that rely on capital gains from real estate, securities, or other assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>. It's important to note that these changes do not affect sole proprietors and self-employed individuals, who will retain the 50% inclusion rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:55">[00:01:55]</a>.

#### [[strategies_for_investors_and_business_owners_in_response_to_tax_changes | Strategies for managing increased tax liabilities]] under new Capital Gains Rules:

*   **Reassessing Investment Portfolios** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>: Businesses investing in high-growth assets or stocks that generate significant capital gains may pay more tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:13">[00:02:13]</a>. A simpler option might be to switch to Canadian stocks that pay dividends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>. Dividends often come with tax perks, such as the ability to recover some tax through the Refundable Dividend Tax On Hand (RDTOH) mechanism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:46">[00:02:46]</a>. Careful planning and analysis are crucial before implementing this strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:51">[00:02:51]</a>.
*   **Contributing to an Individual Pension Plan (IPP)** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>: IPPs offer a tax-efficient retirement planning tool for business owners <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. Contributions are deductible from corporate income, which can help offset taxable gains, reduce immediate tax liabilities, and build a retirement fund <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:08">[00:03:08]</a>. An IPP is similar to an RRSP for a corporation but offers higher limits and flexibility for succession planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>.

### CPP 2025 Enhancement and Contribution Updates

Significant changes to Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions will impact business owners' payroll costs and cash flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>. The updates maintain two levels of contributions: regular CPP (up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings or YMPE) and CPP2 (up to the Year's Additional Maximum Pensionable Earnings or YAMPE) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>.

For 2025:
*   The YMPE has increased to $71,500 (up from $69,700 in 2024) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.
*   The YAMPE has risen to $81,300 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>.

Contribution rates in 2025:
*   **Regular CPP**: 5.95% on income up to $71,500 for employees, matched by employers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:26">[00:04:26]</a>. Self-employed individuals pay both portions, totaling 11.9% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:38">[00:04:38]</a>.
*   **CPP2**: 4% for employees on income between $71,400 and $81,300, also matched by employers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:43">[00:04:43]</a>. Self-employed individuals pay 8% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.

A self-employed person earning over $81,300 in 2025 will contribute a total of $8,888 to their CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07">[00:05:07]</a>. While these changes aim to improve retirement security by gradually increasing the income replacement rate from 25% to 33.33% over 40 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:19">[00:05:19]</a>, the higher contribution rates mean extra costs for employers and self-employed individuals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>.

*   **Adjusting Budgets** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>: Small businesses may need to account for higher payroll costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47">[00:05:47]</a>.
*   **Exploring Tax-Efficient Compensation** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>: Investigate smarter ways to compensate employees to reduce financial strain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:49">[00:05:49]</a>.

### Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)

For some good news, the LCGE limit has increased to $1.25 million in 2025 (up from approximately $1 million in 2024) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:09">[00:06:09]</a>. This allows individuals to shield a portion of capital gains from taxation when selling qualified small business corporation shares <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:18">[00:06:18]</a>. Each individual shareholder can claim this exemption, which can significantly reduce the overall tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:39">[00:06:39]</a>.

### Canadian Entrepreneurs Incentive (CEI)

The CEI provides tax relief by reducing the capital gains inclusion rate to 1/3 (33.33%) on a lifetime maximum of $2 million in eligible capital gains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>. This incentive is being phased in gradually, with the limit increasing by $400,000 annually, reaching the full $2 million cap by 2029 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:13">[00:07:13]</a>.

While the CEI reduces the inclusion rate, it does not address investment income taxation, which remains subject to regular corporate tax rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:36">[00:07:36]</a>. For many business owners, the LCGE often remains a more effective tool as it offers a complete exemption for eligible capital gains, especially when multiple shareholders are involved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>.

#### [[planning_taxsaving_strategies | Strategies for managing increased tax liabilities]] by combining CEI and LCGE:

*   **Strategic Allocation of Gains** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>: By strategically allocating gains across shareholders, businesses can maximize the benefits of the LCGE and reduce tax liabilities without relying solely on the CEI <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>. Combining strategies like the CEI and LCGE can be powerful if implemented correctly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.

## Other Notable Changes Affecting Businesses

### Electronic Filing Requirements

Starting in 2025, new CRA rules require businesses filing six or more information returns (e.g., T4s or T5s) to file electronically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>. Corporations with over $1 million in revenue must also submit T2 returns online <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:28">[00:08:28]</a>. Smaller businesses or those using manual methods will need to adjust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:38">[00:08:38]</a>. It is advisable to sign up for a My Business Account <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>.

### Credit Card Transaction Fee Reductions

While not tax-related, this change positively impacts business cash flow. Starting October 19, 2024, qualifying small business owners will see credit card transaction fees drop by up to 27% under new agreements with Visa and MasterCard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>. This initiative is expected to save eligible businesses around $1 billion over five years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:07">[00:09:07]</a>, significantly reducing operational costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>. Over 90% of credit card-accepting businesses will qualify <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:19">[00:09:19]</a>, with in-store transaction rates dropping to an annual average of 0.95% and online transactions seeing up to a 7% reduction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. To qualify, businesses must meet annual sales thresholds of under $300,000 for Visa and under $175,000 for MasterCard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:32">[00:09:32]</a>.
