## Economic Pressures and Migration Trends

A recent Angus Reid poll indicates that a shocking 40% of recent immigrants are considering moving to another province or leaving Canada entirely, primarily due to the high cost of living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:15">[00:00:15]</a>. This highlights significant [[migration_trends_due_to_economic_pressures | migration trends due to economic pressures]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:20">[00:00:20]</a>.

In broader terms, about 28% of all Canadians are contemplating leaving their current province because of high housing costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>, with young adults and renters being most likely to consider such a move <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>. This trend is particularly evident in Ontario and British Columbia, where housing costs have been elevated for a longer period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>.

Popular relocation destinations include Alberta, where 18% of Canadians considering a move are looking due to more affordable housing and better job opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>. Between 2022 and 2023, net interprovincial migration out of Toronto, Montreal, and Vancouver exceeded 130,000 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:10">[00:03:10]</a>.

## The Rising Cost of Living

Canadians are facing immense frustration due to the [[rising_food_and_housing_expenses | rising cost of everyday items]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>.

### Food Prices
David Olive's article in the Toronto Star notes that a basket of 18 household items costing $82 in March 2021 now costs $113.32, a staggering 38.2% increase in just three years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>. The latest Canada Food Price Report predicts an average family of four's annual food bill will increase by about $711 this year, totaling $16,295 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:06">[00:01:06]</a>. This follows a $1,065 jump in 2023 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:22">[00:01:22]</a>. Food prices have significantly outpaced the average rate of inflation in recent years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>.

### Rental and Housing Costs
Rent is another major concern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:32">[00:01:32]</a>. In April 2021, the average rent for a two-bedroom apartment in Vancouver was $2,598 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>. By July 2024, this average rose to $3,648 per month, a 40% increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>. Essential costs like shelter and food have become significantly more expensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:48">[00:01:48]</a>.

The average cost to buy a house in Canada was $413,000 in 2015 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>. By May 2024, this figure skyrocketed to $699,000, a 69.3% increase over nine years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>. Alongside food and housing, transportation, entertainment, and other general costs have also risen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:16">[00:02:16]</a>. Rampant inflation has made it difficult for many Canadians to manage their bills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>.

## Income Growth Challenges

Unfortunately, income growth has not kept pace with the soaring cost of living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:22">[00:03:22]</a>. According to the latest data from Statistics Canada, the median after-tax income for Canadian families and unattached individuals was $70,500 in 2022 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>. This represents a decrease from $73,000 in 2021 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42">[00:03:42]</a>. It is likely that average wages have not significantly increased in 2023 or 2024, certainly not enough to keep pace with inflation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:45">[00:03:45]</a>.

The rising costs of groceries, household goods, rent, and housing have greatly outpaced income growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:56">[00:03:56]</a>, making it very tough for many Canadians to get by <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>. The discontinuation of pandemic-related government benefits and the return to pre-pandemic Employment Insurance programs have further strained finances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:08">[00:04:08]</a>.

## Widespread Unhappiness

Unhappiness is prevalent across Canada, particularly among Canadian youth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:17">[00:04:17]</a>. The latest World Happiness Report by the Gallup World Poll ranks Canada as the 15th happiest country overall <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:20">[00:04:20]</a>. However, Canadians under the age of 30 fall to 58th place, highlighting a significant disparity in happiness levels among younger generations and painting a bleak picture for the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:28">[00:04:28]</a>.

## Actionable Tips to Combat the Cost of Living Crisis

While many issues stem from uncontrollable economic factors, individuals can manage what they control and hope for better economic conditions and effective government solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:46">[00:04:46]</a>. The two main approaches are to earn more money or to spend less money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>.

### Strategies to Earn More Money

Regardless of income level, strategies can help boost earnings:

*   **For Lower Income Earners:**
    *   **Start a Side Hustle:** Consider a part-time job or freelance business (e.g., freelance writing on platforms like Upwork, driving for Uber, delivering for DoorDash, or starting an online store on Etsy) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.
    *   **Skill Development:** Invest in learning new and in-demand skills through free or low-cost online courses (e.g., Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Udemy) to earn certificates in fields like digital marketing, or participate in government-subsidized training programs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:58">[00:05:58]</a>.
    *   **Job Search:** Constantly search for better-paying job opportunities by regularly checking job boards (e.g., Indeed, Glassdoor), setting automatic alerts, networking on LinkedIn, attending job fairs, or joining professional groups <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.

*   **For Middle Income Earners:**
    *   **Career Advancement:** Focus on advancing within your current company by taking on more responsibilities, seeking promotions, and negotiating for higher pay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>.
    *   **Additional Qualifications:** Obtain further qualifications or certificates that could lead to higher-paying roles, such as post-graduate degrees or specialized training, ideally paid for by an employer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:43">[00:06:43]</a>.
    *   **Passive Income:** If saving is possible, explore passive income opportunities like investments, rental properties, or creating digital products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>.

*   **For Higher Income Earners:**
    *   **Investments:** Make money work for you by investing in stocks, real estate, or other higher-return opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a>.
    *   **Business Ventures:** Consider starting or investing in businesses with high profitability potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:14">[00:07:14]</a>.
    *   **Consulting:** Leverage expertise to offer consulting services in your field, which can command higher fees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:21">[00:07:21]</a>.

### Strategies to Spend Less Money

Reducing expenses can significantly improve financial situations, regardless of income level:

*   **For Lower Income Earners:**
    *   **Government Programs:** Utilize government assistance programs to reduce financial burden, offering services like food banks, emergency shelters, financial counseling, job training, and utility bill assistance (e.g., Salvation Army, YMCA, United Way, provincial government programs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:40">[00:07:40]</a>.
    *   **Strict Budgeting:** Create and stick to a strict budget, tracking expenses to identify areas to cut back <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:01">[00:08:01]</a>.
    *   **Reduce "Big Three" Spending:** Focus on reducing food, shelter, and transportation costs, as these are typically the biggest household expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>.
    *   **Negotiate Bills:** Negotiate bills for services like cable, internet, and phone to secure lower rates or better deals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.

*   **For Middle Income Earners:**
    *   **Debt Management:** Focus on paying off high-interest debt to reduce monthly expenses and save money in the long run <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:29">[00:08:29]</a>.
    *   **Smart Shopping:** Use coupons, discounts, and cashback offers to save on everyday purchases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>.
    *   **Evaluate Lifestyle Choices:** Identify areas to cut back, such as dining out less or choosing more affordable entertainment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:46">[00:08:46]</a>.

*   **For Higher Income Earners:**
    *   **Tax Planning:** Work with a financial advisor or accountant to implement tax-saving strategies to reduce the overall tax burden <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a>.
    *   **Mindful Luxury Spending:** Be mindful of luxury spending and consider if it aligns with long-term financial goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>.
    *   **Automatic Savings:** Set up automatic transfers to savings and investment accounts to ensure consistent contributions and reduce spending temptation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>.

## Out-of-the-Box Solutions

These solutions may not apply to everyone but can help reduce the high cost of living in Canada:

*   **Tiny Houses:** The rise of tiny houses in Canada offers a way to live in compact, efficient homes (200-400 sq ft) to save on housing costs and embrace a minimalist lifestyle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:24">[00:09:24]</a>. Examples include Mint Tiny House Company in Vancouver and Minimalist Houses in Quebec <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:43">[00:09:43]</a>.
*   **Digital Nomads:** With remote working on the rise, individuals can live and work from anywhere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>. Reducing living expenses by temporarily moving to affordable locations abroad (e.g., Thailand, Portugal, Mexico) is an option, requiring only a reliable internet connection and careful planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:56">[00:09:56]</a>.

While Canada faces challenges, its core values and economy are still considered worth investing in <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:17">[00:10:17]</a>. There is hope that the government will implement effective strategies to control the cost of living, and inflation has shown signs of cooling off slightly, potentially bringing some relief <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>.
