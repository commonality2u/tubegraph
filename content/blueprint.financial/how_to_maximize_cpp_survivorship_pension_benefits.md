The Canada Pension Plan (CPP) doesn't always end upon an individual's death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. A surviving spouse or family member may be eligible to receive a CPP survivorship pension, potentially up to $818 per month for survivors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>.

## [[eligibility_criteria_for_cpp_survivorship_pension | Eligibility Criteria for CPP Survivorship Pension]]

To qualify for the CPP survivorship pension, two main requirements must be met:
*   **Relationship Status**: The applicant must be the legal spouse or common-law partner of the deceased CPP contributor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:26">[00:00:26]</a>.
*   **CPP Contributions**: The deceased contributor must have contributed to the CPP for at least three of the last six years before their passing, or have contributed for a minimum of 10 years in total <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:35">[00:00:35]</a>.

> [!PRO TIP] Apply as soon as possible after your partner's death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:48">[00:00:48]</a>. Retroactive payments for the survivorship pension are limited to 12 months, meaning any delay beyond that period could result in a loss of entitled funds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:55">[00:00:55]</a>. For example, if Emma is eligible for $700 per month in CPP survivor benefits but waits 18 months to apply, she would miss out on 6 months of payments, totaling $4,200 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:07">[00:01:07]</a>.

## How Much Money Can You Receive?

The amount of CPP survivorship pension received depends on two primary [[factors_affecting_maximum_cpp_benefit | factors]]:
1.  The survivor's age at the time of the spouse's death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:45">[00:01:45]</a>.
2.  The amount the deceased spouse contributed to the CPP throughout their working years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:51">[00:01:51]</a>.

The pension calculation is always based on what the deceased contributor would have received at age 65, regardless of their actual age at death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:11">[00:03:11]</a>.

### Scenarios Based on Survivor's Age

Using an example where the deceased, John, would have received $1,200 per month in CPP at age 65 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>:

*   **Survivor Under 65 Years Old**:
    *   The survivor receives a flat rate amount ($227 for 2024) plus 37.5% of John's calculated CPP retirement pension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:17">[00:02:17]</a>.
    *   Example: 37.5% of $1,200 is $450. Adding the flat rate of $227, the total benefit would be $677 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>.
*   **Survivor 65 Years or Older**:
    *   The survivorship pension is recalculated, and the survivor receives up to 60% of John's calculated pension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:48">[00:02:48]</a>.
    *   Example: 60% of $1,200 is $720 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:01">[00:03:01]</a>.

### Real-Life Data for Survivor Benefits

Based on actual data:
*   **For survivors under 65**: The average payment is $524 per month, with a maximum of $739 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27">[00:04:27]</a>. This includes both the flat rate and 37.5% of the deceased spouse's pension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:34">[00:04:34]</a>.
*   **For survivors 65 or older**: The average payment is $323 per month, with a maximum of $818 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:42">[00:04:42]</a>. Survivors over 65 receive 60% of their spouse's pension, without the flat rate portion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:48">[00:04:48]</a>.

The gap between average and maximum payments suggests that many survivors do not receive the full potential pension, often due to the deceased spouse's contribution history or the survivor's own CPP benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:56">[00:04:56]</a>. The maximum payout is typically achieved only if the deceased spouse contributed the maximum allowable amounts to the CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>.

## Maximum Survivor Pension Cap Explained

There is a cap on the total CPP benefits an individual can receive if they are also collecting their own CPP retirement or disability pension <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>. The combined amount of your CPP benefits and the survivorship pension cannot exceed the maximum CPP retirement or disability benefit amounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.

### Examples of Combined Benefits:

*   **Retirement Pension and Survivorship Pension**:
    *   If Emma receives $800/month from her own CPP retirement pension and qualifies for $700/month from John's survivorship pension, her combined total would be $1,500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:57">[00:05:57]</a>.
    *   Due to the 2024 retirement cap of $1,375, her benefits would be reduced to that cap amount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.
*   **Lower Retirement Pension**:
    *   If Emma's CPP retirement pension is $400/month and she qualifies for $600/month from John's survivorship pension, her combined total would be $1,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>.
    *   Since this is below the cap, she would receive the full $1,000 per month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>.
*   **Disability Pension Combined with Survivorship Pension**:
    *   If Emma receives $1,200/month from CPP disability benefits and qualifies for $500/month from John's survivorship pension, her combined total would be $1,700 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:38">[00:06:38]</a>.
    *   However, due to the combined survivor and disability pension cap of $1,613 per month (2024), her total benefits would be adjusted to that cap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:51">[00:06:51]</a>.

## [[strategies_to_optimize_cpp_benefits | Advanced Planning Strategies Around the CPP Cap]]

[[strategies_to_maximize_cpp_payout | Strategies to maximize CPP payout]] after a spouse's death, especially concerning the cap:

*   **Start CPP Early if Below the Cap**: If your own CPP retirement pension is expected to be significantly below the maximum, starting it early might be a good option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:11">[00:07:11]</a>. This allows you to collect both your CPP and the survivorship pension sooner without hitting the cap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:24">[00:07:24]</a>. For example, if your CPP is $400/month and survivorship pension is $500/month, the combined $900 is well below the retirement cap of $1,375 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:32">[00:07:32]</a>. While delaying CPP is generally recommended, this is a special case <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:45">[00:07:45]</a>.
*   **Delay CPP if Near the Cap**: If your expected CPP is close to the maximum, delaying your own CPP can maximize your overall benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:52">[00:07:52]</a>. This way, you receive a higher survivorship pension without exceeding the cap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:58">[00:07:58]</a>. For example, if your CPP at 65 is $1,000/month and your survivorship pension is $600/month, the combined $1,600 already exceeds the $1,375 cap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:05">[00:08:05]</a>. Delaying your own CPP to age 70 will increase your benefit later while still receiving the full survivorship benefit from age 65 to 69 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.

## Duration of Survivorship Pension Payments

The CPP survivorship pension is received for life, with no time limits, as long as the survivor is alive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.

*   **[[impact_of_remarriage_on_cpp_survivorship_pension | Impact of Remarriage]]**: Remarrying or entering a new common-law relationship does not affect eligibility; the pension will continue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>.
*   **Death of the Survivor**: Payments will cease upon the death of the survivor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:56">[00:08:56]</a>.

## [[application_process_for_cpp_survivorship_and_child_benefits | Application Process for the Survivorship Pension]]

Applying for the survivorship pension is straightforward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:03">[00:09:03]</a>. Complete the application on the Government of Canada's website and provide supporting documents, such as the spouse's death certificate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>. Remember the pro tip to apply as soon as possible to avoid missing out on payments due to the 12-month retroactive window <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:13">[00:09:13]</a>.

## Related Benefits

### CPP Children's Benefit
If there are children, they may be eligible for the CPP children's benefit, providing financial support after the loss of a contributing parent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:26">[00:09:26]</a>. This benefit is available to both biological and adopted children of the deceased <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:34">[00:09:34]</a>.

*   **Amount**: For 2024, the CPP child benefit offers a flat rate payment of $294 per month per child, regardless of parental earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:45">[00:09:45]</a>.
*   **Eligibility**: The child must be under 18 years old or between 18 and 25 and enrolled in full-time school <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:00">[00:10:00]</a>. The benefit stops when the child reaches 25 or is no longer attending school full-time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:09">[00:10:09]</a>.
*   **Application**: The application is similar to the survivorship benefit, completed through Service Canada, requiring proof of enrollment for older children <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:16">[00:10:16]</a>.

### CPP Death Benefit
The CPP death benefit is a one-time lump sum payment, typically $2,500, paid to the estate of the deceased, though in some cases it can be up to $5,000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:31">[00:10:31]</a>. This amount can help cover immediate expenses like a portion of funeral costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43">[00:10:43]</a>.

## Taxability of CPP Benefits
All CPP benefits mentioned, including the survivorship pension, child benefit, and death benefit, are taxable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:54">[00:10:54]</a>. These amounts must be reported on your tax return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:00">[00:11:00]</a>.
