In a recent interview with Dr. David Friedman, an economist and author, the topic of the efficiency of dating markets and the challenges facing online dating systems was explored deeply. This discussion revolved around the nuances of how dating markets work and why online dating hasn't revolutionized the search for romantic partners as one might expect.

## Understanding the Dating Market

Dr. Friedman describes the dating market as a "sorting problem" rather than a traditional competitive market. Unlike markets for standard goods where products are substitutable, in the dating market, individuals are unique and irreplaceable, adding complexity to the market [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:32">[00:00:32]</a>]. This uniqueness makes it challenging to create a standardized approach to matching potential partners.

## The Economic Model of Dating

Dr. Friedman has discussed dating in the context of economic models in his works, where he uses simplified models to represent marriage markets. In this framework, everyone is deemed identical, and an implicit 'price' is determined by the marriage terms agreed upon by the parties involved [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>]. However, the real-world application is far more complex due to the varying preferences and tastes among individuals, which these models can't easily account for. Such complexity mirrors the [[limitations_of_large_language_models_in_solving_novel_tasks | challenges faced by AI models]] in accounting for varied human preferences.

## Challenges in Online Dating

Despite the potential of computer algorithms to improve the efficiency of dating by matching partners based on objective characteristics, online dating still does not meet expectations. Dr. Friedman highlights the issue of high search costs and the complex nature of human preferences that make online dating challenging [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:36">[00:03:36]</a>].

Moreover, even with advanced filtering systems intended to pair people with compatible traits, online dating services have reportedly not significantly improved success rates or the quality of matches. Anecdotal evidence suggests that platforms like OkCupid have deteriorated over time as they transitioned from enthusiast-driven projects to profit-driven businesses [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:33">[00:04:33]</a>]. The scenario reflects the [[economic_and_political_implications_of_ai | economic challenges inherent in technology-driven solutions]].

## Efficiency Comparison

Dr. Friedman compares modern dating systems with traditional ones, such as arranged marriages commonly practiced in some cultures [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:00">[06:00]</a>]. These traditional systems often involved parents arranging prospective matches for their children, theoretically increasing efficiency by eliminating the 'search cost.' Notably, these systems aren't without merit, with examples showing successful, long-lasting unions selected through parental guidance [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:35">[06:35]</a>]. This is reminiscent of the impact of [[the_role_of_kinship_and_social_organization_in_human_history | social organization on human history]].

## Insights and Speculations

The conversation ventured into speculative territory, questioning whether computer systems would eventually be able to empirically determine successful match traits and apply those algorithms to improve dating outcomes. Dr. Friedman suggests that, in theory, such data-driven approaches could provide insights into successful pairings, but the challenge lies in the execution and complexities involved in gathering and analyzing such nuanced data [<a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:18">[00:09:18]</a>]. This is akin to the [[challenges_in_achieving_artificial_general_intelligence | difficulties in achieving AGI]].

> [!info] Implications for Future Research
> Understanding search costs and improving algorithms in online dating could radically alter the landscape of the dating market. This presents an intriguing area for [[importance_of_new_approaches_in_ai_research | future research]], focusing on behavioral economics and algorithmic matchmaking.

In conclusion, while technological advances hold promise for improving the efficiency of dating markets, the inherent complexities and unique nature of individual preferences pose significant challenges. The development of better algorithms and systems that consider these complexities may eventually transform online dating, though current systems have not yet reached this potential.
