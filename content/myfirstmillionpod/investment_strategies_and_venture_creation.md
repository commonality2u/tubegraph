## The Evolution of Investment Strategy

Rob Dyrdek's early career involved starting his first company at 18 years old and experiencing years of making and losing millions through various ventures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>. He characterized his past approach as investing and building "any which way but loose," often with a "scared money doesn't make money" mentality, which he now rephrases as "dumb money doesn't make money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:34">[00:39:34]</a>. He would reinvest profits into new projects and bigger ideas without proper management, leading to significant overhead and minimal savings after taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:08">[00:38:08]</a>.

In 2013, a pivotal moment occurred when a private equity group, considering a 360 deal for his professional skateboarding league, deemed him "uninvestible" due to his financial management and lack of value creation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:17">[00:25:17]</a>. This served as a deep awakening, prompting him to redefine his identity and embark on a journey to understand business at a high level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:27">[00:25:27]</a>. He hired a consultant to teach him how to build a company from beginning to end <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:00">[00:26:00]</a> and adopted the philosophy from the book "Start at the End," which advises deciding what one wants from a business before starting it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:09">[00:26:09]</a>. This approach extended to his personal life, aiming to design the life he wanted <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:22">[00:26:22]</a>.

This transformation led to exponential growth, transitioning him from being "uninvestible" to creating generational wealth within a five-year period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:37">[00:26:37]</a>. His focus shifted from self-preservation to generational preservation, contemplating a 500-year plan for his family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:56">[00:26:56]</a>.

### Key Learnings and Mistakes

*   **Alien Workshop Acquisition:** Rob acquired Alien Workshop for $4 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:45">[00:06:45]</a>, despite advisors warning about its lack of profitability and cash burn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>. His motivation was purely sentimental, desiring a "hometown kid buys back" story <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:20">[00:07:20]</a>. This venture proved to be a "hornet's nest," teaching him about operational business, culture, and toxic environments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:37">[00:07:37]</a>. He eventually gave the company back to its original founders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>. This experience highlighted the importance of aligning passion with creating ROI (Return on Investment) and IRR (Internal Rate of Return) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>.
*   **Horse Racing:** He owned 12 or 13 racehorses and successfully sold one for $2 million after paying $200k <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:50">[00:10:01]</a>. While he "got out unscathed," he noted that horse ownership is generally not a good [[investment_strategies_and_decisionmaking_in_startups | investment strategy]] for many, primarily serving as enjoyment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:13">[00:10:13]</a>.
*   **Market Timing (Ultra Cast):** Rob founded Ultra Cast, a live 360 and VR video platform, aiming to be at the forefront of live content and VR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:57">[00:49:17]</a>. However, the VR market "died" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:26">[00:49:26]</a>, demonstrating the risk of being too early or misjudging market trends <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:52">[00:49:52]</a>.
*   **Founder Market Fit:** A crucial lesson learned is the importance of "founder market fit" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:18">[00:50:18]</a>. He avoids investing in or building companies with founders who are new to a particular industry, as they often "get crushed in the learning curve" trying to understand it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:51">[00:51:10]</a>.

## The Dyrdek Machine LLC: A Hybrid Family Office and Venture Creation Studio

Rob Dyrdek now operates through a single entity, The Dyrdek Machine LLC, functioning as a hybrid entertainment family office and venture creation studio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:34">[00:11:34]</a>. This LLC owns all his assets, including Rob Dyrdek Inc. (his entertainment business) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>.

### Capital Allocation and [[Reinvestment strategy in business growth | Reinvestment Strategy]]

Capital flowing into The Dyrdek Machine LLC is strategically distributed into two main areas:
1.  **Real Estate Holdings:** He invests in non-correlated, cash-flowing real estate, including manufactured housing, multi-family units, storage units, and RV parks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:58">[00:12:21]</a>. The goal is for his expenses to be covered by dividends from these real estate investments, allowing him to play "the entire game for free" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:00">[00:14:24]</a>.
2.  **Ventures:** He co-founds and creates almost every company he builds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:06">[00:12:06]</a>.
    *   **Funding Structure:** Initial funding for ventures typically ranges from $800k to $1.5 million (pre-money valuation) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:16">[00:12:16]</a>. He invests between $250k and $500k into each venture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>.
    *   **Ownership Target:** His aim is to own between 25% and 70% of every venture at maturity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:26">[00:12:26]</a>.
    *   **Track Record:** Since launching this company in 2016, he has built 17 companies and sold six, netting $450 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:58">[00:13:36]</a>.

### The Unified Theory: Built-to-Sell Approach

Every venture created under The Dyrdek Machine operates on a "Unified Theory," where the entire capital path and growth path are laid out from the very beginning, with a clear target for when and how much the company will be sold for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:37">[00:13:37]</a>. "Everything is built to sell from the very beginning" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:49">[00:13:49]</a>. This structured approach, combined with an understanding of tax efficiencies and IRR on every dollar, allows for exponential growth and liquidity events that feed back into the overall system <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:03">[00:14:34]</a>.

His production company, for example, was the first company he built entirely within this "built to sell" structure, allowing him to sell it in three years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:28">[00:28:41]</a>. The acquisition, which could reach $125-$130 million for his 70% stake, was particularly poetic as it was acquired by the same group that had deemed him uninvestible years prior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:45">[00:29:51]</a>. He even negotiated the inclusion of his professional skateboarding league in the roll-up <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:58">[00:30:01]</a>.
