## Core Investment Principles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:28">[00:27:28]</a>

### Asymmetrical Risk-Reward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:28">[00:27:28]</a>
A recurring theme among top investors is the concept of asymmetrical risk-reward: strategies where the potential downside is minimal, but the upside is substantial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:28">[00:27:28]</a>.

#### The "Nickels" Strategy (Kyle Bass) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:36">[00:28:36]</a>
One illustrative example comes from Kyle Bass, an investor who turned $30 million into $2 billion during the 2008-2009 economic downturn by betting against problematic mortgages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:17">[00:27:17]</a>. When asked how to explain asymmetrical risk-reward simply, Bass proposed the "nickels" strategy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:00">[00:28:00]</a>:
*   **The Cost of Production:** It costs the U.S. government 11 cents to produce a 5-cent nickel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:42">[00:28:42]</a>.
*   **Inherent Value:** A nickel will never be worth less than its face value of 5 cents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:02">[00:29:02]</a>.
*   **Upside Potential:** While melting coins is prohibited, the underlying metal value (which would yield a 30% return immediately) or future rule changes could significantly increase their worth, similar to how older copper pennies became more valuable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:08">[00:29:08]</a>.
*   **Bass's Action:** Kyle Bass purchased millions of nickels, stating he would convert his entire assets to nickels if he could, due to the guaranteed minimum value and potential upside <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:35">[00:29:35]</a>.

#### Protecting the Downside (Sir Richard Branson) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:53">[00:29:53]</a>
Another example comes from [[Investment strategies and experiences | Richard Branson]], who is adventurous in life but highly cautious in investing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:00">[00:30:00]</a>. When launching Virgin Atlantic to compete with British Airways, Branson negotiated a unique deal with Boeing. He committed to buying numerous jets, but with a clause allowing him to return all of them within a year if the business wasn't profitable, incurring no cost or credit loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:26">[00:30:26]</a>. This eliminated his downside risk, leaving only upside potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:41">[00:30:41]</a>.

### The Holy Grail of Investing: Uncorrelated Assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:05">[00:31:05]</a>
Robbins emphasizes a principle he learned from [[Investment strategies and experiences | Ray Dalio]], which Dalio calls "The Holy Grail of Investing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:35">[00:31:35]</a>. This principle states that by finding 8 to 12 uncorrelated investments, one can reduce risk by 80% while slightly increasing upside <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:02">[00:32:02]</a>.
*   **Definition:** Uncorrelated assets move independently of each other. Stocks and bonds are a basic example, as they often perform differently depending on economic conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:25">[00:32:25]</a>.
*   **Challenge:** In today's interconnected public markets, finding truly uncorrelated assets can be difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:42">[00:32:42]</a>.

## Focus on Private Equity and Sports Teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:56">[00:32:56]</a>

### The Power of Private Equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:56">[00:32:56]</a>
Robbins' research revealed a significant trend:
*   **Higher Returns:** Over the last 35 years, private equity has on average outperformed every global stock market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:56">[00:32:56]</a>. While the S&P 500 averaged a 9.2% compounded return, average private equity returned 14.2% annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:10">[00:33:10]</a>. This difference compounds dramatically: $1 million invested 35 years ago would be $26 million in the S&P 500, but $139 million in average private equity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:57">[00:33:57]</a>.
*   **Ultra-High Net Worth Allocation:** Ultra-high net worth individuals allocate about 44% of their assets to private equity and private real estate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:18">[00:34:18]</a>.
*   **Resilience and Flexibility:** Private equity firms are often more resilient because they typically hold investments for five years. This allows them to buy when markets are down and sell when they are up, without being forced to liquidate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:06">[00:35:06]</a>. They also actively improve companies by bringing in new management, marketing, or systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:18">[00:35:18]</a>.
*   **Largest Source of Billionaires:** Financial services, specifically private equity, is the sector with the largest number of billionaires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:53">[00:34:53]</a>.

### Investing in Sports Teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:41">[00:37:41]</a>
A significant shift has occurred in sports team ownership, making them an attractive uncorrelated asset class <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:36">[00:38:36]</a>:
*   **Inflation Hedge:** Sports teams perform well during inflationary periods, as they can easily raise prices (e.g., hot dogs) and fans continue to pay <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:40">[00:38:40]</a>.
*   **Media Enterprises:** Modern sports teams are primarily media enterprises, not just "butts in seats" businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:42">[00:39:42]</a>. Sporting events dominate viewership, with 92 of the top 100 most-watched shows being sports in a recent year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:42">[00:39:42]</a>. Viewers watch ads during live sports, unlike other streaming content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:49">[00:39:49]</a>.
*   **Real Estate Holdings:** Teams also acquire and develop real estate, adding another revenue stream <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:59">[00:39:59]</a>.
*   **Dodgers Example:** Peter Guber, Robbins' partner in the Golden State Warriors and LA Dodgers, purchased the Dodgers for $2 billion when others believed it was only worth $1 billion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:53">[00:38:53]</a>. Shortly after, Guber sold the local TV rights for $7 billion, generating a $5 billion profit instantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:33">[00:39:33]</a>.
*   **Historical Returns:** Over the past decade, NBA, MLB, soccer, and hockey teams have averaged 18% compounded returns, compared to 11% for the S&P 500 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:43">[00:40:43]</a>.
*   **Accessibility:** A rule change allows investors to buy small slivers of teams, providing ownership benefits without needing billions of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:26">[00:38:26]</a>. Robbins now owns pieces of multiple teams, including the Dodgers, Warriors, Red Sox, and Pittsburgh Penguins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:04">[00:40:04]</a>.

## The Importance of Giving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:25">[00:41:25]</a>
[[Tony Robbins on giving and philanthropy | Tony Robbins]] stresses that giving should not be postponed until one is rich. He believes that if someone won't give a dime out of a dollar, they won't give $10 million out of $100 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:26">[00:42:26]</a>. Giving, especially when it's not easy, transforms one's mindset out of scarcity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:34">[00:42:34]</a>.

An early life experience reinforced this belief for Robbins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:37">[00:43:37]</a>:
*   **The Salad Bar Moment:** At 24, broke and struggling, Robbins spent almost all his remaining money on a salad bar meal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:04">[00:43:04]</a>. Upon leaving, he was moved by a small boy holding a door and pulling out a chair for his mother. Robbins, feeling a profound emotional connection, gave the boy all his remaining cash—about $19—with no expectation of return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:50">[00:44:50]</a>.
*   **Immediate Reciprocity:** The next morning, a person who owed Robbins $1,000 from two and a half years prior, and had been unresponsive, unexpectedly sent him a check for $1,200 (including interest) with an apology <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:54">[00:45:54]</a>.
*   **Shift in Mindset:** This event instilled in Robbins the belief that when he gave everything he had, he received abundance. He states he has never experienced scarcity since that day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:45">[00:46:45]</a>.
*   **Tithing and Wealth:** Robbins cites the first self-made billionaire investor, Sir John Templeton, who stated that "the secret to wealth is gratitude" and that he had never met anyone who tithed (gave away 10% of earnings) for at least 10 years who didn't become wealthy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:31">[00:47:31]</a>. Robbins himself is proud to have given away 17% of his earnings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:48">[00:47:48]</a>.
