Jess Ma, a successful entrepreneur who has built multiple companies with a combined value of $950 million, shares her journey of navigating significant personal and professional obstacles. Her experiences highlight that even highly respected individuals face profound challenges and the importance of mindset in [[overcoming personal and career setbacks | overcoming them]].

## Early Life and Professional Setbacks
Jess Ma's early life was marked by difficulties. She describes herself as a "total geek loser" in high school, bullied even in the computer club, and struggled to fit in or get good grades <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:09">[00:01:09]</a>. Feeling uninterested and unable to relate to her peers, she eventually dropped out of high school halfway through her sophomore year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:11">[00:44:11]</a>. However, she found an early path to entrepreneurship, starting a dedicated server and collocation business as a teenager that generated hundreds of thousands of dollars annually, far exceeding her initial goal of just covering video game expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:26">[00:01:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:17">[00:43:17]</a>.

Her first major company, Indera, a financial SaaS business, faced numerous professional challenges. Ma describes it as being "weeks away from insolvency multiple times over" due to being under-capitalized and running things "a little too close to the edge" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:27">[00:10:27]</a>. Despite these near-death experiences, she grew Indera to several hundred employees and a valuation in the hundreds of millions of dollars <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:56">[00:09:56]</a>.

## Personal Tragedy and Shifting Mindset
A pivotal moment in Ma's life came with the unexpected death of her boyfriend. This tragedy plunged her into a "really dark period" where she was "super depressed" and unable to work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a>. This rock-bottom experience prompted a profound re-evaluation of her life and career. She asked herself: "What would I do if I was going to die this year?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:08">[00:12:08]</a> and "What would I do if I wasn't scared of failure?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:14">[00:13:14]</a>.

This period led her to:
*   **Re-evaluate success metrics** Ma realized that the Silicon Valley rhetoric of needing to be a CEO and build a unicorn company to avoid being a "failure" did not resonate with her <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:54">[00:10:54]</a>. She observed that many billionaires built diversified holding companies without necessarily being the CEO, like Warren Buffett <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:11">[00:11:11]</a>.
*   **Hire CEOs for existing businesses** Immediately, she hired a CEO for Indera and another friend to run her litigation fintech company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:19">[00:12:19]</a>.
*   **Embrace "Bold Science"** The realization that she had "seen Rock Bottom" and her life "can't get any worse" freed her from the fear of trying and failing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:26">[00:13:26]</a>. This courage propelled her into the biotech sector, where she now funds "bold science bets" focused on non-incremental, transformative advancements, such as new approaches to curing cancer or extending human lifespan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:55">[00:34:55]</a>.
