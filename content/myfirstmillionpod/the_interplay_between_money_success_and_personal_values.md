### Philosophy on Money and Success
For the interviewee, money and capital primarily represent two things:
1.  **Solving specific problems**: Money can alleviate certain burdens like childcare costs or medical bills, allowing him to focus on other aspects of life that money cannot solve, such as marriage or personal well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:27">[01:05:27]</a>.
2.  **Opportunity and creative freedom**: It provides the ability to say "no" to undesired opportunities and to "imprint my world view upon the world on my terms" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:09">[01:06:09]</a>. He emphasizes owning his work, like his comedy show, which he owns "outright" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:13">[01:06:13]</a>.

His financial goal is not to be a billionaire, but to achieve a level of financial safety and security for his family for a specified runway (e.g., 10 years of burn rate) and to have the capital to fund his creative projects independently <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:00">[01:10:00]</a>. He wants to be able to continue making art that is honest for the rest of his life <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:13">[01:13:13]</a>, like Dick Gregory, who died with tour dates on the books <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:36">[01:12:36]</a>. This requires significant capital to cover production costs for touring and deep creative work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:17">[01:13:17]</a>.

He acknowledges that artists often struggle with managing money and aims to learn how to avoid common pitfalls, such as squandering lump sums or becoming dependent on industry structures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:03">[01:07:03]</a>. He advocates for artists to understand the business side to gain leverage and "capture the value" they create <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:52:19">[01:52:19]</a>.

He identifies as a "truth teller" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:15">[01:12:15]</a>, even if truths are "ugly" or "painful" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:19">[01:12:19]</a>.

### The "Not Waiting" Mindset
A significant aspect of his [[personal_development_and_financial_independence | personal development]] and [[the psychology of financial_success_and_humility | financial success]] is his "not waiting" mindset. He was advised not to mortgage his present by doing things he didn't want to do for a future that might not happen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:58">[01:37:58]</a>. Instead, he aims to "carry yourself like the guy who has that thing" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:39:31">[01:39:31]</a>. This means not deferring the feeling of security, relaxation, or confidence until a future financial goal is met, but tapping into that feeling now <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:17">[01:41:17]</a>.

This mindset yields two benefits:
1.  **Immediate well-being**: Feeling good today instead of anxious or stressed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:27">[01:41:27]</a>.
2.  **Strategic advantage**: Carrying oneself with confidence can open doors and lead to better results, as seen in studies where happy individuals perform better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:41:36">[01:41:36]</a>.

He emphasizes not limiting himself in areas he truly cares about, such as hiring a personal chef for health and family well-being, with the belief that he can "afford it" because he trusts his "ability to go get that money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:49">[01:45:49]</a>. However, he is also selective about purchases, recognizing that possessions can "own you as much as you own it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:27">[01:46:27]</a>, and prioritizes intrinsic satisfaction regardless of external circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:46:57">[01:46:57]</a>.
