### Financial Journey and Investment Philosophy

*   **Multi-dimensional opportunity assessment** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:05">[00:39:05]</a>: He always looked at media and marketing multi-dimensionally, seeking various ways to monetize rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:09">[00:39:09]</a>. ### Entrepreneurial Ventures and Lessons Learned

#### Momentous Protein: A Case Study in Persistence and Dilution
[[Rob Dyrdeks ventures and investment philosophies | Dyrdek co-founded Momentous protein]] with Matt Wan in 2016, with a vision for "the Ferrari of supplements" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:56">[00:09:56]</a>.
*   **Initial Challenges**: The company struggled for years, selling nothing due to its high price, which was 35% above competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:32">[00:11:32]</a>. Matt, an 18-year-old prodigy who forewent Harvard to build the company, lacked understanding of company operations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:07">[00:12:07]</a>. Dyrdek initially invested $300,000 for a 30% stake <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:39">[00:13:39]</a>.
*   **Struggles and Advice**: Sales were only a few million annually, with continuous losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:20">[00:13:20]</a>. Board meetings were "painful" due to no revenue growth and burning capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:57">[00:13:57]</a>. Dyrdek advised Matt to sell or merge, feeling he had given "bad uncle" advice by encouraging him to skip Harvard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:54">[00:14:54]</a>.
*   **The Turning Point**: The business merged with Amped and then "exploded overnight" after securing a promotion deal with Andrew Huberman <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:55">[00:15:55]</a>. Sales grew "like 20 times," transforming the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:06">[00:16:06]</a>. Huberman's authenticity and deep scientific credibility provided the perfect match for the product's quality, something traditional advertising and athlete endorsements couldn't achieve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:42">[00:16:42]</a>.
*   **Lessons Learned**: Despite the eventual success, Dyrdek's stake was diluted to about 4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:14">[00:17:14]</a>. He calls this his "perfect story" because it highlights the honesty of venture creation, where an investment might yield a significant return (2.5x his initial $300k, potentially millions), but falls short of the "venture creation game" goal of much larger exits (like $100 million for his 20% stake) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a>. He states, "Would I start a supplement brand with an 18 year old ever again? I would not" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:57">[00:26:57]</a>. This story illustrates the long, often painful, path to success and the importance of finding the right market fit and leverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:20">[00:18:20]</a>.

#### Jolie (Filtered Shower Water): A Model for Success
In contrast, Dyrdek highlights his investment in Jolie, a filtered shower water company, as an example of a well-executed venture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>.
*   **Strategic Approach**: He was approached by a seasoned CEO with experience in Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) footwear <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:13">[00:27:13]</a>. *   **Business Model**: Jolie offered a hardware product with recurring revenue from filter replacements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:12">[00:28:12]</a>. While the market for filtered shower heads was tiny (under $1 billion), it represented pure white space <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:00">[00:29:00]</a>. High friction to remove the shower head would lead to low churn on subscriptions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:01">[00:30:01]</a>.
*   **Launch and Growth**: Jolie launched by showing potential customers water contaminants based on their zip code, a data-driven customer acquisition strategy used for months before product launch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:41">[00:31:41]</a>. Pre-orders were strong, and first-quarter churn was exceptionally low at 1.2% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:21">[00:32:21]</a>. The company grew from $4 million in sales in year one to nearly $40 million in year two <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:41">[00:31:41]</a>.
*   **Valuation**: The business achieved a valuation of over $200 million in 1.5 years due to its unique "extraordinary Hardware subscription service" model, which is rare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:09">[00:33:09]</a>. It was built with only three people, was profitable, and never needed to raise additional capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:20">[00:33:20]</a>. Dyrdek invested $800,000 in this venture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:30">[00:33:30]</a>.

#### Production Companies: A "Worst Business"
Dyrdek, who sold his production company for $200 million, describes it as "the worst business that you could ever get in in your life" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:14">[01:21:14]</a>.
*   **Shoot-What-You-Kill**: Production is a "shoot-what-you-kill" game where distributors control all the money <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:22">[01:21:22]</a>. A hit show getting canceled can render the company worthless <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:32">[01:21:32]</a>.
*   **Challenges**: Shows are rarely picked up for long periods, making sustained value creation difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:42">[01:21:42]</a>. Production companies must build extensive infrastructure (camera, finishing, music licensing) and then extract a slim 20-30% margin from a network's budget <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:13">[01:22:13]</a>.
*   **Vertical Integration**: Success often comes from vertical integration, owning post-production, finishing, and music divisions to increase margins <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:15">[01:23:15]</a>.
*   **Valuation and Risk**: Companies trade on EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) at 5-6 times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:03">[01:24:03]</a>. Buyers often seek partnerships or earn-outs to incentivize long-term creative output and mitigate the risk of a show's cancellation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:13">[01:24:13]</a>. The key is in aggregating creative minds and having multiple shows to offset potential failures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:46">[01:24:46]</a>. Distributors like Netflix and Paramount remain the gatekeepers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:47">[01:25:47]</a>.

#### Real Estate: Cash Flow and Long-Term Growth
Dyrdek's real estate strategy focuses on "cash flowing assets," particularly multi-family units <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:08">[00:42:08]</a>.
*   **Tax-Free Cash Flow**: He aims for 5-6% tax-free cash flow and 7-10% equity growth over the long term <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:08">[00:43:08]</a>. He uses 1031 exchanges to reinvest profits from sales, achieving 35-42% IRRs (Internal Rate of Return) on some buildings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:34">[00:43:34]</a>.
*   **Liquidity Management**: He keeps significant liquid dollars in high-yield funds (blended 10% return) and money markets (5%), which provide cash for expenses without growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:50">[00:43:50]</a>. This "modern cash flow portfolio" underwrites his living expenses and family office operations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:23">[00:44:23]</a>.
*   **Passive Income**: He distinguishes true passive income (giving money to an operator who returns cash) from active management (like owning a rental property with ongoing maintenance) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:16">[00:46:16]</a>. He focuses on evaluating "world-class" operators to deploy capital with minimal personal time investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:42">[00:46:42]</a>.

#### "Forever Estates": A Lesson in Personal Investment

This shift came from maturing, having kids, and reaching a "generational level of wealth" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:25">[00:55:25]</a>.
