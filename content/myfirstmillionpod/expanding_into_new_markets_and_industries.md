From: [[myfirstmillionpod]] <br/> 

The Zuru empire, founded by brothers Nick and Mat, began with humble and often naive beginnings, evolving from a small toy company into a multi-billion dollar enterprise that leverages its core strengths to [[scaling_business_through_acquisition_and_partnerships | expand into diverse new markets and industries]]. Their journey highlights the power of persistence, a "first principles" approach, and a strong emphasis on automation and speed of innovation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>.

## Origin Story and Early Challenges
The company's roots trace back to a dairy farm in New Zealand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:08">[01:08:08]</a>, where Nick and his brother Matt started by selling model hot air balloons door-to-door at festivals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a>. This early experience in door-to-door sales taught Nick the valuable life lesson of persistence and how to handle rejection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:00">[02:27:00]</a>.

A pivotal, albeit naive, decision was to move to China with no money, no plan, no relationships, and no language skills to build their own factory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:00">[01:17:00]</a>. They even slept in a bush at Hong Kong airport due to high hotel costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a>. Their initial factory was a small shed by a river where they invested all their money in an injection molding machine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:01:00">[07:01:00]</a>.

### Learning from Failures and Lawsuits
Their early products, such as a Night Frisbee and a Money Gobbler, were direct copies of existing products <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:19:00">[08:19:00]</a>. This led to multiple lawsuits because they were unaware of intellectual property (IP) rights <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:25:00">[09:25:00]</a>. Nick recalls being sued for the Night Frisbee and having no money to defend themselves, eventually hiring a lawyer who let them write the defense themselves for a minimal fee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00">[10:31:00]</a>. These experiences forced them to start innovating and creating their own ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:27:00">[10:27:00]</a>.

During these challenging times, they lived on less than a dollar a day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:47:00">[12:47:00]</a>. Nick's brother even lived in a tiny room within their factory for 10 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:23:00">[13:23:00]</a>. This period instilled a deep-seated commitment to profitability:
> "From this day on we will never have a day, a month, a week, a year will we lose money. Like if we're losing money in a month we will sit down, we will like eat nothing or we will like live on nothing just to ensure that we're like profitable" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:20:00">[33:20:00]</a>

## Breakthroughs and Business Philosophy
The company's first significant break came from a large order for a David Beckham Tamaguchi from Walmart, an order worth nearly $30 million USD, far exceeding their previous sales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:53:00">[22:53:00]</a>. Despite the order being significantly reduced and eventually leading to a five-year blackball from Walmart, it provided enough capital to survive and grow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:41:00">[24:41:00]</a>.

They developed a unique business model, realizing they were bad at designing products initially, so they partnered with US companies like Zing and Zard to sell their products internationally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:05:00">[27:05:00]</a>. This strategy of taking others' products and leveraging their distribution channels proved successful <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:31:00">[27:31:00]</a>.

Key tenets of their operational philosophy include:
*   **Persistence and Grit**: Continuously fighting and pushing forward, as exemplified by Nick's relentless emailing of buyers and "buying" his own products in retail tests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:36:00">[31:36:00]</a>.
*   **"Win or Learn"**: They view failures as learning opportunities, not losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:24:00">[16:24:00]</a>.
*   **"Firing Bullets and Failing Fast"**: This involves making minimal viable investments to test an idea. If it works, it becomes a "cannonball" for larger investment; if not, they fail fast and learn <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:00">[00:17:00]</a>.
*   **"First Principles" Thinking**: They deconstructed traditional toy manufacturing and business models, rebuilding from the ground up, leading to highly efficient and profitable operations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:16:00">[34:16:00]</a>.
*   **Automation**: Zuru focuses heavily on automating production, even for complex products, minimizing human intervention from raw material to finished product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="59:03:00">[59:03:00]</a>.
*   **Continuous Improvement**: They aim for a "2% improvement a week," believing in the power of compounding small gains over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:59">[01:05:59]</a>.
*   **Hiring for Grit**: Beyond talent, they prioritize hiring individuals with immense grit, competitive drive, and a bias for action <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:30">[01:06:30]</a>.

## Diversification into Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG)
A personal health crisis forced Nick to return to New Zealand, prompting him to rethink the toy industry's market ceiling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="42:28:00">[42:28:00]</a>. He observed that FMCG (Fast-Moving Consumer Goods) or CPG (Consumer Packaged Goods) industries were dominated by a few large, slow-moving companies with duopolies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:01:00">[44:01:00]</a>. Nick's thesis was that Zuru could disrupt these markets by:
1.  Delivering more margin to retail partners <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:47:00">[44:47:00]</a>.
2.  Innovating faster <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:48:00">[44:48:00]</a>.
3.  Reaching customers more efficiently through targeted digital advertising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:56:00">[44:56:00]</a>.
4.  Moving at the speed of culture and platforms (e.g., TikTok) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="46:46:00">[46:46:00]</a>.

This led to the establishment of Zuru Edge, focusing on a "Five Vertical Strategy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:07:00">[52:07:00]</a>:

*   **Diapers**: Zuru launched a diaper brand in New Zealand, quickly gaining 40% market share and causing a local competitor to go out of business <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="45:47:00">[45:47:00]</a>. They then replicated this success in Australia and the US (Walmart, Target), producing 2 billion diapers last year within 5.5 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="48:24:00">[48:24:00]</a>. Their success was attributed to bringing high-quality Chinese diaper technology to the global market at competitive prices, a price and performance-driven category <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:37:00">[49:37:00]</a>.
*   **Beauty**: Partnering with Jamie, who had a background in luxury PR and beauty, they launched Monday Haircare, which became known as "the most famous shampoo and conditioner bottle in the world" by Forbes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:09:00">[49:09:00]</a>. This success was driven by innovative design, creative marketing, and strong TikTok presence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:20:00">[50:20:00]</a>.
*   **Pet Food**: With a young entrepreneur, they launched into pet food, with their brand Bonkers driving 30% of all cat treat growth in America last year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:31:00">[51:31:00]</a>.
*   **Home Care**: They are actively building several brands in this vertical <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:02:00">[52:02:00]</a>.
*   **Supplements**: This was one of their failures; their brand Dose & Co, despite working well internationally, failed in the US and was eventually sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:43:00">[51:43:00]</a>.

Nick states that successful categories for them are either price and performance-driven, innovation-driven, or design and creative-driven <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:57:00">[49:57:00]</a>. Once a product or category shows promise, Zuru goes "super deep," automating production and optimizing every part of the process <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:13:00">[51:13:00]</a>.

## Moonshot: Zuru Tech
The latest and most ambitious undertaking is Zuru Tech, a project focused on disrupting the construction industry <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:15:00">[53:15:00]</a>. The core idea is to create the world's first factory with a customized input (building design) and a fully automated output (finished building), significantly reducing costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="53:45:00">[53:45:00]</a>.

Key components of Zuru Tech include:
*   **Dreamcatcher Software**: Built on Unreal Engine, this software has a simple user interface but a complex logic layer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:05:00">[54:05:00]</a>. It catalogs every global building code, maps terrain, and allows users (even a 10-year-old) to design houses with automated structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing calculations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:18:00">[54:18:00]</a>.
*   **Quira AI Assistant**: An AI assistant trained on the works of great architects allows users to interact with their building designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:30:00">[54:30:00]</a>.
*   **Automated Factories**: Starting with a 1/10 scale factory to test software-hardware integration, they have progressed to a three-hectare test production line building a house every two weeks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:25:00">[55:25:00]</a>. The ultimate goal is a 25-acre commercial factory and eventually one of the world's largest factories, aiming to build houses for $500 per square meter with high quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="55:56:00">[55:56:00]</a>.
*   **Self-Funding**: This massive undertaking, involving about 700 software and hardware engineers across offices in India, Italy (through acquisition of architectural software company), and China, is entirely self-funded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="56:34:00">[56:34:00]</a>.

This project embodies their "first principles" thinking, akin to Elon Musk's approach of breaking down problems to their fundamental components <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:52">[01:03:52]</a>.

## Current Scale and Future Vision
Today, Zuru's empire generates over $2 billion USD in annual revenue, growing at 25-30% year-on-year, and is remarkably profitable, running at 40% net profits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:06:00">[41:06:00]</a>. This profitability has allowed them to [[advice_on_business_growth_and_investment | organically fund their growth]] and diverse ventures without outside capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:40:00">[34:40:00]</a>.

Nick and his brother remain highly motivated, driven by the love of competition and the desire to solve bigger problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:20">[01:00:20]</a>. Each of their five new verticals (diapers, beauty, pet food, home care, and supplements/others) is envisioned to become larger than their original toy company within the next two to three years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:13:00">[52:13:00]</a>. Their consistent commitment to their core DNA of persistence, speed, and automation allows them to enter and disrupt new industries effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:14">[01:04:14]</a>.
