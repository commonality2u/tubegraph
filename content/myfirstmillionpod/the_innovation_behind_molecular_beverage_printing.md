Cana, a venture from TPB (The Production Board), is focused on revolutionizing the beverage industry through molecular beverage printing, allowing for decentralized manufacturing and significant environmental benefits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:58">[00:55:58]</a>. The core mission of TPB is to transform global production systems to use less energy, land, and natural resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. This approach posits that sustainability in the 21st century comes from building technology-based solutions that allow consumers to consume more while simultaneously dropping prices and environmental impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:18">[00:00:18]</a>.

## The Concept of Molecular Beverage Printing

The fundamental insight behind Cana's [[innovative_startups_in_food_and_drink | innovative beverage printing]] technology is that 99% of most beverages are water, or water combined with sugar or alcohol <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:58">[00:31:58]</a>. Only about 1% of a beverage consists of the chemistry that creates its flavor, smell, color, and texture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:07">[00:32:07]</a>. For example, wine has about 500 flavor compounds, but only 30 to 40 of these truly impact the sensory experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:44">[00:32:44]</a>. Similarly, coffee, tea, juice, soda, liquor, and beer all contain hundreds of compounds, but only a few dozen are essential for their distinct taste <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:08">[00:33:08]</a>. Crucially, these few dozen compounds often overlap significantly between different beverages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:30">[00:33:30]</a>. This means minor chemical adjustments can transform one type of wine into another, or a white wine into a red wine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:34">[00:33:34]</a>.

The idea is to encapsulate these essential compounds into a cartridge, similar to how an inkjet printer uses CMYK colors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:41">[00:34:41]</a>. With approximately 80 compounds, Cana believes it can recreate the flavors of virtually all beverages, from orange juice and Gatorade to beer, iced tea, iced coffee, and margaritas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:49">[00:34:49]</a>.

### The Genesis of the Idea

The inspiration for Cana came from a dinner in late 2019 or so with a professor from UC Davis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:18">[00:35:18]</a>. The professor shared research from a German scientist at the Technical University of Munich who runs a flavor science lab <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:31">[00:35:31]</a>. This scientist demonstrated that by reducing a glass of wine from 500 compounds to about 37-40, sensory panels could not distinguish it from the original wine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:41">[00:35:41]</a>. The scientist posited that all beverages could be recreated with just a few dozen compounds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:53">[00:35:53]</a>. This sparked the idea to create a "Star Trek replicator for beverages" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:00">[00:36:00]</a>.

## Technology and Product Development

Cana spent about three years on this [[biotechnology_ventures_and_bold_science | biotechnology venture]] project, beginning with chemical research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:09">[00:34:09]</a>. They used a gas chromatography-mass spectrometer (GCMS) to analyze the molecular components of thousands of beverages, effectively "digitizing the world's beverages" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:17">[00:34:17]</a>. Based on this chemical data, they developed software that uses heuristics and master combinations to reduce the number of necessary compounds, aiming for a common overlapping set that can recreate any beverage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:48">[00:34:48]</a>.

The technical challenges were significant, including precisely dispensing micro-liter and sub-micro-liter volumes of flavoring compounds into a water stream within 15-20 seconds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:59">[00:36:59]</a>. The device also needs to chill the water and offer optional carbonation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:36">[00:38:36]</a>. Heating for hot beverages is not available in Gen 1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:45">[00:38:45]</a>.

Cana currently has fully working prototypes of its Gen 1 device and plans to begin production soon, with shipments expected early next year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:30">[00:37:30]</a>. Pre-orders are available for $99, with the device priced at $499 for the first 10,000 orders, increasing to $799 thereafter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:42">[00:37:42]</a>.

### Cartridges and Business Model

The device uses three main cartridges:
*   A master flavor cartridge, lasting 1-3 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:01">[00:39:01]</a>.
*   A sugar cartridge, lasting about one month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:07">[00:39:07]</a>.
*   An alcohol cartridge, also lasting about one month <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:13">[00:39:13]</a>.

Cartridges are provided for free and auto-ship to the customer's home when the device senses they are running low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:17">[00:39:17]</a>. The business model charges per drink, with prices expected to be 25-50% cheaper on average than retail equivalents <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:25">[00:39:25]</a>. Drinks range from 29 cents for flavored sparkling water to $2.99 for a nice cocktail <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:07">[00:51:07]</a>.

## Personalization and the "Long Tail" of Beverages

Cana's device allows for extensive personalization, offering options like low-sugar juice, varied caffeine levels in iced tea, or electrolyte boosts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:17">[00:40:17]</a>. The vision is to offer hundreds or even thousands of new brands on the device, moving beyond the limited choices available in retail stores (typically only 150 slots for various beverage categories) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:34">[00:40:34]</a>.

This parallels the "long tail" phenomenon seen in media, where platforms like YouTube and Netflix offer vastly more content than traditional television channels, allowing for niche interests to be served <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:27">[00:42:27]</a>. Similarly, Cana aims to enable a proliferation of unique beverage brands created by individuals or influencers, without the insurmountable costs and logistical hurdles of traditional beverage manufacturing and distribution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:54">[00:42:54]</a>.

> "If you're someone with less than 100 million followers, you've only got 3 million followers, you're a great YouTube Creator, people know you, they love you, they're passionate about you, you're never going to go make a beverage brand, it's going to cost $5 million to do that. You got to go make formulate it, you got to make packaging, you got to make a logo, you got to box it then you got to go convince retail stores to carry it. I mean the whole process is like an insurmountable process generally speaking you have to be huge like the rock to get that and you know there's probably 90,000 times more people in that middle that are really influential, have a lot of followers, aren't as big as George Clooney and the Rock and so they could make a brand that doesn't require any investment that doesn't require any Capital they don't have to take any risk" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:15">[00:50:15]</a>

Cana allows creators to own their brands and potentially launch them elsewhere if they gain mass appeal, operating as a platform for permissionless [[innovative_product_development_and_launch_strategies | product development]] and [[innovative_marketing_strategies_for_startup_growth | marketing for startup growth]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:46">[00:52:46]</a>.

## Environmental Impact

The current centralized manufacturing and distribution system for beverages is highly inefficient and environmentally damaging <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:06">[00:57:06]</a>.
*   It takes approximately 600 liters of water to make a single liter of wine, and about 40 liters of water for a liter of orange juice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:01">[00:56:01]</a>.
*   This vast amount of water and the crops grown for beverages are then packaged in CO2-intensive glass, plastic, and cans <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:21">[00:56:21]</a>.
*   These products are transported by trucks, contributing to carbon emissions, through warehouses and stores before reaching consumers' homes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:31">[00:56:31]</a>.
*   This entire system contributes about half a billion tons of CO2 to the atmosphere annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:44">[00:56:44]</a>.
*   Around 400 trillion liters of water are used in the production cycle of bottled beverages <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:55">[00:56:55]</a>.
*   An estimated 120 million acres of farmland could be returned to natural ecosystems if these crops were not grown for beverage production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:15">[00:57:15]</a>.

By decentralizing manufacturing and allowing consumers to create beverages in their homes using existing water infrastructure, Cana aims to significantly reduce carbon emissions, water waste, and land use, while offering a cheaper and better experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:23">[00:57:23]</a>. This represents a paradigm shift from incremental efficiency improvements to eliminating an entire wasteful supply chain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:32">[00:58:32]</a>.
