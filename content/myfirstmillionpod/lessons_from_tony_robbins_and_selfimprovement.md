## Key Self-Improvement "Rewiring" Concepts

### Overcoming Arrogance and Learning from Everyone
Spear candidly describes his "awful attitude" of believing he was smarter than most people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:55:10">[02:55:10]</a>. He realized that true wisdom comes from learning from "every man" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:10:07">[03:10:07]</a>. This included the crucial lesson of remembering and using people's names, a concept from Dale Carnegie's "How to Win Friends and Influence People," which [[tony_robbins_influence_on_successful_individuals | Warren Buffett]] also valued <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a>.

### Reciprocation and Micro-Interactions
Inspired by the "psychology of influence," Spear deliberately practiced reciprocation, even by giving small gifts like bags of sweets to doormen or taxi drivers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:19:08">[03:19:08]</a>. This was a tactical effort to foster positive feelings in others on a micro-level, something his academic background hadn't taught him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>.

### The "Act-Then-Feel" Principle
A significant realization was that feelings can follow actions: "you don't have to feel it to do it; do it and you'll feel it later" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:47:00">[03:47:00]</a>. By consistently writing thank-you notes, even when initially just "going through the motions," Spear found he genuinely started to care more about the people he was writing to <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>. This practice led to becoming a more caring and interested person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:55:00">[03:55:00]</a>.

### The Reticular Activating System (RAS) and Intentional Focus
Tony Robbins uses an exercise (looking for red, then being asked about blue) to illustrate the Reticular Activating System (RAS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>. This part of the brain acts as a "heat-seeking missile," filtering stimuli and focusing on what you've programmed it to prioritize <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:44:00">[03:44:00]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Programming Your Brain
> If you're considering buying a BMW, you start noticing BMWs everywhere because your brain is now programmed to pay attention <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:56:00">[03:56:00]</a>. Similarly, a gratitude practice (listing three things you're grateful for daily) trains your brain to actively seek out moments of appreciation, making you a more grateful person <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

## Managing Psychology in Investing and Life

Spear, who has run an investment fund for over 25 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:00">[02:59:00]</a>, reflects on managing his psychology during periods of underperformance. He acknowledges that his long-term success of beating the S&P 500 (by about 80 basis points net of fees) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:04:00">[03:04:00]</a> could be attributed to luck <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:41:00">[04:41:00]</a>. His strategy is to prioritize compounding and avoid jeopardizing it for the sake of merely beating the index <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:14:00">[06:14:00]</a>.

He emphasizes the importance of playing the "infinite game" in life and investing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:20:00">[06:20:00]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Finite vs. Infinite Games
> *   **Finite games** have clear rules, boundaries (time and space), and a defined winner/loser (e.g., chess, American football) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:33:00">[06:33:00]</a>.
> *   **Infinite games** have no clear rules, boundaries, or end. Players often drop out rather than "win" or "lose" (e.g., the Cold War, life, investing) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:37:00">[06:37:00]</a>.
> The key mistake is treating an infinite game as a finite one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:55:00">[06:55:00]</a>.

For Spear, the goal is to "compound well and live well" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:43:00">[09:43:00]</a>, rather than chasing external metrics of success like those pursued in Silicon Valley's "win big or fizzle out" culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:13:00">[09:13:00]</a>.

## Applying Principles Consistently

Spear uses [[lessons_from_failures_and_successes | a framework]] of asking: "If I do the same contemplated action given the same set of circumstances throughout the rest of my life, how's it going to turn out?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:40">[01:10:40]</a>. This helps in making quick decisions, especially in saying "no" to things that might lead to negative long-term consequences if repeated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:10">[01:11:10]</a>. This aligns with [[tony_robbins_influence_on_successful_individuals | Warren Buffett's wisdom]] of avoiding the "just this once" fallacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:50">[01:13:50]</a>.

Another powerful question is: "If everybody in the world did this contemplated thing that I'm contemplating on doing, what would the world look like?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:47">[01:11:47]</a>. This helps evaluate the broader societal impact of individual actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:04">[01:13:04]</a>. Spear envisions a more beautiful world where people pursue satisfying work, like watchmaking or novel writing, rather than solely striving for grand-scale empires <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:40">[01:12:40]</a>.
