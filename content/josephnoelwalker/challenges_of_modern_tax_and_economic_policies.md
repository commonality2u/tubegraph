Australia has experienced a significant slowdown in economic reform, with its last major economic overhaul being the introduction of a consumption tax in 2000 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>. This 25-year period of limited improvement coincides with stagnant or falling productivity growth, which has in turn slowed the growth of real GDP per person – a key measure of living standards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:30">[00:00:30]</a>.

## Productivity and Living Standards

The slowdown in real GDP per person indicates a decline in living standards growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:40">[00:00:40]</a>. Understanding the drivers of productivity is crucial. Productivity growth has two principal drivers:
1.  **Capital Deepening**: Augmenting labor with capital assistance, including AI assistance, which makes each hour of work more productive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:21">[01:24:21]</a>. This is driven by a national investment rate matched to workforce growth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:40">[01:24:40]</a>. Australia has experienced "capital shallowing" in the last decade due to a collapse in the investment rate, a level previously seen only during recessions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:06">[01:25:06]</a>.
2.  **Multi-Factor Productivity (MFP) Growth**: This is a residual measure, often described as "the measure of our ignorance" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:25:47">[01:25:47]</a>. It relates to finding new, smarter ways of doing things or new processes without necessarily using more capital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:24">[01:26:24]</a>.

Economists believe that while microeconomic reform can improve efficiency and move a country closer to its production possibility frontier, sustained long-term growth in GDP per capita primarily comes from technological progress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:07">[01:36:07]</a>. The challenge for policy is to [[Technological revolutions and economic growth | create an environment conducive to technological innovation that can be commercialized domestically]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:01">[01:37:01]</a>. Australia has success stories, but spreading these learnings broadly has been difficult, partly due to geography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:38">[01:37:38]</a>. Many smart technology people from Australia spend their careers overseas, building technological developments elsewhere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:58">[01:37:58]</a>.

Australia's productivity level compared to the US (the technological frontier) is around 80%, having peaked at 85% in the late 1990s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:50">[01:48:50]</a>. Studies suggest that 95% of the US level might be the best Australia can realistically achieve due to factors like geographic isolation and the small size of its national market <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:30">[01:22:30]</a>.

### Missed Opportunities for Investment
Australia has significant deposits of critical minerals, essential for digital and electrical technologies, but undertakes almost no processing of these minerals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:10">[01:29:10]</a>. The markets for these minerals are often not orderly, with most going to China, and Australia has a chance to enter critical minerals processing despite technical challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:11">[01:30:11]</a>. Similarly, in the forestry sector, most timber from native forests is exported as raw chips or pulp, or used as firewood, with very little hardwood used in Australian construction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:59">[01:30:59]</a>. Australia imports almost all engineered wood products from the northern hemisphere, which is "bizarre" given transport costs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:15">[01:32:15]</a>. This highlights a mindset that Australia is only good at extracting and exporting raw materials <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:42">[01:32:42]</a>.

## Tax Reform Challenges

The Henry Review (2010), a major review of Australia's tax system, made numerous recommendations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:18:18">[01:18:18]</a>.
Areas of potential reconsideration for the Henry Review recommendations include:
*   **Capital Income Taxation**: A flat 25% Nordic-style tax on capital income might be preferable to the previously recommended capital income discounts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:32">[00:40:32]</a>.
*   **Company Income Tax**: The structure of the company income tax system is "unfinished work" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:02">[00:41:02]</a>.
*   **Road User Charging**: Comprehensive road user charging was recommended to replace fuel excise, motor vehicle registration, driver's license fees, and stamp duties on vehicles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:36">[00:43:36]</a>. This would allow for charges based on vehicle weight, distance, location (congestion), and carbon emissions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:00">[00:44:00]</a>. Despite support from motoring and trucking groups, this reform remains undone <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:44">[00:44:44]</a>.
*   **Mining Tax**: While not a change in recommendation, the failure to sell the mining tax strongly to the public was regrettable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:05">[00:42:05]</a>. The argument against it, that it would "kill the goose that's laying the golden eggs," was economically "stupid" as the mining boom caused "crowding out" and damaged other trade-exposed sectors through a 70% real exchange rate appreciation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:37">[00:46:37]</a>.

### Fiscal Discipline and Intergenerational Equity
The Charter of Budget Honesty Act was developed in the 1980s and 1990s to instill fiscal discipline through principles, rules, and transparency guarantees <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:55">[00:50:55]</a>. However, it has been "trashed by both sides of politics for a long time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:32">[00:51:32]</a>. This lack of commitment to fiscal discipline creates a negative tail risk for Australia's credit rating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:00">[00:52:00]</a>.

A priority for tax reform should be personal income tax, specifically indexing personal income tax scales <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:16">[00:53:16]</a>. The current reliance on fiscal drag (where nominal wage increases push workers into higher tax brackets, reducing real income and increasing average tax rates) is undermining social cohesion, particularly [[challenges_of_modern_society_and_governance | intergenerational harmony]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:50">[00:53:50]</a>. The preference for taxing capital income less favorably than labor income, combined with very low capital gains tax, means younger generations feel "robbed" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:02">[00:55:02]</a>. This is exacerbated by housing affordability issues and increasing higher education debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:09">[00:59:09]</a>. The distribution of tax bases between the Commonwealth and states is a significant issue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:04:40">[02:04:40]</a>. There is a strong case for a review of Commonwealth-State roles and responsibilities, starting with expenditure, to improve outcomes for citizens and resolve issues like overlapping complexities seen during the COVID-19 pandemic regarding aged care responsibilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:05:00">[02:05:00]</a>. Many of the most "egregious" or "fragile" taxes (e.g., stamp duties on property transfers, taxes on insurance) are at the state level <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:07:13">[02:07:13]</a>.

## Population and Housing Policy
Australia's reliance on a high population growth strategy through net migration, seen as "kicking the can down the road" to address stagnant productivity and rising dependency ratios, has led to second-order consequences, particularly for housing affordability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:05">[01:02:05]</a>.

Historically, a sustainable population level for Australia was estimated around 15 million people, though policies could theoretically support 50 million with creative planning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:17">[01:03:17]</a>. The current policy approach, concentrating growth within existing major cities (Sydney and Melbourne grew by 150,000 each in one year), is unsustainable and leads to congestion and environmental degradation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:39">[01:07:39]</a>. The "optimal city size" literature suggests around 150,000-200,000 people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:30">[02:10:30]</a>.

Instead of focusing solely on the number of immigrants, a "spatial lens" is needed to consider where people will live and what skills they bring <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:48">[01:59:48]</a>. This could involve building new cities, like the example of Port Macquarie which grew rapidly after the construction of a new hospital, attracting service industries and professionals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:16">[01:11:16]</a>. Such initiatives require asking "why would anybody want to live here and who would want to live here?" to attract people and businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:27">[01:14:27]</a>.

Regarding fertility, while the baby bonus policy seemed to temporarily boost birth rates, "you can pay people to have babies" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:27">[01:20:27]</a>. However, achieving a total fertility rate above replacement level (around 2.1) would require significantly more support, including comprehensive childcare, to make raising families attractive for dual-income households <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:19:32">[01:19:32]</a>. The optimal rate of population growth is the replacement rate, as it minimizes the total dependency ratio <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:51">[01:15:51]</a>.

## The End of the Reform Era

The era of ambitious economic reforms in Australia (roughly 1983-2000) was followed by 25 years of "comparatively lackluster reform" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:34">[01:38:34]</a>. This can be attributed to several factors:
*   **Complacency**: Driven by the "mining myth," a narrative that Australia's prosperity is solely underpinned by mining <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:56">[01:43:56]</a>. The terms of trade rising significantly since 2002 fostered this complacency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:44:28">[01:44:28]</a>.
*   **Political Strategy**: The "small target strategy," where political parties offer minimal policy proposals to avoid controversy, combined with the belief that a government lacks a mandate to implement policies not put to the electorate pre-election, results in "nothing" being done once in power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:19">[01:27:19]</a>.
*   **Lack of Political Courage and Ambition**: Modern politicians often lack the personal ambition to tackle "hard stuff" and create an impressive legacy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:49:02">[01:49:02]</a>. Past leaders like Paul Keating and John Howard pursued difficult reforms (e.g., the GST) to demonstrate their leadership capabilities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:13">[01:45:13]</a>.
*   **Absence of a "Burning Platform"**: Political leaders can create a sense of urgency, as seen with Keating's "Banana Republic" statement in 1986, which spurred microeconomic reforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:01:16">[02:01:16]</a>. Such a "burning platform" narrative is deemed "absolutely indispensable" for sustained economic reform, as it motivates people to change from a state of complacency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:02:07">[02:02:07]</a>.

## The Role of the Public Service

The public service has seen significant changes, with the decline of roles like typist pools due to technological advancements like Microsoft Word and Excel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:55">[00:02:55]</a>. In the early 1980s, common tasks included using punch cards for computing, a process that took days to debug <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>. The introduction of Lotus 1-2-3 and later Excel dramatically sped up modeling and analysis, becoming instrumental in complex policy work like the development of the consumption tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:45">[00:46:45]</a>.

The internet's introduction to government was initially restricted for security reasons <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:13">[01:11:13]</a>. While the internet is a "greatest invention of humanity" for its access to knowledge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:40">[01:11:40]</a>, it changed the dynamic between ministers and the public service. Ministers now have immediate access to information, potentially challenging the public service's advice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:25">[01:22:25]</a>.

### Impact of [[AI applications in economic policy making | AI]] on Governance and Public Service
The potential for highly powerful AI systems, even those short of artificial general intelligence (AGI), raises questions about the future of governance and the role of ministers and the public service.
*   **Displacement of Human Roles**: It's speculated that AI could remove the need for judiciary and potentially politicians in the future <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:17:02">[01:17:02]</a>. If AI can perform most cognitive tasks, the public service could be significantly automated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:31:59">[01:31:59]</a>. This is a profound shift from previous technological developments that augmented human productivity without displacing them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:32:26">[01:32:26]</a>.
*   **Decision-Making Quality**: While AI could produce "impeccably researched briefs in days or even hours," increasing speed might not improve policy quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:16">[01:16:16]</a>. Instead, it might make politicians "even more cautious" because "the more they know the less courageous they are" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:39">[01:22:39]</a>.
*   **Human Accountability and Trust**: Ministers will likely still prefer to go via the public service for human accountability and trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:56">[01:23:56]</a>. They want advisors they can criticize or explain their political realities to, and they don't want AI to "second-guess" or tailor advice based on private thoughts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:55">[01:24:55]</a>.
*   **Ethical and Moral Responsibility**: The primary responsibility of human leaders in an AI-dominated world would shift from cognitive tasks to "morality and ethics" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:01">[02:11:01]</a>. AI cannot be trusted with irrationality or randomness like humans, nor can it bear the full responsibility for policy decisions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:40">[02:11:40]</a>.
*   **Distribution of Income**: A key challenge with advanced AI is the potential for the production process to no longer offer a secure or even insecure form of income for most people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:37">[01:36:37]</a>. This raises questions about how citizens will derive income to spend, and whether existing tax systems can effectively redistribute wealth from highly profitable AI-driven industries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:20">[01:37:20]</a>.
*   **Preserving Governance Structures**: The potential for AI to displace human roles underscores the importance of preserving human checks and balances and the separation of powers in governance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:10">[02:00:10]</a>. Decentralization of advice and diverse human perspectives remain crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:44">[02:00:44]</a>.

## Qualities of an Effective Senior Public Servant
To be an effective senior public servant, one must be:
*   **Trusted**: Building and retaining trust is paramount <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:13:01">[02:13:01]</a>.
*   **Technically Expert**: This is indispensable for commanding trust, especially when advising political leaders on complex issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:06">[02:14:06]</a>.
*   **Collaborative**: Ability to work effectively with colleagues in other government agencies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:30">[02:14:30]</a>. The Treasury, for example, aimed to be seen as an agency that "helped" other departments with policy development, rather than merely saying "no" to spending proposals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:15:47">[02:15:47]</a>.
*   **Team Leader**: Capable of creating highly effective, motivated teams and making "unrealistic demands" while ensuring they are on the journey with you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:15:55">[02:15:55]</a>.
*   **Comfortable with Ambiguity**: Essential for navigating complex policy environments, as losing comfort with ambiguity can lead to burnout <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:33">[02:16:33]</a>.
