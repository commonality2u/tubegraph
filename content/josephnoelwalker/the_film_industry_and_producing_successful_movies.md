The film industry, particularly in the realm of distribution and production, faces inherent challenges due to the unpredictable nature of what resonates with audiences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:44">[00:06:44]</a>. Mark Cuban, through his ownership of Magnolia Pictures, a film distribution company, has first-hand experience with these challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:06">[00:06:06]</a>. The core challenge for Magnolia Pictures is to earn more at the box office than it costs to bring people to theaters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:10">[00:06:10]</a>.

## The Unpredictability of a Hit

Veteran Hollywood screenwriter William Goldman famously stated that "nobody knows anything" about what will or will not work at the box office <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:02">[00:07:02]</a>. He noted that films like *Raiders of the Lost Ark* and *Star Wars*, which became massive successes, were initially turned down by multiple studios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>.

Mark Cuban affirms this perspective, emphasizing that systematically producing hits is difficult <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>. He explains that a movie's success depends on numerous interconnected elements beyond just the script, including:
*   **Casting** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:51">[00:07:51]</a>: The right lead actor, like Harrison Ford for *Raiders of the Lost Ark*, is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>.
*   **Direction** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:59">[00:07:59]</a>: The director, such as Steven Spielberg, plays a significant role <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.
*   **Production** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>: The producer and director are vital components <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.
*   **Timing** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:14">[00:08:14]</a>: The release timing, considering what else is happening in the world and audience mood (e.g., wanting to escape or wanting seriousness), impacts reception <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:14">[00:08:14]</a>.

> [!quote] Mark Cuban
> "Systematically it's hard and it's not even so much about picking a good script it's you just don't know how all the pieces are going to work together... it's like anything else luck is always more important" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:43">[00:07:43]</a>

Cuban highlights that [[evaluating_success_and_failure_rates | luck]] plays an important role in people's lives, a rare trait in a successful [[business_strategies_and_innovations | businessman]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:56">[00:01:56]</a>.

### Mark Cuban's Experience with Film Production

In 2003, Mark Cuban and his partner Todd Wagner created HDNet Films and 2929 Pictures with the vision of vertical integration: producing movies through their companies (HDNet and Magnolia Pictures) and distributing them via Landmark Theaters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:33">[00:08:33]</a>.

Cuban shares two initial successes:
*   **Enron: The Smartest Guys in the Room** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>: Cuban greenlit this documentary in just 12 minutes based on an email from Alex Gibney, who had exclusive video footage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:11">[00:09:11]</a>. Costing $770,000 to produce, it became one of the top-grossing documentaries at the time and was nominated for an Academy Award <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>.
*   **Good Night, and Good Luck** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:48">[00:09:48]</a>: This black-and-white film, starring George Clooney, was picked up by his partner Todd Wagner, with their share of the production budget being $4 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:41">[00:09:41]</a>. It received six Academy Award nominations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>.

After these initial successes, leading to seven Academy Award nominations across two films, Cuban felt "this is easy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:06">[00:10:06]</a>. However, he quickly learned that the film industry is "really hard to know and do," as subsequent projects "went straight into the sh_t" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:28">[00:10:28]</a>. Bethany McLean, former guest of The Jolly Swagman podcast, praised Cuban as an excellent executive producer for *Enron: The Smartest Guys in the Room* <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:57">[00:11:57]</a>.

## Innovation and Audience Demand

Similar to [[Innovation and Technology Development | innovation]] in other industries, customers often don't know what they want until they see it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:40">[00:10:40]</a>. Cuban draws parallels to the early days of AudioNet (which became broadcast.com), where people questioned why they needed streaming when they had radios <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:31">[00:11:31]</a>. He emphasizes that if you just give people what they want, society might still be "on horses" or "listening to records" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>.

## The Future of Content Distribution: Blockchain and NFTs

Mark Cuban believes that the future of content distribution, including film, will be profoundly impacted by blockchain technology and non-fungible tokens (NFTs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:32">[00:21:32]</a>. He envisions a future where digital content like music and podcasts can be "minted" on the blockchain, available for free or purchase using tokens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:10">[00:21:10]</a>.

> [!info] Blockchain and Creator Royalties
> A "game-changing" aspect of NFTs is the ability for original creators to receive a percentage of every subsequent sale of their digital work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:01">[00:23:01]</a>. Cuban experienced this personally by listing artwork on rarable.com, earning 10% royalties every time his pieces were resold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:27">[00:23:27]</a>.

This model allows digital assets to be sold and resold indefinitely, with the original creator continuously earning money if the content is good <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:35">[00:25:35]</a>. This is seen as a significant shift for content producers, including musicians, filmmakers, and photographers, providing a new way to monetize their work beyond traditional advertising or sponsorship models <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:40">[00:25:40]</a>.

Cuban attributes the accelerated development and exploration of blockchain applications to the 2020 pandemic, as many people stayed home, received stimulus checks, and invested in crypto, leading them to learn about decentralized finance and the broader blockchain ecosystem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:42">[00:21:42]</a>. He notes that for Gen Z, digital assets (like pictures, music, and videos) are among their most valuable possessions, making the concept of easily buying and selling these things natural <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:25">[00:26:25]</a>. This new paradigm could fundamentally change the [[investment_and_future_of_technology | investment and future of technology]] in the content industry.
