## The Future of Work: Transformation vs. Minimal Impact

There are conflicting views on the [[Impact of AI on job displacement | future of work]]: some believe [[AI and the future of work | AI]] will revolutionize every job, while others expect minimal impact <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:38">[00:46:38]</a>. Both perspectives can be true, as seen with the internet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:47">[00:46:47]</a>.

The internet transformed nearly every cognitive task, yet its [[Impact of AI on labor markets and global productivity | impact on GDP]] has been minimal, leading to the saying "the computer revolution shows up everywhere except in the productivity statistics" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:09">[00:47:09]</a>. This is because eliminating some bottlenecks in workflows often introduces new ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:29">[00:47:29]</a>. The way things are done is different, but job categories largely remain the same <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:34">[00:47:34]</a>. A similar dynamic might unfold with [[AI and the future of work | AI]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:48">[00:47:48]</a>.

The Industrial Revolution, by contrast, radically transformed the nature of work, shifting from manual labor to what is now considered work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:50">[00:47:50]</a>. As many cognitive tasks become automated by [[AI and the future of work | AI]], the definition of "work" could evolve to primarily involve [[AI and the future of work | AI]] control, alignment, and safety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:20">[00:48:20]</a>.
