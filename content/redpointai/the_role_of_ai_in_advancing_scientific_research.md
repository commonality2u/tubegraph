## Current Capabilities and Future Potential

Initially, there was skepticism about how quickly AI would reach "super intelligence" or be able to handle complex tasks, as early models struggled with basic reasoning problems like Tic-Tac-Toe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:07">[00:07:07]</a>. However, the rapid progress in scaling inference compute, also known as test-time compute, has changed this perspective significantly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:09">[00:08:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:04">[00:22:04]</a>.

One of the most exciting applications is seeing these models advance scientific research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:10">[00:42:10]</a>. While models have been broadly capable, they are increasingly starting to surpass expert humans in specific domains <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:26">[00:42:26]</a>. This opens up the possibility for AI to advance the frontier of human knowledge, acting as a partner rather than a replacement for researchers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:49">[00:42:49]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:51">[00:42:51]</a>.

## Applications in Social Sciences and Beyond

AI models, especially those trained on vast amounts of human data, are well-suited for social science experiments and even neuroscience research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:09">[00:36:09]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:13">[00:36:13]</a>.

A key advantage is their scalability and cost-effectiveness compared to hiring human subjects for experiments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:24">[00:36:24]</a>. This allows for research that might otherwise be prohibitively expensive <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:24">[00:39:24]</a>.

### AI in Game Theory Experiments

An example of AI's utility in social science research is replicating game theory experiments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:01">[00:37:01]</a>. Historically, these involved hiring undergraduates for small payments to study their rational behavior, response to incentives, or even desire for revenge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:06">[00:37:06]</a>.

A specific example is the "ultimatum game" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:01">[00:38:01]</a>:
*   Player A offers a percentage of money to Player B <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:10">[00:38:10]</a>.
*   Player B accepts or rejects; if rejected, neither player gets anything <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:14">[00:38:14]</a>.
*   Humans typically reject offers below 30% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:31">[00:38:31]</a>.
*   Investigating how this behavior changes with much larger sums (e.g., $10,000) is cost-prohibitive with human subjects, often requiring experiments in impoverished communities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:52">[00:38:52]</a>. AI models could provide insights into how people might react in such situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:21">[00:39:21]</a>.

The ability of AI models to closely match human behavior in these quantifiable settings improves as their capabilities advance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:00">[00:40:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:08">[00:40:08]</a>.

## Milestones and Future Directions

A significant milestone for future models is becoming more [[advancements_and_implications_of_ai_agents | agentic]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:51">[00:23:51]</a>. Current models like GPT-40 are often too brittle for long-horizon tasks requiring many intermediate steps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:16">[00:24:16]</a>. While prompting can help, it's often fragile and not general enough <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:33">[00:24:33]</a>.

The development of o1 serves as a proof of concept that models can independently figure out and tackle intermediate steps for hard problems, a capability far beyond what previous models could achieve without extensive prompting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:42">[00:24:42]</a>. This points to a future where AI agents can autonomously solve complex, multi-step problems.

### Overcoming Limitations

The "Bitter Lesson" essay by Richard Sutton, a creator of the RL field, suggests that techniques scaling well with more compute and data ultimately succeed over human-coded domain knowledge or scaffolding <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:05">[00:26:05]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:30">[00:26:30]</a>. While current builders often use orchestration and scaffolding to overcome model limitations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:25">[00:25:25]</a>, it's expected that these will eventually become unnecessary as models improve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:22">[00:27:22]</a>. The rapid pace of AI progress means that models might soon perform tasks out-of-the-box that previously required significant custom engineering <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:02">[00:28:02]</a>.
