## Information Control and Censorship
An "information iron curtain is coming down" in Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:35">[00:01:35]</a>. Alternative media outlets have "practically ceased to exist" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38]">[00:01:38]</a>, with many forced to close due to the inability to speak freely <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:55]">[00:01:55]</a>. This includes publications like "Ural building sign" and radio stations like "Silver Rain," which has stopped producing news and politically relevant content <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00]">[00:02:00]</a>. The current media landscape painfully reminds some of the Soviet Union, with only a few dominant channels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18]">[00:02:18]</a>.

YouTube is also under threat of closure, despite its popularity for non-political content and as a source of income for many <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:46]">[00:02:46]</a>. Monetization for Russian users has already been sharply reduced <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:42]">[00:03:42]</a>. The authorities may be hesitant to close YouTube due to its large, non-politicized audience, fearing their potential politicization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50]">[00:03:50]</a>.

A new law on "military centuries" was adopted by the State Duma on a Friday, expected to be signed by the president the next day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:14]">[00:04:14]</a>. Under this law, any phrase or information about the events in Ukraine can be potentially regarded as false or discrediting if desired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:27]">[00:04:27]</a>. New amendments to the administrative code are also being prepared, which may prohibit even the term "anti-war movement" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:41]">[00:04:41]</a>.

## Military Campaign in Ukraine
The military campaign in Ukraine, referred to by Russian propaganda as a "military special operation," continues with its stated goals preserved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:07]">[00:05:07]</a>. According to a conversation between President Putin and French President Macron, the "worst and the hardest is yet to come" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30]">[00:05:30]</a>.

The objective is to take Kharkiv and Kyiv, which requires squeezing out the population first <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37]">[00:05:37]</a>. Humanitarian corridors will be kept open, and pressure will be built up to encourage residents to leave these major cities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:47]">[00:05:47]</a>. The experience of the two Chechen campaigns is recalled as a reference point <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21]">[00:06:21]</a>. The aim is to force Ukraine to sign capitulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:27]">[00:06:27]</a>. If this does not happen, there is an apparent intention to remove Zelensky <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36]">[00:06:36]</a>. The *Times* newspaper reported three failed assassination attempts on Zelensky within the first week of military actions, suggesting his security services were informed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:40]">[00:06:40]</a>.

## Potential Mobilization and Martial Law
There are "creeping spreading rumors" about mass mobilization and the possible introduction of martial law <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28]">[00:07:28]</a>. While Russian presidential press secretary Peskov denies these rumors, his past statements (e.g., denying military actions against Ukraine) suggest his assurances may be unreliable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:39]">[00:07:39]</a>.

### Arguments for Mobilization
From a logical perspective, several factors support the idea of mobilization:
*   **Deployed Forces** Russia has already deployed 90% of its armed forces assembled for the special operation, but military tasks remain unsolved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:25]">[00:08:25]</a>.
*   **Unemployment Solution** Mobilization could address rising unemployment, for example, from factory closures like the Volkswagen plant in Nizhny Novgorod, affecting 5,000 workers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49]">[00:08:49]</a>. These individuals could find a place in the active army, sign contracts, and receive "decent money" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10]">[00:09:10]</a>.
*   **Expanded Scope** If the conflict extends beyond Ukraine, as previous statements suggest, a larger army would be needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:31]">[00:09:31]</a>.

### Arguments Against Mobilization
The main factor against mobilization is the "possibility of political discontent," specifically "mass political discontent," which authorities want to avoid <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58]">[00:09:58]</a>. If mobilization occurs, especially on a significant scale (potentially half a million people), it would likely coincide with the introduction of martial law, either full or partial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:10]">[00:10:10]</a>.

### Martial Law Implications
Even elements of martial law are already present, such as censorship, which is de facto martial law without legal justification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43]">[00:10:43]</a>. If martial law is introduced in some regions, military authorities gain the right to seize private property and equipment for their needs, massively violating the right to private property <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:02]">[00:11:02]</a>.

Pro-government sociological agencies (FLOP and VTsIOM) claim 70% support for the Kremlin's operation in Ukraine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51]">[00:11:51]</a>. The Kremlin interprets this as a "carte blanche" for its actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:30]">[00:12:30]</a>. However, the speaker expresses doubt about these figures, suggesting the reality is "far from being so rosy" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:11]">[00:12:11]</a>.

## Threat of Nuclear Weapons and Economic War
The world is described as being in a situation "close to the Cuban Missile Crisis or even repeating the Cuban Missile Crisis" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:49]">[00:12:49]</a>. Russian officials previously stated that economic sanctions would be perceived as a declaration of war <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:03]">[00:13:03]</a>. The West is now openly waging an "economic war" against the Russian Federation, inflicting "extremely prolonged blows" on the Russian economy and society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:15]">[00:13:15]</a>. The Russian economy is acknowledged as very weak and highly dependent on external relations, particularly with Europe and the United States <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35]">[00:13:35]</a>.

If economic pressure from the West increases, which it is expected to do as military operations continue, it will have "very serious socio-political consequences," including the possibility of mass protests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:09]">[00:14:09]</a>. The Kremlin's perceived solution is to "threaten with nuclear weapons" and, if necessary, "demonstrate these nuclear weapons on a limited scale" (tactical nuclear weapons) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:25]">[00:14:25]</a>. This nuclear blackmail is believed to be a way to achieve "significant concessions" from the West, beyond what they were previously willing to offer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:48]">[00:14:48]</a>. The speaker recalls Putin's phrase: "They will all die and we will go to heaven" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:09]">[00:15:09]</a>, implying the ruling elite is ready to sacrifice the population.

American partners are taking the threat of nuclear strikes "extremely serious," having canceled training firings of intercontinental ballistic missiles to avoid provocation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:28]">[00:16:28]</a>. A recent US Congress briefing by intelligence services discussed the mental state of the Russian President <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:47]">[00:16:47]</a>.
