## Information Suppression and its Effects
Governments in authoritarian regimes often attempt to suppress information through refutations, lies, and denials <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:23:23">[03:23:23]</a>. However, this strategy can backfire, as increased refutations and lies on a particular topic can actually accelerate the spread of the very information they wish to suppress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:26:26">[03:26:26]</a>. This process draws new groups into the discussion, causing the news to grow like a snowball <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:35:35">[03:35:35]</a>. An "experienced PR person" would advise governments to contain and ignore such information rather than giving it attention through denial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:46">[03:46:46]</a>. However, governments often make mistakes in this regard <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:56:56">[03:56:56]</a>.

Even after the reported death of a leader, attempts are made to maintain the status quo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:17:17">[05:17:17]</a>. This implies that there will be no increase in [[societal_changes_and_challenges_in_postdictatorship_russia | liberties and freedoms]] or de-escalation of military tensions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:20:20">[05:20:20]</a>. Instead, there might be further [[repression_and_political_arrests_in_russia | tightening of controls]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37:37">[05:37:37]</a>, driven by the government's fear of [[cultural_and_societal_repression_within_russia | public reaction]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:40:40">[05:40:40]</a>. This initiates a process that cannot be stopped <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:11:11">[07:11:11]</a>.
