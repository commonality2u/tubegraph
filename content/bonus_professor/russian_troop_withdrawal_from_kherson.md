## Strategic Implications and Consequences

This withdrawal signifies a [[strategic_turning_point_in_ukraine_conflict | strategic turning point in Ukraine conflict]] that began around August-September, which has now been consolidated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. The strategic initiative in the armed confrontation has definitively passed to Ukraine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>.

While not considered a military catastrophe, the loss of Kherson is characterized as a "moral and psychological and political catastrophe" for the Russian leadership and elite <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:26">[00:02:26]</a>. This is because faith in victory has been lost; the Russian establishment now faces the inevitability of military defeat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:40">[00:02:40]</a>. In the eyes of the establishment, Putin has transformed into a "toxic loser," with everything he touches turning into its opposite of gold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>.

Consequences of this moral and political catastrophe include:
*   Confusion within the propaganda machine and political headquarters, which are at a loss for how to respond <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:26">[00:03:26]</a>. This confusion is expected to be overcome, likely through tightened censorship <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:35">[00:03:35]</a>.
*   An intensification of the search for a way out of the impasse created by the Kremlin's actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:01">[00:04:01]</a>.
*   The recognition of Putin as the primary cause of this catastrophe, which is seen as not only moral and political but also partly global <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:12">[00:04:12]</a>.
*   A noticeable increase in elite discontent, posing a fundamental threat to Putin's power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>.

## Peace Proposals

Immediately following the withdrawal from Kherson, the Russian leadership received a peace proposal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>. The outline of these proposals, agreed upon with Ukraine, was transferred by a "more than serious" side <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:13">[00:05:13]</a>.

Key points of the proposed global agreements include:
*   **Russian Withdrawal:** Russia would completely leave Ukrainian territory, including the DPR and LPR, with the exception of Crimea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>.
*   **Crimea Status:** Crimea would receive an indefinite status for seven years, during which negotiations would occur <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:52">[00:05:52]</a>. Crimea would also be demilitarized, and the Black Sea Fleet would have to leave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:00">[00:06:00]</a>.
*   **Demilitarized Zone:** A 100-kilometer deep demilitarized zone would be established along the Russian and Belarusian borders with Ukraine, with heavy weapons removed from Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:16">[00:06:16]</a>.
*   **Ukraine's Commitments:** Ukraine would promise not to join NATO or raise questions about joining for seven years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:23">[00:06:23]</a>.
*   **Transnistria:** The Russian military base in Transnistria would be moved to Russia, and Russia would not interfere with Transnistria's integration into Moldova in 2023 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:34">[00:06:34]</a>.
*   **Immunity:** Russian civilians, military personnel, and political figures would not be subject to punishment, guaranteeing their freedom and security, and allowing them to preserve some assets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:00">[00:07:00]</a>. Scapegoats, however, are expected to be punished <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>.
*   **Reparations/Compensation:** Financial compensation for damages to Ukraine is not mentioned in the received materials but should be discussed during negotiations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>. Russia would not use the term "reparations" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:29">[00:10:29]</a>.

Indirect signs that the Russian leadership received and took these proposals seriously include:
*   The collapse of plans for tactical nuclear weapons use on Friday (November 11) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>.
*   Turkish President Erdogan's announcement of his intention to meet with Putin to discuss peace prospects with Ukraine, followed by a visit or call to Zelensky <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>.
*   A potential third sign would be Russia abandoning the idea of inflicting another massive blow to Ukraine's energy infrastructure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.

Accepting these proposals doesn't mean the Kremlin is ready to accept them fully, but rather that it is ready to consider them, potentially to gain time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:12">[00:09:12]</a>.

## Attribution of Blame in Russia

In the aftermath of the withdrawal, a search for those to blame will begin. Initially, "Turbo Patriots" – those who were most vocal in their support and who now criticize the authorities – will be targeted for repression and intimidation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:02">[00:18:02]</a>. Figures like Igor Strelkov and Aleksandr Dugin are mentioned, with Dugin having been subtly warned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:13">[00:18:13]</a>.

Military figures, however, will not be blamed immediately, but rather "later," specifically after a peace treaty is signed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:34">[00:17:34]</a>. Censorship is also expected to tighten <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:09">[00:19:09]</a>. The government might create a "false political target" in the form of a new political party, potentially led by figures like Strelkov and Dugin, to divert discontent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:24">[00:19:24]</a>.
