Historically, a major politician's legacy is judged by their ending, with a successful ending painting all previous activities in positive tones, and an unsuccessful one leading to negative assessments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:36:00">[02:36:00]</a>.

### Early Putin Era (2000s)

Putin's early years as president were characterized by highly favorable conditions that no other Russian politician had previously enjoyed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>:
*   **High Oil Prices** High oil prices provided significant revenue <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:40:00">[03:40:00]</a>.
*   **Absence of External Enemies** Russia faced no major external enemies, and relations with the West were generally benevolent, allowing access to loans and technology until 2014 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:42:00">[03:42:00]</a>. This technology was even used to rearm the armed forces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:03:00">[04:03:00]</a>.
*   **Broad Support** Putin initially received widespread support from both ordinary citizens and nearly all elite groups, receiving a "huge credit of trust" after the Yeltsin era <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:12:00">[04:12:00]</a>.

During this period, Russia's [[economic_conditions_in_russia | economy]] experienced significant growth, with personal incomes rising <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>. This led to a tangible increase in prosperity, allowing people to afford new iPhones, travel to foreign resorts like France and Spain, buy apartments with mortgages, and build country houses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:50:00">[04:50:00]</a>. While regions outside Moscow continued to degrade, the overall standard of living improved significantly compared to the 1990s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:21:00">[05:21:00]</a>. This improvement in living standards meant that the populace largely overlooked the gradual restriction of political freedoms and non-competitive elections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:06:00">[06:06:00]</a>.

Initially, Putin's administration implemented liberal economic policies, including a package of liberal laws and a government with liberal economic views, which was a "forced necessity" given lower oil prices at the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:56:00">[14:56:00]</a>. However, as oil prices rose, the focus shifted. Decisions were made to abandon domestic industrial development (except for defense), relying instead on buying everything from abroad, as oil revenue was deemed sufficient for social programs and military rearmament <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:03:00">[16:03:00]</a>. This strategy adopted a model similar to resource-exporting countries like Australia or Norway <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:50:00">[16:50:00]</a>.

### Later Putin Era and Current Challenges

A decline in Putin's popularity began around 2011-2012, fueled by public perception of a "deception" regarding the "castling" with Dmitry Medvedev <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:36:00">[06:36:00]</a>. People also experienced psychological fatigue from prolonged rule, a phenomenon typically seen after 8 to 12 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:06:00">[07:06:00]</a>.

Since 2014, Russia has experienced a [[economic_challenges_in_russia_and_governmental_strategies | crisis]] with declining incomes for six consecutive years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:29:00">[08:29:00]</a>. The government's inability to cope with this crisis, coupled with unpopular decisions like the pension reform, has caused "colossal irritation" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:55:00">[08:55:00]</a>. This reform was perceived as a mockery, as many Russian men do not live to 60 years old <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:36:00">[09:36:00]</a>.

The annexation of Crimea in 2014 provided a "colossal rise in popularity" for Putin, but this was temporary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:55:00">[09:55:00]</a>. By the time of the transcript, Russia had "returned to the eleventh year" (2011) in terms of declining ratings, but with the added burden of significant negative public experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:08:00">[10:08:00]</a>.

#### Missed Opportunities and Corruption

From a historical perspective, Putin is viewed as having missed a unique chance for Russia's rapid development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:31:00">[10:31:00]</a>. Instead of fostering national welfare, resources were directed towards enriching a small group of friends and financing "cheap, incomprehensible projects" like the Olympic Games <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:49:00">[10:49:00]</a>. The Sochi Olympics were the most expensive in history, while many Russians still live in monstrous housing conditions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:57:00">[10:57:00]</a>.

It is estimated that a tenth of the money Russia earned from raw material sales could have provided all citizens with normal, quality housing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:00:00">[19:00:00]</a>. Instead, vast sums were lost, taken to offshore accounts, and spent on unproductive or "completely stupid" projects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:19:00">[19:19:00]</a>. This squandering of resources has led to the situation where people in Russia still "can't eat their fill," and low-quality provisions are sold in stores, even in Moscow <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:49:00">[19:49:00]</a>.

The abundance of "oil money" is believed to have corrupted both the elite and parts of the populace, leading to complacency and a lack of incentive for innovation and development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:09:00">[20:09:00]</a>. The elite, often former security officers or associates from Putin's St. Petersburg days, saw their sudden wealth as a privilege of their position rather than something earned through competition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:08:00">[21:08:00]</a>. They are perceived as treating ordinary people like serfs or "lower class creatures" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:04:00">[22:04:00]</a>.

#### Governmental Inefficiency and Public Discontent

The state system is seen as inefficient, neglecting critical issues like the nationwide garbage problem despite allocated funds for solutions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:14:00">[31:14:00]</a>. There is a "phantasmagoric picture" where money is spent on superficial improvements in Moscow, such as laying tiles and repainting, while other cities and villages are neglected, leading to widespread insult among the population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="32:30:00">[32:30:00]</a>.

There is a growing belief that the government deliberately antagonizes society. An example is the restriction of flights to Georgia, which was perceived as an insult to citizens who had enjoyed visiting the country <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:31:00">[33:31:00]</a>. The authorities are seen as treating citizens like ignorant children, deciding where they can and cannot go <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:59:00">[33:59:00]</a>. This approach of taking taxes and controlling people's lives while failing to address core needs creates an "amazing talent" for the government to create enemies for itself <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:26:00">[34:26:00]</a>.

#### Putin's Isolation and Deformed Personality

Putin is believed to operate within an "information cocoon" where he is shielded from public criticism and surrounded by those who praise him as a genius <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:27:00">[13:27:00]</a>. This prolonged isolation at the "top of power" (almost 20 years) has led to a "deformation of the personality" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:51:00">[12:51:00]</a>. He holds a messianic complex, believing everything he has done was "absolutely right" and could not have been otherwise <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:13:00">[14:13:00]</a>. This conviction is seen as a sign of his detachment from reality, as evidenced by his apparent unawareness of ordinary people's struggles during direct lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:35:00">[13:35:00]</a>. He is also described as being afraid to make decisions during crises, hiding behind others or disappearing, fearful of facing victims of attacks or disasters <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:35:00">[22:35:00]</a>.

### Future Outlook and Recommendations

The current [[political_changes_in_russia_post_putin | political and economic landscape of Russia]] is described as "too unstable" and "turbulent" for a smooth transition of power <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:13:00">[25:13:00]</a>. Discontent is growing, and there are too many problems without clear solutions, with the state seemingly unwilling to address them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:24:00">[25:24:00]</a>. The state is "ceasing to perform its functions," de facto being eliminated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:43:00">[25:43:00]</a>.

A future transfer of power is anticipated to be "very risky" and unlikely to proceed as planned by the Kremlin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:01:00">[26:01:00]</a>. While models like Kazakhstan's leadership transition are observed, Russia's deep contradictions make a smooth shift improbable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:36:00">[26:36:00]</a>. Unlike Yeltsin's smooth transfer of power, which was facilitated by the resolution of accumulated post-90s problems and the "oil needle," current contradictions are deeper and unresolved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:39:00">[30:39:00]</a>.

Grassroots pressure in the form of mass protests is considered the most likely catalyst for change, forcing the authorities to consider "self-transformation" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:55:00">[27:55:00]</a>. Recent instances where authorities in Yekaterinburg and Moscow retreated in the face of public pressure demonstrate this potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:30:00">[28:30:00]</a>.

#### Potential Political Forces

No single force is expected to rule Russia after the current regime <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:07:00">[35:07:00]</a>. A broad coalition, possibly including liberals, leftists, nationalists, and social democrats, will likely be necessary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:17:00">[35:17:00]</a>. Liberals are seen as having the greatest chances to emerge, not due to popularity, but because they control significant information resources, media, and hold key positions in ministries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:02:00">[36:02:00]</a>. To gain broad support, liberals would need to adopt "social rhetoric" and emphasize "justice," particularly regarding equality before the law and respect for human dignity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="36:46:00">[36:46:00]</a>.

Leftist movements are deemed unlikely to achieve significant power, as Russia's society is seen as highly individualistic rather than embracing solidarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:43:00">[37:43:00]</a>. While social democratic parties might find a place in a reformed parliamentary system, radical left movements would likely remain niche <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:17:00">[41:17:00]</a>.

Ultimately, at the helm of the state, there should be "idealists" – people whose motivation extends beyond material gain, genuinely wanting to do good for the people and the country <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="44:41:00">[44:41:00]</a>. The state should serve society, not the other way around <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="52:08:00">[52:08:00]</a>.

#### Essential Reforms

To avoid further societal breakdown, Russia needs immediate and long-term reforms:
*   **Freedom for Small and Medium Businesses:** This can be achieved quickly by removing restrictions and canceling certain laws <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="49:50:00">[49:50:00]</a>.
*   **Complete Reform of the Judicial and Legal System:** This is critical because "where there is no court...there can be no justice" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:02:00">[50:02:00]</a>. A "total illustration" (vetting) of the judicial corps may be necessary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:03:00">[51:03:00]</a>.
*   **Official Travel Restrictions:** To prevent officials from leaving the country while reforms are implemented, all officials and their family members should be barred from leaving Russia for an indefinite period, except for official delegations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:37:00">[50:37:00]</a>.
*   **Fair Redistribution of Income:** Regions should retain a much larger share of their income to address the dangerous "disproportions" between Moscow and the rest of Russia, which threaten national unity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="48:12:00">[48:12:00]</a>.

Longer-term reforms in healthcare and education would require 10 to 15 years to implement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="50:16:00">[50:16:00]</a>. The most important goal is for these changes to occur peacefully, without civil war or extreme violence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="51:31:00">[51:31:00]</a>.
