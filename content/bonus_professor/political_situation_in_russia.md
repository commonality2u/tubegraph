The political landscape in Russia is characterized by several complex dynamics, including questions of leadership legitimacy, the pervasive influence of state propaganda, internal power struggles among elites, and a public that often appears apathetic or resistant to significant change.

## Presidential Legitimacy and Elections

The current Russian presidency is described as illegitimate. The speaker contends that the individual who "won" the recent presidential elections was actually a "double" of the late President Putin, who allegedly died on October 26 of the previous year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:42:47">[01:42:47]</a>. This means the elections were "initially illegal by definition" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:47">[02:20:47]</a>. Furthermore, even in this purportedly illegal process, the double did not secure more than 50% of the votes according to "Hamburg," with all official numbers being "lies and falsification" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:20:47">[02:20:47]</a>.

Ukrainian intelligence, specifically its head Budanov, was reportedly aware of Putin's death a year prior but was instructed to keep silent on the matter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:50:47">[14:50:47]</a>. The speaker suggests that the current military actions continue because those in power are "interested in its continuation" to "prolong power" and "strengthen it" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:27:47">[15:27:47]</a>.

The recognition of Putin as president by the USA is attributed to the fact that "he was recognized as the president of Russia by the Russians" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:44:47">[35:44:47]</a>. The speaker argues that the U.S. State Department should not impose its opinion on Russian citizens, especially if they "like to live with a double" or "not to see the obvious" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="35:56:47">[35:56:47]</a>.

Recent appointments of individuals claimed to be Putin's relatives to government positions are viewed as a "smokescreen" to create the illusion that the real Putin is alive, despite his historical practice of not appointing relatives to such roles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:56:47">[41:56:47]</a>.

## State Propaganda and Public Perception

State propaganda is seen as a major factor in the "zombified part of the Russian population" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:41:47">[02:41:47]</a>. To counter this, the speaker believes the first step is to switch off the "state propaganda machine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:47">[03:13:47]</a>. This would allow a "significant part of society" to "come to its senses immediately" within a "relatively short time," up to six months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:23:47">[03:23:47]</a>. While a minority might persist in their delusions, the primary goal is a "change of climate in the country from malignant to benign" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:41:47">[03:41:47]</a>.

Despite ongoing political events, a considerable portion of the population remains oblivious or dismissive of significant changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:14:47">[33:14:47]</a>. This is attributed to a "common cognitive error" where people struggle to imagine a future different from the present, resulting in "blindness" or an unwillingness to understand <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:45:47">[40:45:47]</a>. The speaker acknowledges that a "majority of such people" exist, particularly in Russia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:15:47">[41:15:47]</a>.

## Internal Power Dynamics and Elite Conflict

The speaker identifies a serious and "unstoppable" conflict within the elites as the starting point of a [[political_crisis_and_instability_in_russia | crisis]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:05:47">[13:05:47]</a>. The ruling regime is perceived as having "outlived its usefulness" by the very people who support it, leading to "fierce discussions" and "political actions" aimed at transitioning to a "new quality" of governance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:27:47">[12:27:47]</a>.

Two key power blocs mentioned are the Patrushev bloc and the Chemezov bloc. While Patrushev remains influential, his influence is now shared with the Chemezov group, which is "no less influential" particularly in socio-economic policy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:48:47">[31:48:47]</a>. Neither Patrushev nor Chemezov are described as supporters of replacing the indigenous population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:07:47">[09:07:47]</a>. The policy encouraging immigration from Central Asia and the Caucasus, which was a concern for some, is attributed to the late President Putin's belief that new citizens would be more reliable in voting and more dependent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:12:47">[09:12:47]</a>.

## Future Political Changes and Reforms

The speaker's team, if it comes to power, plans significant reforms:
*   **Abolition of repressive laws**: Laws blocking internet resources will be repealed "virtually automatically" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:31:47">[26:31:47]</a>.
*   **Judicial reform**: Those who committed "state crimes," regardless of their position, will face punishment under the criminal code <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:12:47">[04:12:47]</a>. Cases against officials like Golikova will be reviewed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:55:47">[15:55:47]</a>.
*   **Establishment of a new Russian republic**: This will lead to gradual changes in all legislative acts and potentially document forms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:57:47">[19:57:47]</a>.
*   **Restoration of constitutional legality**: Special departments or commissions will be created to deal with civil and political rehabilitation, as well as property issues for those who fled Russia due to fabricated cases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="34:30:47">[34:30:47]</a>.
*   **Pensions**: Pensions will be "increased significantly" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:59:47">[20:59:47]</a>, and the retirement age will be returned <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:04:47">[24:04:47]</a>. However, equal pensions for all, regardless of labor contribution, are deemed "categorically unfair" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:17:47">[24:17:47]</a>.
*   **Housing and communal services**: This sector will be "thoroughly and radically reformed" to ensure "complete transparency" of financial flows <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:07:47">[22:07:47]</a>.
*   **Political parties and elections**: The speaker does not support limits on mandates for successful parties but advocates for "real party competition" by returning to an "old election law" and forming "full-fledged political parties" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="26:56:47">[26:56:47]</a>.
*   **Film industry**: The film industry, like nearly all sectors in Russia, will require significant reforms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="40:08:47">[40:08:47]</a>.

The "mysterious forces" awaiting the opportune moment for change are described as having a "very serious basis" for success, expecting "noticeable improvements" almost immediately after taking power, with a full recovery and "sustainable development" trajectory within "up to a year and a half to two years" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:03:47">[39:03:47]</a>.
