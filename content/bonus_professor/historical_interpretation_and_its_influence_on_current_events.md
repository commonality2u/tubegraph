## Putin's Historical Views

Vladimir Putin is described as being genuinely interested in history <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:42">[00:17:42]</a>. However, his knowledge of history is considered "very poorly" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:44">[00:17:44]</a>, and his relationship with it is biased <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:49">[00:17:49]</a>. He reportedly engages in "biased confirmation," selecting historical elements that align with his pre-existing views <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:51">[00:17:51]</a>. These views were largely shaped by Soviet-era textbooks, which offered a very one-sided perspective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:57">[00:17:57]</a>.

A key example of this bias is Putin's conviction that Russians and Ukrainians are "one people," not just fraternal people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:15">[00:18:15]</a>. From a historical and cultural standpoint, this assertion is deemed "completely untrue" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:27">[00:18:27]</a>.

## Societal Psychology and Historical Narratives

The phenomenon of Russian society demonstrating a "perverted form of Stockholm syndrome" towards the state and an "imperial syndrome of the beggars" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:30">[00:22:30]</a> is linked to [[philosophical_and_historical_reflections_on_societal_trends | psychological mechanisms]] like rationalization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:51">[00:23:51]</a>. [[influence_of_propaganda_and_delegitimization_of_power | Propaganda]] plays a role in throwing "some of these explanations" at them, such as the idea of "state superiority" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:08">[00:24:08]</a>.
