Valery Solovyov discusses the intricate relationship between historical understanding and its influence on current political decisions and societal views, particularly within the context of Russia. He emphasizes how a leader's grasp of history, or lack thereof, can shape national policy and public perception.

## Putin's Historical Perspective
Valery Solovyov notes that President Putin frequently discusses historical topics publicly, and his former wife, Lyudmila, mentioned his extensive knowledge of history in a book published in the early 2000s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:29">[00:17:29]</a>. However, Solovyov asserts that while Putin is genuinely interested in history, he "knows it very poorly" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:44">[00:17:44]</a>.

According to Solovyov, Putin's relationship with history is characterized by a "biased confirmation" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:49">[00:17:49]</a>, meaning he selects historical facts that align with his pre-existing views <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:52">[00:17:52]</a>. These views were primarily formed during the Soviet era, influenced by Soviet textbooks, which Solovyov describes as "not the best" and providing a "very one-sided" perspective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:57">[00:17:57]</a>.

### Influence on Russia-Ukraine Relations
A significant example of this biased historical understanding is Putin's conviction that Russians and Ukrainians are "one people" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:15">[00:18:15]</a>. Solovyov dismisses this from a historical and cultural standpoint, calling it "completely untrue" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:23">[00:18:23]</a>.

### Lessons from World War II
Putin also demonstrated a strong interest in the period leading up to and including World War II (referred to as the Great Patriotic War in Russia) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:32">[00:18:32]</a>. While it's plausible he drew lessons from this period, Solovyov believes these lessons have proven "harmful and counterproductive" for him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:47">[00:18:47]</a>.

## Societal Impact and "Imperial Syndrome"
Solovyov addresses the "imperial syndrome" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:39">[00:22:39]</a> and a "perverted form of Stockholm syndrome" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:32">[00:22:32]</a> he observes in Russian society's relationship with the state <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:37">[00:22:37]</a>. He attributes these phenomena to psychological mechanisms, specifically "rationalization" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:51">[00:23:51]</a>, where people seek explanations that flatter themselves to cope with a "terrible present" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:37">[00:23:37]</a>.

Propaganda plays a crucial role in feeding these self-flattering narratives, including the idea of "imperial state superiority" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:08">[00:24:08]</a>. This fosters a belief that despite hardships, Russia is a "cool country" that is "respected" and "feared" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:18">[00:24:18]</a>. When confronted with facts, individuals often withdraw and refuse to engage <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:27">[00:24:27]</a>. This entire dynamic falls within the field of psychology and [[the_influence_of_media_and_public_opinion_in_russian_politics | political socialization]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:43">[00:24:43]</a>.

Solovyov also mentions "Moscow syndrome" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:30">[00:28:30]</a>, referring to a form of [[historical_and_current_political_implications_in_russia | imperial centrism]] felt by those who move to Moscow and adopt a sense of superior importance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:33">[00:28:33]</a>.

He aims to demonstrate that academic knowledge of history can be both fascinating and useful for navigating current realities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:13">[00:29:13]</a>.
