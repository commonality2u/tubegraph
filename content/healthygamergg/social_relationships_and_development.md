### The "Failure to Launch" Phenomenon

In psychiatry, this pattern is termed "Failure to Launch" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:16:28">[02:16:28]</a>. It describes individuals who performed adequately during primary and middle school, maintaining some friendships and passing classes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:27:28">[02:27:28]</a>. However, as they age, the gap between them and their peers widens <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:40:28">[02:40:28]</a>. Unlike situations caused by traumatic events, these individuals often have no specific external event to blame for their struggles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:47:28">[02:47:28]</a>. They simply find themselves slipping further behind each year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:28">[03:14:28]</a>.

Many people in this situation may have had possibilities and momentum at age 16, perhaps even finishing college, but their "plane never takes off" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:35:28">[03:35:28]</a>. Over time, childhood friendships and connections may fade as peers move on, form new relationships, and dedicate less time to them, while the individual is unable to form new connections <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:28">[03:54:28]</a>. Despite appearing "okay" externally, they are internally lonely and isolated, with the situation worsening annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:25:28">[04:25:28]</a>.

### From Loner to "Loser" and Systemic Issues

Eventually, the self-perception and external perception can shift from "loner" to "loser," implying personal fault <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:28">[04:56:28]</a>. However, this is largely a systemic issue, evidenced by culturally specific terms for this phenomenon worldwide, such as "laying flat" in China <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:37:28">[01:37:28]</a>, "nini's" (ni estudiar ni trabajar) in Latin America or Spain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:28">[01:45:28]</a>, "neets" (Not in Employment, Education, or Training) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:48:28">[01:48:28]</a>, and "hikokomori" (shut-ins) in Japan <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:57:28">[01:57:28]</a>. The increasing number of young people, especially men, entering the workforce is at a historic all-time low <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:28">[02:06:28]</a>.

The challenge is that individuals tend to accept responsibility not only for their personal actions but also for systemic issues, leading to a sense of overwhelming responsibility for things beyond their control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:54:28">[05:54:28]</a>. Understanding the systemic nature of the problem allows individuals to discern what they are truly responsible for and devote energy to that <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:40:28">[06:40:28]</a>.

### The [[impact_of_technology_on_social_skills | Impact of Technology on Social Skills]] and Social Environment

The advent of the internet has profoundly changed the [[role_of_personal_and_social_environment | role of personal and social environment]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:56:28">[11:56:28]</a>. Historically, communities were tight-knit, with people seeing the same individuals daily, fostering persistent social contacts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:47:28">[13:47:28]</a>. The world was a more localized concept <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:30:28">[12:30:28]</a>.

Today, globalization, remote work, and increased mobility have fractured traditional social structures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:13:28">[14:13:28]</a>. This necessitates a greater need for parents to intervene in teaching social skills, which was not previously required <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:17:28">[14:17:28]</a>. The subjective experience of social anxiety is also increasing because parts of the brain responsible for interpreting body language and providing reassurance in social situations atrophy with online communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:20:28">[28:20:28]</a>.

### Understanding "Karma" and Responsibility

From a psychological perspective, there is "no such thing as a loser" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:54:28">[17:54:28]</a>. This perspective views an individual's current circumstances—their parenting, attachment style, traumatic experiences, and specific social teachings—as their "karma" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:28:28">[16:28:28]</a>. Karma, in this context, refers to the confluence of events that shape a human being, acknowledging that an individual controls very little about their initial circumstances <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:14:28">[18:14:28]</a>.

This mindset shift is crucial: it's not one's fault where they are, but it is their responsibility to act <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:48:28">[18:48:28]</a>.

### Strategies for [[improving_social_participation | Improving Social Participation]]

To move forward, the first step is not to blame oneself for all the challenges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:10:28">[19:10:28]</a>. The second is to identify what one is truly responsible for <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:26:28">[19:26:28]</a>.

#### Focusing on "What Can You Do"

A common pitfall is focusing on "shoulds" – what one *should* be able to do or accomplish <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:44:28">[19:44:28]</a>. This gap between current reality and perceived potential leads to dissatisfaction and can make progress feel worse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="20:17:28">[20:17:28]</a>. Instead, the focus should be on "what can you do today" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:00:28">[23:00:28]</a>.

This means:
*   Forget about future goals or potential <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:16:28">[23:16:28]</a>.
*   Identify the smallest possible action, such as messaging someone on Discord or texting an old friend <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:36:28">[23:36:28]</a>.
*   Success is defined by taking the action, not the outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:03:28">[24:03:28]</a>. For example, sending a message is a success if the goal was to send a message, regardless of whether the friend responds <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="38:36:28">[38:36:28]</a>. One cannot control the actions of others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="39:39:28">[39:39:28]</a>.
*   This approach aligns with the scientifically proven methods of physical therapists helping stroke patients regain movement, focusing on one small step at a time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:21:28">[25:21:28]</a>.

#### Addressing Social Anxiety and Emotional Processing

If social anxiety is a clinical diagnosis, the first step is treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:04:28">[41:04:28]</a>. Beyond treatment, individuals need psychological rehabilitation to make up for lost developmental milestones and [[rehabilitating_social_skills_in_adults | rehabilitating social skills in adults]] that may have atrophied due to mental illness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="41:35:28">[41:35:28]</a>.

When negative social experiences lead to negative emotions, there are two options: process them or numb them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:51:28">[31:51:28]</a>. Turning to technology like phones can numb awkwardness but prevents processing, leading to a build-up of unaddressed emotions that fuel future anxiety <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="31:58:28">[31:58:28]</a>. To break this cycle, it's essential to process or vent these piled-up emotions through methods like therapy, meditation, journaling, or long walks <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="33:43:28">[33:43:28]</a>.

Even if one feels they have nothing to say, communication primarily requires a willingness to listen <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:06:28">[43:06:28]</a>. Learning about a topic from others can be a form of social engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="43:27:28">[43:27:28]</a>.
