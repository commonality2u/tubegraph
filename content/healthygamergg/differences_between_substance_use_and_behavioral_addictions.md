## The Cycle of Cravings and Resistance
When the brain desires something, it initially tries to entice an individual to engage in the behavior <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:50">[00:01:50]</a>. If met with resistance, the craving intensifies, leading to feelings like jitters and eventually withdrawal symptoms such as headaches, stomach aches, whole body pain, and diarrhea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:06">[00:02:06]</a>. If resistance continues, the brain "gets angry," intensifying the withdrawal symptoms further, leading to vomiting <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:23">[00:02:23]</a>. If the individual gives in, the brain perceives this as a punishment for not complying earlier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>. This process is likened to hunger or thirst, where depriving the body leads to negative signals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:58">[00:02:58]</a>.

A paradoxical challenge arises: as cravings are resisted, the brain learns what signals to send to achieve its desired outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:31">[00:03:31]</a>. In addiction, the more a patient resists and fails, the more intensive their cravings become <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>. Unlike most life experiences where fighting against something leads to improvement (e.g., gym, chess, studying), in addiction, fighting it makes things worse <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:53">[00:03:53]</a>. This leads to a cycle where despite experience, life moves in the wrong direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:22">[00:04:22]</a>. The brain paradoxically amplifies cravings because it learns that higher intensity signals are required to bypass initial resistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:54">[00:04:54]</a>.

### Brain's Learning Mechanism
This amplification of signals isn't exclusive to addiction. For example, if a family member is deaf, one starts by calling their name, then raises their voice, and eventually yells to be heard, leading the brain to default to yelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:08">[00:05:08]</a>. Similarly, children experiencing bullying might develop physical stomach aches to avoid school, as their brain learns that physical symptoms lead to their needs being met, even if they're not consciously faking it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:37">[00:05:37]</a>. The brain is designed to get needs met, and if resisting cravings fails, it intensifies them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>.

## Evidence-Based Interventions
Effective interventions for addiction, such as psychotherapy, peer support (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous, Narcotics Anonymous), medications, and environmental changes, generally avoid directly fighting cravings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>.
*   **Psychotherapy and Peer Support** When seeing a therapist or attending a meeting, individuals are not actively resisting cravings; instead, they are engaging in activities that divert their focus from the illness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>. Sponsors, for instance, distract individuals from cravings by suggesting alternative activities like going for a walk or coffee <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>.
*   **Environmental Changes** For instance, alcoholics in fraternities often find it difficult to resist cravings when surrounded by drinking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>. A "social transplant," or finding a new group of sober friends, is recommended to make resistance easier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>. The more one resists and fails in a triggering environment, the more the brain learns to induce stronger cravings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:56">[00:07:56]</a>.
*   **Medications** Medications for addiction, such as naltrexone and buprenorphine for opioid addiction, directly reduce or eliminate cravings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:11">[00:08:11]</a>. Buprenorphine, a partial opioid agonist, partially satisfies the opioid receptor, thereby eliminating cravings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.

The overarching strategy in addiction psychiatry is to avoid fighting cravings directly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:43">[00:08:43]</a>. Grappling with cravings repeatedly does not typically lead to success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:58">[00:08:58]</a>.

## Differences Between Substance Use and Behavioral Addictions
[[addiction_and_compulsive_behavior_analysis | Behavioral addictions]], such as [[understanding_pornography_addiction | pornography addiction]], have some key differences from [[ADHD and substance use disorders | substance use disorders]] that can be leveraged for treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:25">[00:09:25]</a>.

### Strategic Resistance: Picking Your Battles
Instead of randomly resisting cravings and failing (which intensifies future cravings), a strategy of "picking your battles" can be effective for [[understanding_pornography_addiction | pornography addiction]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:37">[00:09:37]</a>. This involves scheduling pornography use for specific times, such as two designated windows during the day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:46">[00:09:46]</a>. This approach makes it easier to resist outside those windows and strengthens the frontal lobe by training it to delay gratification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:59">[00:09:59]</a>.

This strategy is generally not feasible for [[ADHD and substance use disorders | substance use disorders]] because consuming a small amount of a substance, such as alcohol, can trigger a full relapse due to genetic modifications in receptors (e.g., GABA or mu receptors) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:23">[00:10:23]</a>. In contrast, moderation is more accessible in behavioral addictions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:41">[00:10:41]</a>.

### Waiting Out the Craving: Urge Surfing
A significant difference is the physiological response to waiting out a craving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:17">[00:14:17]</a>. For [[ADHD and substance use disorders | substance use disorders]], waiting longer to use a substance increases physiological withdrawal symptoms. For example, abruptly stopping alcohol can lead to seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, and can even be fatal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:26">[00:14:26]</a>.

However, with [[understanding_pornography_addiction | pornography addiction]] (a behavioral addiction), there is no severe physiological withdrawal that intensifies over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:32">[00:14:32]</a>. The craving may intensify initially but will eventually decrease, similar to hunger pangs that subside if food is not immediately consumed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:07">[00:15:07]</a>. By resisting for a short period (e.g., 15-30 minutes), one can "urge surf," allowing a homeostatic tolerance mechanism to kick in, and the intensity of the craving will decrease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:25">[00:15:25]</a>.

## Addressing Underlying Driving Forces
Two main underlying forces drive [[understanding_pornography_addiction | pornography addiction]]:
1.  **Emotional Regulation** Pornography is often used as a source of emotional regulation, providing a [[effects_of_dopamine_on_behavior_and_addiction | dopamine]] rush and temporarily suppressing negative emotions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:41">[00:13:41]</a>. Proactive emotional regulation techniques, such as meditation, journaling, talking about emotions, or working with a therapist, are crucial for developing alternative coping mechanisms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:38">[00:13:38]</a>.
2.  **Inability to Tolerate Negativity** If one cannot tolerate negativity, they are more likely to turn to pornography <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:58">[00:17:58]</a>. Finding a "greater purpose" in life allows individuals to tolerate unpleasant experiences, similar to how parents tolerate the difficulties of raising children for the sake of their well-being <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:31">[00:17:31]</a>. Purpose provides the ability to endure negativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:52">[00:17:52]</a>.

## Conclusion
The goal in addiction treatment is not to fight battles and lose, as this intensifies cravings and leads to hopelessness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:04">[00:18:04]</a>. Instead, the approach is to "set the field of battle" by reducing cravings and shaping the environment to make resistance easier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:13">[00:18:13]</a>. This aligns with clinical addiction treatment, which involves shaping neurochemistry with medications or altering the environment by making social changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:23">[00:18:23]</a>. By strategically setting up conditions for success, individuals can accumulate "wins," leading to hope and a sense of power over their addiction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:41">[00:18:41]</a>. While individuals may still use, this process helps build a life worth living <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:01">[00:19:01]</a>. If one resists a craving and loses, the next battle will likely be harder to win <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:10">[00:19:10]</a>.
