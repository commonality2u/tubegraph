Tiredness is a complex feeling resulting from an amalgamation of mental and physiological signals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:06">[00:00:06]</a>. When considering the physiological causes of tiredness, two main categories emerge: the circadian bucket and the metabolic bucket <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:41">[00:03:41]</a>. The circadian bucket relates to the body's natural cycles and rhythms, impacting how we feel tired at different times of the day, our ability to wake up, and our sleep quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>. This dimension of tiredness is distinct from energy levels related to metabolism, although it influences the subjective feeling of energy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.

## Understanding Circadian Rhythms

Our body naturally goes through various cycles throughout the day, influencing when we feel more or less tired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:54">[00:03:54]</a>. Typically, activity levels are highest during the day, with individuals waking around 6 AM, working until 6 PM, and winding down for sleep by midnight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>. However, this natural rhythm can be disrupted, leading to [[circadian_rhythm_disorders | circadian rhythm disorders]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:38">[00:07:38]</a>.

### Circadian Rhythm Disorders

When an individual's [[circadian_rhythm_disorders | circadian rhythm]] is out of sync with societal norms, they may feel exhausted during typical working hours but experience peak productivity between 12 AM and 4 AM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:21">[00:08:21]</a>. This misalignment can make it feel like they have less energy than others, even if their total energy capacity is similar; they are simply "jet-lagged" compared to society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:18">[00:09:18]</a>.

Modern society contributes to [[circadian_rhythm_disorders | circadian rhythm disorders]] because we are less bound by the sun and moon's natural cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:43">[00:11:43]</a>. Factors like blue light from screens suppress melatonin production, a hormone from the pineal gland that helps regulate sleep and maintain schedule <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:55">[00:11:55]</a>. The constant availability of activities and services 24/7 also encourages people to stay awake and active at times that naturally interfere with their circadian rhythm <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:25">[00:12:25]</a>.

One notable example is the shift in teenagers' [[circadian_rhythm_disorders | circadian rhythm]] during puberty and growth spurts, which causes them to naturally shift to a later sleep-wake cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:30">[00:09:30]</a>. Research suggests that later school start times (e.g., 9 AM or 10 AM instead of 8 AM) can lead to better outcomes for teenagers due to this physiological shift <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:33">[00:09:33]</a>.

### Sleep Apnea

A significant medical condition affecting sleep and circadian rhythm is sleep apnea, which impacts an estimated 5-8% of the global population <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:24">[00:06:24]</a>. This condition causes periods of breathing cessation during sleep, leading to oxygen deprivation and triggering a survival response that involves a rush of adrenaline <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. Individuals with sleep apnea may experience dozens or even hundreds of these "apneic episodes" per night, leaving their bodies "ragged" and exhausted upon waking, despite having spent enough time in bed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:27">[00:07:27]</a>.

Common signs of sleep apnea include:
*   Waking up exhausted despite 8-9 hours of sleep, feeling as though 12-13 hours are needed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:37">[00:10:37]</a>.
*   Snoring, which often occurs when the soft palate, epiglottis, or other structures in the throat block airways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>.
*   Obesity or a thick neck, which can increase the risk of sleep apnea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:18">[00:11:18]</a>.

Treatment for sleep apnea often involves medical care, such as continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) devices, or lifestyle changes like weight loss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:14">[00:10:14]</a>.

## Natural Energy Fluctuations

Beyond broad circadian rhythms, human activity naturally fluctuates throughout the day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:42">[00:13:42]</a>. For example, there's evidence that cognitive activity between 4 AM and 6 AM can be highly productive, a phenomenon observed across various religious traditions that practice early morning prayer or meditation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:17">[00:14:17]</a>. This suggests a natural period of peace and focus for the mind <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:35">[00:14:35]</a>. Many individuals who identify as "night owls" actually find their best creative work in the early morning hours (3 AM-4 AM), which aligns with this natural circadian fluctuation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:41">[00:14:41]</a>.

There are also natural periods of lower activity, such as the common afternoon crash <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>. This phenomenon, similar to a "siesta" observed in some cultures and across the mammalian kingdom, indicates a biological tendency to quiet down during the hottest part of the day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:49">[00:14:49]</a>. The afternoon crash is often a combination of being hungry from rushing breakfast, the wearing off of caffeine acting as an appetite suppressant, a large midday meal leading to an insulin spike and food coma, and the natural circadian dip around 2 PM <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:40">[00:36:40]</a>.

### [[sleep_architecture_and_its_impact_on_energy_levels | Sleep Cycles and Architecture]]

[[sleep_architecture_and_its_impact_on_energy_levels | Sleep architecture]] refers to the progression through different sleep stages: Stage 1, 2, 3, 4, and REM sleep <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:18">[00:15:18]</a>. The brain cycles through these stages multiple times during the night <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:02">[00:16:02]</a>. Waking up in the middle of a sleep cycle can lead to grogginess, even if the total sleep duration is sufficient <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:12">[00:16:12]</a>. Some individuals may feel more rested after 6 hours of sleep than 7-8 hours, depending on when they wake up within their sleep cycles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:36">[00:16:36]</a>.

The common recommendation of "8 hours of sleep" is an average based on population data, not necessarily the ideal for every individual <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:13">[00:17:13]</a>. Just as clothing recommendations wouldn't be based on an average size, individual sleep needs vary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:42">[00:17:42]</a>. Longer sleep durations tend to lead to more REM sleep, which is believed to be beneficial for brain function, though its exact mechanisms are still largely unknown <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:54">[00:15:54]</a>.
