## Reward vs. Habit Circuits in the Brain

The brain utilizes two distinct circuits for behavior:

1.  **Reward Circuit (Dopaminergic System)**: Initially, behaviors are reinforced by the release of dopamine, which provides pleasure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:19">[00:01:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:22">[00:02:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. This system, including areas like the nucleus accumbens and mesolimbic system, drives initial enjoyment and behavioral reinforcement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:38">[00:01:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>. For example, the first time someone plays a video game, it's highly pleasurable due to [[effects_of_dopamine_on_behavior_and_addiction | dopamine]] release <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>. Similarly, training a dog involves giving treats (dopamine hits) for desired actions like fetching <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>.

2.  **Habit Circuit (Cannabinoid System)**: Once a behavior becomes a habit, the primary neurotransmitter shifts from dopamine to cannabinoids <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:23">[00:01:23]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:29">[00:01:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:10">[00:02:10]</a>. This circuit operates in "autopilot" mode, meaning the behavior no longer provides a reward or pleasure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:33">[00:02:33]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:06">[00:05:06]</a>. Despite the lack of pleasure, individuals are still driven to engage in the habit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:52">[00:01:52]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:11">[00:05:11]</a>. This is why people addicted to video games might continue playing even when it's no longer fun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:49">[00:01:49]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:17">[00:05:17]</a>. Brushing teeth is another example of a habit that typically offers no inherent reward <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:39">[00:02:39]</a>.

## Marijuana's Impact on Habits

Marijuana appears to interfere with this habit circuit, making it more difficult for individuals to change or break habits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:30">[00:03:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>. Clinical observations suggest that people who use marijuana tend to fall into "bad habits" more easily and find it very challenging to escape them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:50">[00:03:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:55">[00:03:55]</a>. Conversely, when individuals achieve sobriety from marijuana, their ability to modify or break habits significantly improves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>. The exact mechanism is not fully understood, but it is suspected to involve the interaction of cannabis compounds with the brain's cannabinoid receptors, which govern [[addiction_and_compulsive_behavior_analysis | habit formation]] and execution <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:46">[00:05:46]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:56">[00:05:56]</a>. This presents a major [[challenges_of_breaking_habits_associated_with_marijuana_use | challenge for breaking habits associated with marijuana use]].

## Individual Variability

The effects of marijuana are not uniform across all individuals. While scientific studies average effects across many people, the substance interacts with an individual's unique brain, receptors, and metabolism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:19">[00:07:19]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:26">[00:07:26]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:42">[00:07:42]</a>.
