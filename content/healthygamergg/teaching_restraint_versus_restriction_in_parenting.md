When addressing behavioral issues in children, particularly teenagers, two contrasting parenting philosophies emerge: restriction and restraint. While restriction focuses on external control and punishment, teaching restraint emphasizes fostering internal self-regulation and understanding in the child <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:50">[00:26:50]</a>.

The father's initial response was to diagnose the problem as gaming and implement punishments:
*   Grounding the son from his PS4 and Switch <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:02">[00:01:02]</a>.

This approach creates a power struggle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:02">[00:09:02]</a>, where the child learns to fight against the parent's control <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:40">[00:33:40]</a>. ## Embracing Restraint: A New Approach

Teaching restraint focuses on building a collaborative relationship with the child and fostering their internal self-regulation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:50">[00:26:50]</a>. This approach shifts from forcing compliance to supporting the child's growth and understanding.

### Key Strategies for Restraint

1.  **Open Dialogue and Understanding:**
    *   Instead of assuming the problem or dictating solutions, parents should ask their children what's going on <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:52">[00:07:52]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:39">[00:19:39]</a>.
    *   Engage in conversation to understand the child's struggles, like potential depression, ADHD, or fears about academic performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:13">[00:11:13]</a>.
    *   Acknowledge that video games might be a coping mechanism rather than the root problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:39">[00:11:39]</a>. If a child resorts to playing a Game Boy, the core issue is not simply "video game addiction" but likely underlying distress <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:51">[00:11:51]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:05">[00:12:05]</a>.
    *   Ask: "What's going on? Can I help?" instead of immediately attempting to "fix" or punish <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:47">[00:13:47]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:02">[00:14:02]</a>.

2.  **Building an Alliance:**
    *   Parents must work *with* their kids, not against them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:19">[00:17:19]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:19:02">[00:19:02]</a>.
    *   Support the child in achieving *their* goals <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:57">[00:20:57]</a>. For example, if a child wants to go to college, offer help with that goal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:07">[00:20:07]</a>.
    *   Understand that addiction cannot be "kicked" from the outside; the individual must want to change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:55">[00:33:55]</a>.

3.  **Setting Healthy, Collaborative Limits:**
    *   Instead of imposing rules, involve the child in setting boundaries. A powerful technique is to ask a child, in advance, about how they want the parent to act if they resist a decision later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:47">[00:21:47]</a>.
    *   For example, if a child agrees to start a Jiu-Jitsu class, ask them: "Next week when it's time to go and you tell me you don't want to go, do you want me to hold you accountable to what you want now, or do you want me to just let it slide?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:54">[00:21:54]</a>.
    *   This gives the child agency and can lead to them requesting parental accountability, transforming an antagonistic interaction into a shared success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:29">[00:22:29]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:25">[00:23:25]</a>.

4.  **Allowing Children to Fail:**
    *   Parents must learn to let their children face the consequences of their actions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:49">[00:28:49]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:07">[00:31:07]</a>.
    *   If a child is given responsibility (e.g., maintaining a certain GPA for privileges), the parent must avoid nagging or taking over that responsibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:52">[00:29:52]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:01">[00:30:01]</a>.
    *   Protecting children from natural consequences infantilizes them and prevents them from learning self-sufficiency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:54">[00:31:54]</a>.

> [!example] Restraint Over Restriction
> "It's My Philosophy that it's better to teach a child restraint rather than restriction" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>. This means giving children the option to regulate themselves, rather than imposing external controls <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:56">[00:26:56]</a>.

*   Old parenting styles, which relied more on control and less on [[Importance of Communication Between Parents and Children | communication]], are often insufficient for the complexities of modern youth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:39">[00:34:39]</a><a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:51">[00:34:51]</a>.

By understanding their children and building strong alliances, they can help them develop the [[Understanding discipline as an emotion | self-discipline]] and emotional awareness needed to navigate a complex world.
