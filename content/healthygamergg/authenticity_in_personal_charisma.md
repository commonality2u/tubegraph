## The Role of Authenticity

A primary way to boost charisma is to increase one's authenticity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:47:00">[15:47:00]</a>. People are naturally drawn to what is inviting and trustworthy, much like a campfire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:58:00">[15:58:00]</a>. If others cannot trust an individual, they will not be attracted to them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:20:00">[16:20:00]</a>.

In a society built on distrust, where deception is common in dating, employment, and relationships, being authentic can feel counterintuitive and may even lead to negative consequences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:26:00">[16:26:00]</a>. However, developing authenticity makes people more drawn to you because they know what to expect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:59:00">[16:59:00]</a>. Being a trustworthy individual in a distrustful world is a significant contributor to charisma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:10:00">[17:10:00]</a>.

### Nuances of Authenticity

While authenticity is vital, it's crucial to avoid simply "spewing all that crap" or sharing "verbal diarrhea" stemming from trauma <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:28:00">[17:28:00]</a>. The goal is to be an authentic "best self," which can include some negativity, as it showcases genuineness <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:42:00">[17:42:00]</a>. However, sharing every traumatic detail is more appropriate for a therapist than a casual interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:05:00">[18:05:00]</a>.

[[balancing_personal_authenticity_with_public_perception | Authenticity should be tempered with compassion]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:34:00">[18:34:00]</a>. In East Asian traditions with Buddhist and Hindu roots, truthfulness (Satya) is subject to non-violence (Ahimsa) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:44:00">[18:44:00]</a>. This means truthfulness should serve compassion, guiding what to share and when <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:10:00">[19:10:00]</a>.
