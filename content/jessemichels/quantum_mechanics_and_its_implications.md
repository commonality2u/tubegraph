Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:23:59">[07:23:59]</a>. Early physicists initially believed everything was a particle, then transitioned to the concept of everything being a field when quantum mechanics emerged <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:19:08">[07:19:08]</a>.

## Core Concepts

### Quantum Indeterminacy and Superposition
In quantum mechanics, particles can exist in a state of superposition, meaning they are in multiple states simultaneously, like a wave or an interference pattern <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:14:03">[07:14:03]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:14:03">[21:14:03]</a>. This means that before observation, subatomic particles do not exist in discrete locations; their locations are probabilistic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="37:19:09">[37:19:09]</a>. This phenomenon suggests that reality might be based on information theory or quantum code that humans are rendering in real-time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:56:04">[06:56:04]</a>. The universe being in states of superposition until measurement or observation is considered one of nature's mysteries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:21:49">[21:21:49]</a>.

### Wave Function Collapse
A key problem in quantum mechanics, particularly with the Copenhagen interpretation, is how a probability wave collapses to one specific possibility <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:24:00">[27:24:00]</a>. There is no known mathematical way to directly transition from the probability function to a single outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:32:00">[27:32:00]</a>. The only way "real probability" exists is if something is run multiple times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:22:00">[28:22:00]</a>, which lends itself to the idea of a simulated universe <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="28:53:00">[28:53:00]</a>.

### Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle
This principle states that the more precisely one measures a particle's position, the less precisely its momentum can be known, and vice versa <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:05:00">[22:05:00]</a>. This concept is likened to a "computational caching function," where only so much can be stored in local memory <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:12:00">[22:12:00]</a>. Similarly, there is quantum indeterminacy between time and level of energy, implying that precise measurements of time and energy trade against each other, and high energy levels might disrupt time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="54:03:00">[54:03:00]</a>.
