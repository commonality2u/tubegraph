## Current Space Market Limitations

Currently, the space market is broadly split between imaging and internet services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:29:00">[18:29:00]</a>.
*   **Imaging**: This includes government reconnaissance and national security, but it's not a massive market and is dominated by a few satellite manufacturers and launch providers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:34:00">[18:34:00]</a>. The commercial imaging market is even smaller and more speculative <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:43:00">[18:43:00]</a>.
*   **Internet Service Provision**: Dominated by Low Earth Orbit (LEO) mega-constellations like Starlink, the unit economics for LEO internet are somewhat speculative due to the need for expensive ground stations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:47:00">[18:47:00]</a>. Only well-capitalized incumbents like SpaceX and Amazon can compete in this sector <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:12:00">[19:12:00]</a>.

Traditionally, once a satellite is launched, there's no immediate need for another launch for that specific satellite <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="19:26:00">[19:26:00]</a>. This model doesn't provide the continuous demand necessary for the launch industry to thrive and prevent collapse, as seen with past programs like Saturn V or the Space Shuttle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:58:00">[09:58:00]</a>.

The promise of gold, rather than exploration alone, created the sustainable drive for people to move to California, leading to infrastructure development and further exploration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:26:00">[10:26:00]</a>.
