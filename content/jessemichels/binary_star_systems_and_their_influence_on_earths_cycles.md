## Evidence for a Binary Star Companion

Initial evidence for a binary star companion comes from the Pioneer 10 and 11 probes, launched in the 1970s to study gravitational influences on outer planets like Uranus and Neptune <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:36">[00:48:36]</a>.
*   **Pioneer Probe Findings**: In the early 1980s, Pioneer 10 and 11 became the first probes to exit our inner solar system, passing beyond the Kuiper Belt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:15">[00:49:15]</a>. They detected a large, mysterious planet (initially called Planet 10, now Planet 9 after Pluto's demotion) beyond the Kuiper Belt, with a mass four to five times that of Earth <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:41">[00:52:41]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:57">[00:53:57]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:06">[01:06:06]</a>. This planet was observed to be influenced by something else <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:57">[00:53:57]</a>. Both Pioneer probes, sent in completely different directions, detected an "equal pull" of a massive, external object on our entire solar system, including the Sun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:29">[00:57:29]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:42">[00:57:42]</a>. This object was identified as a "dead star," meaning it no longer undergoes fusion like our Sun and is therefore not visible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:53">[00:57:53]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:09">[00:58:09]</a>. In 1983-1984, it was estimated to be 50 billion miles from the Pioneer probes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:06">[00:59:06]</a>.
*   **Astronomical Support & Information Control**: Astronomers Thomas van Flandern and Robert Harrington spearheaded the investigation into this outer planet and the potential binary star companion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:58">[00:59:58]</a>. Intriguingly, both died of throat cancer in the 1990s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:30">[00:59:30]</a>. Following their deaths, another prominent astronomer, Miles Standish, claimed mathematical miscalculations, stating the planet did not exist, leading to information about Pioneer 10 and 11 being largely "scrubbed" from the internet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:13">[01:00:13]</a>. However, Caltech has since investigated and found compelling data suggesting a 99% chance that "Planet 9" (formerly Planet 10) exists, based on its gravitational effects on Kuiper Belt objects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:35">[00:55:35]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:56:06">[00:56:06]</a>. The prevailing scientific understanding is that 80% of all known stars are binary or trinary, making a single-star system like ours a rarity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:36">[00:54:36]</a>.

The binary star system is theorized to be the cause of Earth's cyclical catastrophes and the "great year," a 25,000 to 26,000-year cycle known as the precession of the equinoxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:28">[01:00:28]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:51">[01:00:51]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:53:08">[01:53:08]</a>.
*   **The Great Year and Yuga Cycles**: The "great year" is believed to be caused by the gravitational influences of this binary companion, which causes the Earth's wobble <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:23:34">[01:23:34]</a>. This concept aligns with the Yuga Cycles from India, which describe civilizations rising and falling through golden, silver, bronze, and iron ages, followed by resets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:37">[01:00:37]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:04">[01:01:04]</a>.
*   **Perihelion and Energetic Events**: The binary companion's elliptical orbit brings it closest to our Sun every 12,500 years (perihelion), causing massive energetic explosions from our Sun <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:52">[01:03:52]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:03">[01:04:03]</a>. This expulsion of energy then weakens Earth's magnetosphere <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:06">[01:05:06]</a>.
*   **Past Catastrophes**:
