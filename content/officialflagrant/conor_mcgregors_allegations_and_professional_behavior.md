The discussion highlights a broader issue with highly compensated athletes in intense, aggressive sports:
*   Paying individuals millions of dollars to be "animals" and beat people can lead to problematic behavior when they return to society <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:25">[00:51:25]</a>.
*   [[Conor McGregor's UFC fight and legacy | McGregor's]] entire career has been built on insane, risk-taking decisions, such as going from welfare in Ireland to becoming a sport-revolutionizing, multi-million dollar fighter <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:51">[00:53:51]</a>. This history suggests he is unlikely to listen to others' advice, as his extreme actions have historically paid off, including selling hundreds of millions of dollars worth of whiskey that is "not even good" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:10">[00:54:10]</a>.
*   His behavior is compared to a "Trumpian" tactic, where one wild incident is quickly overshadowed by another, making it difficult for the public to focus on any single controversy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:25">[00:54:25]</a>.

Some argue that society often rewards athletes for aggression and then becomes upset when that aggression manifests outside of their sport <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:11">[00:52:11]</a>. This is compounded by incentives that encourage athletes to leave school early, potentially without a full education <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:52:26">[00:52:26]</a>. This situation is likened to the historical practice of keeping gladiators in cages because they couldn't be free in society after being trained to fight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:10">[00:51:10]</a>.
