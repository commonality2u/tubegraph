## Navigating Controversy

The concept of "comedy beef" or [[comedy_and_comedian_interactions_and_dynamics | comedian interactions and dynamics]] between comics, where they are not friends with each other, is noted as a "wild" phenomenon in the current comedy landscape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:43">[01:06:43]</a>.

### Responses to Accusations and Public Scrutiny

Comedians sometimes joke about strategies to avoid the appearance of controversy. One instance involves joking about adopting a Black child as a way to avoid being labeled racist after a problematic tweet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:39">[01:26:39]</a>.

The discussion also touched on the broader concept of accountability in the age of [[cancel_culture_and_its_impact_on_comedy | cancel culture]], particularly regarding women's perceived lack of accountability by claiming to have been "love-bombed" as an excuse for ignoring "red flags" like a swastika tattoo <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:21">[00:39:21]</a>.

## Specific Incidents and Figures

### Roseanne Barr
[[Controversies and dynamics in standup comedy | Roseanne Barr]] is recalled for famously spitting on a national anthem performance, which "pissed everybody off" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:52">[01:24:52]</a>. Whitney Cummings was the executive producer and ran the reboot of *Roseanne* twenty years after its original run, which was "massive" and garnered 25 million viewers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>. She noted that the original Roseanne "got kind of screwed on creator credit" decades ago despite the ideas being hers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:26:07">[01:26:07]</a>. When asked if she has ever created a show with someone who didn't get cancelled, Cummings responded, "literally no" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:27:20">[01:27:20]</a>.

### Harvey Weinstein
Whitney Cummings recalled a time when Harvey Weinstein came to The Comedy Store to see her after she had roasted him <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:59">[01:36:59]</a>. She chose not to return to the club that night, fearing that seeing her in "that lighting" or with her "personality and eye bags" would ruin the good impression he had of her work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:38:09">[01:38:09]</a>. She jokingly remarked, "I've never had a rape problem" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:36">[01:36:36]</a>, and referenced a joke she wrote for his roast: "Harvey Weinstein has raped so many people, he doesn't have the clap, he has the applause" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:36:41">[01:36:41]</a>.

### Joan Rivers
[[Controversies and dynamics in standup comedy | Joan Rivers]] was a trailblazing comedian whose late-night sets are described as "crazy awesome" and full of "joke joke joke" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:32">[01:43:32]</a>. ## Self-Censorship and Audience Perception

The discussion touched on the self-censorship of some jokes for fear of public reaction. A joke about [[Controversies and dynamics in standup comedy | Joan Rivers]] booking "Montreal New Faces" every year due to plastic surgery was deemed "too inside comedy" for a general audience and was not used <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:33:51">[01:33:51]</a>.

There's also a perception that female comedians must still prove they are funny, whereas male comedians seem to need to prove they are vulnerable or sad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:23">[01:43:23]</a>. This implies a different standard for how female comedians are received and the type of content expected from them.
