## Legal Scrutiny and Rap Lyrics

The use of rap lyrics as evidence in court has become a significant concern for artists, particularly in Atlanta. The climate in Atlanta suggests that it wouldn't be "wise to rap about anything that's really" gangster-related due to potential legal repercussions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:57">[01:29:57]</a>. Artists like Lil Baby, who was perceived to have "dissed" internet personality Akademiks on his album, may be trying to "throw the smoke screen" away from real street issues to avoid legal trouble <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:05">[01:13:05]</a>. ### Streaming vs. Physical Sales

The industry's reliance on streaming is seen as a "coping method" rather than a strategic business move <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:25">[03:12:25]</a>. Unlike the movie industry, which enhanced the theatrical experience to combat piracy, the music industry "can't battle this shit" and instead "go to streaming" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:12:21">[03:12:21]</a>. This dilutes earnings for artists, as money is "distributed across like hundreds of thousands of artists" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:35">[03:13:35]</a>.

Taylor Swift is highlighted as an example of an artist who understands how to "finesse" the system. She not only re-recorded her songs to own her masters but also offered "four songs on the vinyl version" as a collector's edition, with multiple colors, and used vinyl purchases to unlock tour tickets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:48">[03:14:48]</a>. This strategy resulted in "1.1 million CDs, vinyls, cassettes, and just digital albums" sold <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:14:17">[03:14:17]</a>, demonstrating how artists can still leverage physical sales and fan experiences. Her influence even pushed streaming companies like Spotify and Apple to increase payout rates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:15:08">[03:15:08]</a>.
