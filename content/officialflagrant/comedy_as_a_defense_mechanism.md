## Personal Experience with Trauma and Humor
Bobby Lee's therapist observed that due to significant trauma in his life, he developed a "weird sense of humor as a defense mechanism" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:07">[01:20:07]</a>. This coping mechanism eventually became his career <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:31">[01:20:31]</a>. He reflects that if he hadn't had that outlet or if [[comedy_and_standup | stand-up comedy]] wasn't a cultural option, his path might have been very different <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:33">[01:20:33]</a>.

> "My therapist always says that because you had so much trauma in your life, that you had you developed like this weird sense of humor as a defense mechanism and then you chose that one thing that you were able to... " <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:01">[01:20:01]</a>

## Humor in Comedic Interactions
Beyond individual coping, [[comedy_and_comedian_interactions_and_dynamics | humor]] and playful banter frequently serve to navigate social dynamics and defuse tension among the comedians <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:52">[00:04:52]</a>. This is evident in their interactions, where exaggerated reactions, mock arguments, and self-deprecating jokes are common <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>. Even when discussing sensitive or personal topics, the default is to pivot back to a comedic angle, indicating a reliance on humor to maintain comfort and engagement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:47">[00:44:47]</a>.
